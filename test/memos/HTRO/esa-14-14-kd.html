DRIVER #1: DATA CENTER COMPETITIVE MOAT

1.1 Precise Driver Definition

Metric: DC segment organic growth rate + EBITA margin + absolute revenue size Baseline (2025): SEK 1.4bn revenue (estimated), 15.3% EBITA margin (Q4 2025), +39% organic growth Q3 2025, +62% organic growth Q4 2025 Target (2028): SEK 3.0bn revenue, 15% EBITA margin (management guided) Timeline: 8 quarters (Q1 2026 – Q4 2028) Hurdle: Sustain >20% organic growth while scaling to SEK 3bn, which implies doubling revenue in 3 years from ~SEK 1.4bn baseline

1.2 Bull Case Evidence

Evidence 1: Hyperscaler Buildout Tailwind is Real and Quantifiable Corning signed a $6bn multi-year contract with Meta through 2030 for fibre-optic cables in AI data centers. This signals massive capex into DC infrastructure. Corning itself noted that its Spiderweb ribbon fibre achieves 50% density improvement — a direct proxy for the scale of hyperscaler fiber demand. Source: CNBC January 2026 reporting on Corning-Meta deal.

The TAM for DC fiber is growing at approximately 10% annually (management guidance), but HTRO's addressable niche within that TAM (mid-market DCs, regional cloud providers) is growing faster due to:

  • Proliferation of edge data centers (not just hyperscaler megacentres)
  • AI-driven compute spreading to Tier-2 cloud platforms (AWS regions, Azure regions, Google Cloud)
  • European AI infrastructure build (less Corning/Prysmian focus vs. US hyperscalers)

Evidence 2: HTRO's Q3-Q4 2025 DC Growth (39% and 62% organic) Confirms Market Momentum These are not budget-phased cyclical spikes; they reflect:

  • Full 9-month 2025 Data Center growth of ~43% YoY (Q3 2025 data)
  • Q4 acceleration to 62% organic growth suggests pipeline is full and converting
  • 20% of group sales by Q4 2025, but 37% of adjusted EBITA — DC margins (15.3%) are nearly 2x group average (7.2%)

This compares favourably to Corning's organic growth trajectory in connectivity (Corning grew connectivity ~15% in recent years), suggesting HTRO's mid-market niche is outgrowing the overall market.

Evidence 3: Microduct + End-to-End Positioning HTRO's differentiation is documented in technical materials: microduct systems defer cost (build duct infrastructure first, add fibre when needed), enable incremental scaling, and reduce switching costs vs. traditional fixed-count fibre. The acquisition of Weterings (Netherlands, 2021) and subsequent DC-focused acquisitions (DCS in US, 2021; Communication Zone, US, 2024) show deliberate capability stacking.

HTRO's DC business model emphasizes end-to-end solutions (design, installation, managed services). This is higher-margin (15.3% EBITA in Q4 2025) than pure duct manufacturing or pure fibre supply.

Evidence 4: 2028 Target Achievability (Mathematical Check)

  • 2025 baseline: SEK 1.4bn DC revenue (est.)
  • 2028 target: SEK 3.0bn DC revenue
  • Required CAGR: (3.0 / 1.4)^(1/3) - 1 = 38% CAGR
  • 2025 organic growth: ~50% (average of 39% and 62%)
  • 2026-27 requirement: moderating to ~25-30% organic + M&A to close gap

This is tight but not impossible given current momentum and market TAM expansion.

1.3 Bear Case Evidence

Evidence 1: Corning and Prysmian are Vertically Integrated and Targeting Same Market

  • Corning: $6bn Meta contract (through 2030), own glass preform manufacturing (vertical integration), $1bn Arizona plant (BABA premiums), 10.4% global fibre market share
  • Prysmian: acquired preform business in 2024 (insulation from helium supply shocks), acquiring Encore Wire (North American focus), 9% global market share, 50+ country presence

Both are pursuing hyperscaler contracts explicitly. The risk: HTRO's mid-market niche (Tier-2 cloud, edge DCs) is populated by price-sensitive customers. Corning and Prysmian have pricing power due to vertical integration. HTRO must buy glass from suppliers (or from Corning/Prysmian competitors) and competes on services and logistics, not raw material economics.

Evidence 2: Switching Cost Assumption is Untested Bull case assumes microduct differentiation + end-to-end services create switching costs. But evidence of actual switching cost is weak:

  • No disclosed customer lock-in periods or proprietary protocol details
  • Microduct is not proprietary (CommMesh, Corning, and others offer similar systems)
  • "Flexibility" (deferring fibre investment) is a feature, not a moat — it helps the customer, not HTRO

If a customer (e.g., a cloud provider deploying DC in a new geography) can get equivalent services from Prysmian or a regional competitor at 5% lower cost, switching is rational. HTRO's switching cost may be lower than assumed.

Evidence 3: Sustaining >20% Organic Growth Beyond 2026 is Uncertain

  • Q4 2025 organic growth (62%) is elevated by: (a) low comps from Q4 2024, (b) front-loading ahead of capacity constraints, (c) pull-forward from BEAD-related buying
  • Management guidance for 2026 is "normalized" growth (not specified, but context suggests 10-15% range) rather than continued 40%+ growth
  • If 2026-27 organics decelerate to 15-20%, then the 2028 SEK 3bn target becomes highly acquisition-dependent (requires 3-6 acquisitions/year, KD #2 dependent)

Evidence 4: No Disclosed Minimum Viable Scale / Profitability Floor HTRO's 15.3% EBITA margin at ~SEK 1.4bn DC revenue is strong, but unclear if:

  • Margin expands, remains flat, or contracts as scale increases (economies of scale vs. competition pressures)
  • There is a minimum scale below which DC is unprofitable (e.g., do regional customers sustain 15%+ margins?)
  • Organic growth deceleration in 2027 triggers pricing compression

2.3 Bear Case Evidence

Evidence 1: Weterings is a Known Capital Destruction Case

  • Acquisition date: July 2021 for EUR 5.6m (~SEK 56m) on debt-free basis, plus earn-out (up to EUR 2.8m on EBITDA targets)
  • Standalone baseline: SEK 15m revenue, SEK 1.6m EBITDA (10.7% margin)
  • Status as of 2025: Facility closed (per APM brief)

Weterings closure in 2025 = 4 years post-acquisition is capital destruction. Either:

  • (a) Customer demand for duct manufacturing in Netherlands dried up (market risk)
  • (b) Cost structure was uncompetitive post-integration (execution risk)
  • (c) HTRO decided the standalone economics didn't fit portfolio and divested (strategic misalignment)

This is the core bear case risk: If Weterings is a "one-off" (market changed post-COVID, location no longer strategic), it's isolated. But if it reflects a systematic M&A execution problem (integration fails, post-acq synergies don't materialize, seller had hidden liabilities), then 3-6 acquisitions/year at scale becomes high-risk.

Evidence 2: M&A at Scale (12 acquisitions in 3 years) is Ambitious

  • Fröberg has experience with 3-4 acquisitions/year (Ahlsell), but that was industrial wholesale (commodity, fragmented market, integration template clear)
  • Fibre-to-DC is more specialized (customer relationships, technical IP, supply chain integration)
  • Doubling acquisition cadence (from historical baseline) while integrating Weterings and others creates operational risk

Evidence 3: No Disclosed Integration KPIs or Synergy Tracking

  • Management has not disclosed post-acquisition synergy realization rates
  • No disclosure of integration playbooks, retention policies, or capex requirements
  • "Strong pipeline" is stated, but no visibility into: (a) acquisition price multiples, (b) synergy assumptions, (c) expected time-to-standalone profitability

This opacity is a red flag. High-performing acquirers (e.g., Roper Industries, Danaher) typically disclose integration frameworks and synergy capture metrics. HTRO does not.

Evidence 4: Earn-out Leverage and Seller Moral Hazard Weterings had a 2-year earn-out clause (up to EUR 2.8m based on EBITDA). The fact that the facility closed suggests earn-out targets were missed — meaning seller incentives did not align with buyer's long-term success. Risk: future acquisitions may also have earn-outs that encourage short-term gaming vs. sustainable profitability.

DRIVER #3: FTTH TROUGH AND BEAD RECOVERY

3.1 Precise Driver Definition

Metric: Fiber Solutions EBITA margin + North America organic growth rate Baseline (2025): 6.4% EBITA margin (well below 10% target), 16% sales decline in North America (Canada weakness) Target (Recovery): 10% EBITA margin by mid-cycle (management guide) Timeline: H2 2026 inflection point, full recovery by 2027-2028 Hurdle: BEAD programme funding must flow on schedule AND European FTTH must stabilize at current depressed levels (not worsen further)

3.2 Bull Case Evidence

Evidence 1: BEAD Programme is Now Formally Funded and Rolling

  • NTIA (US National Telecommunications and Information Administration) has approved 50 of 56 BEAD state proposals as of April 2026
  • BEAD budget: USD 42.45bn (confirmed in law, not subject to congressional repeal)
  • Deployment timeline: NTIA guidance = funding distributed Q1 2026, construction Q2+ 2026, accelerating through 2027-2028
  • Fibre allocation: 65% of BEAD locations will use fibre (per state final proposals), which is 27.5bn USD of the 42.5bn total = massive fibre TAM inflection

Hexatronic management explicitly guides: "BEAD programme impacting market in H2 2026." This is credible given NTIA timeline.

Evidence 2: North America Fibre Market is Highly Cyclical — Trough Likely Behind

  • 2024-2025: BEAD programme uncertainty caused buyer hesitation; Canadian market (HTRO's largest NA exposure) weakened due to housing slowdown
  • 2025 results: -16% organic decline in Fiber Solutions North America, suggesting capitulation (weakest point of cycle)
  • 2026 outlook: BEAD clarity removes uncertainty; US housing market stabilizing; fibre investment becomes mandatory for underserved areas

Trough timing (Q4 2025 or Q1 2026) is consistent with typical broadband capex cycles.

Evidence 3: European FTTH Market Stabilization is Happening Management commentary: "European FTTH subdued and overcapacitated." This is not improving, but it is stating a floor, not accelerating downside. Stable overcapacity is better than worsening demand.

Additionally, European governments (UK, France, Germany, Netherlands) are all investing in fibre-to-the-home via EU broadband directives (€6bn+ European subsidy programs). HTRO's European FTTH is not growing, but it is not collapsing either.

Evidence 4: 6.4% EBITA Margin Implies Significant Operating Leverage

  • Fiber Solutions 2025 baseline: ~SEK 3.5-4bn revenue, 6.4% EBITA = ~SEK 224-256m EBITA
  • If Fiber Solutions revenue holds at SEK 4bn (flat or slight growth) and margin expands to 10% EBITA, that is +360bp expansion = +SEK 144m EBITA
  • Given current cost structure, a 10% revenue lift + capacity utilization improvement could deliver 10% EBITA margin

This margin bridge is achievable, not hypothetical.

3.3 Bear Case Evidence

Evidence 1: BEAD Timing Uncertainty — H2 2026 May Be Optimistic

  • BEAD approval is confirmed, but state-by-state deployment varies widely
  • Industry commentary (Fierce Network, Benton Institute, Electrical Contractor Magazine) suggests vendor cash flow from BEAD may not arrive until 2027, not 2026
  • Project acceleration (states requesting funding → construction starting) typically lags funding approval by 6-12 months

Risk: If BEAD impact is 18-24 months later than HTRO assumes, Fiber Solutions margin stays depressed through 2026-2027.

Evidence 2: European FTTH Overcapacity is Structural, Not Cyclical

  • Multiple fibre providers (Deutsche Telekom, Orange, BT, Vodafone) all deploying FTTH in parallel = oversupply
  • Government subsidies (EU broadband directives) are capping prices (price regulation risk)
  • Incumbent telcos have zero-marginal-cost fibre on existing duct networks, creating price floor pressure

This is not a trough — this is a new equilibrium at lower margins. European FTTH may never recover to 10% EBITA (5-7% may be the new normal).

Evidence 3: Alternative Broadband Tech (Fixed Wireless Access, LEO) is Cannibalizing Fibre TAM

  • BEAD allocation shows FWA = 11.2% of locations, LEO = 20.6%, fibre = 65%
  • This means 31.8% of BEAD broadband deployment will NOT use fibre — potentially reducing HTRO's addressable market by ~one-third
  • If Fixed Wireless providers (Verizon, T-Mobile, Amazon) win the FWA buildout, fibre volume growth is capped

Evidence 4: Capacity Utilization Metrics Not Disclosed Management does not disclose factory utilization rates or fixed-cost absorption metrics for Fiber Solutions. If utilization is 60-70% and BEAD only drives it to 75-80%, margin expansion will be limited by fixed-cost de-leverage.

3.4 Forward Trajectory Assessment

Status: YELLOW (Cyclical Trough Likely Behind, But Recovery Timing and Magnitude Uncertain)

Reasoning:

  • 2026 Signposts: (a) Q2 2026 North America organic growth (expect +5% to +10% as BEAD pulls forward orders), (b) Fiber Solutions EBITA margin in H1 2026 (expect stabilization at 6.5-7.0%, not yet expansion), (c) management commentary on capacity utilization
  • H2 2026 Inflection: If BEAD projects start construction in Q3-Q4 2026, equipment orders ramp — this should show in Q4 2026 organic growth and Q1 2027 margin expansion
  • Risk: If BEAD impact is 18+ months late, or if FWA wins more than expected in BEAD allocation, Fiber Solutions recovery is delayed to 2028
  • Scenario Probability: Bull (BEAD hits H2 2026, margin to 10% by 2027) = 35%; Base (BEAD delayed to 2027, margin to 8-9% by end 2027) = 50%; Bear (BEAD impact minimal or cannibalised by FWA, margin stays 6-7%) = 15%

Fulcrum Assessment: NO. Fiber Solutions is important to overall 2028 profitability (50%+ of group revenue), but the margin recovery is less certain than DC growth and less strategically critical than M&A execution. HTRO can hit 12% group EBITA margin even if Fiber Solutions only recovers to 8% (offset by DC at 15%+).

Watson Rating: YELLOW (Cyclical trough is likely behind; BEAD is real. But timing is uncertain and magnitude is constrained by structural changes in broadband tech.)

Hexatronic is fundamentally transforming its corporate profile from a legacy Fiber-to-the-Home (FTTH) supplier into a diversified fiber optics and connectivity powerhouse.
