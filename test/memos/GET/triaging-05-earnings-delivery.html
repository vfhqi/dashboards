Brief company description

Section Rating: Yellow.

Core Infrastructure Asset: Getlink SE is the Paris-headquartered owner and operator of the Channel Tunnel concession, a 50km undersea railway connecting Great Britain to continental Europe until 2086.

Business Segmentation: The group is structured into three primary reporting segments: Eurotunnel, Europorte, and ElecLink.

Eurotunnel (Core Operations): Representing approximately 75% of group revenue, Eurotunnel operates the "Fixed Link" rail transport system, which includes Shuttle Services for passenger and freight vehicles, and a Railway Network providing toll-based access for third-party high-speed passenger trains (Eurostar) and rail freight.

Europorte and ElecLink: Europorte serves as a rail freight operator primarily in France, Belgium, and Germany, while ElecLink operates a 1GW electricity interconnector cable running through the Channel Tunnel.

Overview of Getlink's business segments, core assets, and 2025 revenue breakdown

Three-Year Revenue Contraction: The company's top-line performance has contracted sequentially, falling from €1.80 billion in 2023 to €1.61 billion in 2024, and further declining to €1.59 billion in 2025.

Profitability Masking Weakness: Despite the 1% revenue drop in 2025, consolidated EBITDA optically improved by 4% year-over-year to €859 million, heavily supported by a €55 million insurance compensation payout related to ElecLink's operational suspension.

ElecLink Volatility: The ElecLink segment has proven to be a highly volatile earnings stream, with its 2025 revenue plunging 20% to €225 million due to the normalization of European electricity markets and extended technical suspensions.

Structural Freight Challenges: The historical Eurotunnel business continues to struggle with structural headwinds in its LeShuttle Freight division, where truck traffic declined by 3% in 2025 amid intense price competition from ferry operators and a weak UK macroeconomic environment.

Recent Stock Outperformance: The share price experienced a sharp rally heading into the company's Capital Markets Day, rising roughly 14% between mid-December 2025 and late February 2026, significantly outpacing the broader CAC index's 3% gain.

Share Price Context: As of late February 2026, the stock was trading around €17.95, situated near the upper end of its 52-week range of €14.81 to €18.24.

Jefferies' Sceptical View: Analysts at Jefferies maintain a cautious "Hold" rating with a €16.50 price target, noting that the recent 16% year-to-date rally leaves limited positive catalysts, especially since consensus dividend growth already consumes free cash flow growth through 2030.

Morgan Stanley's Bullish View: Conversely, Morgan Stanley holds an "Overweight" rating with an €18.50 price target, emphasizing the medium-term optionality of high-speed rail growth, though they recently trimmed their target marginally due to lower long-term breakeven inflation expectations.

Valuation Premium: Getlink currently trades at a demanding trailing P/E multiple of approximately 33.4x, which represents a substantial premium over traditional European infrastructure and construction peers.

New Rail Operators and Fleet Expansion: To unlock this growth, Eurostar plans to expand its fleet to roughly 40 trains with a major Alstom order. Additionally, new entrants Virgin (12 trains targeted for 2030) and Trenitalia (10 trains for 2029) are actively preparing to break Eurostar's monopoly.

Passenger Shuttle Market Share: In a weak macro environment, Getlink successfully leaned into a customer-centric, yield-management strategy, driving its LeShuttle Passenger car market share up 80 basis points to 56.1%.

Diversification via Customs Services: Getlink has successfully monetized post-Brexit border friction, scaling its Getlink Customs Services unit to approximately €20 million in revenue with 10%+ organic growth, aided by recent acquisitions like ASA and BIMS.

Ferry Competition Remains Irrational: Despite management's hope that environmental regulations (ETS) would force ferry operators to raise prices, cross-Channel competitors continue to engage in irrational price wars to fill vast excess capacity, perpetually suppressing Getlink's freight yields.

ElecLink Profit-Sharing Uncertainty: Regulators have yet to finalize the exact mechanics of the ElecLink profit-sharing agreement. This ongoing lack of clarity creates an overhang on the asset's terminal value and complicates the investment decision for the proposed ElecLink 2 project.

Sell side's focus

Section Rating: Orange.

Sell-side analysts maintain a highly polarized and generally cautious view of Getlink's medium-term setup following the recent earnings and Capital Markets Day (CMD). Management's decision to solely rely on a progressive dividend reset to €0.80 per share for 2026 is viewed as underwhelming and unlikely to satisfy investor demands for balance sheet efficiency.

Getlink is now guiding for €170-220 million in annual capex over the next 5 to 7 years, significantly delaying the expected normalisation of spending until 2032 at the earliest.

Severe FCF Compression: Jefferies warns that this structurally elevated reinvestment cycle will act as a prolonged drag on free cash flow generation through the end of the decade. They forecast annual FCF trending toward a sluggish €300-440 million between 2025 and 2031, pushing Getlink's FCF yield to a historical discount of 3.5-5.0%.

Structural Freight Erosion: Morgan Stanley identifies the LeShuttle Freight division as the primary source of ongoing earnings downgrades over the past three years. Analysts remain deeply concerned by continuous market share losses to aggressive ferry operators like Irish Ferries.

Change in leadership over L12M and N36M

Section Rating: Orange.

CEO Mandate Extension: Yann Leriche, who has served as Chief Executive Officer since July 2020, had his leadership mandate renewed in early 2024 for an additional four-year term running until July 2028. Furthermore, his position as a Director on the Board was extended at the May 2025 Annual General Meeting (AGM) for a four-year period until 2029.

Chairman Succession Delayed: Jacques Gounon’s current term as Chairman and Director is scheduled to expire at the 2026 AGM. Rather than executing a leadership transition, the Board proposed and successfully passed a resolution at the May 2025 AGM to raise the statutory age limit for the Chairman from 70 to 75.

Near-Term Chairman Outlook (N36M): Subject to his reappointment as a Director at the May 2026 AGM, Gounon is slated to be renewed as Chairman of the Board for a further two-year term. This age-limit extension and delayed succession timeline suggests a potential lack of internal readiness or a reluctance to relinquish legacy control during a complex capital expenditure cycle.

Press Release • 26 Feb

Consolidation of Operational Power: Géraldine Périchon was formally elevated to Directrice générale adjointe (Deputy CEO) in March 2024. She now oversees a vast and critical portfolio including finance, digital systems, CSR, purchasing, and M&A, effectively centralizing significant operational execution power directly beneath the CEO.

Major Shareholder Dominance: Getlink's corporate governance is increasingly dominated by its largest shareholders, which threatens to cap independent strategic maneuvering. Eiffage, Getlink's largest shareholder, maintains firm influence over the Board through its two representatives, Benoît de Ruffray and Marie Lemarié.

Mundys Secures Direct Representation: In July 2025, Andrea Mangoni, the CEO of Mundys (Getlink's second-largest shareholder), was co-opted to the Board of Directors to replace the resigning Jean Mouton. While management publicly touted this as evidence of Mundys' long-term strategic commitment, the move further concentrates board power directly into the hands of primary equity holders.

Executive Committee Refresh: Nicola Lyons was appointed as Group Chief Human Resources Officer and joined the Executive Committee in May 2025. Her immediate mandate is highly defensive, tasked with managing significant skills renewal as the company projects a 30% natural retirement rate within its workforce over the next decade.

Reduction in Staff Representation: To comply with the European Union's "Women on Boards" (WoB) Directive ahead of the 2026 AGM, the Board elected to reduce the number of Staff Representative Directors from three to two. Stéphane Sauvage and Mark Cornwall were appointed to new four-year terms beginning in May 2026, while Philippe Vanderbec will depart the Board.

Morgan Stanley on Governance Constraints: Analysts at Morgan Stanley highlight that Eiffage's equity stake is creeping closely toward the 30% mandatory takeover threshold.

Morgan Stanley on Capital Allocation Friction: Morgan Stanley argues that this concentrated shareholder dynamic makes any substantial share buyback highly unlikely from 2027 onwards, as reducing the share count would force Eiffage to seek complex regulatory exemptions to avoid breaching the 30% ceiling.

Jefferies on Takeover Probabilities: Analysts at Jefferies maintain a base-case assumption that Eiffage will likely remain at or below the 30% holding threshold.

Opaque Long-Term Succession Framework: The Nomination and Remuneration Committee claims to be actively refining its succession planning, outlining emergency and long-term talent pool scenarios. Despite this posturing, the explicit extensions granted to the Gounon-Leriche tandem indicate a severe aversion to disruptive leadership transitions, locking the current strategic trajectory in place for the foreseeable future.

Change in priorities/ambition

Section Rating: Orange.

Pivot from Resilience to Aggressive Growth: During the recent Capital Markets Day, CEO Yann Leriche formally declared an end to the group's post-COVID recovery phase, stating that foundations laid over recent years enable a new phase of "execution, acceleration and value creation." This rhetoric marks a distinct psychological shift from defending the core historical business to aggressively pursuing systemic expansion.

The €1 Billion Ultimate Ambition: The cornerstone of this newly defined strategic ambition is an explicitly stated target to reach €1 billion in consolidated EBITDA by 2030. However, from a sceptical standpoint, management describes 2030 as "just one milestone" before even stronger momentum kicks in, strongly suggesting the near-term path to this €1 billion figure may be heavily back-end loaded and reliant on distant catalysts.

Investor Presentation • 26 Feb

High-Speed Rail as the Supreme Priority: Leriche's verbal commentary confirms that the company's highest structural priority is unlocking third-party High-Speed Rail capacity. He emphatically noted to investors that the executive team is "incredibly excited about the opportunity that is international high-speed rail." The stated ambition is to facilitate an additional 10 million passengers by 2035, which fundamentally shifts the group's economic dependency away from its proprietary shuttle operations.

A Questionable Freight "Rebound": Despite multi-year structural declines in the LeShuttle Freight division, the CEO expressed staunch optimism regarding a turnaround, claiming the business is "now positioned for a real rebound." Leriche anticipates benefiting from a strengthened competitive edge and improving market fundamentals, particularly in fresh products and small parcels. This ambition assumes that upcoming environmental regulations will permanently force ferry competitors to behave rationally, which is a historically dangerous assumption to underwrite.

Phase 2 Yield Management Ambition: To extract more value from stagnant shuttle volumes, management is prioritizing a comprehensive overhaul of its pricing architecture. ElecLink 2 Speculation: The company's ambitions stretch deep into the mid-2030s with the potential development of a second 1GW interconnector, ElecLink 2. This underscores a long-term priority to replicate the outsized financial success of the original cable, though it remains highly speculative and entirely dependent on future cross-border regulatory frameworks.

Data and AI as a Core Enabler: To support these ambitious volume and pricing targets, Getlink has elevated technological integration to a top-tier operational priority. The company is actively deploying artificial intelligence for predictive maintenance and operational efficiency, aiming to reinforce speed and 24/7 accessibility as key competitive moats.

Jefferies on the Ambition Gap: Analysts point out that management stopped noticeably short of providing a granular bridge to justify its €1 billion EBITDA guidance for 2030. They note that while the company is heavily relying on new entrants like Virgin and Trenitalia to drive this growth, these catalysts are unlikely to materialize until 2029 at the earliest, leaving a significant gap in near-term fundamental momentum.

Morgan Stanley on Execution Reality: Sell-side commentary highlights that delivering on these new priorities requires near-flawless execution across multiple fronts simultaneously. Analysts emphasize a long list of pre-conditions required to hit near-term targets, including a stark inflection in truck volumes, pricing resilience, steady Eurostar growth, and successfully mitigating the impact of rising UK business taxes.

Section Rating: Orange.

A Structurally Cutthroat Market: The Short Straits cross-Channel market operates as an intense oligopoly, with Getlink's proprietary LeShuttle services battling directly against a heavily entrenched triopoly of maritime ferry operators: DFDS Seaways, P&O Ferries, and Irish Ferries.

The Low-Cost Contagion: Competitive intensity skyrocketed following the 2021 entry of Irish Ferries, which deployed a highly aggressive low-cost operating model. This sparked a race to the bottom, prompting legacy operator P&O Ferries to drastically restructure in March 2022 by abruptly dismissing 800 UK seafaring staff to replace them with cheaper outsourced agency labor.

Market Share Fragility: Despite Getlink's structural speed advantage, this irrational ferry pricing war has continually eroded the company's freight dominance. In 2025, Getlink's LeShuttle Freight market share slipped to 35.4% (down from 35.6% in 2024), underscoring the extreme price sensitivity of haulage customers during macroeconomic downturns.

Evolution of LeShuttle Freight market share against ferry operators (2019-2025)

Consolidation and Capacity Sharing: Rather than reducing excess capacity, ferry competitors are collaborating to maximize asset utilization. In May 2024, P&O and Irish Ferries entered into a space charter agreement on the Dover-Calais route, allowing them to load freight onto the first available ship and essentially mimicking Getlink's "turn-up-and-go" frequency advantage.

Regulatory Lifelines (Social): Getlink's management is heavily relying on legislative intervention to artificially inflate its competitors' cost bases. The UK's Seafarers' Wages Act (effective December 2024) and France's "Loi Le Gac" (effective July 2024) mandate national minimum wages and strict shore rest periods for ferry crews, theoretically closing the labor arbitrage loophole exploited by low-cost operators.

Regulatory Lifelines (Environmental): Further competitive relief is anticipated from the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS), which imposes carbon surcharges on maritime operators.
