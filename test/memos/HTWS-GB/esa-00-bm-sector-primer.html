1. Industry Overview & Sector Structure

  • Global tower market size: ~7 million towers globally. US ~500,000 towers. Africa ~250,000-300,000 towers across ~55 countries. Africa is structurally under-towered relative to its population of 1.4 billion.
  • African tower market growth rate: ~4-6% annual net new tower additions. New builds driven by 4G coverage rollout (still incomplete as of 2026 in many sub-Saharan markets) and data traffic growth (15-20% p.a. in Africa vs. ~8-10% in mature markets).
  • Penetration context: Africa has ~50-55% mobile penetration by subscriber (vs. 80-85% in Western Europe). 4G population coverage ~50% in sub-Saharan Africa (vs. 95%+ in US/EU). This structural gap underpins long-term tower demand.
  • Stage of development: Africa is approximately where the US was in 2000-2005 — still in tower rollout phase, not yet in densification/small cell phase. This means tenancy ratio improvement has a long structural runway but is slower to materialise than optimistic models assume.
  • Key sector dynamics: (1) MNO-to-towerco sale-leaseback wave (2010-2020) largely played out — most major MNO tower portfolios have been divested; (2) organic tenancy additions now the primary growth driver; (3) energy/power management increasingly core to towerco competitive advantage.
  • Comparison to US/EU mature towercos: US towercos (AMT, CCI, SBAC) trade at 20-25x EV/EBITDA with tenancy ratios 2.0-2.5x and EBITDA margins 60-65%. European towercos (Cellnex, Vantage Towers) trade at 15-20x with 50-60% margins. African towercos trade at 10-14x EV/EBITDA — a discount for FX/political risk and lower tenancy ratios.
  • Number of towers in key Helios markets: Tanzania ~16,000 total industry towers; DRC ~10,000; Ghana ~11,000; Cameroon ~8,000; South Africa ~40,000+; Egypt ~25,000; Oman ~6,000.
  • Africa revenue pool: African towerco revenue estimated at ~$3-4 billion annually across all operators. Helios revenue ~$600-650m — approximately 15-20% market share by revenue.
  • Macro tailwinds: rising smartphone penetration (40-45% of African connections as of 2025, growing ~5-7% p.a.), mobile data traffic growth, mobile money digitisation, and growing urban middle class all support structural tower demand.

2. Value Chain

  • Passive vs. active infrastructure split: Towercos own and operate passive infrastructure — the physical tower structure, ground lease, power supply (generators/solar), fencing, and civil works. MNOs own the active equipment — antennas, radios, base transceiver stations (BTS), microwave backhaul links.
  • Towerco position in value chain: Towercos sit between landowners (ground lease) and MNOs (active equipment). They earn rental income by providing a managed physical platform for MNO equipment installation.
  • What Helios owns: Steel or concrete tower structure; tower lighting; land lease; perimeter security; power supply infrastructure (generator, batteries, solar panels); monitoring/management systems. Capital cost per new tower ~$120,000-200,000 depending on height and geography.
  • What MNOs own: Antennas, RRUs (remote radio units), BTS cabinets, microwave dishes. MNOs pay a monthly or quarterly rental fee to Helios for "space" on the tower.
  • Power supply: A key differentiator in Africa. Unlike US/EU where grid power is reliable, African towers often require 24/7 diesel generator backup or standalone solar hybrid systems. Power cost management is a direct EBITDA lever.
  • Ground leases: Helios holds long-term (10-30 year) ground leases with landowners. Ground lease cost typically $1,000-5,000/year per tower in Africa — a structural cost advantage vs. US/EU ($12,000-20,000/year).
  • Co-location mechanics: When a second MNO adds equipment to an existing tower (amendment), Helios earns incremental revenue with minimal incremental cost. The first tenant covers the majority of tower fixed costs. Each additional tenant generates ~70-80% incremental EBITDA margin.
  • Value chain risk: The primary risk is MNO disintermediation. In Africa, this is LOW risk — MNOs are actively seeking to reduce capex intensity, not increase it.

4. Barriers to Entry (BtE)

  • Regulatory/planning permits: Each tower requires individual planning permission from local authorities. 3-18 months per site. For 14,200 towers, Helios has 14,200 individual permits — a years-long replication barrier.
  • Capital intensity: A 14,200-tower portfolio at $150,000 average construction cost = ~$2.1 billion in replacement value for tower structures alone. Total asset replacement value likely $3-4 billion. Substantial capital barrier.
  • MNO anchor tenant relationships: The critical commercial barrier. To build a new tower, you need an MNO to sign an anchor lease. Existing towercos have embedded relationships and preferred supplier status.
  • Scale economies in operations: Helios's field service network, generator fuel supply chains, NOC (network operations centre), and procurement leverage are all scale-dependent. A small-scale entrant faces 2-3x higher per-tower operating costs.
  • Summary BtE rating: HIGH. Capital intensity, planning/regulatory complexity, MNO relationship dependency, and scale advantage make meaningful new entry extremely unlikely in Helios's core markets.

7. Business Model Mechanics

  • Master lease agreement (MLA) structure: Long-term MLAs with MNOs — typically 5-10 year initial terms, with renewal options. MLAs govern per-tower monthly rental, escalation mechanism, power terms, SLA requirements.
  • Anchor tenant rate: First MNO on tower pays ~$600-1,200/month in Africa. Covers full cost recovery plus margin.
  • Amendment (co-location) economics: Second or third MNO pays ~60-80% of anchor rate at ~70-80% EBITDA margin. Core economic engine.
  • Escalation mechanisms: Annual CPI escalators (local CPI or USD CPI blend). Some contracts include USD indexation — preferred form providing FX protection. Proportion of USD-indexed revenue: ~60-70%.
  • Power pass-through: MNOs pay actual cost of diesel/electricity consumed. As Helios transitions to solar, solar savings accrue to Helios (lower cost, same pass-through billing) — direct EBITDA uplift.
  • The incremental margin math: Tower with anchor at $1,000/month. Fixed costs: ~$800. EBITDA = $200 (20%). Second tenant adds $700 revenue. Incremental cost: ~$75. Incremental EBITDA: $625. New total EBITDA: $825 on $1,700 revenue = 49% margin. Third tenant: similar economics. At 2.0x tenancy, group margin approaches 55-60%.
  • SLA requirements: Helios commits to 99%+ uptime. Failure = service credits. Power management quality directly linked to revenue protection.
  • Contract remaining life: Most MLAs signed 2012-2021. Wave of renewals coming 2022-2031 — managed risk but potential repricing upside.

BM Mechanics — MLA Structure Detail

Master Lease Agreement (MLA) structure — key contractual terms:

  • Initial term: typically 10 years with 2-3 renewal options of 5 years each.
  • Take-or-pay minimum: MNO commits to pay minimum rent regardless of actual equipment deployment. Provides revenue floor.
  • Escalation mechanisms: (a) annual CPI escalator (typically 2-3% in USD-indexed markets; local CPI in non-indexed markets), (b) power cost escalator (quarterly/annual fuel index pass-through), (c) additional equipment escalator (fee per additional equipment type installed on tower).
  • Colocation fee structure: anchor tenant (first MNO) pays full site rent. Second and third tenants pay a colocation fee typically at 60-80% of anchor rent. The delta between anchor rent and colocation fee is the incremental margin lever.
  • Exit provisions: termination for convenience typically requires 12-24 month notice plus penalty payment equal to 12-36 months remaining contract value. This makes MNO exits structurally expensive, providing de facto revenue protection beyond the contractual floor.

Revenue per tower economics at different tenancy ratios:

  • 1.0x tenancy: revenue approximately equal to anchor rent — covers fixed costs with minimal margin.
  • 2.0x tenancy: anchor rent + 1 colocation fee. At 70% colocation rate: $X anchor + $0.7X colocation = $1.7X revenue vs. $X operating cost → approximately 40-45% EBITDA margin.
  • 2.5x tenancy: anchor rent + 1.5 colocation fees average. $X anchor + $1.05X colocation = $2.05X revenue → approximately 50-55% EBITDA margin.
  • 3.0x tenancy (US mature market equivalent): $X anchor + $1.4X colocation = $2.4X revenue → 60-65% EBITDA margin.

This arithmetic explains why the tenancy ratio is the single most important operational metric: each 0.1x improvement across Helios's 14,325 towers generates approximately $15-25m of incremental EBITDA at near-zero marginal cost.

Industry Structure and Market Context

The sub-Saharan African and Middle Eastern telecommunications infrastructure landscape is characterized by several powerful structural forces that are reshaping how mobile network operators (MNOs) deliver connectivity. At the macro level, Africa's demographic trajectory is unlike any other continent. The United Nations projects the population to exceed 2.5 billion by 2050, with the proportion of urban dwellers rising from roughly 44% currently to nearly 60% by the end of the decade. This is creating an enormous wave of first-time mobile subscribers. The GSMA forecasts that the continent will gain approximately 800 million unique mobile subscribers, with Africa's mobile subscription growth rate running 70% faster than global averages. Even more significant is the rapid adoption of mobile internet access. Current smartphone penetration remains at approximately 48%, suggesting substantial headroom for growth as device affordability improves and 4G network coverage extends.

From a regulatory standpoint, the sector has evolved significantly. For nearly a decade, the dominant regulatory theme has been the liberalization and licensing of spectrum, coupled with an increasing encouragement—or in some cases, formal mandates—for passive infrastructure sharing among network operators. Governments across the region recognize that allowing multiple operators to share tower infrastructure accelerates network deployment timelines, reduces redundant capital expenditure, lowers barriers to entry for new operators, and extends coverage to underserved populations faster and more cost-effectively.

The intersection of explosive demographic demand, constrained capital availability among MNOs, and supportive regulatory frameworks has firmly established the independent towerco model as the default architecture for mobile infrastructure deployment across the region.

Structural Demand Drivers

The towerco value proposition in sub-Saharan Africa is anchored by a convergence of demographic, technological, and economic forces. The African continent's population trajectory is projected to add over one billion people by 2050, with mobile internet penetration currently at approximately 48%. This means roughly half of the continent's population has yet to connect to mobile data services, creating an unprecedented greenfield opportunity for network infrastructure expansion. As urbanization accelerates—with the share of Africa's population living in cities projected to reach nearly 60% by the end of the decade—mobile networks will face intensifying capacity demands from increasingly data-hungry urban populations.

Mobile data traffic is a powerful leading indicator for tower demand. The volume of data consumed across African networks is forecast to expand at a compound growth rate of approximately 30% per annum through the end of the decade, driven by smartphone adoption, social media usage, mobile payments (with Africa's mobile money ecosystem processing over $1 trillion annually), and the proliferation of streaming media. Currently, 4G network rollouts remain the primary catalyst for tower demand growth, as 5G technology is still in its embryonic stages across most of Sub-Saharan Africa. Morgan Stanley estimates that as operators transition from 3G to 4G architectures, they typically require 30-50% more physical sites to maintain adequate coverage and capacity levels. The eventual evolution to 5G will necessitate a further 25-50% increase in site density. These densification requirements create a secular tailwind for independent tower companies.

Alongside the organic network expansion driven by subscriber growth and data consumption, a global trend of MNO consolidation is reshaping the telecom landscape. Market rationalization toward three to four well-capitalized operators per market, while theoretically increasing customer concentration for towercos, simultaneously pushes the surviving MNOs to invest heavily in network quality as a competitive differentiator. To fund these capital-intensive 4G and 5G rollouts, MNOs are increasingly monetizing their tower portfolios through sale-and-leaseback transactions with independent operators like Helios Towers.

Helios Towers: Business Model and Competitive Positioning

Helios Towers operates as a leading independent telecommunications infrastructure provider, managing a portfolio of approximately 14,800 tower sites across nine markets in sub-Saharan Africa and the Middle East: Tanzania, Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC), Congo Brazzaville, Ghana, South Africa, Senegal, Madagascar, Malawi, and Oman. As of Q4 2025, the company supports approximately 31,944 tenancies on its tower portfolio, yielding a blended tenancy ratio of 2.2x across the group.

The company's business model is structured around long-term master lease agreements with mobile network operators. Under these agreements, Helios provides space on its tower structures for MNO equipment, along with essential auxiliary services including power supply, maintenance, security, and site access management. The critical economic proposition is colocation: the ability to host multiple tenants on a single piece of tower infrastructure. The economics of this model are compelling — while the initial tower build requires significant capital outlay (typically $90,000-$120,000 per site depending on geography and specifications), the marginal cost of adding a second or third tenant is minimal, predominantly limited to incremental power consumption, some additional structural reinforcement, and modest administrative costs.

This operational leverage generates exceptional financial returns as towers mature and lease up. For a single-tenant tower, ROIC typically registers around 11%. With the addition of a second tenant, ROIC effectively doubles to approximately 19%. By the time a tower hosts three tenants, returns can expand to as high as 32%, dramatically exceeding the company's cost of capital. This compounding dynamic means that for every 0.1x improvement in the portfolio-wide tenancy ratio, Helios can expect 50-100 basis points of incremental ROIC expansion.

Revenue Quality and Contractual Architecture

Underpinning its operations, Helios Towers structures its initial tenant contracts with durations spanning 10 to 15 years. These agreements feature automatic renewal provisions and strictly limited cancellation rights, resulting in a historically sticky customer base with minimal churn. As of the end of 2025, the company boasted a contracted revenue backlog of $5.3 billion without assuming any new business generation. This robust backlog features an average remaining contract life of 6.6 years, affording management exceptional visibility into future cash flows.

Approximately 98% of the group's revenue is derived from multinational, blue-chip MNOs such as Airtel Africa, Vodacom, and Orange. Furthermore, around 70% of total revenue comes from investment-grade clients, ensuring reliable payment cycles and counterparty stability. To maintain diversification, no single customer accounts for more than 26% of the group's overall revenue.

A critical component of these long-term contracts is the embedded protection against macroeconomic volatility. Operating in emerging markets exposes infrastructure providers to inflationary pressures and fluctuating fuel costs. To mitigate these risks, Helios embeds annual CPI escalators and power price pass-through mechanisms directly into its lease agreements. The CPI escalators—typically indexed to local consumer price indices—ensure that rental revenues keep pace with inflation. Power cost pass-throughs transfer fuel and electricity price volatility directly to the tenant, insulating Helios's margin profile from commodity swings. Moreover, by deliberately concentrating operations in markets with hard-currency linkages, the company has engineered significant natural currency resilience. In 2025, approximately 71% of Adjusted EBITDA was generated in currencies that are either dollarized (DRC), pegged to the US Dollar (Oman's rial), or pegged to the Euro (CFA Franc in Congo Brazzaville and Senegal). This strategic currency positioning markedly reduces the foreign exchange translation risk for the predominantly USD and GBP-denominated investor base.

Strategic Initiatives and Growth Strategy

The company formally transitioned from an aggressive geographic M&A phase to organic lease-up and cash compounding under the IMPACT 2030 strategy. This new roadmap shifts the company deeper into its cash-compounding phase, heavily prioritizing colocation lease-ups over aggressive geographic M&A. Under IMPACT 2030, Helios targets expanding its total tenancy footprint to over 42,000 points of service by the end of the decade. Crucially, the company aims to push its average tenancy ratio above 2.5x across the portfolio.

Analyst models indicate that every 0.1x increase in the tenancy ratio yields between 50 and 100 basis points of direct ROIC expansion. By marching toward the 2.5x target, Helios is expected to comfortably outpace its estimated 11% cost of capital, driving sustained value creation throughout the 2026-2030 strategic cycle. The successful execution of this tenancy growth algorithm is expected to support an expansion of group ROIC up to 14%.

The newly launched IMPACT 2030 strategic framework explicitly outlines corporate plans to return over $400 million to shareholders over the next five years. This progressive policy marks a historic shift for the infrastructure operator, initiating an inaugural dividend of $25 million for the 2026 fiscal year. Furthermore, this new distribution strategy is heavily complemented by a multi-year share buyback program. The company has already executed $24 million in open-market repurchases during 2025 and committed to a further $51 million active allocation for 2026.

Operational Efficiency and Power Management

A defining operational characteristic of the sub-Saharan African tower market is the fragmented and unreliable nature of grid electricity access across most operating environments. Unlike mature markets where tower operators simply connect to stable national grids, Helios must actively generate, store, and manage power at a significant proportion of its sites. This operational complexity transforms power management from a utility cost line into a core competitive differentiator.

Management has aggressively pivoted toward hybridised and renewable energy architectures. This transition is spearheaded by "Project 100," a strategic commitment to invest $100 million between 2022 and 2030 into proven, lower-carbon power solutions. By the end of 2025, the company had deployed $44 million under this initiative, directing capital toward grid integration, advanced battery storage, and solar power.

Solar hybridisation has become a cornerstone of this efficiency drive. Helios has deployed solar solutions at over 1,500 sites across its footprint, utilizing the technology extensively at off-grid and limited-grid locations. Ghana has emerged as the group's carbon innovation hub, where 409 sites have been upgraded with solar arrays that significantly reduce grid and diesel dependency during daylight hours. Concurrently, the company is upgrading its energy storage capabilities by transitioning to longer-life lithium battery technology. Currently, 71% of the company's hybrid sites are equipped with lithium batteries, a shift that has enabled the complete removal of diesel generators from 100 sites in Senegal.

Supply chain optimization represents another major area of strategic focus. Through its centralized procurement function, the company has achieved significant cost reductions in diesel (its largest single operating cost) by implementing bulk purchasing agreements, dynamic fuel management systems, and remote monitoring capabilities that minimize unnecessary generator run time. Additionally, the company's in-house field operations teams — numbering over 2,000 operational staff — handle over 90% of all routine maintenance internally, reducing reliance on expensive third-party contractors and ensuring service quality consistency.

Rural Connectivity and Digital Inclusion

Helios Towers has positioned rural network expansion as both a social impact initiative and a commercial growth opportunity. Across the company's nine operating markets, mobile internet adoption stands at approximately 48%, with rural connectivity representing a significant addressable market. Because rural networks typically generate lower subscriber revenues for MNOs, the shared economics of the towerco colocation model are essential for making rural deployments financially viable. Demonstrating its commitment to bridging this divide, Helios Towers expanded its rural infrastructure footprint to 6,114 sites in 2025, exceeding its medium-term rural connectivity targets.

Financial Performance and Capital Allocation

Helios Towers' financial foundation is anchored by highly predictable revenue streams derived from multi-year infrastructure agreements. As of the end of 2025, the company successfully secured a contracted revenue backlog totaling $5.3 billion. This committed revenue base features an average remaining contract life of 6.6 years, calculated without assuming any new business generation or automatic renewal provisions. The quality of these future cash flows is exceptionally robust, with approximately 70% of the revenue originating from investment-grade counterparties.

Management has historically demonstrated a near-perfect 0.97 R-squared correlation between new tenancy additions and EBITDA growth, confirming the predictability and linearity of the operating model. In 2025, Adjusted EBITDA margin reached a record 52.9%, expanding by approximately 80 basis points year-over-year.

Deleveraging has been a central management priority and a key driver of credit rating improvements. Net debt-to-Adjusted EBITDA has been progressively reduced from a peak of 5.1x in 2022 following the Oman and DRC acquisitions to 3.4x by the end of 2025. The company has set a formal target of reaching below 3.0x, operating comfortably within its stated medium-term target range of 2.5x to 3.5x. This substantially improved financial positioning, combined with proactive debt management, earned the company consecutive credit rating upgrades to BB- from S&P and Fitch, and Ba3 from Moody's.

Competitive Landscape

The global towerco sector features a diverse competitive set ranging from large-cap US-listed REITs to regionally focused emerging market specialists. Helios Towers occupies a distinctive niche as a pure-play sub-Saharan African and Middle Eastern tower operator. In terms of regional competition, IHS Holding represents the most direct peer, operating approximately 40,000 towers across Sub-Saharan Africa with a heavier Nigeria concentration. The pan-African landscape also includes American Tower, which has significant presences in Nigeria, South Africa, Ghana, Kenya, and Uganda following its 2020 Telkom deal.

The Shift from M&A to Organic Growth

Having successfully executed its previous strategic mandate to double the size of its platform and expand from five to nine markets, Helios Towers is fundamentally shifting its growth architecture. Management has formally declared that the business has entered a "cash compounding" phase, prioritizing the extraction of value from its existing lease-up-ready portfolio over aggressive new market entries. While the company retains the flexibility to pursue opportunistic, in-market bolt-on acquisitions to cement local leadership, large-scale geographic M&A is currently deprioritized.

Instead, future capital deployment will be overwhelmingly directed toward organic expansion. To support the exponential surge in regional data consumption, management expects organic built-to-suit (BTS) site rollouts to account for 20% to 25% of new tenancies over the next five years. This targeted infrastructure deployment equates to constructing approximately 400 to 500 new, highly strategic towers annually, selectively placed in areas identified by MNO demand signals and capacity requirements.

Risks and Key Considerations

DRC Political Instability: The DRC accounts for approximately 30% of group revenue and remains subject to significant geopolitical risk, including recent escalations such as the fall of Goma to M23 rebels, substantially increasing the political risk premium. However, analysts note that despite these escalations, telecommunications are deemed critical infrastructure, and only an estimated 1-2% of Helios's total towers are situated in directly affected conflict zones.

Alternative Technologies: The rapid deployment of Low-Earth Orbit (LEO) satellite constellations, such as Starlink, introduces a theoretical disruption to traditional ground-based networks.

Satellite Mitigant: Analysts conclude that satellite connectivity in these markets acts as a complementary technology rather than a direct substitute, hindered by inherent capacity limits in dense urban environments.

Satellite Opportunity: Furthermore, direct-to-device satellite capabilities may ultimately benefit towercos by bringing mobile devices to previously disconnected rural populations, thereby expanding the total addressable market of smartphone users requiring terrestrial network capacity when they migrate or travel to urban centers.
