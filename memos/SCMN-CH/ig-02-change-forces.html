[W][C] SCMN-CH — Swisscom AG

Change Forces (IG Query #2)

Source: Claude native research | Date: 04-May-26 | Role: RESEARCHER

1. Key Findings

  • The change event is real and early-stage. The Fastweb+Vodafone Italia merger closed 31 December 2024, creating Italy's third integrated fixed/mobile player. Year-one synergy delivery of EUR 95m beat management's own target by EUR 35m (37% beat). The 2026 target is EUR 300m run-rate. This is the only credible, quantifiable change catalyst in the stock — it is not priced in at run-rate because consensus discounts delivery risk heavily.
  • Italian subscriber losses are the dominant near-term risk. In 2025, Fastweb+Vodafone lost 2.7% of mobile customers (B2C) and 3.4% of broadband customers. Revenue fell 1.1% pro forma. This is not yet a stabilised platform — it is still a churning, revenue-declining legacy business with synergy upside running against a shrinking top line.
  • Swiss domestic = treadmill, not flywheel. Swisscom is losing share across every segment — mobile, fixed broadband, and TV — to Sunrise and cable operators. The Swiss market has no structural inflection. Swiss operations generate approximately 55-60% of group EBITDAaL and are predictably declining 1-2% per year in volume terms.
  • The Goldilocks test: possible but not probable at base case. A 35-45% probability of >15% EPS vs consensus in 3Y requires EUR 300m+ synergy delivery in 2026, Italian revenue stabilisation by 2027, and CHF/EUR staying above 0.95. All three are non-trivial. The synergy trajectory is the clearest upside variable; the subscriber loss trend is the clearest risk.
  • Dividend constraint is structural, not cyclical. CHF 26/share in 2025 (91% payout ratio), target CHF 27 in 2026. The Swiss Confederation receives 51% of every franc. Buybacks are effectively precluded; leverage must be reduced post-EUR 8bn acquisition. Capital allocation is the most binding constraint on investment case upside.
  • CEO Aeschlimann is a genuine operator. Computer science + MBA, CTO/CIO background, responsible for Swiss network transformation pre-CEO. Not a utility manager. His fingerprints are on the fibre strategy and AI platform build. Operator grade: B+ (above median for European incumbent CEOs; below Ilkka Raiskinen at Elisa or Eric Sudan at KPN on pure capital discipline metrics).
  • CHF/EUR translation is a structural drag. In 2025, the EUR fell 1.7% vs CHF, creating CHF 21m of EBITDAaL headwind. Italian operations now represent roughly 35% of group revenue and 30% of group EBITDAaL. Every 5% CHF/EUR move = approximately CHF 25-30m EBITDA translation impact at current scale. 🚩 RARE: no European peer runs a dual-currency P&L of this magnitude across two completely distinct competitive markets.
  • The TIM-Fastweb+Vodafone 5G RAN sharing deal (January 2026) is underappreciated. EUR 250-300m of savings per party over 10 years if finalised — subject to MIMIT/AGCM regulatory sign-off (expected Q2 2026). If approved, this adds a meaningful second leg to Italian cost synergies beyond the base EUR 600m merger target.
  • Iliad Italy is not in retreat — it is still growing. Q3 2025: 30th consecutive quarter as Italy's net addition leader. 12.8m subscribers by Q3 2025, 15.3% market share. Iliad's entry-level pricing structurally floors Italian mobile ARPU at low levels. Fastweb+Vodafone cannot reprice aggressively while Iliad is still taking net adds.
  • Rating summary: Change catalyst = Orange (real but delivery-uncertain). Swiss domestic = Red (structural decline, no inflection). Italian platform = Yellow/Orange (transition year done, synergy track ahead of plan, but revenue still declining). Overall investment case: Yellow — monitor for Italian revenue stabilisation signal before elevating conviction.

3. Change in Leadership

Section Rating: Yellow — Competent operator, but tenure predates the Italian transformation. Still proving himself on the merger integration.

  • Christoph Aeschlimann became CEO June 2022, having joined Swisscom in 2019 as CTO/CIO and Head of IT, Network & Infrastructure. His background: computer science degree (EPFL Lausanne), MBA (McGill), early career in software development and systems integration (Zühlke, ERNI), then Swisscom CTO. He is a technology-first leader, not a finance-first or commercial-first CEO. This is relevant: the Italian integration is fundamentally a technology and systems integration problem, which plays to his strengths. (Source: Swisscom.ch/en/governance, 2022)
  • Unlike KPN's CEO Joost Farwerij (commercial/sales background) or Elisa's Veli-Matti Mattila (long-tenured capital allocator), Aeschlimann came up through network engineering. In a European incumbent cohort where CEOs are often ex-consultants or ex-finance directors, a sitting CTO-turned-CEO is a differentiator for technical execution credibility. He personally drove the Swiss 5G network strategy before assuming the CEO role. (Source: Swisscom.ch, FutureNet World profile, 2024)
  • His stated strategic priorities for 2025-2026 are: Italian integration on-plan, AI platform expansion (Swiss AI Platform + FastwebAI Suite in Italy, 25,000+ AI licences sold in 2025), fibre rollout to 60% Switzerland by end-2026. These are sequenced correctly — Swiss cash generation funding Italian transformation. The risk is that dual-market operational complexity diverts executive bandwidth. (Source: Swisscom 2025 Annual Results, February 2026)
  • The PwC CEO interview (2024) reveals Aeschlimann's framing of AI as a structural differentiator rather than a cost-cutting tool. He positions the Swiss AI Platform as sovereign cloud infrastructure for Swiss enterprises — a B2B revenue stream beyond connectivity. Revenues from AI/cloud services are not yet material but the strategic intent is credible given the customer base (Swiss corporate market, federal government IT contracts). (Source: PwC CEO Series, 2024)
  • Governance constraint: The Swiss Confederation as 51% shareholder appoints the Board and exercises influence over executive remuneration and major strategic decisions. Aeschlimann has limited room to make transformative Swiss domestic moves (e.g., forced merger with a competitor) without federal sign-off. The EUR 8bn Vodafone Italia acquisition was the largest strategic move in Swisscom's history — executed under political scrutiny, with the SVP (largest Swiss party) publicly opposing the deal as too risky. (Source: SWI Swissinfo.ch, March 2024)
  • There has been no senior management churn at the Group Executive Board level since the Italian closing (end-2024). This is a positive stability signal during an integration year. Fastweb+Vodafone Italia CEO role: Walter Renna (former Fastweb CEO) retained to lead the combined entity. This continuity is operationally sensible — Renna knows the Italian regulatory and competitive landscape. (Source: Swisscom news, January 2025)
  • Aeschlimann's April 2026 announcement of price increases in Switzerland — CHF 1.90/month per Internet and mobile subscription from April 2026 — signals willingness to push pricing in the domestic market. This is a positive commercial signal against the backdrop of Swiss share erosion, though it carries churn risk. Peer comparison: similar to KPN's repricing of legacy PSTN lines. (Source: Swisscom.ch news, January 2026)
  • Risk: a technology-native CEO's weakness in a dual-market operating environment is commercial prioritisation. The Italian business needs subscriber retention focus as much as network engineering. Whether Aeschlimann's team has the Italian commercial firepower to compete with Iliad's price-led acquisition machine is unproven. The answer will be visible in Italian B2C net add trends through 2026.
  • Operator quality vs cohort: Aeschlimann rates B (top quartile of European incumbent CEOs on operator credentials). Above median on technology execution, below median on capital allocation sophistication vs KPN's Farwerij. Elisa's Mattila remains the gold standard for capital efficiency in the European telco peer group.

4. Change in Strategy/Structure

Section Rating: Orange — The strategic change (Italian merger) is transformative in scale but creates a structurally complex, dual-market business that is harder to execute than the original Swiss incumbent model.

  • The defining structural event: acquisition of 100% of Vodafone Italia for EUR 8bn (debt and cash free basis), signed March 2024, completed 31 December 2024. Funded via a combination of existing credit facilities and new debt. This tripled the Italian revenue base (Fastweb was EUR ~2.4bn revenue standalone; Fastweb+Vodafone is EUR ~7.3bn). Swisscom's group revenue jumped 37% to CHF 15bn in 2025. (Source: Swisscom 2025 Annual Results, February 2026)
  • Corporate structure post-merger: Fastweb+Vodafone is a legally merged entity (Fastweb completed the legal absorption of Vodafone Italia during 2025). Brand: "Fastweb + Vodafone." The combined entity has 20m mobile customers and 5.7m fixed customers in Italy. Swisscom holds 100% of Fastweb+Vodafone. (Source: FastwebVodafone.it, December 2025 results)
  • The strategic thesis: Fastweb was fibre-strong but mobile-weak (MVNO-only); Vodafone Italia was mobile-strong but fibre-weak. The combination creates a full fixed-mobile convergence (FMC) operator in Italy, mirroring the structure of KPN (Netherlands) or Elisa (Finland) — both of which trade at EV/EBITDA premiums to Italy-exposed peers precisely because FMC generates superior ARPU and lower churn. (Source: Analysys Mason, March 2024)
  • Swiss strategy is unchanged: incumbent positioning, fibre rollout, premium pricing, B2B IT expansion. Swisscom targets 60% FTTH coverage in Switzerland by end-2026, rising to 75-80% by 2030 and ~90% by 2035. The Swiss regulatory COMCO dispute over P2MP vs P2P network topology created a CHF 18m fine and threatened to stall rollout — this was settled with a 2025 deadline for remediation of single-mode cables already deployed. (Source: SWI Swissinfo, COMCO ruling 2024)
  • Capital structure post-deal: Net debt increased substantially. Swisscom's 2026 CapEx guidance is CHF 3.0-3.1bn (versus CHF ~1.9bn pre-deal). This is the highest absolute CapEx in company history. Integration costs alone: EUR ~109m OpEx + EUR ~217m total in 2025; another EUR ~250m expected in 2026 split between CapEx and OpEx. Free cash flow of CHF 1.92bn in 2025, guided CHF ~2.0bn in 2026 — adequate to sustain the dividend but not to reduce leverage rapidly. (Source: Swisscom 2025 Annual Results, February 2026)
  • The TIM 5G RAN sharing agreement (announced January 2026, confirmed March 2026 tower co-build agreement) represents a second structural change — Fastweb+Vodafone and TIM will share 5G radio access across 10 regions each, targeting 15,500 sites per operator by end-2028. Estimated savings: EUR 250-300m per party over 10 years. Regulatory approvals (MIMIT, AGCM, AGCom) required by Q2 2026. This is potentially a third source of Italian cost reduction beyond the base merger synergy plan. (Source: RCR Wireless, January 2026; TIM Press Archive, March 2026)
  • Strategically, Swisscom has shifted from a Swiss utility incumbent to a dual-market European telecom company with material Italian exposure. This structural change has not been fully repriced by the market — consensus treats the Italian segment largely at a utility multiple — but it should over time generate premium re-rating if Italian revenue stabilises and synergies accrue. The risk is that the transition year becomes a transition multi-year, as Italian subscriber losses compound.
  • One underappreciated structural positive: Fastweb+Vodafone's wholesale fixed unit grew 24.4% to 1.126m ultrabroadband lines provided to other operators in 2025. This is a regulated, recurring revenue stream with limited churn risk — and it grows as Italy's FTTH market expands. No parallel exists in the old Fastweb-only model. (Source: FastwebVodafone.it, 2025 full year results)

5. Change in Priorities/Ambition

Section Rating: Yellow — Ambition is clear (Italian synergy delivery + Swiss premium maintenance), but the sequencing of priorities in 2026 is operationally demanding. Risk of divided focus.

  • Priority 1 (Italy): Achieve EUR 300m synergies in 2026. Key levers: completion of SIM migration from MVNO to Vodafone Italia network (almost complete by end-2025), progressive internalisation of Vodafone group services, network consolidation. The 2025 EUR 95m outperformance vs plan (original target ~EUR 60m) gives management credibility, but 2026 requires a step-change to EUR 300m — tripling the 2025 base — predominantly from network cost savings. (Source: Swisscom 2025 Annual Results, February 2026; FIRSTonline, December 2025)
  • Priority 2 (Swiss): Hold market share and push pricing. The April 2026 price increase (CHF 1.90/month on Internet + mobile) is a direct test of Swisscom's pricing power hypothesis. If churn does not spike materially, it validates the premium Swiss market thesis and directly lifts ARPU. If churn rises, it confirms that Sunrise and Salt are eroding the pricing umbrella. Q2 2026 results will be the first readthrough. (Source: Swisscom.ch, January 2026)
  • Priority 3 (AI/B2B): Swisscom sold 25,000+ AI licences in Switzerland in 2025. FastwebAI Suite launched in Italy for enterprise and public sector. These are early-stage revenue streams — material to narrative, not yet material to financials. The priority signals Aeschlimann's ambition to shift beyond connectivity toward ICT/cloud, echoing the BT/Atos/Deutsche Telekom B2B services strategy. The risk of over-investing in low-return IT services is real; Swisscom's B2B IT services margins have historically been lower than connectivity. (Source: Swisscom 2025 Annual Results, February 2026)
  • The 5G rollout in Switzerland is being repositioned from infrastructure necessity to revenue enabler. Swisscom's 5G+ covered 89% of the Swiss population by end-2025. The ambition: monetise 5G speed tiers via speed-tiered plans and convergence bundles. Converged FMC packages already represent 44% of Swiss households — this is a strong base for ARPU uplift, but it is not growing the way Elisa's Finnish FMC portfolio has grown (Elisa's blended ARPU rising 3-5% annually). Swiss ARPU uplift from FMC is more modest at 8-12% vs stand-alone. (Source: Swisscom Q2 2025 earnings, August 2025)
  • Aeschlimann's stated ambition for the Italian business is to become a genuine #3 player in Italy — competing with TIM on quality and with Iliad on price where necessary in postpaid. This is an ambitious framing given current subscriber losses. The 2026 priority is stabilising the Italian B2C base; the ambition of gaining Italian market share is a 2027-2028 objective at best. (Source: Swisscom 2025 full year earnings call, February 2026)
  • Dividend ambition: CHF 27 if 2026 targets achieved. At 91% payout ratio, this signals the Board is committed to a high payout policy regardless of leverage. The political economy here matters: the Swiss Confederation depends on Swisscom dividends as a revenue stream (CHF 581m received in 2024 from its 51% stake). A dividend cut is politically infeasible. This effectively floors downside for income investors but caps upside for growth investors — management cannot accumulate cash to accelerate the Italian transformation.
  • Ambition gap vs peers: Elisa (Finland) targets 5-7% annual EPS growth driven by FMC ARPU and cost efficiency; KPN targets 4-6% FCF per share growth. Swisscom's ambition is implicit in its synergy path — it can deliver 15-20% group EPS growth by 2027-2028 IF Italian synergies fully materialise AND Swiss is flat-to-slightly-growing. This is achievable but not embedded in consensus. The EPS bridge from CHF ~25 (2025 base) to CHF 30+ by 2028 requires: EUR 600m Italian synergies (CHF ~580m at current rates), CHF costs stable, Swiss revenue flat, and no material CHF/EUR deterioration.

6. Financial Profile

Section Rating: Orange — Group revenue growth is a merger artefact. Underlying performance is flat to negative. The synergy path is the only organic growth lever.

  • Group revenue 2025: CHF 15,048m (+36.6% as reported; -2.0% pro-forma, i.e., as if Vodafone Italia had been owned all of 2024). Swiss domestic revenue: approximately CHF 8.5-8.8bn (consensus estimate); Italian (Fastweb+Vodafone): EUR 7.29bn (CHF equivalent ~7.1bn at 2025 average rates). Revenue in both segments declined in 2025 — Switzerland -1 to -2% organic, Italy -1.1% pro-forma. This is a top-line-declining business with a synergy-driven cost recovery story. (Source: Swisscom 2025 Annual Results, February 2026; FastwebVodafone.it, 2025 full year)
  • EBITDAaL group: CHF 4,984m in 2025 (+22.6% as reported; -1.2% pro-forma). Italian EBITDAaL: EUR 1,805m in 2025 (-3.1% pro-forma), reflecting integration costs weighing on margins. Swiss EBITDAaL: approximately CHF 3.2-3.4bn (implied from group minus Italy). Swiss EBITDAaL margin structurally in the 40-42% range — among the highest in European telco, reflecting Switzerland's premium pricing and high barriers to entry. Italian EBITDAaL margin (EUR 1,805m on EUR 7,290m revenue): approximately 24.8% — consistent with Italian market margins pre-synergy realisation. EUR 600m run-rate synergies would add approximately 8-9 percentage points of Italian EBITDAaL margin, lifting toward 33-34%. (Source: Swisscom 2025 Annual Results; FastwebVodafone.it 2025)
  • Net income 2025: CHF 1,270m (-17.6% YoY), penalised by acquisition-related costs (EUR ~200m recognised in 2024 accounts + 2025 integration costs). EPS 2025: approximately CHF 24.60 (implied). Consensus 2026 EPS: CHF 26.81 (average of 22 analysts). Consensus 2027: approximately CHF 28-30 (fewer analysts, wider range). The 9% consensus EPS growth from 2025 to 2026 is almost entirely driven by synergy delivery — not organic revenue growth. (Source: StocksGuide.com, Swisscom consensus page, May 2026)
  • Operating free cash flow 2025: CHF 1,920m (+9.6%). 2026 guidance: CHF ~2.0bn. This supports the CHF 27 dividend (total outlay: ~CHF 1.395bn at CHF 27 × 51.66m shares). Free cash flow after dividend: approximately CHF 600m — barely sufficient to service integration CapEx in Italy (EUR 250m in 2026 = CHF ~245m). There is essentially no surplus FCF for debt reduction or buybacks in 2026. Leverage reduction path post-EUR 8bn acquisition is 3-4 years at current pace. (Source: Swisscom 2025 Annual Results, guidance February 2026)
  • CapEx 2026 guidance: CHF 3.0-3.1bn (vs group revenue of ~CHF 15bn). This is a 20% intensity ratio — high by European telco standards (peer range: KPN ~18%, Elisa ~12-13%, Hellenic Telecom ~15%). Italy drives the elevated intensity (FTTH rollout + integration CapEx); Switzerland is approximately 12-14% intensity standalone. The elevated Italian CapEx will persist until at least 2028 (FTTH target 75%+ Italy). (Source: Swisscom guidance, February 2026)
  • CHF/EUR translation: In 2025, the EUR averaged approximately 0.935 vs CHF (2024: ~0.952). This created CHF 89m of revenue headwind and CHF 21m of EBITDAaL headwind. On a structural basis: Italian EBITDA of EUR 1,805m translates at 0.93 to CHF 1,679m vs at 0.98 rate CHF 1,769m — a CHF 90m swing per 5 cents of EUR movement. The CHF has appreciated steadily vs EUR since 2015 (EUR/CHF from 1.10 to 0.93). There is no structural reason this reverses; Swiss inflation differentials will continue to favour CHF. (Source: Swisscom Q3 2025 earnings transcript, November 2025; Investing.com)
  • Peer comparison on valuation: Swisscom typically trades at approximately 8-10x EV/EBITDAaL — below Elisa (~12-13x) and KPN (~9-10x), above Orange Belgium (~7x) and Hellenic Telecom (~7-8x). The discount reflects Italian complexity and CHF/EUR exposure. If Italian synergies fully materialise and the market starts to value the combined EBITDA at a blended 9-10x, there is meaningful re-rating potential. Consensus "Sell" (10 of 19 analysts) suggests the market is sceptical rather than supportive at current price levels.
  • ❌ Balance sheet leverage is a constraint. Post EUR 8bn acquisition, net debt/EBITDAaL is estimated at 2.2-2.5x (full 2025 figures, including Italian debt). The Telecommunications Enterprise Act constrains Swisscom's ability to issue equity (would dilute the Swiss Confederation below 51% unless the Confederation participates). Debt reduction path is organic — roughly CHF 400-600m per year in surplus FCF after dividends. At this pace, target leverage of ~2.0x is not achieved until 2027-2028.

7. Value Chain — Demand

Section Rating: Yellow — Swiss demand is structurally stable but not growing. Italian demand is in transition — base declining, but FMC penetration upside is real if Fastweb+Vodafone executes.

  • Switzerland: fixed broadband penetration is near-saturated at approximately 98% of households (one of the highest in Europe). The demand growth vector is ARPU — customers upgrading from VDSL to FTTH speed tiers and from single-service to convergent FMC bundles. Swisscom's convergence penetration (44% of Swiss households on FMC bundles) is already high — above KPN's Netherlands penetration — which limits incremental FMC ARPU upside. (Source: Swisscom Q2 2025 earnings slides, August 2025)
  • Swiss mobile demand: postpaid penetration is already 86.5% of the mobile base (vs 59% in 2014). The shift from prepaid to postpaid is largely complete. Future demand growth in mobile is data monetisation (5G speed tiers, enterprise 5G B2B), not subscriber adds. Swisscom's postpaid ARPU: CHF 48 in Q2 2025, up CHF 1 year-on-year — marginal positive momentum from the April 2026 price increase, but structurally this is a high-base, low-growth market. (Source: Swisscom Q2 2025 earnings; Swisscom.ch, April 2026)
  • Italy demand: fixed broadband in Italy is less penetrated than Switzerland (~60-65% of households vs ~98% in Switzerland). FTTH penetration in Italy is 56% of territory (Fastweb+Vodafone network reach) but customer take-up lags infrastructure build — typical of Italian FTTH market dynamics where open-access wholesale (via Openfiber/TIM NetCo) creates multi-provider competition on the same infrastructure. Demand for FTTH is growing in Italy but the competitive intensity on who captures that demand is fierce. (Source: FastwebVodafone.it 2025 results; Analysys Mason Italy country report 2025)
  • Fastweb+Vodafone wholesale demand: the wholesale ultrabroadband business (lines provided to other operators) grew 24.4% to 1.126m lines in 2025. This is a structurally growing recurring revenue stream — it benefits from every FTTH connection made in Italy regardless of who wins the retail customer. This is an underappreciated demand driver. Wholesale margin is lower than retail but more predictable and less churn-exposed. (Source: FastwebVodafone.it 2025 full year results)
  • Italian mobile demand: Italy has among the lowest mobile ARPU in Western Europe (~EUR 10-11 for TIM; Iliad's base is EUR 7-9 per user). Mobile data consumption is rising 20-25% annually across Italian operators, but ARPU is not rising commensurately — the competitive structure (four MNOs in a 60m population market) suppresses value extraction. Fastweb+Vodafone's 20m mobile customers represent ~30% market share — sufficient scale to eventually influence pricing, but only if Iliad's aggressive net-add pace slows. (Source: Iliad Group press release Q3 2025; Italy Telecom Operators Intelligence Report, GlobalData 2025)
  • Enterprise and B2B demand in Switzerland is a genuine incremental vector. Swisscom's B2B IT services division includes cloud hosting, managed security, and AI platform services for Swiss corporate and federal government clients. The AI platform (sold 25,000 licences in 2025) opens a recurring SaaS-adjacent revenue stream. The addressable market in Swiss enterprise ICT is CHF 2-3bn; Swisscom currently captures approximately 15-20% of this. Growth here would not be captured in traditional telco revenue metrics. (Source: Swisscom 2025 Annual Results; PwC CEO interview, 2024)
  • TV and media demand: Swisscom lost 34,000 TV subscribers in 2023 (-2.2%) and continued losing in 2024-2025. Digital TV market is structurally declining due to streaming platform substitution (Netflix, Disney+). This is not a Swisscom-specific issue — it is sector-wide in every Western European telco — but TV was previously a high-ARPU bundle anchor. Erosion of TV attachment to broadband bundles is a long-term headwind to Swiss ARPU. Cable operators (Quickline, regional networks) are also losing TV subscribers, suggesting OTT substitution rather than pure competitive shift. (Source: Swisscom Q3 2024 earnings; ComCom digital TV data)

8. Value Chain — Competition

Section Rating: Red/Orange — Both markets are structurally unfavourable for the incumbent. Swiss share erosion is persistent; Italian market has four MNOs for 60m people.

  • Switzerland mobile: Swisscom holds approximately 54% mobile market share (Q4 2024), Sunrise 27.5%, Salt 17%. Sunrise grew 3.7% in 2024 versus Swisscom's 0.9%. Swisscom's postpaid gains are being partially offset by prepaid losses. The structural driver: Sunrise's cable TV bundle (UPC/Liberty Global heritage) gives it a superior convergent offer in densely populated Swiss urban/suburban markets. Sunrise is not a price discounter — it competes on bundle value, which is a more durable competitive position than Salt's pure price play. (Source: ComCom mobile market share data; market analysis 2026)
  • Switzerland fixed: Swisscom's fixed broadband share approximately 45-48% (2024). Sunrise approximately 28-30%. CATV operators (Quickline, Telecolumbus et al.) approximately 11%. Salt approximately 4-6%. Swiss Fibre Net (SFN) — the utility-owned open-access FTTH consortium — enables Sunrise and Salt to access FTTH without building their own last-mile. This open-access dynamic is a structural competitive threat to Swisscom's fibre differentiation. If SFN continues to expand (38 utility members, nationwide mandate), Swisscom's fibre investment advantage diminishes over time. (Source: ComCom broadband market shares; Swiss Fibre Net details, iclg.com 2026)
  • ❌ Red Queen dynamic confirmed in Swiss market: Swisscom is investing heavily (CHF 1.4-1.6bn CapEx in Switzerland annually) to maintain approximately 45% fixed broadband share and 54% mobile share. This is a treadmill — running hard to stay in the same place. The absolute market position is still dominant, but the directional trend is negative in every segment. No structural re-consolidation of the Swiss market appears likely (Salt is foreign-owned/Xavier Niel; Sunrise is listed and independent).
  • Italy mobile: Four MNOs (TIM, Fastweb+Vodafone, WindTre, Iliad). Combined Fastweb+Vodafone mobile market share approximately 30% after merger. Iliad at 15.3% and still the net-add leader (30th consecutive quarter of leading net additions at Q3 2025). Iliad's model: low-price, high-transparency, no-contract — structurally impossible to out-compete on price without destroying margins. The market is showing early signs of pricing stabilisation (JPMorgan noted "broadly stable mobile pricing" in early 2025; TIM repriced 1.7m mobile lines with positive ARPU impact), but Iliad's continued net-add leadership prevents any industry-wide ARPU acceleration. (Source: Iliad Group Q3 2025 results; JPMorgan analyst note via Investing.com 2025)
  • Italy fixed: TIM is the dominant fixed operator (via NetCo, which is now majority KKR-owned). OpenFiber provides wholesale FTTH infrastructure under the government's Bul (Banda Ultra Larga) plan. Fastweb+Vodafone must compete with TIM and OpenFiber's wholesale infrastructure. The competitive advantage here is Fastweb+Vodafone's own FTTH network (56% territory coverage), which is the second-largest proprietary fibre infrastructure in Italy. WindTre is the weakest competitor in fixed broadband. The Italy fixed market is gradually consolidating to a TIM vs Fastweb+Vodafone duopoly in FTTH, with OpenFiber as wholesale provider. (Source: Analysys Mason Italy report 2025; FastwebVodafone.it 2025)
  • Competitive intensity summary by market: Switzerland mobile (Moderate-High), Switzerland fixed (Moderate, stable duopoly), Italy mobile (Very High, Iliad structural), Italy fixed (Moderate-High, improving toward duopoly). The Italian mobile intensity is the most damaging for Swisscom's investment case — it suppresses ARPU and margins precisely where Swisscom needs to demonstrate synergy-driven margin expansion.
  • Peer comparison on competitive intensity: Elisa (Finland) operates in a 3-MNO market with no major price disruptor — structural advantage reflected in 12-13x EV/EBITDA. KPN (Netherlands) faces T-Mobile/Tele2/VodafoneZiggo but in a regulated market where cable consolidation has stabilised the competitive landscape. Swisscom faces a more adversarial Italian competitive structure than any of its Swiss sub-cohort A peers. This is a key differentiating risk factor.

9. Value Chain — Disruption

Section Rating: Orange — Iliad in Italy is the primary structural disruptor. OTT in Switzerland (streaming substitution of TV) is a secondary, slower-moving disruption.

  • Iliad Italy is the most consequential disruptive force in the SCMN investment case. Since entering Italy in 2018 with EUR 6.99/month unlimited voice/data plans, Iliad systematically destroyed mobile ARPU industry-wide. By Q3 2025, Iliad had 12.8m mobile subscribers (15.3% market share) and was still the net-add leader for the 30th consecutive quarter. Its operating loss nearly halved in H1 2025 (to EUR 53m) — approaching breakeven, which will make it even more aggressive as it needs to demonstrate Italian profitability to its parent company's investors. (Source: Iliad Group press releases 2025; TelecomTV ARPU analysis)
  • 🚩 RARE: Iliad's trajectory toward breakeven is the most dangerous inflection point for Italian incumbent ARPUs. A loss-making disruptor can be tolerated (it has a burning platform eventually). A breakeven/profitable disruptor with 15%+ market share is a permanent structural feature of the market, not a temporary phase. This changes the investment thesis for Italian telco assets materially and is not fully reflected in Fastweb+Vodafone's synergy guidance assumptions. (Source: Iliad Group H1 2025 results, November 2025)
  • Iliad is also expanding into Italian fixed broadband — 0.5m fiber subscribers as of Q3 2025, adding 104,000 in 9 months. This is still a small fraction of total Italian FTTH base, but the directional threat is real.
