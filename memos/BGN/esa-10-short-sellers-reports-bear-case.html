4. ACCOUNTING AND GOVERNANCE RED FLAGS

Recurring vs. Reported Profit Gap

BGN's FY25 reported net profit was €445.8m. Recurring net profit was €362.5m. The €83.4m non-recurring component consists primarily of variable performance fees.

Performance fees are recognised when measurement periods close and performance hurdles are met. BGN uses quarterly crystallisation. In FY25, performance fees fell 31% to €115m. They could approach zero in a 20–30% equity market drawdown. The recurring vs. reported gap is a quality-of-earnings concern rather than an accounting fraud concern. It overstates sustainable run-rate earnings in bull markets.

Generali Parent: Agency Risk

Assicurazioni Generali owns 50.17% of BGN. BGN is formally under Generali's "management and coordination" (direzione e coordinamento) per Article 2497 Italian Civil Code. Specific risks:

1. Generali can direct BGN's product strategy toward Generali-manufactured products at suboptimal fee terms for minority shareholders. 2. BGN's fund manufacturing arm (BG Fund Management Luxembourg, 100% owned by BGN) means product quality decisions are internalised but subject to parent influence. 3. Generali can use BGN as a vehicle in future Italian banking consolidation.

Board Independence

BGN's Board has 9 members, with 5 declared independent. Three of the four non-independent directors are affiliated with Generali. Generali-aligned directors hold the balance of power in any contested vote. Minority shareholders (elected via list voting) hold one or two seats — insufficient to block parent-directed decisions.

Performance Fee Recognition

BGN applies quarterly performance fee crystallisation. Q4 is typically the highest performance fee quarter, creating earnings lumpiness. FY25 Q4 net profit of €131.2m substantially exceeded consensus of €99.8m — reflecting Q4 timing rather than underlying business acceleration.

Intermonte Accounting

Intermonte was integrated post-January 2025 tender offer (95.3% acceptance). Pre-acquisition 2024 NBI was €42m at ~80% C/I. BGN carries Intermonte's cost base while revenue ramp is in early stages. Goodwill and intangibles from the deal are not publicly quantified at sufficient granularity to assess impairment risk. Failure to progress toward €80–90m NBI by 2030 creates potential goodwill write-down risk.

Audit

KPMG audits BGN. No audit qualification, restatement or Consob enforcement action on record. Audit trail is clean.

6. COUNTER-ARGUMENTS TO THE BULL CASE

Bull: "Alleanza partnership is additive distribution." Bear: Alleanza's 10,000 agents are insurance-trained, not wealth managers. Cross-sell between insurance products and discretionary investment advisory requires fundamental changes in agent capability, incentive structure and client relationship model. The 2,700 agents already registered for financial advisory have been in place for some time — conversion rate into meaningful BGN AUM flows remains undemonstrated at scale. Market has not seen proof-of-concept data.

Bull: "Intermonte synergies €10–15m in 2026." Bear: Investment banking is inherently cyclical. Italian SME capital markets activity depends on macro conditions, credit availability and equity market sentiment. Achieving 25–30% synergy realisation by 2026 requires a supportive market environment. The 100+ IB requests submitted by BGN wealth managers in 9M 2025 demonstrate pipeline, not completions. Conversion rate from introduced opportunity to closed transaction has not been disclosed.

Bull: "Recurring profit growth outrunning NII decline." Bear: Recurring fee growth depends on AUM growth and stable management fee margins. AUM growth at 9% in FY25 was partially driven by equity market appreciation. In a risk-off environment with equities down 15–20%, BGN's AUM base would contract. NII guided at €330–340m for 2026 (down from €355m). Performance fees in this scenario approach zero. The recurring profit narrative requires a Goldilocks macro environment.

Bull: "Independent compounder post-Mediobanca." Bear: BGN is not independent — 50.1% owned by Generali, formally under management and coordination. Mediobanca's bid failure does not immunise BGN against future Generali-directed corporate action. MPS-Mediobanca (now combined entity) could initiate another bid. "Independent compounder" thesis overestimates minority shareholders' ability to control BGN's long-term trajectory.

10. SCEPTICAL REBUTTAL TO THE BEAR CASE

Bear: "Performance fees are volatile." Bull: BGN has been intentionally shifting toward recurring fee income for five years. Recurring fees grew 11.3% in FY25. Performance fees are a bonus, not the engine.

Bear: "NII is declining." Bull: NII peaked because of an extraordinary rate environment. The decline is known, managed, and partially compensated by fee growth. Total income above €1bn while cycling out of rate tailwinds demonstrates genuine operational durability.

Bear: "Alleanza conversion rates unproven." Bull: Even a 10% activation rate on 2,700 agents adds meaningful AUM at near-zero marginal cost. The incentive mechanism aligns both BGN and Alleanza economics.

Bear: "Intermonte is overpriced." Bull: Intermonte was acquired for optionality on synergies. If synergies underdeliver, downside is opportunity cost and a modest goodwill write-down — not existential.

Bear: "Generali directs BGN against minority interests." Bull: Generali's commercial interest and BGN minority shareholders' interests are substantially aligned. Generali's wealth management strategy depends on BGN being a high-quality, well-performing private bank.

Where the bear thesis is most vulnerable: The structural bear thesis requires simultaneous compression of multiple value drivers. Individual risk factors are manageable. The bear case requires a correlation of negative outcomes historically unusual for this business model.
