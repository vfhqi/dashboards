Executive Summary

D'Ieteren Group (Euronext: DIE) presents as a compelling value holding: a €9.9bn diversified Belgian conglomerate with a jewel subsidiary in Belron (80% of group value), dominant distribution franchises in automotive, and ancillary exposures through TVH Parts and Moleskine. Management's 2025 guidance of low-to-mid single-digit earnings growth, underpinned by Belron's march toward 23% adjusted operating margins, appears reasonable on a 12–24 month basis.

Yet the investment case sits atop three structurally weakening foundations and one catalytic risk cluster that could violently reset market expectations downward. This pre-mortem identifies the mechanics, timelines, and probability-weighted scenarios by which D'Ieteren's share price could undergo material drawdown (20–40%+) within 18–36 months, independent of whether underlying fundamentals fully justify such repricing.

Key findings:

  • Belron's operating leverage is stalling. Operating margin compression in H1 2025 (14.2% reported, down from 16.8% YoY) signals that cost headwinds (labour inflation, ADAS-related spend) are beginning to outpace pricing and recalibration revenue gains. The 23% adjusted margin ambition for 2025 is increasingly under threat; the street will reprice if Q4 2025 or FY 2025 results disappoint.
  • Vehicle parc growth and glass repair demand are not insulated from new car sales decline. Europe's fleet is stagnating (256M cars, average age 12.7 years); new car registrations are down 3.9–6.6% YoY across major markets. While repair demand lags sales by 3–5 years, a sustained downturn in registrations now will create a second-derivative demand cliff for Belron in 2027–2028.
  • D'Ieteren's automotive distribution franchise is in secular decline. Belgian new car market fell 6.4% in 2024, 7.5% in 2025, with further headwinds expected in 2026. VW Group pivot toward EV distribution and dealer consolidation is eroding D'Ieteren Auto's pricing power and margins. This segment represents ~15% of group profit but increasingly capital-intensive as EV inventory bloats.
  • The Belron IPO, pitched as value unlocking, introduces valuation risk and refinancing uncertainty. Belron is exploring a 2H 2026 listing with an indicative €32bn EV (multiples of 18–20x EBITDA). If market conditions deteriorate (rates, sentiment, sector rotation), an IPO miss or priced-down valuation could trigger forced restructuring, deleveraging, or opportunistic secondary market selling by D'Ieteren—downside catalyst for DIE stock.
  • Geopolitical and tariff risks are explicitly excluded from guidance yet materially conceivable. US-Iran tensions, secondary tariffs on third-party trade partners (China, India, UAE), and potential EU trade retaliation could cascade into supply chain costs, vehicle pricing pressure, and working capital stress. Management flagged these risks for 2026+; market has not priced severity.
  • TVH Parts and Moleskine are drag-not-driver narratives. TVH's equipment distribution aftermarket is under margin pressure from price deflation and e-commerce disintermediation. Moleskine's premium notebook positioning faces secular demand headwinds; recent pivots to smart notebooks and ESG are defensive, not transformative. Collectively, these represent 5–10% of group value but consume management attention.

I. Belron's Operating Margin Compression: The First Domino

A. The Evidence: H1 2025 Results Signal Structural Pressure

[Belron H1 2025 results, per D'Ieteren announcements](https://www.dieterengroup.com/press-releases/dieteren-group-half-year-2025-results/):

  • Reported operating margin fell 250 bps YoY (16.8% → 14.2%)
  • Operating profit declined 15% YoY despite 4.1% revenue growth
  • Adjusted operating margin improved to 21.4% (vs. underlying 20.1%), suggesting one-time benefits masked underlying pressure
  • Additional financial charges of €62.1m from 2024 leverage increase now hitting the P&L

The waterfall is revealing: Belron grew top-line in line with expectations, but labour cost inflation and elevated marketing/transformation spend ate through 250 bps of operating leverage. Critically, Belron flagged lower-than-anticipated volumes in the US—a critical margin jurisdiction—and front-loaded transformation costs that were supposed to be temporary.

B. The Structural Squeeze: Labour Inflation vs. ADAS Monetization

Two conflicting forces are now visible:

Cost side (headwind):

  • Glass repair technician wages are rising 4–6% YoY across Western markets (UK, US, Benelux) as labour supply tightens post-COVID
  • ADAS recalibration infrastructure deployment (training, equipment, capex) is more expensive than initially modeled
  • Administrative costs (legal, compliance, audit) are rising in preparation for an IPO

Revenue side (tailwind, but insufficient):

  • Belron is capturing ADAS recalibration as a new service line, pitched at 15–20% price premium over glass repair alone
  • Pricing power is real (4–5% price/mix growth achieved in H1)
  • But ADAS penetration rates are lower than expected; many customers still opt for repair-only or are unaware of recalibration benefits

The widening gap: Belron's 23% adjusted operating margin target for 2025 (previously guided) is increasingly optimistic. A more realistic 21–22% is now the "beat" scenario. If FY 2025 results show 20–21%, the stock reprices 10–15% downward as the market re-anchors on a lower normalized margin (21% vs. 23%).

C. Second-Order Pressure: IPO Debt Service Assumption Risk

D'Ieteren financed a Belron equity stake reload in late 2024 with additional debt, anticipating IPO proceeds in 2H 2026. This assumes: 1. IPO completion by Q4 2026 2. Valuation of €32–40bn (18–20x EBITDA multiple) 3. Equity issuance reducing D'Ieteren's leverage by 300–500 bps

If the IPO is delayed or priced down, Belron remains leveraged at unplanned levels, and D'Ieteren must refinance at higher rates (2026 environment: 4–5% cost of debt, vs. 3.5% assumed). Each 50 bps of additional cost = €5–8m drag on annual profit. Over 12 months of delay, that's a 1–2% earnings headwind—material for a company trading on low single-digit earnings growth expectations.

II. Vehicle Parc Stagnation and the Repair Demand Cliff (2027–2028)

A. New Car Market Deterioration: The Leading Indicator

D'Ieteren's own market: Belgium automotive registrations fell 6.4% in 2024, 7.5% in 2025. Broader Europe (Jan 2026): EU new car registrations down 3.9% YoY, with Germany down 6.6%, France down 6.6%.

[Per ACEA data](https://www.acea.auto/pc-registrations/new-car-registrations-3-9-in-january-2026-battery-electric-19-3-market-share/), Europe's vehicle parc comprises 256 million registered cars, with an average age of 12.7 years. The fleet is stagnating: fewer new registrations + stable scrappage = flat parc growth.

B. The 3–5 Year Lag: Why Repair Demand Won't Be Immune

Belron's business model assumes that repair and recalibration demand is decoupled from new car sales because it serves the existing fleet, not new buyers.

This is true in the short term (1–2 years) and false in the long term (3–5 years):

  • True: Existing fleet of 2015–2020 cars will need windscreen repair regardless of 2024–2025 new car sales
  • False: The composition of the fleet shifts over time. If new car registrations fall 6% annually for three years, that's an 18% cumulative drag on parc growth. By 2028–2029, Belron will be repairing a fleet that includes 18% fewer 2025–2026 vintage cars, and that cohort's replacement demand (which arrives in 2033–2035) will be materially lower.

Timeline of pressure: 2026–2027 = parc stabilization; 2027–2028 = first visible deceleration in repair job volumes as early-cohort scrappage exceeds new entries; 2029+ = structural margin compression if new car sales remain depressed.

C. The Confounding Factor: EV Powertrain Shift

Europe's BEV market share rose to 19.3% in Jan 2026 (vs. 15.8% a year ago). Within a decade, the parc will be majority-EV.

The glass repair implication: EV windscreens are similar to ICE windscreens (glass doesn't change), but EV vehicles have lower real-world accident rates (no engine vibration = lower acoustic stress, smoother acceleration profiles, lower high-speed collisions on congested European roads). Studies suggest 15–25% lower glass breakage on per-vehicle basis for EVs vs. ICE.

This is positive for fleet parc but negative for Belron's job volume. As EV penetration rises, repair jobs per vehicle will decline. Belron's response (higher pricing, ADAS upsell) can partially offset, but if EV parc reaches 50% by 2033, Belron's repair volume could be down 7–12% on a normalized basis, vs. what the pre-2020 fleet model predicted.

III. D'Ieteren Auto: Structural Decline in Belgian Automotive Distribution

A. The Market Context: Belgium's New Car Sales Are in Freefall

[D'Ieteren 2024 full-year results](https://www.dieterengroup.com/press-releases/dieteren-group-full-year-2024-results/) show:

  • Belgian new car market declined 6.4% in 2024
  • D'Ieteren Auto maintained 22.8% market share despite headwinds
  • Adjusted profit before tax grew 13.4% YoY to €238.9m, a record, driven by strong pricing and cost discipline

The headline is positive. But the subtexts are ominous:

1. Market share gains in a shrinking market are a value trap. D'Ieteren Auto's margin expansion (5.1% adjusted operating margin, +30 bps YoY) came from pricing and cost-cutting, not volume growth. Belgium's auto market fell another 7.5% in 2025; if D'Ieteren takes another +40 bps of market share, it's still managing decline, not growth.

2. VW Group's shift to direct-to-consumer EV sales is fragmenting the dealer network. Volkswagen's own sales force is increasingly competing with franchised dealers like D'Ieteren for high-margin EV orders. Volvo (also in D'Ieteren's portfolio) has announced a full shift to online-direct sales by 2026. This will fragment dealer economics and reduce D'Ieteren's pricing power.

3. EV inventory drag is a hidden headwind. D'Ieteren is stocking EV inventory ahead of weak demand. The margin on EV sales is lower (less room for dealer installation packages, financing cross-sells) than on ICE vehicles. If EV demand softens (tariffs, affordability, charging anxiety), D'Ieteren will face inventory impairment risk and forced provisioning.

B. Belgian Tariffs and the VW Group's Cost Structure

If Trump's secondary tariffs (25% on China-related trade) are implemented, and if the EU retaliates, Belgium's VW Group imports could face additional logistics costs. D'Ieteren, as the exclusive importer, would absorb some of this cost or pass it to customers (further pressuring demand).

Belgian-specific risk: The country has limited auto manufacturing (mostly assembly) and no tariff production benefit. D'Ieteren is purely a distribution play, with 100% exposure to cost inflation and demand compression.

IV. Belron IPO Valuation and Refinancing Risk

A. The IPO Ambition: €32bn EV in 2H 2026

D'Ieteren appointed Rothschild & Co. to explore strategic options for its Belron stake (50.3% ownership). [IPO guidance suggests](https://www.investing.com/news/stock-market-news/dieteren-upgraded-by-jefferies-as-belron-eyes-2026-listing-at-32-bln-valuation-4456783):

  • Listing likely in US or Europe (Amsterdam most probable)
  • Indicative enterprise value: €32–40bn
  • Timing: 2H 2026, subject to market conditions

B. The Valuation Math: Why It's Aggressive

Belron's 2024 financials:

  • Revenue: €6.5bn
  • EBITDA: €1.7bn (~26% margin)
  • Adjusted operating profit: €1.35bn (~21% margin)

An IPO at €32bn EV implies:

  • 18.8x EV/EBITDA
  • 23.7x EV/Operating Profit

For comparison:

  • Reputable European automotive aftermarket/services comps trade 10–13x EV/EBITDA
  • Boralex (Canadian water treatment/industrial services, 2% growth) trades 10.5x
  • Heico Corporation (aerospace, 5–7% growth) trades 13–15x

Belron's growth rate (4–5% organic, with ADAS upside to 6–7%) does not justify 18–20x multiples unless investors believe in structural margin expansion to 23–25%. But H1 2025 results suggest margin expansion is stalling.

C. Market Conditions Risk: The Timing Problem

An IPO in 2H 2026 depends on:

  • Credit markets: Rate cuts or at least no rate hikes (current path: 4–5% base, likely stable 2026)
  • Equity sentiment: Automotive sector not in disfavor; large-cap IPOs executing successfully
  • No geopolitical shocks: Iran conflict, US tariffs, EU trade wars must not escalate

What could derail it:

  • Q4 2025 / FY 2025 results disappointing (margin miss = market loses confidence in 23% target)
  • Tariff escalation driving recession concerns (equity IPO windows slam shut)
  • Rate environment deteriorating (increases Belron's cost of capital, lowers exit valuation)

If IPO is delayed 12–18 months: D'Ieteren carries unplanned leverage on its balance sheet, must refinance existing 2024 debt at higher rates, and becomes forced seller if leverage covenants tighten. Forced secondary market sales by D'Ieteren (10–15% stake) could depress IPO price when it finally happens, and DIE stock reprices downward in anticipation.

V. Geopolitical and Tariff Risks: Explicitly Out of Guidance

A. What D'Ieteren Flagged

[Per 2025 full-year guidance](https://www.dieterengroup.com/press-releases/dieteren-group-full-year-2025-results/), D'Ieteren explicitly excluded from its 2026 outlook:

  • "Potential impact from geopolitical tensions or additional increase in US trade tariffs"
  • Other unquantified risks

This is a tell: management knows the risks are real, non-negligible, but unquantifiable. The market has not priced them.

B. The Iran/Tariff Cascade

[Current geopolitical context (Mar–Apr 2026)](https://www.automotivemanufacturingsolutions.com/analysis/iran-conflict-sends-shockwaves-through-auto-production-and-supply-chains/2616710):

  • Iran conflict ongoing; Strait of Hormuz shipping partially disrupted
  • Trump administration threatening 25% secondary tariffs on China, India, Turkey, UAE—all major automotive/parts manufacturing hubs
  • VW Group already absorbing €35.4bn in cumulative tariff costs since 2025

C. D'Ieteren's Specific Exposure

Direct exposure (low):

  • D'Ieteren itself has no US manufacturing or tariff-protected production

Indirect exposure (high): 1. VW Group cost inflation: If tariffs on Chinese BEV parts or Indian electronics escalate, VW will raise prices. Belgian market demand is price-sensitive; D'Ieteren's volume and margin both suffer. 2. Belron US operations: Belron derives ~25% of revenue from North America. If tariffs inflate repair costs (glass sourcing, recalibration equipment imports), Belron's margins in the US compress. US is highest-margin geography. 3. TVH Parts: Construction/agriculture equipment parts are imported globally. Tariff escalation directly increases COGS; pass-through is limited in a deflationary aftermarket.

Second-order effect: Tariff-driven inflation could force rate hikes in 2H 2026, collapsing IPO sentiment and delaying Belron's exit. This triggers refinancing risk on D'Ieteren's balance sheet (see Section IV.C).

D. The Pricing Mechanism

If tariffs escalate by Q2 2026, the market would reprice DIE stock downward 15–25% in anticipation of:

  • 2–3% earnings headwind (FY 2026–2027 earnings compression)
  • 2–3x multiple compression (lower growth expectations, cyclical discount)
  • Delayed IPO (valuation uncertainty)

VI. TVH Parts: Secular Margin Decline in Equipment Aftermarket

A. The Market Dynamic: Price Deflation and Disintermediation

[European automotive aftermarket 2024–2025](https://www.rolandberger.com/en/Insights/Publications/Automotive-Aftermarket-Pulse-2025.html):

  • Overall market growing slowly (3–4% CAGR)
  • Wholesale/distributor segment (TVH's core) under pressure: 57% of consumers now choose independent aftermarket (IAM) parts vs. OEM, up 14 percentage points YoY
  • Price transparency: e-commerce and price aggregators have eliminated distributor-retailer margin, squeezing both

TVH operates in equipment parts distribution (construction, agriculture, material handling—not automotive). This market is even more fragmented and e-commerce penetrated than auto parts.

B. TVH's Performance: The Guidance Clue

[D'Ieteren FY 2024 results](https://www.dieterengroup.com/press-releases/dieteren-group-full-year-2024-results/) show TVH as a minor profit contributor (~3–5% of group operating profit). Management rarely highlights TVH specifically in guidance updates, suggesting flat to negative organic growth.

Recent news (Argo Tractor partnership) is positive, but it's a single OEM deal, not indicative of market trends.

C. The Risk: Forced Integration or Divestiture

If TVH's margins compress further (to 4–6% from 6–8%), D'Ieteren faces two paths: 1. Hold and rationalize: Close underperforming locations, integrate back-office with other units. This generates one-time charges and management distraction. 2. Divest: Sell TVH to a larger industrial distributor. Likely outcome if buyer sees consolidation synergies. D'Ieteren would realize a small gain (8–10x EBITDA multiple, not competitive) but lose a cash-generative asset.

Either scenario is value-destructive to DIE equity in the near term (2–3 years), though the underlying asset value is not materially impaired.

VII. Moleskine: Premium Positioning Under Secular Headwinds

A. Market Context: Paper Notebooks in a Digital World

[Moleskine's market](https://www.mordorintelligence.com/industry-reports/paper-notebook-market):

  • Global paper notebook market: ~$76bn in 2025, forecast to grow 3–4% CAGR to 2030
  • Moleskine's premium positioning: ~€100–150 per notebook, targeting professionals, creatives, luxury consumers
  • Competitive threat: Leuchtturm (German, premium), Rhodia (French, budget-premium), mass-market notebooks

B. Strategic Pivots: Smart Notebooks and ESG (Defensive, Not Transformative)

Moleskine's recent moves: 1. Smart notebooks with Adobe integration (2024): Addresses digitalization by bridging physical–digital workflows. Good product, but requires stylus (€100+), limiting addressable market. 2. 100% recycled paper line (2024): Sustainable credentials, but no price premium; actually margin-compressive vs. virgin fiber. 3. Expansion to 150 stores by 2030 (from ~60 now): Retail expansion in weak consumer environment is capex-heavy and risky.

C. The Risk: Margin Compression and Strategic Abandonment

Moleskine represents ~2–3% of D'Ieteren's group profit but consumes management bandwidth (requires dedicated CEO, international operations, brand building). If margins compress (e.g., from 15% adjusted operating margin to 12% due to sustainability costs and retail overhead), the strategic case for retention weakens.

In a downturn scenario (2027–2028), D'Ieteren might divest Moleskine for 6–8x EBITDA multiple to a luxury conglomerate (LVMH, Kering) or financial buyer. Realized price would likely be below D'Ieteren's carrying value, forcing a write-down and capital loss.

VIII. Earnings Quality and Guidance Credibility

A. Adjusted vs. Reported Earnings: The Gap Is Widening

FY 2024:

  • Adjusted profit before tax: €819m
  • Reported profit before tax: lower due to FX headwinds and financial charges

H1 2025:

  • Adjusted operating margin: 21.4% (Belron)
  • Reported operating margin: 14.2% (same segment)
  • Gap driven by financial charges, FX, one-time costs

The problem: Adjusted earnings are increasingly divorced from reported earnings. If the gap widens, the street will shift focus to reported earnings, which will show slower growth (or contraction in a weak scenario).

B. FX Headwinds: USD Weakness Is a Structural Drag

Belron derives ~25% of revenue from North America (USD). D'Ieteren Automotive has minor FX exposure (Belgium-focused). But the consolidated 2025 results show material FX drag (EUR/USD was ~1.08 in early 2025, down from 1.12 in early 2024).

If USD remains weak (or weaker, 1.00–1.05 range), Belron's reported revenue will be underreported on translation, and the street will penalize the stock for slower reported growth even if organic growth is solid.

C. 2026 Guidance: Low-to-Mid Single Digit Is Soft

D'Ieteren guided for "low-to-mid single-digit" growth in adjusted profit before tax for 2026. At face value:

  • FY 2025 adjusted profit before tax: ~€850–900m (estimated, pending FY results)
  • 2026 guidance: +1–4% = €860–940m

This is below historical growth expectations and leaves little room for upside. Any miss (e.g., 0% growth vs. +2% guided) will trigger sharp repricing.

X. Why the Market Has Not Priced These Risks

A. Belron's Dominant Value Share Anchors Sentiment

Belron represents 80%+ of D'Ieteren's intrinsic value. Investors focus on Belron's resilience (repair demand tied to fleet size, not new car sales) and miss second-derivative dynamics (parc composition, EV penetration, labor inflation).

B. IPO Narrative Provides Upside Cover

The Belron IPO is positioned as "value unlocking"—a positive story. Investors price-in the upside scenario (successful 2H 2026 IPO at €32bn+) and ignore the downside (delayed IPO, forced refinancing, secondary overhang).

C. Tariff Risks Are Abstract and Excluded

Management explicitly excludes geopolitical risks from guidance. Investors treat this as "not our problem" rather than recognizing that excluded risks are often the most consequential when they materialize.

D. Guidance Is Soft, But Not Alarming

"Low-to-mid single-digit growth" sounds reasonable. Investors don't focus on what it implies: mature growth, limited margin upside, and limited room for error. Relative to historical performance, it's deceleration.

XII. Conclusion: The Timing of Reckoning

D'Ieteren is a "prove it" story for the next 12–18 months. The street has priced-in: 1. Belron margin expansion: Needs to reach 23% in 2025 to justify current valuation 2. IPO value unlock: Belron IPO in 2H 2026 at €32bn+ EV 3. Automotive segment stabilization: D'Ieteren Auto holds market share despite market decline 4. Benign macro: No tariff escalation, no recession, stable labor inflation

If any of these four assumptions breaks, repricing is material. Most probable is a 15–25% drawdown in DIE stock over 12–18 months, driven by:

  • FY 2025 results missing margin expectations (Q1 2026)
  • IPO guidance pushed or pricing expectations reduced (Q2 2026)
  • Tariff uncertainty or escalation beginning to pressure automotive (Q2–Q3 2026)

The pre-mortem identifies that D'Ieteren's value depends on operational execution in a deteriorating macro environment. Belron's margin expansion, automotive distribution pricing power, and IPO timing are all under pressure. While not immediately catastrophic, the sequence of disappointments could quickly shift sentiment from "quality compounder" to "value trap with execution risk."

For a 12–24 month investor: Current positioning is appropriate only if conviction is high that Belron can expand to 23% margins while navigating tariff risks and that the IPO will proceed on plan in 2H 2026. Any doubt on either front warrants a significant reduction in position size and a move to sidelines ahead of Q1 2026 FY results and Q2 2026 IPO commentary.
