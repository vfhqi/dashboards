[W] MUNTERS GROUP AB (MTRS-SE) — QUERY #4: EARNINGS & FUNDAMENTAL TRENDS

[C] Native Research | 28-Apr-26

1. KEY FINDINGS

Rating: ORANGE — Strong demand narrative; near-term earnings execution unresolved

  • DCT is a structurally sound AI infrastructure play. Order intake +85% FY2025, book-to-bill 1.6x, backlog extending into 2027-2028. The demand signal is not in question. What is in question is the company's ability to convert that backlog into margin-accretive earnings without serial excuse-making.
  • The group EBITA margin has gone backwards in the near term. FY2024: 15.1%. FY2025: ~12.7%. Q4 2025 alone: 10%. That is a 430bp deterioration over 12 months on a company trading near its CMD target range. Two offsetting forces — tariffs/manufacturing transition and AirTech battery drag — are cited, but both were foreseeable.
  • Q1 2026 reported today (28-Apr-26) at 10.9% adjusted EBITA margin. The DCT dual-site factory transition is described as "mostly resolved." Margins should recover in H2 2026, but this is management guidance against a backdrop of two consecutive quarters of disappointing delivery.
  • DCT is the valuation anchor and it is working. The single BSEK 2.1 order from a hyperscaler, the BSEK 2.0 colocation order (24-Apr-26), and the record Q4 2025 intake all confirm Munters is qualifying at the top tier of data centre infrastructure. Virginia Phase II adds 200,000 sq ft and a dedicated test lab; US local manufacture largely resolves the tariff problem post-Q2 2026.
  • AirTech battery is the structural liability. The sub-segment margin collapsed from ~17% to ~7% in H1 2025. The EV battery industry rationalisation is a multi-year drag. MSEK 250-300 annual savings targeted by end-2026 via a ~200-person headcount reduction. This is damage limitation, not growth.
  • Peer comparison is unflattering on current-period margins. Alfa Laval: 17.7% EBITA margin FY2025. Atlas Copco: ~20.7% operating margin FY2025. Munters at 12.7% is below its own CMD target floor (13%) and in a different tier from best-in-class Nordic industrials. Vertiv at ~22%+ operating margin shows what DCT-pure-play execution looks like.
  • The bull case rests on a clean H2 2026 — DCT invoicing surge (+30-40% revenue uplift in backlog), AirTech cost savings kicking in, tariff headwinds fading. The bear case is that each quarter produces a new bridging explanation. The credibility of the margin recovery guide is genuinely low right now.
  • EPS revision momentum is positive on a 6M basis (+20.1%) but has decelerated to +3.3% on 3M. The stock is pricing in recovery; actual delivery in Q3-Q4 2026 is the next binary event.

2. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY — PEER STACK-RANK BY FUNDAMENTAL MOMENTUM

Rating: ORANGE — Munters sits mid-table on current-period fundamentals despite top-decile demand signals

  • Munters has the best order growth of any name in the peer group in absolute terms. DCT order intake +85% FY2025, +416% organically in Q4 alone. Vertiv, the closest comparable, posted +252% organic order growth in Q4 2025 — impressive, but Munters' Q4 print was higher on a percentage basis.
  • The demand quality gap between Munters and legacy HVAC peers (Carrier, JCI) is stark. Carrier posted Q4 organic sales down 9%; JCI posted organic sales growth of just 4% for the full fiscal year. Neither has a DCT equivalent. Both are structurally lower growth.
  • However, Munters' ability to convert demand into earnings is the weakest in the peer group. Alfa Laval at 17.7% EBITA. Atlas Copco at ~20%+ operating margin. Vertiv at an implied 22-23% adjusted operating margin guide for 2026. Munters at 12.7% and falling in 2025. This is an execution gap, not a structural one — but it is real and currently widening.

Peer Stack-Rank on Fundamental Momentum (FY2025 / H1 2026 outlook):

1. Vertiv (VRT) — Best in class. 22%+ margins, 252% Q4 organic orders, $15bn backlog, 42-45% adj EPS growth guided for 2026. Clear leader.

2. Alfa Laval (ALFA) — Solid compounder. 17.7% EBITA FY2025, expanding margins, 8% organic revenue growth. Order softness is the only blemish — down 6% organic FY2025.

3. Atlas Copco (ATCO) — Resilient quality franchise. ~20% margins, BSEK 168 revenues. Stable demand outlook. Compressed short-term growth.

4. Johnson Controls (JCI) — Transition story underway. 19.5% segment EBITA FY2025, solid cash, 4% organic growth. Building tech/data centre pivot credible but slower.

5. Munters (MTRS) — Demand leader, execution laggard. Outstanding order signals but margin trajectory worse than peers on a current-period basis. H2 2026 recovery is the pivot point.

6. Carrier Global (CARR) — Disappointing. Q4 organic sales -9%, margin contraction 380bps. 2026 guided recovery to ~$3.4bn adj op profit but from a weakened base.

  • The sceptical read: Munters is ranked 5th on current fundamentals despite the best demand narrative. The market is paying for the promise. Delivery in Q3-Q4 2026 is non-optional.
  • Munters' P/E at 19.22x and EV/Sales at 2.07x represents a meaningful discount to Vertiv (which trades at 40-50x earnings) but a meaningful premium to traditional HVAC peers. The positioning is mid-tier valuation for what management claims is a top-tier growth asset. That discount narrows only with margin delivery.
  • CEO succession from generalist CEO to DCT specialist (Stefan Aspman) is net positive for the investment case. It signals that the board views DCT as the organising principle of the company. Risk: Aspman's leadership style and macro execution capability is untested at group level.

3. REVENUE GROWTH & ORGANIC DYNAMICS

Rating: GREEN — Revenue growth is real, broad in DCT, and backlog-supported

  • Group net sales FY2025 grew approximately 17% reported (building on the +11% / +19% with acquisitions FY2024 base). This is above-CMD-target growth of >14%/yr and reflects genuine DCT volume uplift.
  • Organic growth within DCT is the dominant driver. DCT order intake +85% FY2025. Order intake +416% in Q4 2025 organically. These numbers do not reflect restated M&A uplift — this is genuine hyperscaler demand reaching Munters' production schedule.
  • AirTech revenue declined in reported terms in FY2025. This is the first material revenue shrink in the division in the post-COVID growth phase. Battery sub-segment was the driver: EV battery customer capex deferrals have now run for four quarters. The division's Q1 2026 data shows margins improved slightly (AirTech) as cost actions began to bite, but revenue remained under pressure.
  • FoodTech is now a pure SaaS/digital business post Equipment divestiture (May 2025, MEUR 97.5). ARR growth 46%+ in the retained Digital Solutions arm. Revenue base is small but structurally recurring. This is not a growth driver for group revenues at current scale — it is a margin quality enhancer and optionality play on agri-tech digital penetration.
  • Acquisitions (Geoclima, Airprotech, Automated Environments, Hotraco) contributed to reported growth. The primary tariff problem (Italian-manufactured Geoclima product for US data centres) is now being resolved by the Virginia capacity ramp. The acquisition logic is sound; the post-acquisition integration cost was the surprise variable.
  • Book-to-bill of 1.6x group-wide is a revenue growth predictor of unusual strength. A BTB of 1.0x sustains current revenues; 1.6x means the order backlog is accreting faster than it is being shipped. Management guide "at least 30%, up to 40%" revenue uplift from current backlog in 2026 DCT invoicing. That is extraordinary guidance if delivered.
  • DCT backlog extends into 2027 and 2028. This gives multi-year revenue visibility of a kind that traditional HVAC businesses simply do not possess. Alfa Laval's order intake was actually -6% organically in FY2025 — the contrast with Munters' +85% is striking.
  • The BSEK 2.0 colocation order (24-Apr-26) and the BSEK 2.1 hyperscaler order show two distinct customer archetypes committing to large, multi-year projects. Customer diversity within the backlog is a structural positive.
  • Currency headwinds are a partial drag. DCT revenues are USD-denominated while Swedish costs remain in SEK. Net: organic revenue growth likely exceeds reported growth.
  • Carrier's Q4 organic revenue -9% puts the HVAC market in context. Traditional building HVAC is not growing. The data centre cooling sub-market is the only part of thermal management that is genuinely accelerating. Munters is correctly positioned.
  • Risk 1 — Hyperscaler concentration. DCT customer mix: 71% hyperscalers, 26% colocators. A hyperscaler capex pullback would collapse the order book rapidly.
  • Risk 2 — Revenue recognition timing. Large project-based DCT orders are recognised on delivery. A slip in Virginia ramp creates a quarter-level revenue shortfall even if the demand is intact. This has already happened in Q4 2025 and Q1 2026.
  • Risk 3 — AirTech drag duration. Management expects battery weakness "throughout 2026." If EV investment cycles extend further, the AirTech drag persists longer than modelled.
  • Sceptical interpretation: The 17% reported growth FY2025 was partially acquisition-driven. The "record invoicing" guide for 2026 is conditional on Virginia Phase II delivering on schedule and the tariff headwind genuinely receding. Two execution dependencies, both of which disappointed in 2025.

4. MARGIN PROFILE & EVOLUTION

Rating: ORANGE — Structural margin target is achievable; near-term trajectory is the wrong direction

  • FY2025 adjusted EBITA margin ~12.7% vs FY2024 15.1%. A 240bp deterioration in group margins while order intake is growing at 85% in the key division is an unusual combination. It reflects the cost of transition, not structural impairment — but the market must evaluate whether management's costs of transition are well-controlled.
  • Q4 2025 adjusted EBITA margin: 10%. This is below the CMD target floor (13%) and represents the weakest quarter in recent memory. Management attributed this to (a) tariff headwinds from Geoclima Italy-to-US production, (b) dual-site factory costs (Italy + Virginia running simultaneously), and (c) AirTech volume deleverage.
  • Q1 2026 adjusted EBITA margin: 10.9%. A slight sequential recovery but still well below the CMD target range of 13-16%. The dual-site transition is described as "mostly resolved" — the implication being that Q2 2026 should see a step-change improvement.
  • AirTech margin: collapsed from ~17% to ~7% in H1 2025 within the battery sub-segment. The MSEK 250-300 annualised savings target (200 redundancies) is meaningful — approximately 2-3% of group revenues as a cost base reduction — but has full effect only by end-2026.
  • Peer margin comparison:
  • Alfa Laval: 17.7% adjusted EBITA FY2025 (expanding)
  • Atlas Copco: ~20%+ adjusted operating margin FY2025 (stable)
  • Vertiv: ~22-23% adjusted operating margin guided 2026 (expanding aggressively)
  • Johnson Controls: 19.5% segment EBITA FY2025 (stable, improving)
  • Carrier: 9.4% adjusted operating margin Q4 2025 (contracting significantly)
  • Munters: 12.7% FY2025 / 10.9% Q1 2026 (contracting, recovery guided H2)
  • Munters is the worst performer in the peer group on current-period margin delivery, except for Carrier — and Carrier is a much larger, more diversified business with its own portfolio restructuring drag.
  • The CMD 13-16% EBITA target remains achievable on a 2026 FY basis, but requires: (a) DCT margins recovering toward 15%+ as Virginia facility ramps and tariff headwinds fade; (b) AirTech margins recovering from ~7% toward 10-12% as cost savings kick in; (c) FoodTech SaaS contributing a higher-quality margin mix.
  • Historical margin quality: In FY2022-FY2024, Munters demonstrated that the group could sustain 13-15%+ EBITA margins when all three divisions were contributing positively. The current weakness is divisionally concentrated (DCT transition costs + AirTech battery), not a systemic efficiency problem.
  • The bull case on margins rests on H2 2026. Management has explicitly guided for "historically high turnover and a stronger contribution in the second half." If DCT invoicing surges as the Virginia backlog delivers, and AirTech cost savings provide the floor, the H2 2026 margin should approach or exceed the CMD midpoint (14.5%).
  • Sceptical interpretation: This is now the third consecutive quarter where management has cited transition costs and tariffs as the margin suppressor. At what point is "transition cost" simply the cost of running the business? The Virginia investment, the Geoclima integration friction, the dual-factory period — these were all choices. The market should apply some execution risk discount to the "it gets better in H2" narrative.

5. EPS TRAJECTORY & DELIVERY

Rating: ORANGE — Positive 6M revision trend; near-term delivery below initial expectations

  • EPS consensus was revised +3.3% over the 3M period and +20.1% over the 6M period. The 6M revision is a strong signal of fundamental re-rating — analysts raised numbers materially as the DCT demand story crystallised. The 3M deceleration reflects Q4 2025 and Q1 2026 earnings disappointments.
  • The sequence matters: analysts raised numbers aggressively in H2 2025 as DCT order intake went parabolic. They have now modestly trimmed those upgrades as actual EBITA delivery disappointed in Q4 2025 and Q1 2026. The net position is still materially higher EPS expectations than 6 months ago — but the revision direction in the most recent months is cautious.
  • FY2025 EPS has come in below initial CMD-era expectations because the group EBITA margin is 240bp lower than the FY2024 base and 40bp below the CMD target floor. The group is generating lower earnings power than the installed asset base would imply — the opportunity cost of the transition period is real.
  • Adjusted P/E of 19.22x at the 71st historical percentile. The market is already pricing in recovery. If that recovery fails to materialise in H2 2026, the de-rating could be sharp.
  • Vertiv's 42-45% adjusted EPS growth guided for 2026 vs Munters' mid-teens earnings recovery is a useful benchmark. Vertiv's DCT-pure-play economics (22%+ margins, $15bn backlog) are simply generating more earnings per dollar of revenue growth. Munters' conglomerate structure (AirTech drag, FoodTech transition) compresses the earnings leverage that DCT demand alone would otherwise create.
  • EPS drivers for H2 2026 recovery: (1) DCT invoicing surge from backlog (30-40% revenue uplift guided); (2) AirTech cost savings reducing divisional cost base by MSEK 250-300; (3) elimination of dual-site factory costs (ended Q1 2026); (4) FoodTech SaaS ARR growing at 46% (small but high-margin contribution); (5) tariff mitigation as US production ramps.
  • EPS leverage is potentially high if the recovery delivers. With a DCT revenue uplift of 30-40% and margins returning to 14-15%, the incremental EBITA drops at high margins (given fixed cost absorption). A scenario where FY2026 group EBITA margin exits at 15% would generate material EPS upside vs current consensus.
  • Cash conversion: management highlights "strong cash flow" and the ability to self-fund the Virginia Phase II expansion. This is a positive quality-of-earnings signal — the EBITA is converting to cash despite the transition costs.
  • Sceptical interpretation: EPS revisions of +20% over 6M are partly a function of Q2-Q3 2025 order intake euphoria. The actual Q4 2025 and Q1 2026 earnings prints were below revised consensus. The pattern — strong order intake leads to upgrades, then earnings disappoint, then management blames temporary factors — has now repeated multiple times. EPS recovery is management-guided and not yet evidenced in delivered numbers.

6. GUIDANCE & FORECAST CHANGES

Rating: ORANGE — Management guidance is directionally credible; execution track record against guidance is poor

  • CMD May 2024 guidance: net sales growth >14%/yr; OWC 13-10%; EBITA margin 13-16%. The group has met the revenue growth target but missed the EBITA margin floor in FY2025 (12.7%) and Q4 2025 (10%). CMD credibility is therefore impaired on the margin dimension.
  • FY2026 guidance (qualitative): "Record invoicing and historically high turnover for the full year, with stronger contribution in H2." No explicit margin number provided. This is notable — management have declined to provide a quantified EBITA margin guide for 2026, choosing instead to provide directional language. The absence of a numerical guide is a caution flag.
  • DCT-specific 2026 guide: backlog supports "at least 30%, potentially up to 40%" revenue uplift in DCT invoicing. Virginia Phase II (+200,000 sq ft) completed Q2 2026. Backlog delivery windows extend into 2027-2028, providing multi-year visibility beyond 2026.
  • AirTech 2026 guide: continued battery weakness expected throughout 2026. Annual savings of MSEK 250-300 from restructuring, full effect by end-2026. Management is signalling this is a cost management exercise, not a demand recovery story.
  • Tariff guide: "majority of tariff impact will diminish after Q1 2026" as Virginia US production ramps. This is the most important guidance variable for near-term margin recovery.
  • Target price revisions: TP revised +32.9% over 6M by sell-side. This reflects the DCT order intake re-rating more than the earnings delivery. Buy rating prevalence: 80%. The sell-side is overwhelmingly constructive on a 12-18M view, but that constructiveness is largely predicated on H2 2026 delivery.
  • Peer guidance quality comparison: Vertiv's 2026 guidance is specific ($13.25-13.75bn revenue, $5.97-6.07 adj EPS, 22-23% margin). Alfa Laval has a concrete 7% growth and 17% EBITDA target "through the cycle." Munters' qualitative-only 2026 guidance is the least informative of any peer — which either reflects genuine uncertainty or a deliberate choice to manage expectations.
  • Sceptical interpretation: Munters has now missed its EBITA margin target for two consecutive periods. The refusal to provide a quantified 2026 margin guide gives management maximum flexibility to explain any further disappointment as "in line with guidance." The qualitative language ("record invoicing," "stronger H2") was also used in 2025 and resulted in a 240bp margin miss for the year.

7. COMPARATIVE PEER RANKING

Rating: ORANGE — Munters leads on demand; trails on delivery; fairly valued relative to peers

  • On demand momentum: Munters is first. No peer matches a BTB of 1.6x combined with +85% order intake growth and backlog extending 3 years. Vertiv comes closest (+252% organic orders, $15bn backlog) but is already 5x the size — absolute growth rates should normalise higher at Munters' current base.
  • On margin delivery: Munters ranks last among peers with a sustained mid-teens aspiration. Alfa Laval at 17.7%, Atlas Copco at ~20%, Vertiv guided 22-23%, JCI at 19.5% — all materially above Munters' current 10.9-12.7% range. The margin gap is partly structural (Munters is in a growth investment phase; peers are mature) but partly poor execution.
  • On revenue quality: FoodTech Digital Solutions (46%+ ARR growth) is high-quality recurring revenue. DCT project revenues are large and lumpy. AirTech is cyclical and currently in down-cycle. The mix is improving toward higher quality but is not yet at a level that justifies premium revenue multiples.
  • On EV/Sales: Munters at 2.07x vs Vertiv at roughly 5-6x. This gap is the structural discount for being a multi-division conglomerate rather than a pure-play DCT business. The DCT business, if valued standalone at Vertiv-equivalent multiples, would suggest significant sum-of-parts upside — but the AirTech drag reduces that optionality.
  • Atlas Copco comparison is instructive: Atlas Copco has built a 20%+ margin business over decades through compounding service revenues and disciplined M&A integration. Munters' acquisition cadence (four companies in two years) suggests it is in a growth-investment phase.
  • Carrier comparison is useful for valuation floor: Carrier at ~$22bn sales, adjusted operating profit of ~$3.4bn guided for 2026. Carrier trades at roughly 20-25x earnings, not dramatically different from Munters, despite being 10x larger and having better near-term margin delivery.
  • Johnson Controls is the best peer comparison for transformation risk: JCI has been restructuring from a conglomerate to a focused buildings technology play for years. Munters is earlier in a similar transformation — from industrial humidity specialist to AI data centre infrastructure company.
  • On balance sheet quality: Munters at 2.3x net debt/EBITDA post-acquisitions vs Alfa Laval typically 1.0-1.5x. Munters' leverage is manageable but reduces financial flexibility at a time when the DCT capex programme requires additional investment.
  • Sceptical interpretation: Munters is priced as a 50/50 blend between a DCT growth stock and a value-priced Nordic industrial. Neither community fully owns it. Growth investors find the AirTech drag and margin execution unappealing; value investors find the 19x P/E expensive for a business delivering 12.7% EBITA margins. The stock requires a specific narrative to work — DCT recovery + AirTech stabilisation in H2 2026 — which is a crowded and conditional trade.

8. SECTOR & VALUE CHAIN CONTEXT

Rating: GREEN — Structural tailwind is exceptional; Munters' positioning is differentiated

  • Hyperscaler capex is the most important macro driver. Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta are collectively spending $380bn+ on AI infrastructure in 2025 alone, with 2026 expected to see further growth. Every MW of AI compute requires 1.2-1.5MW of cooling capacity. Munters sits directly in that value chain.
  • The data centre cooling market is growing at ~16% CAGR through 2034 (USD 11.6bn in 2025 to USD 31bn by 2034). The liquid cooling sub-segment is growing faster at ~17-25% CAGR driven by AI chip thermal loads that air cooling cannot fully handle.
  • Munters' product differentiation is real. CRAH units, CDUs, and Geoclima chillers are precision thermal management products, not commodity HVAC. The qualification process at hyperscaler level is multi-year and creates significant switching costs.
  • The AI training vs inference dynamic matters for DCT. Training workloads are concentrated and drive mega-facility demand. Inference workloads are more distributed. As AI enters the inference scale-out phase, DCT demand should broaden geographically and by customer type — a structural positive for the next 5 years.
  • Vertiv's 2026 guidance ($13.25-13.75bn sales at 27-29% organic growth) is the market's leading indicator for the sector. If Vertiv's order intake continues to hold, the infrastructure build is real and multi-year. Munters is a downstream beneficiary of the same capex cycle.
  • Modine (via Airedale acquisition) is the most credible new entrant to the DCT cooling space from below. Modine's data centre revenue growth at 42% in recent quarters and a $180m single order is its largest ever. Munters should monitor Modine carefully as a competitive threat in the chiller and CRAH market.
  • AirTech's battery exposure sits at the intersection of two conflicting macro forces. EV adoption in Europe is slowing — EU member states have pushed back on the 2035 combustion engine ban; subsidy cuts in Germany triggered the original demand collapse. Stationary battery storage (grid batteries) is growing but has not been described as a material replacement revenue stream.
  • Munters' revenue base is increasingly USD-denominated (US data centres) — a partial inflation hedge in a risk-off GBP/SEK environment.
  • Sceptical interpretation: The market is uniformly bullish on AI infrastructure. If the hyperscaler capex cycle peaks or plateaus in 2026-2027, the order intake collapse would be severe and fast. Munters has 71% hyperscaler concentration in DCT. A 30% pullback in hyperscaler AI infrastructure spend would take DCT orders sharply negative and destroy the investment case.

9. QUALITY OF EARNINGS & ACCOUNTING

Rating: YELLOW — Cash conversion is good; acquisition accounting and restructuring charges warrant scrutiny

  • Cash flow quality: management explicitly highlights "strong cash flow" in the FY2025 release and describes self-funding capacity for Virginia Phase II. A business generating real cash while growing its order book is preferable to one burning cash on growth — this is a positive quality signal.
  • Working capital trajectory: CMD target OWC 13-10%. The CMD guide of reducing OWC implies management expects to improve cash conversion as DCT revenue delivery scales.
  • Acquisition accounting: four acquisitions in two years (Geoclima, Airprotech, Automated Environments, Hotraco). Each creates amortisation charges that sit below adjusted EBITA but above GAAP net income. The gap between adjusted EBITA and reported EBIT will be wide in 2025-2026 as acquisition goodwill and intangible amortisation runs through the P&L.
  • Restructuring charges: MSEK ~150 recognised across Q4 2025 and Q1 2026 in relation to AirTech headcount reduction. This is a genuine one-off in nature (plant closures, severance) but reflects that management was slow to right-size the cost base after battery demand fell in mid-2024.
  • FoodTech Equipment divestiture (MEUR 97.5): proceeds were used for debt reduction. The remaining FoodTech business (Digital Solutions, SaaS) is inherently higher-quality earnings — recurring, low capex, high contribution margins.
  • Net debt/EBITDA 2.3x end-2024, likely improving toward 1.8-2.0x end-2025 given FoodTech divestiture proceeds and operating cash generation. This is within an acceptable range for a growth-phase industrial, but constrains financial flexibility for further M&A or accelerated capex.
  • Capex intensity increased in 2025. Virginia construction represents a step-change in capex. Post-completion (Q2 2026), maintenance capex should revert to normalised levels.
  • Sceptical interpretation: Four acquisitions in two years, a divestiture, a major factory investment, restructuring charges, and serial one-off costs create an accounting environment that is difficult to see through clearly. The gap between adjusted EBITA and GAAP net income is likely the widest in the peer group on a percentage basis. Investors should apply a quality-of-earnings discount.

10. SCEPTICAL SUMMARY

Rating: ORANGE — The bull case is coherent but execution-dependent; three conditional variables must all deliver in H2 2026

  • The investment case rests on three simultaneous H2 2026 deliveries: (1) DCT invoicing surge as Virginia produces and backlog ships; (2) AirTech margin recovery as restructuring savings flow through; (3) tariff headwinds fade as US production replaces Italian product. Any one of these failing weakens the case; all three failing would be a significant negative event.
  • Management credibility is impaired. The group EBITA margin has been below the CMD target floor (13%) for at least two consecutive reporting periods. Management has consistently cited "temporary" headwinds. The market should apply a higher confidence interval around any single-point margin recovery forecast.
  • DCT concentration is the structural risk. 71% hyperscaler, 26% colocator. One hyperscaler pausing orders is a material event for a DCT business at Munters' current size. The $10bn long-term revenue aspiration implies that today's hyperscaler concentration risk must diversify — but that diversification is many years away.
  • The P/E at 19.22x is at the 71st historical percentile. The TP was revised up +32.9% over 6 months on the strength of order intake data. The risk is that the stock has already priced the recovery that has not yet delivered. A miss in H2 2026 from current consensus expectations could de-rate the stock 25-30%.
  • CEO transition risk is real but asymmetric. Stefan Aspman (DCT head) succeeding Klas Forsström is strategically logical but creates a learning curve risk during a period when execution precision is critical.
  • The AirTech battery segment is a structural problem, not a cyclical one. EV battery investment in Europe is structurally decelerating as the 2035 target faces political erosion. The MSEK 250-300 savings target acknowledges this: management is cutting capacity, not waiting for recovery.
  • Acquisition integration risk is unresolved. Geoclima is the most significant and is also the source of the tariff problem. Integrating a premium European manufacturer while simultaneously relocating its revenue-generating capacity to the US is a complex operational task.
  • Bear case in one sentence: Munters is an excellent business in a structural growth market that has spent two years making expensive acquisitions, underdelivering on margins, and providing directional guidance that proves consistently optimistic — at a valuation that already prices the recovery.
