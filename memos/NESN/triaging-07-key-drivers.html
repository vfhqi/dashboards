Driver 1: Primary Fulcrum Driver - Volume Recovery (RIG)

The Strategic Pivot to Volume-Led Growth

Nestle's primary fulcrum driver over the next 24 months hinges on its ability to transition away from a heavily pricing-dependent growth model back to historical norms driven by Real Internal Growth (RIG). Under the leadership of CEO Laurent Freixe, the company has explicitly stated that RIG-led growth is its highest priority. The scale of this challenge is evident in recent historical performance, where FY 2025 organic growth of 3.5% was heavily skewed by 2.8% pricing, leaving RIG at a modest 0.8%. Management has maintained a medium-term organic growth guidance of 4% or more, which implicitly requires RIG to return to the 3-4% range in normal market conditions. Early signs of a turnaround materialized in late 2025 and early 2026, with RIG accelerating from 0.2% in H1 2025 to 1.4% in H2 2025, and settling at 1.2% in Q1 2026.

Driver 2: Margin Trajectory and Growth Reinvestment

The Tension Between Profitability and Reinvestment

Nestle's operating margin trajectory represents a critical balancing act between restoring structural profitability and aggressively funding brand reinvestments. In 2025, the company's Underlying Trading Operating Profit (UTOP) margin contracted by 110 basis points to 16.1%. This compression was primarily driven by the severe impact of input cost inflation—particularly in coffee and cocoa—alongside negative foreign exchange movements and higher tariffs. However, management has explicitly refused to protect short-term margins at the expense of long-term brand equity. Consequently, consumer-facing investments were heavily prioritized throughout the year. Advertising and marketing (A&P) expenses were stepped up by 50 basis points to 8.6% of total sales.

The strategic imperative is to return marketing investments to historical norms of approximately 9% of sales by the end of 2026. To drive Real Internal Growth (RIG), Nestle is expanding the scope of its high-potential growth platforms from 10% to 30% of Group sales. This expansion requires an additional CHF 0.6 billion in targeted investments scheduled for 2026. Management has guided that UTOP margin will improve sequentially in 2026, with a medium-term structural floor established at 17.0% or higher.

Structural Efficiencies: The "Fuel for Growth" Engine

To fund this massive reinvestment without permanently diluting operating margins, Nestle has radically accelerated its structural efficiency initiatives. The company's "Fuel for Growth" cost-savings program was recently upsized to target CHF 3.0 billion in total savings by the end of 2027. Execution has been remarkably rapid, with the company delivering CHF 1.1 billion in savings during 2025, beating its initial CHF 0.7 billion target by over CHF 350 million.

A significant portion of these savings relies on aggressive organizational streamlining and headcount reductions. Nestle announced a global reduction of approximately 16,000 roles, including 12,000 white-collar professionals, which alone is expected to generate CHF 1.0 billion in annual operational savings by 2027. The remaining efficiencies are being extracted through supply chain optimization, manufacturing productivity, and advanced AI-powered procurement strategies. Importantly, these Fuel for Growth savings are entirely incremental to the company's ongoing baseline efficiency programs, which independently deliver over CHF 1.0 billion annually.

Looking ahead, the broker views Nestle's structural initiatives constructively, modeling an improving operating margin trajectory from 2026 onward. The broker anticipates that the combination of easing commodity comparables and the realization of the Fuel for Growth cost savings will successfully offset the targeted step-up in A&P investments. Driver 3: Portfolio Simplification and Capital Reallocation

Strategic Sharpening: The Four Core Pillars

Nestlé is radically simplifying its organizational structure by shifting away from a sprawling matrix to concentrate resources on four powerhouse businesses: Coffee, Petcare, Nutrition, and Food & Snacks. This structural pivot is designed to eliminate operational overlap and direct capital toward categories with the highest structural growth and returns. As part of this sweeping realignment, the company is formally integrating its traditional Infant Nutrition segment with Nestlé Health Science. This integration dismantles the isolated "Globally Managed Business" structure of Health Science, pushing P&L accountability down to local markets while extracting back-office synergies.

Timeline and Proceeds: Water, VMS, and Ice Cream

To fund its aggressive growth reinvestments, Nestlé is actively pruning non-core and underperforming assets. The most significant structural change involves Nestlé Waters and Premium Beverages, which management has carved out into a standalone global unit headquartered in Paris. The formal engagement process to find strategic partners for the Water business commenced in the first quarter of 2026. Management expects this division to be entirely deconsolidated from the Group by 2027. This shift allows Nestlé to distance itself from a category increasingly plagued by commoditization, supply chain disruptions, and ESG-related scrutiny regarding clean water availability.

Simultaneously, the company is actively correcting past missteps in its Health Science acquisition strategy. While retaining highly profitable premium segments like Garden of Life and Pure Encapsulations, Nestlé is advancing the sale process for its mainstream and value Vitamin, Mineral, and Supplement (VMS) brands. Furthermore, the company is in advanced negotiations to offload its remaining ice cream operations to its joint venture partner, Froneri.

Analysts at Rothschild & Co Redburn anticipate that the Froneri ice cream transaction will likely close by the second quarter of 2026. The broker projects that this specific disposal will be broadly neutral to Nestlé's earnings per share.

Driver 4: Purina Pet Care Growth and GLP-1 Risks

The Crown Jewel's Growth Normalization

Purina Pet Care has historically operated as Nestlé's primary growth engine, frequently delivering double-digit organic growth and anchoring the company's valuation premium. However, recent financial disclosures indicate a material normalization in this trajectory as pandemic-era pet adoption booms fade and pricing power peaks. In the first quarter of 2026, Petcare delivered a relatively subdued 2.7% organic growth rate, supported by 1.7% Real Internal Growth (RIG). This performance was heavily polarized, driven by continued strong demand and capacity improvements in wet cat food, but partially offset by a noticeable slowdown in the dry dog food segment.

Despite this deceleration in mature markets, the broader global pet care category is still projected to grow at a healthy 4% to 5% compound annual rate between 2025 and 2027. Nestlé is actively defending its market-leading position by leaning heavily into premiumization, specifically targeting science-based and therapeutic diets. The company's functional and veterinary portfolios, such as Purina Pro Plan Veterinary Diets, address specific health conditions like obesity, diabetes, and gastrointestinal issues, providing a high-margin buffer against mainstream commoditization.

Analysts at Nuvama Institutional Equities observe that Purina continues to exhibit strong momentum in emerging markets, clocking high-single to low-double-digit growth over recent quarters, driven by brand penetration from Felix and Friskies.

Analysts at Huatai Research highlight that Purina benefits from significant technical and research barriers to entry in the functional food space. The broker notes that Purina ONE and Pro Plan maintain a formidable competitive moat, as lower-tier brands struggle to replicate the rigorous clinical trials and specialized ingredient sourcing required to compete in premium therapeutic segments.

The Systemic GLP-1 Risk to Broader Food

While Purina anchors the portfolio, Nestlé faces a systemic, macro-level threat from the rapid proliferation of GLP-1 receptor agonists (weight-loss medications) across its broader food and beverage categories. The adoption of these drugs fundamentally alters consumer behavior by suppressing appetite and reducing total caloric intake. This shift is causing a notable decline in the consumption of high-fat, high-calorie, and starch-based products, directly threatening Nestlé's sales volumes in legacy categories like chocolate, frozen pizza, and indulgent snacks.

To mitigate this structural volume drag, Nestlé is being forced to aggressively pivot its portfolio toward functional nutrition. Consumers utilizing GLP-1 therapies frequently experience a disproportionate loss of lean muscle mass, driving a rapid behavioral shift toward high-protein supplements and specialized nutritional interventions. Furthermore, the broader consumer market is exhibiting increased demand for hydration and digestive health products, forcing legacy food manufacturers to rethink their core formulations. Nestlé has attempted to capitalize on this through its Health Science division, launching targeted products under brands like Vital Proteins to capture the GLP-1 user base.

Analysts at Rothschild & Co Redburn explicitly caution that greater-than-expected adoption of GLP-1 agonists represents a material downside risk to their financial forecasts. The broker warns that widespread GLP-1 usage could lead to persistent, long-term volume declines across the crucial US food category, further compounding the margin pressures currently facing the company.

However, this headline figure obscures a much stronger underlying momentum across the broader EM landscape. Driver 6: Competitive Response and Private Label Threats

The Private Label Squeeze in Key European Markets

Nestlé is navigating an increasingly hostile competitive landscape, particularly in Europe where retail consolidation and inflation have fundamentally altered consumer behavior. Private label penetration has steadily increased post-COVID as retailers prioritize their own asset-light brands to capture higher margins. In the European pet food market, a category Nestlé heavily relies on for growth, private label market share now averages 15% to 20%, but spikes as high as 40% to 45% in specific channels like German specialty retailers.

The threat is compounded by the divergent pricing strategies utilized during the recent inflationary supercycle. While Nestlé and other premium branded peers implemented high-single to low-double-digit price increases to offset raw material costs, private label prices in categories like chocolate remained broadly stable. This significantly widened the absolute price gap between Nestlé's offerings and store brands, driving heightened consumer elasticity and accelerating volume share losses across mainstream European markets.

Heightened Promotional Intensity

Brand Investment ROI and Strategic Defense

To defend its market share against these mounting threats, Nestlé is fundamentally transforming its marketing capabilities and reversing the underinvestment of the pandemic era. The company has stepped up its advertising and marketing (A&P) expenditures to 8.6% of sales, with a stated target of reaching 9.0% by the end of 2025.

Crucially, management is shifting from a fragmented approach to a "fewer, bigger, better" strategy. Media support is being sharply concentrated on 150 priority brands, down from over 400 previously supported initiatives. To ensure this capital reallocation generates a positive Return on Investment (ROI), Nestlé is deploying advanced analytics and marketing mix modeling to rigorously optimize both short-term promotional sales and long-term brand equity. Furthermore, the company is leveraging AI-driven tools to generate digital content rapidly, allowing local sales teams to execute in-store promotions with much higher efficiency.

Driver 7: Capital Returns and Shareholder Yield

Capital Allocation Framework and the Buyback Pause

Nestlé's overarching capital allocation framework strictly prioritizes investments in organic growth, followed by sustainable dividend payouts, systematic balance sheet deleveraging, and finally, targeted share repurchases. Following the completion of a massive CHF 20 billion share buyback program in December 2024, management explicitly guided that no new repurchase program will be initiated in 2025. This strategic pause is heavily dictated by the company's current leverage profile. Nestlé ended 2025 with a net debt-to-EBITDA ratio of 2.85x, sitting uncomfortably near the upper boundary of its historical 2.0x to 3.0x target corridor. Consequently, management intends to direct surplus free cash flow toward reducing this ratio closer to the midpoint of the range before aggressively returning capital via market repurchases.

The mathematical reality of the company's current financial algorithm simply does not support this magnitude of aggressive wealth creation. Weighing the Positives: The Defensive Floor

While the aggressive 25%+ bull case falls short, the underlying thesis benefits from robust downside protection driven by management's self-help initiatives. Nestlé's margin trajectory and portfolio simplification efforts represent the strongest pillars of the current investment narrative. The company has demonstrated formidable execution on structural efficiencies, over-delivering on its "Fuel for Growth" cost savings program by achieving CHF 1.1 billion in savings during 2025. This operational discipline ensures that the massive planned reinvestments in brand marketing—targeted at 9% of sales—will not permanently dilute the operating margin.

Furthermore, the strategic decision to deconsolidate the capital-intensive Waters business and sell off underperforming assets like mainstream vitamins acts as a significant positive catalyst. These divestitures will fundamentally streamline the organization, allowing capital to be reallocated toward structurally advantaged powerhouse businesses like Coffee and Petcare. This strategic sharpening, combined with a progressive dividend policy that generated CHF 9.2 billion in free cash flow in 2025, establishes a formidable valuation floor that protects investors against severe downside volatility.

Weighing the Failure Scenarios: The Structural Ceilings

Conversely, the failure scenarios evaluated across the key drivers present persistent structural ceilings that actively throttle the upside potential. The competitive landscape in key European and US markets remains fiercely hostile, with private label encroachment and heightened promotional intensity forcing Nestlé into a defensive posture. Additionally, the systemic risk posed by the rapid adoption of GLP-1 agonists threatens to exert long-term, irreversible volume pressure on Nestlé's legacy frozen food and confectionery categories.

Ultimately, the plausibility of a 25%+ IRR thesis over the next 24 months is rejected.
