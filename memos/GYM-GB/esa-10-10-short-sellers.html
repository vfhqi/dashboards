4. Accounting & Governance Red Flags

(a) Revenue recognition. Monthly membership subscriptions recognised as earned. No complexity. Pre-sale of annual memberships deferred appropriately. No concern.

(b) Non-GAAP adjustments. The company reports "EBITDA less normalised rent" as its primary performance metric. This strips out the IFRS 16 accounting uplift and replaces it with actual cash rent costs. This is the correct economic metric for a lease-heavy business — honest and consistently applied.

(c) IFRS 16 lease treatment — material. Total property lease obligations (IFRS 16) approximate £385m based on available data. The reported non-property net debt of £61m (as at Dec 2024) significantly understates total economic obligations. Adjusted leverage of 1.0x uses a metric that partially excludes these lease obligations. The company discloses this transparently; the risk is analytical, not intentional obfuscation.

(e) Working capital. Subscriptions are prepaid. Strong negative working capital is a structural feature. No concern.

(i) Legal/regulatory. No material pending litigation identified. Standard UK employment law risks around zero-hours contracts and minimum wage compliance apply to the sector.

6. Counter-Arguments to Bull Case

Bull thesis: Low-cost gym market is structurally growing; GYM is a structural beneficiary.

Bear rebuttal: The structure is growing, but PureGym is capturing disproportionate share. GYM's club count grew from ~200 to ~244 in 2024-2025. PureGym added 57 clubs in the same two years. On a like-for-like basis, GYM is losing relative competitive position every year.

Bull thesis: Out-of-town expansion strategy differentiates GYM from PureGym.

Bear rebuttal: PureGym is also expanding out-of-town — explicitly targeting towns and rural areas with a similar strategy. The differentiation narrative is compelling in management presentations but challenged by observable PureGym behaviour. Both operators are running the same playbook.

Bull thesis: Leverage at 1.0x is conservative; balance sheet is clean.

Bear rebuttal: The 1.0x figure uses adjusted EBITDA as denominator. Including full lease obligations, the leverage picture is materially different. Total obligations of ~£385m against annual EBITDA-less-rent of ~£55m (estimated FY2025) implies true economic leverage closer to 7x rent-adjusted EBITDA. It is standard for the sector, but bears can use it to argue GYM is more vulnerable to a consumer downturn than the clean balance sheet implies.

Bull thesis: Margins are recovering to pre-COVID levels.

Bear rebuttal: Operating margins at 9% (trailing 12-month) vs 15.8% in 2018. The bull case assumes continued margin recovery. But permanent structural cost increases (minimum wage, energy, property) that did not exist in 2018 suggest 15.8% may not be achievable again. A more realistic ceiling might be 12-13%.

7. Historical Pattern

No prior short campaigns against The Gym Group.

Key historical episodes:

  • 2020 COVID closure: All gyms shut. GYM issued equity and drew on facilities. Raised approximately £80m in two tranches. Member recovery was rapid post-reopening.
  • 2022-2023 cost inflation period: Energy costs approximately doubled. Minimum wage rose. Margins compressed sharply. Stock fell from ~175p to ~80p trough. Bears were correct on the interim earnings impact.
  • 2023-2025 recovery: Energy costs partially hedged, wage inflation absorbed via pricing (ARPM rising), and margins began recovering. Bears from the 2022-2023 period were squeezed.
  • 2024 refinancing: Repaid £56m RCF on 1 July 2024; drew £45m term loan + £11m RCF under new facility. Lower interest costs, extending debt maturity.

Management response pattern: communicate clearly, manage investor expectations conservatively, execute operationally. No aggressive guidance, no acquisition-driven leverage. Conservative and credible management style.
