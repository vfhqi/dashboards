GYM Q2 CHANGE FORCES [C]

The Gym Group plc (GYM-GB) | Change Forces Assessment | April 2026

KEY FINDINGS

1. Will Orr's "Next Chapter" strategy has delivered a structural step-change in profitability. Group Adjusted EBITDA Less Normalised Rent rose 19% to £56.7m in FY2025, with adjusted PBT more than doubling to £10.6m. The turnaround from a statutory loss of £19.3m in FY2022 to profit is substantive.

2. The 75-site expansion target (upgraded from 50) is now funded entirely from free cash flow. FCF of £38.3m in FY2025 underpins a 20-site opening programme in 2026, the highest ever annual target. Hamish Latchem's appointment as Chief Property Officer from Aldi is the key operational hire to execute this.

3. ARPMM growth decelerated from 7% in FY2024 to 4% in FY2025. This is the primary single-line watchpoint in the investment case. Whether deceleration reflects normalisation or the ceiling of affordable pricing in the budget segment is the central empirical question.

4. Balance sheet risk is materially reduced. Non-property net debt fell to £59.3m from £61.3m. Adjusted leverage is 1.0x against a 3.0x covenant. Bank facilities extended to June 2028 and upsized to £102m.

5. The £10m buyback — the first in company history — is a signal of capital confidence, not merely financial engineering. It runs to December 2026 and is being executed by Peel Hunt. All shares are cancelled.

6. Member average tenure has lengthened to approximately 18 months through improved early-life engagement protocols. The 45-day churn intervention is producing measurable results. This extends the lifetime value curve and reduces acquisition cost drag.

7. Competitive structure in UK budget gyms is intensifying. PureGym is targeting 200+ additional sites in Europe over 3-4 years. The budget segment now accounts for 41% of all private sector UK gym members. Saturation risk is real but geographically uneven.

8. New sites opened post-strategy reboot are performing ahead of historical maturity curves. Mature site ROIC reached 27% in FY2025 (30% excluding workforce-dependent gyms), up from 25% in FY2024.

9. Technology investment is now a retention weapon. The rebuilt member app launched June 2024 delivered 100-150bps conversion improvement. Digital-first member engagement is embedded in the operational model.

10. FY2026 guidance is for EBITDA LNR at the top end of £59.6m-£60.7m analyst consensus, with revenue year-to-date up 9% and LFL growth of approximately 3% assumed.

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Gym Group entered Will Orr's tenure in September 2023 as a structurally impaired business. Post-COVID recovery was incomplete, debt was elevated, new site openings had slowed, and member retention was weak. The strategic problem was an operational machine that had grown rapidly in the 2016-2020 period but never built the retention, yield, or cost discipline to translate scale into durable returns.

Orr's Next Chapter strategy addressed this directly through three pillars: strengthen the core; accelerate the rollout of quality sites; broaden growth through new revenue streams. Two years in, the evidence of delivery is compelling. Adjusted PBT more than doubled in FY2025. FCF rose 10% to £38.3m. The company generated enough cash to fund 16 new sites, invest in technology, service its debt, begin a share buyback, and retain headroom to accelerate.

The investment case now pivots on three change forces: (1) whether the site expansion programme can be executed without diluting returns; (2) whether ARPMM can sustain positive growth as the company increasingly reprices new members while managing churn among existing ones; and (3) whether the balance sheet can absorb an acceleration from 16 to 20+ sites per year while also funding the buyback.

The competitive environment adds a fourth force. Pure Gym is the dominant competitor with roughly 46% share of search in the UK budget gym market and is expanding aggressively both in the UK and internationally via the Blink Fitness acquisition in the US. JD Gyms is also growing. The total low-cost estate in the UK grew 8.5% year-on-year to 806 clubs. GYM's 260-club estate is a fraction of the addressable market but the concentration of good-return locations — specifically high-density urban — is finite.

The bear case is that the expansion programme, while cash-funded, creates a 3-4 year period where FCF is heavily consumed by new site capex (£60-65m guided in 2026 vs £51.2m in 2025). If ARPMM decelerates further or member volumes disappoint, the operating leverage works in reverse. The company remains profitable but the step-change narrative stalls.

The bull case is that Latchem's Aldi background — 13 years of disciplined, cost-focused, volume-driven site development — is exactly the skillset needed to systematise the opening programme. Aldi built 900+ UK sites using relentless site economics discipline. Applying that capability to a pipeline of 75 gym sites over three years, funded from FCF, at 27-30% ROIC, is a highly credible capital allocation story.

CHANGE FORCE 1: STRATEGY TRANSITION UNDER WILL ORR

Will Orr joined in September 2023 from a background in consumer and leisure businesses. His first act was to stop the existing CEO's strategy and diagnose the operational base before recommitting to growth.

The prior strategy under John Treharne and subsequently Richard Darwin had prioritised site count expansion with insufficient discipline around yield and retention. The estate grew to over 230 sites but member economics were deteriorating. Average revenue per member was stagnant. Churn at the 45-day mark was structurally high. New site returns were running below the 25% target.

Orr's diagnosis was that the business had three problems: insufficient member engagement in the first 45 days, underdeveloped pricing discipline, and a site opening programme that had lost focus on location quality over quantity. The Next Chapter strategy addresses all three.

On retention: the company identified the 45-day window as the critical inflection point. Members who visit fewer than four times in their first 45 days are materially more likely to cancel. The company built a digital intervention protocol — personalised exercise plans, induction nudges, early-life checklists — that runs via the member app. The result is measurable: average member tenure has extended to approximately 18 months. This is a direct ARPMM lever because a member retained for 18 months instead of 12 months generates 50% more lifetime revenue.

On pricing: headline standard membership price in December 2025 was £25.64 per month, up from £24.53 in December 2024. Joining fees were also increased. Selective base membership repricing has been layered in where market conditions allow. ARPMM of £21.60 is up 4% year-on-year.

On site quality: the shift was explicit. Orr stated that ROIC targets take precedence over site number targets. The prior 50-site target was replaced by a 75-site target only after new sites demonstrated they could hit the 27-30% ROIC threshold. The addition of Latchem as CPO in December 2024 was the structural execution hire — somebody with proven capability to identify, negotiate, and build sites at volume and speed.

This is a genuine strategic change, not incremental improvement. The company is transitioning from a scale-first model to a returns-first model that happens also to be scaling.

CHANGE FORCE 2: EBITDA TRAJECTORY AND GUIDANCE

FY2025 EBITDA LNR of £56.7m was a 19% improvement on FY2024's £47.7m. This followed a 16% improvement in FY2024 over FY2023. The cumulative improvement from the nadir is substantial.

The components of the improvement are: member count growth (FY2025 year-end membership not disclosed for FY2025 full year but H1 2025 average was 953,000 vs 914,000 in H1 2024), ARPMM growth of 4%, and cost leverage as the fixed-cost base is spread across a larger revenue base. Revenue rose 8% to £244.9m. EBITDA LNR margin reached 23%, confirming growing operating leverage.

FY2026 guidance is for EBITDA LNR at the top end of the £59.6m-£60.7m consensus range. This implies circa 6-7% growth. The guidance is accompanied by 9% year-to-date revenue growth in early 2026, which suggests the first months of FY2026 are tracking ahead. The company is also targeting a run rate of £65m EBITDA LNR as the estate scales toward its target.

The sustainability question is whether the EBITDA margin can expand as new sites are added. New sites are dilutive in year one — they typically take 18-24 months to reach mature membership levels. With 20 new sites planned for 2026 (vs 16 in 2025), there is a meaningful cohort of sub-mature sites that depresses near-term margin. The operational leverage narrative requires time.

The long-run trajectory, if 75 new sites are added over 3 years at 27-30% site ROIC, implies EBITDA LNR well above £65m by 2028-2029. The company has not published specific longer-term financial targets.

CHANGE FORCE 3: SITE EXPANSION PROGRAMME

The acceleration from a 50-site to 75-site three-year target is the most significant strategic commitment the company has made. It is backed by specific execution capability (Latchem), a specific capital allocation framework (FCF-funded only), and specific economic targets (30% average ROIC).

The FY2025 estate ended at 260 sites. By end-2026, the target is approximately 280 sites. By end-2028, approximately 335 sites. This represents a 29% increase in estate size over three years from the current base.

The critical question is location availability. The best-returning sites are in high-density urban areas and out-of-town retail parks. GYM has opened 60 out-of-town sites since 2020 and the Savills analysis of the low-cost gym market confirms that retail warehouse locations are the most actively contested. PureGym was the most acquisitive operator in retail warehouse parks in both 2024 and 2025.

Latchem's Aldi background is directly relevant here. Aldi negotiated and built hundreds of UK sites — many in the same retail park locations GYM targets — by combining speed of execution with disciplined site selection. The institutional knowledge of how to source, negotiate, and build at volume is transferable.

Capex guidance for 2026 is £60-65m, up from £51.2m in FY2025. The FCF of £38.3m does not directly cover this — the difference is funded from the RCF. Non-property net debt of £59.3m at end-2025 will likely increase in FY2026 before the new sites begin generating mature returns. Leverage at 1.0x has ample headroom under the 3.0x covenant.

CHANGE FORCE 4: HAMISH LATCHEM AND PROPERTY EXECUTION CAPABILITY

Latchem joined in December 2024 as Chief Property Officer. He spent 13 years at Aldi in multiple operational and property roles, most recently as National Store Development Director. His mandate covers all property acquisition, facilities management, estate management, and gym format and design.

The appointment signals that the company treats property execution as the primary bottleneck to growth — not capital, not members, not competition. This is the correct diagnosis. The capital is available (FCF, RCF). The market opportunity is clear (structural growth in budget gym penetration). The constraint is whether the company can identify, negotiate, secure, and build 20-25 sites per year systematically.

Aldi built from approximately 300 UK stores in 2010 to over 1,000 today. The infrastructure — site identification, lease negotiation, planning, fit-out, launch — was built to sustain 50-80 openings per year. GYM's 20-site annual target is modest by comparison.

The risk is that GYM's opening economics are more complex than a food retail store. A gym requires planning permissions, equipment installation, staffing, and member onboarding. A failed opening — a site that misses ROIC targets — is a multi-year drag because lease commitments are typically 10-15 years.

CHANGE FORCE 5: ARPMM TRAJECTORY

ARPMM rose 7% in FY2024 and 4% in FY2025. The deceleration is real and worth scrutiny.

The deceleration has several possible explanations. First, the company may be hitting a natural ceiling on how much it can charge within the budget gym price positioning. At £25.64/month for a standard membership, the product is still clearly budget, but further increases risk churn among price-sensitive members. Second, the mix of new members — who join at the current headline price — is growing as a share of the member base. New members have shorter tenures on average, which can dilute measured ARPMM. Third, the company is using pricing as a retention tool — locking in existing members at their current price while charging new members higher rates. This creates a dual-pricing dynamic that limits blended ARPMM growth.

The counter-argument is that membership tenure is increasing. If the average member stays for 18 months rather than 12-14, the blended ARPMM figure stabilises because the member population is aging into a higher-loyalty profile. The lifetime value improvement does not show up fully in ARPMM but does show up in FCF.

The 2026 guidance assumes approximately 3% LFL growth. This is consistent with 3-4% ARPMM growth combined with modest volume growth from new sites ramping. The number is achievable but not a high bar.

CHANGE FORCE 6: CAPITAL ALLOCATION SHIFT — BUYBACK AND BALANCE SHEET

The £10m buyback is the company's first return of capital in its history as a public company. It was announced alongside the FY2025 results in March 2026.

The signal value of a first-ever buyback from a company that was posting statutory losses as recently as FY2022 is meaningful. Management is communicating that the business model is now generating excess capital beyond what is required for operations, maintenance capex, and growth.

The buyback runs to December 2026. At the current market cap of approximately £220m (182p × ~120m shares), £10m represents approximately 4.5% of the float over 12 months. The impact on EPS is mechanical but not transformative.

The more important capital allocation question is the trade-off between buybacks, site expansion, and leverage reduction. The company's stated framework is: maintain leverage at 1.0x; fund expansion from FCF; return surplus capital via buyback. This is a credible and conservative framework. The risk is that 2026 capex of £60-65m combined with the buyback programme strains the FCF envelope. FCF of £38.3m in FY2025 would not cover both. The company has £102m of bank facilities at 1.0x leverage — it has runway.

CHANGE FORCE 7: COMPETITIVE DYNAMICS

The UK budget gym market is not saturated at the national level but is intensifying at the site level in specific geographies.

PureGym dominates with 46% share of search, approximately 680 UK sites, and a globally expanding business following the Blink Fitness acquisition. PureGym opened 32 UK sites in 2025 — double GYM's 16. JD Gyms, owned by JD Sports, is also expanding. Anytime Fitness operates a franchise model that requires less capital but produces variable quality.

The structural dynamic is favourable. The low-cost segment is growing at 8.5% per year by club count. Market value of the budget segment surpassed £986m in 2025 and is projected to exceed £1 billion in 2026. Penetration remains at 16% of UK adults vs a pre-COVID peak of 18% — there is room to recover lost ground.

GYM's differentiation from PureGym is primarily geographic concentration (GYM is more heavily London/urban weighted), product quality (GYM positions on a "premium budget" proposition with staffed sites), and increasingly on digital member experience. GYM's staffing model costs more but delivers higher member satisfaction and retention than the fully unmanned PureGym model.

The market saturation risk is concentrated in specific sub-markets: Central London and major city centres where the pipeline of viable sites is finite. Out-of-town retail parks remain less contested. Latchem's Aldi experience in exactly this format (retail warehouse, standalone purpose-built) may give GYM a structural sourcing advantage.

THEMATIC SENSITIVITIES

T1 — Bear Market / Risk-Off Resilience: Rating A (Highly Resilient)

Budget gym memberships exhibit near-counter-cyclical characteristics. During the 2022-2024 UK cost-of-living crisis — the most severe consumer squeeze in decades — GYM's member volumes and ARPMM both increased. The company grew revenue 17.99% in FY2023 and 11% in FY2024.

The mechanism is clear. At £25/month, gym membership is the cheapest structured health and social activity available to a UK adult. Members under financial pressure cancel restaurant subscriptions, streaming services, and holidays before they cancel the gym. PureGym's own data shows 73% of members plan to maintain or increase gym frequency despite financial stress.

The generational dimension reinforces this. Gen Z and Millennials rank gym membership as a priority spend. 43% of 16-34 year olds planned to increase gym spend in 2025. This demographic cohort is GYM's primary market.

The risk is at the margin: if a recession is severe enough to produce significant unemployment in GYM's target demographic (urban young professionals and blue-collar workers), absolute member count could decline. But the £25/month price point means even a 5% revenue decline from churn would be a modest earnings hit.

Rating: A — Highly Resilient. Budget gym is one of the most defensive consumer spending categories. GYM benefits specifically from being at the cheapest end of the fitness spectrum.

T2 — AI Disruption / Digital Fitness: Rating C (Manageable Medium-Term Risk)

The at-home digital fitness disruption narrative peaked with Peloton in 2020-2021 and has materially unwound. Peloton's market cap fell from over $50bn to under $2bn. The core insight is that at-home fitness requires capital investment (equipment), self-discipline, and space — all of which are less available to GYM's target demographic than to premium fitness users.

The medium-term AI disruption risk is more subtle. AI coaching, personalised programming, and smart home gym equipment are improving rapidly. A Gymbox-at-home product combining an AI coach with a wall-mounted screen could theoretically replace a budget gym visit for exercises that do not require specialist equipment.

GYM's response is the upgraded member app (launched June 2024) with 300+ exercise guidance videos, personalised workout plans, and early-life engagement protocols. The app is a digital retention tool, not a digital product substitute. The company correctly recognises that the gym floor experience — equipment, space, social environment — is not replicable at home at a budget price point.

The real AI risk for GYM is competitive: if PureGym or a tech-native entrant builds a significantly superior digital experience that drives member acquisition, GYM could lose market share on the basis of product rather than price. GYM's app investment (partnered with Ensono for digital infrastructure) is addressing this but is not yet a differentiating product advantage.

Timeline for material AI disruption: 5-7 years for any genuine at-home substitution effect in the budget segment. Rating: C — Manageable but requires ongoing digital investment to maintain relevance.

T3 — Iran/Oil/Geopolitical Risk: Rating B (Low Direct Risk, Indirect Consumer Impact)

GYM has no direct geopolitical exposure. It is a domestic UK consumer services business. Its cost base is primarily labour, energy, and property. Oil price does not directly drive energy costs for a gym estate — the primary energy consumption is electricity for equipment, HVAC, and lighting.

The indirect risk channel is: higher oil prices → UK consumer confidence decline → discretionary spending reduction → gym membership at risk. However, the T1 analysis demonstrates that GYM's price point insulates it from most consumer confidence effects. Members who cut discretionary spending in a stressed environment cancel premium subscriptions, not budget gym memberships.

The energy cost channel is more direct. Higher energy prices increase operating costs per site. GYM's energy costs are embedded in site-level operating expenses. The company has not disclosed its energy hedging position, but budget gym operators typically enter into fixed-price supply contracts for 1-2 years ahead, limiting near-term exposure.

At £25/month membership and approximately £100-150k annual energy cost per site (estimated), even a 20% energy cost increase adds only £20-30k per site per year — a fraction of the circa £500k+ site-level EBITDA at mature occupancy.

Rating: B — Low direct risk. Iran/oil primarily affects GYM through broader UK consumer confidence, which is a secondary effect. The business is largely insulated by price point and demographic resilience.

SCEPTICAL SUMMARY

GYM's financial recovery is real but the execution risk ahead is substantial. The company is moving from a stabilisation phase into an acceleration phase simultaneously — more new sites, higher capex, a buyback programme, and an ARPMM growth rate that is decelerating.

The three things that could go wrong: (1) New site ROIC disappoints. Sites opened in 2025-2026 perform below the 27-30% target because Latchem's pipeline identified fewer genuinely high-density locations than assumed, forcing GYM into second-tier sites. (2) ARPMM growth stalls at 2-3% per year as price sensitivity among existing members increases and the company reaches the ceiling of premium budget positioning. (3) PureGym's scale advantage — both in marketing spend and site count — creates an asymmetric competitive position where GYM loses the "best" sites in a competitive bidding situation.

These risks are real. They are not the base case. The evidence of execution over 2023-2025 — consistent delivery against targets, improved site returns, genuine operational improvement — justifies reasonable confidence. But the 75-site target is ambitious and Will Orr has been in post for less than three years.

The investment case requires ongoing monitoring of: ARPMM trajectory (quarterly), new site opening cadence vs guidance, site-level ROIC disclosure, and any sign that Latchem's site pipeline is less deep than management indicates.
