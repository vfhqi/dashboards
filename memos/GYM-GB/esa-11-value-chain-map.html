Executive Summary

The Gym Group operates as a pure-play budget gym operator in the UK, sitting at the downstream retail end of a fitness value chain that runs from equipment manufacturing through property, staffing, and technology to end consumer. Its role is simple: aggregate capacity (space + equipment + access infrastructure), price it at a low monthly subscription, and drive volume sufficient to cover a predominantly fixed cost base. The business model is structurally high-operating-leverage: circa 30%+ of revenues are locked in property lease costs before a single member walks through the door, with labour, equipment maintenance, and technology stacked on top. Revenue is almost entirely recurring membership fees, supplemented by nascent PT revenue (Gym Team model, still loss-making at the NIC-burdened run-rate). At 244 sites, the company has meaningful density in the UK but sits at roughly half the footprint of PureGym (422 sites), creating a structural disadvantage in brand recognition, negotiating leverage, and fixed-cost absorption. #A

The UK fitness sector value chain is not vertically integrated at the operator level. GYM buys all equipment from third parties (Life Fitness, Matrix, Technogym), leases all real estate from landlords, and runs proprietary technology only at the app and access-control layer. This means switching costs are largely behavioural (member inertia, location convenience) rather than structural: there is no proprietary equipment, no owned property, no captive supplier relationship. The one quasi-structural moat is the app ecosystem — 80% app penetration and 93% of add-on sales through app — which creates a data and engagement flywheel, though this is replicable by PureGym at scale. The Gen Z skew (44% of members) is a positive demand signal for the long run but also a warning: Gen Z members are digitally mobile, price-sensitive, and prone to churn when lifestyle or location changes. #A

Position sustainability is under genuine pressure from the scale gap. PureGym's 2x site count confers negotiating advantages across the entire upstream chain — better lease terms, better equipment pricing, better technology contract rates — and its marketing spend per member is lower. GYM's white-space opportunity (PwC analysis: 600-850 sites addressable in the UK) is real, but the capital and balance sheet required to close the gap materially is substantial, and PureGym is not standing still. The Gym Team PT model is a credible attempt to differentiate the in-gym experience and build higher ARPU, but it is currently dilutive: the £1.3m NIC headwind from UK employer NIC increases arrived before the PT revenue line had matured. Until PT revenue demonstrably exceeds PT cost burden, it is an investment with uncertain payback. #J

On balance, GYM holds a durable but fragile second-place position in UK budget fitness. The low-cost segment itself is structurally attractive — recurring, non-cyclical, growing — and GYM's app infrastructure and Gen Z penetration are real assets. But scale disadvantage versus PureGym is the dominant competitive fact, and the lease-heavy cost structure limits pricing and margin flexibility. Position sustainability rating: YELLOW.

1. End-to-End Value Chain (14 Stages)

The UK budget fitness value chain runs from raw material and component manufacturing through equipment production, real estate development, gym fit-out, digital infrastructure, staffing pipelines, and finally to the end consumer paying a monthly subscription. The Gym Group participates directly in only the downstream half — fit-out, operations, digital access, and the consumer interface — making it dependent on upstream players for the critical inputs that determine unit economics.

Stage 1: Raw Materials and Component Supply. Steel, rubber, electronics, and foam form the base inputs for fitness equipment. Highly commoditised; no direct GYM interaction. Commodity price movements flow through with a lag via equipment procurement pricing. #I

Stage 2: Fitness Equipment Manufacturing. OEMs including Life Fitness, Matrix Fitness, Technogym, and Precor manufacture commercial-grade cardio and resistance equipment. This is a consolidated global market — three to four major players dominate commercial supply — giving them pricing power over operators. GYM buys all equipment; zero manufacturing in-house. Equipment typically depreciates over 7-10 years with maintenance contracts layered on top. #I

Stage 3: Equipment Distribution and Logistics. Manufacturers operate direct-to-operator logistics for large commercial orders or use third-party logistics for smaller deployments. GYM coordinates equipment delivery as part of new-site fit-out capex. No direct margin participation here; this is a pass-through cost. #I

Stage 4: Commercial Real Estate Development. Developers and landlords create the physical shells — retail parks, high street units, leisure park anchor sites — that GYM occupies. The shift post-Covid toward landlords actively seeking footfall-generating tenants (gyms, food, health) has been structurally positive for GYM's lease negotiation position. GYM does not own any real estate; 100% leased. #I

Stage 5: Site Acquisition and Lease Negotiation. GYM's property team identifies locations, negotiates leases, and secures planning. Lease tenure is typically 15-20 years. Rent represents approximately 30%+ of the total cost base — the single largest fixed cost line. At 244 sites vs PureGym's 422, GYM is a smaller counterparty in the landlord relationship and likely commands less favourable lease terms on average. #A

Stage 6: Gym Fit-Out and Capex Deployment. New site fit-out involves structural works, flooring, equipment installation, access control hardware, and branding. Typical capex per new site is circa £1-2m depending on shell condition. This is funded from operating cash flow and debt facilities. Fit-out quality and equipment density are table-stakes competitive factors in budget gym; members compare equipment range and maintenance quality across operators. #I

Stage 7: Technology Infrastructure — Access Control and App. Budget gym economics depend on unstaffed or lightly-staffed access — the app (or fob/card) is the gateway to the club. GYM has invested heavily here: 80% of members use the app as their primary access and engagement tool; 93% of add-on purchases (PT bookings, class upgrades, etc.) flow through app. This creates a captive digital engagement layer that is the closest thing GYM has to a structural switching cost. #A

Stage 8: Payment Processing and Membership Management. Direct debit infrastructure, subscription management, and payment failure handling form the back-office plumbing of a membership business. GYM runs standard UK DD infrastructure (Bacs) supplemented by app-based payment for add-ons. Churn management (lapsed DDs, voluntary cancellations) is operationally critical given the low-priced, no-commitment nature of the product. #I

Stage 9: Staffing and Labour Markets. GYM employs gym floor staff (part-time, minimum-wage adjacent), Gym Team PTs (employed, not franchised — a deliberate differentiation vs PureGym's self-employed PT model), and head office/regional management. Labour is the second-largest cost driver. The employer NIC increase (April 2025) delivered a £1.3m headwind specifically against the PT payroll, arriving ahead of PT revenue scale. #I

Stage 10: Personal Training Services (Gym Team Model). GYM's employed PT model is an attempt to upgrade the in-gym service proposition, add an ARPU lever, and differentiate from PureGym on experience quality. PTs are on GYM payroll (vs competitor models where PTs rent floor space or operate as concessionaires). This eliminates the variability of independent PT quality but adds fixed labour cost. Revenue is still lagging the cost burden at current penetration; the model's economics improve materially as PT session volumes scale. #J

Stage 11: Group Exercise and Class Programming. Budget operators have historically competed primarily on equipment access, not class programming. GYM has added class functionality (e.g., Les Mills virtual and live class content) through the app and in-studio. This is a minor but growing value-added layer targeted at female and Gen Z members who have class-heavy preferences. #I

Stage 12: Marketing and Member Acquisition. Predominantly digital — social media, SEO, referral codes, and location-based search. Gen Z acquisition skews heavily toward social media (TikTok, Instagram). At 44% Gen Z membership, GYM has the strongest Gen Z penetration of any UK budget operator, which is a distribution and brand relevance asset in the fastest-growing adult gym demographic. Traditional above-the-line spend is minimal versus premium operators. #A

Stage 13: Member Experience and Retention. The gym floor experience — equipment availability, cleanliness, staffing responsiveness, app functionality — drives net promoter scores, app ratings, and churn rates. Budget gyms compete hard on cleanliness and equipment uptime because pricing is homogenous. The Gym Team PT model is partly a retention mechanism: members with a PT relationship churn at lower rates. #A

Stage 14: Exit and Churn. Low-commitment memberships (monthly rolling) mean churn is structurally higher than premium operators but the barrier to re-acquisition is also lower. The no-contract model is a competitive necessity in the budget segment — members would defect to PureGym or easyGym if locked in. Churn visibility is high via the app (lapsed bookings, access frequency drop) and creates a proactive re-engagement opportunity that GYM is developing. #I

2. The Gym Group's Position in the Value Chain

GYM's competitive position is defined by where it sits (downstream retail operations, entirely dependent on upstream suppliers), what it controls (digital access layer, brand, site selection), and what it does not control (real estate values, equipment pricing, labour markets). The following dimensions map the specifics.

Dimension 1: Real Estate Dependency. Lease costs represent 30%+ of the total cost base, all at landlords' terms, with 15-20 year tenure commitments. This is the dominant structural vulnerability: in a rising rent environment, GYM cannot easily renegotiate or exit. The post-Covid shift in landlord appetite for leisure tenants provided favourable entry conditions for recent new sites, but this tailwind is not permanent. At 244 sites, GYM is a meaningful but not dominant tenant counterparty; PureGym at 422 sites negotiates from a stronger position across the same landlord base. #J

Dimension 2: Equipment Procurement — No Proprietary Advantage. GYM sources all equipment from standard commercial OEMs (Life Fitness, Matrix, Technogym). There is no proprietary equipment, no exclusive supply agreement, and no manufacturing capability. Equipment is a commodity input. Pricing is volume-driven — at 244 sites vs PureGym's 422, GYM pays more per unit or gets worse terms. The equipment mix is industry-standard, which means members can and do comparison-shop across operators on equipment count and quality. #A

Dimension 3: Digital Infrastructure — Primary Competitive Asset. The app is GYM's strongest proprietary asset: 80% member penetration and 93% of add-on sales channelled through the platform. This creates a data flywheel (usage frequency, class preferences, PT demand signals), a retention lever (engaged digital members churn less), and an ARPU expansion channel (add-on upsell). The app also enables access-control at low staffing cost, which is the operational backbone of the budget model. This is replicable by PureGym but GYM's execution here is demonstrably strong. #J

Dimension 4: Scale Position — Meaningful Deficit Versus PureGym. 244 sites vs 422 sites is not a rounding error. PureGym is approximately 73% larger by site count and has been the UK market leader since acquiring Soho Gyms and LA Fitness assets through prior cycles. Scale drives better lease terms, equipment pricing, technology contract leverage, and per-member marketing efficiency. GYM is the clear second-place operator; the gap is not insurmountable but it requires sustained capital deployment over multiple years to close meaningfully. #J

Dimension 5: White Space Opportunity — Structural Long-Runway Growth. PwC analysis identifies 600-850 addressable sites in the UK. GYM currently operates 244 — implying penetration of 29-41% of the addressable market at the low end. This is a genuine long-duration growth opportunity but it is shared: PureGym is also targeting the same white space. The question is execution speed and capital efficiency. GYM's site roll-out pace relative to PureGym's will determine whether the gap widens or narrows. #A

Dimension 6: Labour Model — Employed PT as Differentiator. The Gym Team model — PTs on GYM payroll rather than operating as concessionaires or self-employed contractors — is a structural differentiation from PureGym and most budget competitors. Employed PTs provide consistent service quality, are incentivised to drive member outcomes rather than just session volumes, and create a more curated in-gym experience. The downside is fixed payroll exposure: the £1.3m NIC headwind from the April 2025 employer NIC increase arrived ahead of the revenue ramp, creating a near-term margin drag. #I

Dimension 7: Revenue Model — Predominantly Flat Subscription with Limited Upsell Penetration. The core membership product is a low monthly fee with no commitment. Add-on revenue (PT sessions, premium classes, guest passes) is the ARPU growth vector. Currently, 93% of add-on sales flow through app — the infrastructure is there — but add-on penetration as a percentage of the member base remains modest. PT revenue is growing but from a low base against a live cost burden. The revenue model is simpler and less sticky than premium operators with contract commitments. #A

Dimension 8: Gen Z Penetration — Demand Asset, Retention Risk. At 44% Gen Z membership, GYM has the strongest youth skew of any UK budget operator. This is a structural positive: Gen Z adults are the fastest-growing gym-going demographic and their lifetime value (if retained) is high. The risk is that Gen Z also exhibits the highest churn rates — they move cities, change life routines, and have the highest price sensitivity. GYM's app engagement model is the right tool to manage Gen Z retention, but the demographic is inherently volatile. #J

Dimension 9: Cost Structure — High Fixed, Low Variable. Leases (30%+), depreciation, and labour are predominantly fixed. This creates high operational leverage: member count growth drops through to EBITDA at elevated incremental margins; member count decline hits EBITDA hard and fast. The no-commitment model means membership volumes can move quickly in either direction. This is the key risk in any macro downturn scenario — though historically, budget fitness has proven relatively resilient as members trade down from premium operators. #A

Dimension 10: Technology Switching Costs — Moderate. Members' primary switching cost is habitual convenience (location, routine) rather than technology lock-in. The GYM app is well-regarded but a member can cancel, join PureGym, and download the PureGym app in under 10 minutes. The app creates engagement stickiness, not structural lock-in. This is a key asymmetry versus business models with genuine contractual or data-based switching costs. #J

Dimension 11: Competitive Set — Budget Segment is Consolidating. The UK budget gym market is effectively a two-horse race: PureGym and GYM account for the overwhelming majority of low-cost gym memberships. Secondary players (easyGym, Bannatyne, 24/7 Fitness, local independents) are fragmented and subscale. This consolidation is a medium-term positive — the competitive intensity is known and manageable — but it also means there is no weaker third-place operator to take share from; growth must come from white space or defections from premium operators. #A

Dimension 12: Barriers to Entry at Site Level — Moderate. Opening a new gym requires lease negotiation, fit-out capex, equipment procurement, staffing, and a local marketing push. This is not trivial but it is not prohibitive. The main entry barrier is capital and site availability — and with PwC identifying 600-850 sites of white space, the raw location opportunity is ample for both GYM and PureGym. A new national entrant would face scale and brand disadvantage, but local/regional independents can still enter and compete on specific sites. #A

3. Key Risks and Opportunities (10 bullets)

The following 10 bullets cover the most material risks and opportunities identified at ESA stage for GYM's value chain position.

Risk 1: Lease Cost Inflation Traps Margin. #J Rent as 30%+ of costs is the structural vulnerability. If rent reviews at lease renewal run ahead of membership revenue growth — which is constrained by price positioning in the budget segment — GYM's margin compresses with no operating lever to pull. The no-ownership model eliminates upside from property appreciation but also eliminates balance sheet risk; the P&L risk is the one to watch.

Risk 2: PureGym Accelerates Site Roll-Out, Widening the Gap. #J PureGym at 422 sites is already dominant; if it rolls out 40-50 sites per annum while GYM rolls 20-25, the relative scale disadvantage widens each year, compounding the procurement, marketing, and landlord-negotiation gap. The strategic imperative to accelerate roll-out must be weighed against capital efficiency and balance sheet capacity.

Risk 3: Employer NIC Headwinds Arrive Ahead of PT Revenue Scale. #J The £1.3m NIC cost increase (April 2025) is a live margin drag. The Gym Team PT model will be economically value-accretive only if PT session volumes ramp materially in the next 12-24 months. If PT revenue growth is slow — due to low member awareness, pricing sensitivity, or staffing attrition — the model imposes ongoing fixed cost ahead of revenue, which is a worse trade than the original status quo.

Risk 4: Gen Z Churn Spikes in Macro Stress. #A Gen Z's 44% share of membership is a demand asset in a growth market. In a downturn or cost-of-living squeeze, Gen Z members are the most likely to cancel a discretionary monthly subscription — even a low-cost one. Budget fitness has historically been defensive, but the empirical track record for a Gen Z-heavy budget gym through a genuine recession is limited.

Risk 5: Equipment Supplier Pricing Power. #A With no proprietary equipment and a vendor set consolidated around three or four global OEMs, GYM has limited ability to push back on equipment price increases or negotiate supply priority during capacity-constrained periods. At 244 sites, the order size does not confer meaningfully better pricing than mid-scale regional competitors.

Opportunity 1: PT Revenue Scaling to ARPU Uplift. #J If Gym Team PT achieves significant penetration — even 5-10% of the member base engaging in regular PT sessions — the incremental revenue per member is material against the fixed employed-PT cost structure. The employed model means the marginal cost of an additional PT session is near-zero once PT headcount is in place; revenue scaling is high-margin at the unit level.

Opportunity 2: White Space Roll-Out at Disciplined Returns. #J 600-850 addressable UK sites with 244 currently occupied implies a multi-year growth runway that is visible and well-supported by third-party analysis. If GYM can sustain site economics (payback periods, lease returns) as it scales, this is a rare case of an operator with both a credible long-duration growth opportunity and a tested site-level model. The execution risk is in quality of site selection and capital allocation discipline, not in the existence of the opportunity.

Opportunity 3: App as ARPU and Retention Platform. #A The 80%/93% app metrics suggest GYM has built the plumbing for a meaningful digital upsell engine. As PT, class bookings, and other add-ons deepen, the app becomes an increasingly high-margin revenue channel. Data from app usage also improves targeting, reduces churn through proactive re-engagement, and builds a member relationship that is harder to replicate at the point of use.

Opportunity 4: Gen Z Lifetime Value if Retained. #J The 44% Gen Z penetration is a first-mover positioning advantage in the demographic most likely to build long-term gym habits. If GYM's app and PT service create genuine retention among Gen Z members in their 20s, the lifetime value of the cohort is substantial. The strategic imperative is to convert early acquisition into durable habit before PureGym narrows the brand preference gap.

Opportunity 5: Landlord Tailwind — Footfall-Generating Tenant Premium. #I Post-Covid, retail and leisure landlords have been actively seeking tenants that drive footfall rather than e-commerce-threatened retail. Budget gyms are high-footfall, daily-visit operators — exactly the profile landlords want to anchor sites. This creates an opportunity to negotiate better lease terms, landlord contributions to fit-out, and shorter initial lease breaks on new sites. GYM has explicitly referenced this tailwind; the question is duration.
