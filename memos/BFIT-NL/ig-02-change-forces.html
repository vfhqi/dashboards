BFIT Q2 CHANGE FORCES [C]

KEY FINDINGS

1. The primary change force is a structural FCF inflection. Basic-Fit generated positive free cash flow for the first time at scale in FY2025 (~€26m), confirming the business has crossed a critical capital-efficiency threshold after a decade of growth investment.

2. The Clever Fit acquisition (completed November 2025, €160m cash + €15m earn-out) is transformational. It makes Basic-Fit the largest gym franchisor in Europe, the outright market leader in Germany with ~450 combined clubs, and expands the group to 12 countries and ~2,150 clubs overnight.

3. France 24/7 staffless approval is a material structural cost reduction. The Q1 2026 regulatory change permits ~200 ground-floor French clubs to operate unstaffed. BFIT expects ~€10m in annualised savings, with further amendments signalled. This is permanent, not cyclical.

4. The convertible bond put option (June 2026, €303.7m outstanding) is the single most important near-term financial risk. Management has pre-positioned €290m in new bank facilities (ABN AMRO, ING, Rabobank). Execution risk is moderate but real — any liquidity stress would crystallise a bear case rapidly.

5. Revenue and EBITDA guidance raised post-Q1 2026. FY2026 revenue guide is €1.64–1.69bn, EBITDA guide raised €10m to €415–455m. Q1 2026 revenue came in at €396m (+19% YoY), ahead of expectations.

6. ARPM trajectory is measured but constructive. Average revenue per member per month rose from €24.24 (2024) to €24.91 (2025) for owned clubs. The upward path is slow but structurally supported by Premium tier adoption and incremental pricing.

7. The franchise model entry is a strategic pivot with option value. Clever Fit provides operating proof of concept. BFIT is now exploring BFIT-branded franchises in France, Germany, and Spain — a capital-light growth lever that did not exist 18 months ago.

8. Competitive moat is strengthening. Basic-Fit now leads Europe with 6m members and €1.4bn+ revenue. Planet Fitness entered Spain only in 2024 with one JV club. McFit/RSG remains at ~1.8m members. No immediate threat to BFIT's scale advantage.

9. Operating leverage is visible but not yet optimised. Gross margins approach 80%. EBITDA per club improves materially as clubs mature past year 3. The 2025 cohort carries embedded earnings growth potential over the next 2–3 years.

10. The path to leverage below 2.0x EBITDA by end-2026 is achievable but depends on converting bond put option without crisis and on EBITDA delivery at the top half of guidance range.

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Basic-Fit is in the middle of a genuine earnings inflection. The investment case in early 2026 rests on four converging forces that, if they compound, could revalue the stock materially from its current price near its 52-week midpoint.

The first and most critical force is the FCF turn. For the first time in its listed history, Basic-Fit generated meaningful positive free cash flow in FY2025 — approximately €26m on a reported basis. The trajectory is steep. With EBITDA guided to €415–455m in 2026, capex constrained to ~50 net new clubs (versus 125 in 2025), and lease-adjusted cash generation improving, FCF could reach €80–120m by year-end 2026. The market has not yet priced this step-change.

The second force is the Clever Fit acquisition. The €175m deal closed November 2025 and transformed BFIT's competitive position in Germany — the largest gym market in Europe by population — virtually overnight. Clever Fit brought ~383 franchise clubs and ~39 owned clubs, immediate leadership in a market BFIT was only penetrating gradually through organic openings. The franchise model is new for BFIT and introduces a more capital-efficient growth engine. Integration in early months has already yielded profitability improvement, per management commentary.

The third force is France 24/7. With ~900 clubs in France and the labour-intensive staffed operating model previously mandated by law, the April 2026 regulatory change is both a cost catalyst and a proof-of-concept for further deregulation. The €10m annualised saving is immediate. Management has flagged expectations of further amendments — suggesting a runway to materially higher savings over 24 months.

The fourth force is the franchise model expansion. Having bought the franchise infrastructure via Clever Fit, BFIT is now assessing BFIT-branded franchise roll-out in France, Germany, and Spain. Capital-light club openings at franchise economics would dramatically reduce capex intensity while sustaining top-line growth. This is an option, not a guarantee — but the optionality has real value.

Against these forces sit three risks. First, the June 2026 convertible bond put. Management has €290m in committed facilities ready, but the mechanics of a large debt redemption amid ongoing capital investment are not trivial. The non-cash €10.8m finance cost adjustment in H1 2025 signalled management's own rising concern about the put being exercised. Second, integration execution on Clever Fit — 383 franchise clubs across Germany, Switzerland, and Eastern Europe carry diverse quality, IT systems, and franchisee relationships. Third, the pace of France 24/7 implementation — only ~200 clubs qualify immediately, and the 50-club pilot commencing May 2026 suggests full rollout will take 18–24 months.

Overall assessment: the change forces are real, the timing is now, and the risk-reward on a 12–36 month view is positive — with the bond put risk as the key near-term gating item.

CHANGE FORCE 1: FCF INFLECTION AND STRUCTURAL CAPITAL EFFICIENCY

Basic-Fit's historical financial narrative was defined by heavy investment. Between 2017 and 2021, the company opened hundreds of clubs annually, financed through debt and equity, with FCF chronically negative. The post-COVID rebuild (2022–2024) saw the estate mature and operating leverage emerge.

FY2025 delivered positive FCF for the first time at material scale: approximately €26m reported. The driver was threefold. Club openings slowed from a peak of ~250/year to 85 net owned clubs in 2025. Operating cash generation improved as the 2021–2023 club cohort matured past breakeven. Capex intensity per club remained contained.

The trajectory matters more than the absolute number. With net openings capped at ~50 owned clubs in 2026 and EBITDA guidance of €415–455m, FCF could reach €80–120m in 2026 if EBITDA delivers at the mid-to-upper end and the bond put is managed cleanly. This would represent a 3–4x improvement in FCF in a single year.

The market consistently discounts BFIT on a backward-looking leverage basis. FCF yield relative to current enterprise value could shift from sub-2% to 5–7% by end-2026. This re-rating catalyst is not yet priced, in Watson's judgement.

CHANGE FORCE 2: CLEVER FIT ACQUISITION — STRATEGIC PIVOT TO FRANCHISE

The Clever Fit acquisition, closed November 2025 for €160m cash plus €15m earn-out, is the most strategically significant event in BFIT's history since its 2018 IPO.

The strategic rationale is threefold. First, Germany scale: Clever Fit operated ~383 franchise clubs and ~39 owned clubs, primarily in Germany, Switzerland, and Eastern Europe. Basic-Fit had just 67 clubs in Germany at year-end 2025. Combined, the group is the immediate leader in Germany with ~450 clubs. Germany is the largest gym market in Europe by absolute population and penetration growth potential. Second, franchise model: Clever Fit is an established franchisor with documented systems, brand recognition, and franchisee relationships. This capability is entirely new for BFIT. Third, geographic diversification: Clever Fit's presence in six additional countries (including Switzerland and Central/Eastern Europe) creates future optionality.

Integration progress appears positive. Management noted in Q1 2026 commentary that profitability improvements were already visible in the acquired estate. The first months of ownership are typically the hardest — early-stage positive signals are credible.

Key risks: franchise management is operationally different from owned clubs. BFIT's management DNA is operational efficiency in a company-owned, standardised model. Managing 383 independent franchisees requires different capabilities in contracts, QA, brand standards, and relationship management. The earn-out structure (€15m) suggests performance milestones — a sign management anticipated execution variability.

Synergy guidance has not been formally quantified in public disclosures. Watson estimates €10–20m in cost synergies (procurement, technology, overhead) over 24 months, with additional revenue synergies if BFIT-branded franchise launches accelerate. The deal is EPS-accretive by 2027 on most reasonable assumptions.

CHANGE FORCE 3: FRANCE 24/7 STAFFLESS — REGULATORY BREAKTHROUGH

France is BFIT's single largest market. At year-end 2025, 894 clubs were located in France — 54% of the owned estate. Under the previous regulatory regime, French labour law effectively mandated staffed operations at all hours, making the Netherlands/Belgium 24/7 staffless model illegal.

The April 2026 regulatory change (revised amendment to Article PE 27-1 of French labour code) permits 24/7 unstaffed operations for ground-floor clubs with camera monitoring, communication options, and a maximum of 19 members via reservation system. Approximately 200 clubs qualify immediately. BFIT begins transitioning 50 clubs to the model from May 2026.

The financial impact is quantified at ~€10m in annualised cost savings. At BFIT's current underlying EBITDA run-rate of ~€400m, this adds ~2.5% to EBITDA. Marginal contribution is high — the savings flow directly to the bottom line with minimal offset.

The more important signal is management's explicit expectation that "further amendments will lead to additional cost savings." If the regulation evolves to permit upper-floor clubs (currently excluded), the addressable French estate expands from ~200 to potentially 700+ clubs. The full-scenario savings could reach €40–60m annualised over a 3–5 year period.

This is a genuine structural shift in the unit economics of the French estate. France has been BFIT's highest-cost operating market due to mandatory staffing. As staffing costs normalise toward the Belgian/Netherlands model, French club margins will re-rate upward. The timing of broader regulatory progression is the key uncertainty.

CHANGE FORCE 4: CONVERTIBLE BOND PUT — JUNE 2026 FINANCIAL EVENT

This is the most acute short-term financial risk in the investment case.

Basic-Fit issued €303.7m in senior unsecured convertible bonds in June 2021, maturing June 2028. The instruments include a put option exercisable by bondholders at par (plus accrued interest) on 17 June 2026. In H1 2025, management reassessed the likelihood of put exercise as "increased," triggering a €10.8m non-cash catch-up finance cost adjustment. This is material — it signals management itself views full or partial exercise as likely.

The company pre-positioned €290m in new facilities from ABN AMRO, ING, and Rabobank. Combined with operating cash generation, management believes the group can meet any redemption requests.

Watson's assessment: the liquidity position appears adequate assuming: (a) facilities are drawn in an orderly manner; (b) no concurrent stress event (e.g. market dislocation, revenue miss); (c) lender covenants remain intact. The risk is not insolvency — it is refinancing friction, higher interest costs on replacement facilities, and potential earnings dilution if conversion to equity is used. Net debt post-put exercise could temporarily elevate to ~3.5x EBITDA before FCF normalisation brings it down.

The market appears to be partially pricing this risk. The stock trades at ~€30 versus an analyst consensus target of ~€38. The discount to target (~21%) is partly attributable to bond put uncertainty. Successful navigation of June 2026 removes this overhang and is a potential re-rating catalyst.

CHANGE FORCE 5: ARPM AND REVENUE PER CLUB EXPANSION

ARPM grew from €24.24 to €24.91 for BFIT owned clubs in 2025 — a 2.8% increase. This is modest in absolute terms but structurally important for two reasons.

First, the pricing floor is defended. BFIT's value proposition at €23–€30/month is highly resilient. The core Basic membership (€19.99–€24.99) is below the threshold where incremental price increases cause material membership attrition. Premium adoption (Multi + access) at ~€29.99–€34.99 drives mix-shift upward without raising headline prices. The franchise fee revenue from Clever Fit adds a high-margin revenue stream that will increase reported ARPM at group level in 2026.

Second, average members per club increased 8% YoY in Q1 2026 (from 2,764 to 2,981). This is operating leverage in its purest form — more revenue through the same fixed-cost base. Each additional member in a mature club generates near-100% incremental contribution margin. The club maturity curve is a powerful earnings driver as 2022–2024 clubs cross the 36-month maturity inflection point.

Ancillary revenue (vending, app coaching subscriptions, personal training upgrades, Yanga Water) adds a further ARPM tailwind. These channels are structurally small but growing, and they carry higher margin than the base membership fee.

CHANGE FORCE 6: FRANCHISE MODEL AS CAPITAL-LIGHT GROWTH ENGINE

The transition from a pure owned-club operator to a hybrid owned/franchise model is the most structurally significant long-term change force.

Under the owned model, each new club requires €1.0–€1.5m in fit-out capex plus lease commitments, with a 3-year payback period. Under a franchise model, the franchisee bears the capex. BFIT earns a franchise fee (typically 5–8% of revenue for European gym franchises) plus supply chain margin and brand licensing fees. The capital intensity per new club effectively falls to near-zero.

The implications for long-term FCF are profound. If BFIT deploys franchise rollout at scale in France, Germany, and Spain through 2027–2030, the 3,000–3,500 club target becomes achievable with a fraction of the historical capital burden. Combined with the owned estate approaching maturity, FCF yield could re-rate toward 8–12% of enterprise value by 2029–2030 in a bull case.

Near-term, the franchise model is nascent. In Q1 2026, only 5 of 33 new club openings were franchise. The system is in pilot phase. Management credibility on franchise execution will be assessed at H1 2026.

THEMATIC SENSITIVITIES

T1: Bear Market / Risk-Off — RATING: B (Moderately Resilient)

Basic-Fit's value proposition is explicitly designed for economic stress. At €23–€30/month, gym membership competes with one restaurant meal. In prior recessions, low-cost gyms demonstrated counter-cyclical tendencies — consumers trading down from premium gyms to budget alternatives, and gym membership being the last discretionary item cancelled. The 2008–2009 period saw Planet Fitness and LA Fitness grow through the US recession. Basic-Fit experienced strong membership growth during the 2022–2023 European cost-of-living crisis — the period where membership accelerated most rapidly.

However, Basic-Fit is not fully counter-cyclical. The key risk is not existing member attrition but new member acquisition. In a severe recession (unemployment above 8–10%), new joiner volumes could slow materially. The fixed-cost base (rent is the dominant cost at ~60% of revenue) means revenue pressure flows directly to EBITDA with high operating leverage — in both directions. A 5% revenue decline would produce a ~15% EBITDA decline given fixed cost structure.

On balance: resilient but not immune. BFIT earns a B rating — meaningfully more resilient than premium fitness, consumer discretionary, or luxury; less resilient than utilities or healthcare. The bear market scenario most threatening to BFIT is a deep, prolonged recession coupled with energy cost spikes that compress consumer confidence AND increase BFIT's own energy cost base.

T2: AI Disruption — RATING: B/C (Low Near-Term Threat, Monitor Medium-Term)

AI disruption to in-person gyms is a medium-term structural watch, not a near-term threat.

The primary channel of AI disruption to basic gyms is via digital fitness substitutes (AI coaching, virtual classes, at-home equipment). Peloton's AI IQ launch (October 2025) and the growth of AI personal trainer apps represent genuine product innovation. However, the disruption thesis faces a core constraint: the social gym experience cannot be replicated digitally. Weight equipment, pool access, group class physicality, and the social motivation of a physical environment are durable differentiators.

The at-home fitness surge of 2020–2021 reversed sharply from 2022. Peloton posted a $118.9m net loss on $2.5bn revenue in FY2025. The AI personal trainer market is growing but targets a different cohort — the premium, self-directed, tech-native consumer. Basic-Fit's target customer (value-conscious, community-seeking, price-sensitive) is substantially less susceptible to AI substitution.

More relevant AI applications for BFIT are internal: AI-assisted dynamic pricing, churn prediction, class scheduling optimisation, and AI monitoring in 24/7 staffless clubs. These are cost-saving and retention tools, not threats.

Medium-term monitoring point: if AI coaching quality reaches the level where it genuinely replicates the guided group class experience at home, the threat to mid-tier gym attendance grows. For a sub-€25/month operator, this risk remains limited — the marginal cost of gym attendance is too low to abandon.

Rating: B/C. Near-term threat is low (B). If AI coaching quality substantially improves by 2028–2030, the threat rises to C. No acute risk in the 12–36 month investment horizon.

T3: Iran/Oil/Geopolitical Risk — RATING: B (Limited Direct Exposure, Indirect Watch)

Direct oil price exposure for Basic-Fit is via energy costs for club operations. BFIT operates 2,100+ clubs across continental Europe. Each club is a high-energy-consumption venue (lighting, HVAC, equipment). Energy costs are the second-largest variable cost after labour.

An oil price spike from Iran tensions (e.g. Strait of Hormuz disruption) would flow through to European electricity and gas prices. This is a meaningful cost risk. In 2022–2023, European energy cost inflation materially compressed BFIT's EBITDA margins during the Ukraine-driven energy crisis. Management responded with energy procurement diversification and hedging, but a return to 2022-level energy prices would be a significant earnings headwind.

Indirect risk: geopolitical uncertainty suppresses consumer confidence in continental Europe. BFIT's membership growth depends on new joiners — a confidence shock could slow new member acquisition in 2026, particularly in France and Germany (the two markets most exposed to European recession risk from trade disruption).

However: BFIT has no direct Iran exposure, no Middle East operations, and no significant exposure to oil-price-linked commodities in its cost base. The transmission mechanism is indirect and lagged.

Rating: B. Energy cost risk is real but manageable within BFIT's current hedging posture. Consumer confidence risk is a secondary watch. The geopolitical overlay is not an investment-case killer at current oil price levels — it becomes material above $120/barrel sustained or in an extreme demand shock scenario.

SCEPTICAL SUMMARY

The change forces are genuine, but the bull case carries execution dependencies that the current multiple already partially prices in.

The core bear case: BFIT's EBITDA guide of €415–455m for 2026 represents 30–35% growth from 2025's €348m. This is an aggressive step-up. It requires: (a) Clever Fit integration delivering profitability improvements on schedule; (b) the France 24/7 €10m saving materialising in full within the year; (c) Q1 momentum sustaining through Q2–Q4 without churn; (d) club openings delivering on schedule; and (e) the convertible bond put being navigated without credit stress.

If EBITDA delivers at the low end of guidance (€415m) and the convertible put triggers higher refinancing costs, 2026 earnings growth could disappoint versus street expectations. The stock has already re-rated from €19.47 to €30.14 — a 55% move from the 52-week low. The easy money in the recovery has been made.

Monitoring priorities for the 12-month view: (1) June 2026 bond put resolution — timeline and terms; (2) France 24/7 pilot progress — how many clubs converted by Q3 2026; (3) Clever Fit EBITDA contribution in H1 2026 results; (4) free cash flow reported at H1 2026 — the key proof point; (5) ARPM trajectory — any price increases announced for H2 2026.
