Delivery Hero has crossed a critical inflection point toward profitability, but a yawning gap between operational progress and market valuation reflects deep structural questions about the company's portfolio. Adjusted EBITDA nearly tripled in FY2024 to €693 million, free cash flow turned positive for the first time, and the Talabat IPO crystalized €1.8 billion in proceeds while validating MENA as a premium asset. Yet the stock trades at roughly €17—down 87% from its 2021 pandemic peak—as activist shareholders demand a breakup and management credibility remains under scrutiny. The core investment tension is straightforward: DHER is a collection of businesses at vastly different stages of maturity, and the market assigns a steep conglomerate discount to the whole.

FY2024 Results: The Inflection Point

Earnings & Growth Metrics

Delivery Hero's FY2024 results, reported in preliminary form on February 13, 2025 and audited in the annual report published April 24, 2025, demonstrated the strongest financial improvement in the company's history. Group GMV reached €48.8 billion (+8.3% in constant currency excluding hyperinflation), while total segment revenue hit €12.8 billion (+22% CC). The revenue-to-GMV gap—22% revenue growth on 8% GMV growth—is the headline story, driven by the shift to own-delivery models, expanding quick commerce, and rapidly growing advertising technology revenue that now exceeds €1.2 billion annually.

EBITDA & Cash Flow Inflection

Adjusted EBITDA of €693 million (audited) represents a 173% year-over-year improvement, though this figure was revised down from the preliminary €750 million disclosed in February due to a €183 million non-cash provision for Glovo rider reclassification liabilities in Italy covering 2016–2024. The EBITDA/GMV margin expanded approximately 90 basis points to roughly 1.4%. Free cash flow turned positive for the first time at approximately €100 million under the old definition, a €466 million swing from negative €367 million in FY2023.

Regional Portfolio Maturity

The regional picture reveals a portfolio of dramatically different maturity profiles. MENA delivered €12.8 billion in GMV (+28% YoY) with an adjusted EBITDA margin exceeding 6% of GMV—comfortably the group's profit engine. Europe reached adjusted EBITDA breakeven in H2 2024, and the Americas turned EBITDA-positive for the full year. Asia, dominated by South Korea's Baemin, saw GMV decline to €23.4 billion from €25.4 billion as the company phased out free delivery for non-subscribers—a deliberate margin-accretive but volume-destructive decision. The integrated verticals (Dmart/quick commerce) segment improved EBITDA by over 50% and generated positive adjusted EBITDA in December 2024, a milestone on the path to segment breakeven.

FY2025 Guidance: Margin Expansion Continues

Management Targets

Management issued FY2025 guidance on February 13, 2025, subsequently confirmed in the Q1 2025 trading update on April 24, 2025. The targets are:

Metric FY2025 guidance
GMV growth +8–10% (CC, ex-hyperinflation)
Total segment revenue growth +17–19% (CC, ex-hyperinflation)
Adjusted EBITDA €975–1,025 million
Free cash flow >€200 million

Q1 2025 Trading Momentum

The Q1 2025 trading update showed encouraging momentum: GMV of €12.4 billion (+9% like-for-like), with GMV excluding Korea growing 22%—described by management as the fastest growth rate since early COVID. Total segment revenue reached €3.5 billion (+22% LfL). Management highlighted three specific efficiency initiatives contributing to the EBITDA bridge: improved rider algorithms reducing waiting time by 0.7 minutes for up to €41 million in savings, an enhanced auction engine and new ad formats driving €39 million in incremental EBITDA, and a global logistics platform rollout delivering up to 9% faster delivery times and 20% higher logistics efficiency.

Take Rate & Long-Term Vision

The revenue-over-GMV growth differential is expected to persist. Take rates continue expanding through three mechanisms: the shift from marketplace to own-delivery (capturing more of the transaction), advertising revenue growth toward the long-term target of >4% of GMV (from ~3.0% in FY2025), and platform fee optimization. Management explicitly avoids giving take rate guidance, stating they "focus on gross profit rather than take rate," but the implied FY2025 take rate approaches 30% of GMV.

For longer-term context, the company's 2030 vision targets GMV exceeding €200 billion and an adjusted EBITDA/GMV margin of 5–8%—a dramatic expansion from the current ~1.4–1.9% range. Quick commerce GMV is targeted to approach €10 billion by 2026 from over €7.5 billion in FY2025.

Cost Structure & Below-the-Line Drags

Operational Leverage

The cost trajectory shows genuine operational leverage. SG&A plus marketing costs as a percentage of GMV declined from 7.2% in Q1 2023 to 6.0% by Q3 2025, which management describes as "world-leading in our peer group." The company is deploying AI across customer support, vendor management, and rider operations—self-service tools launched in 2023, copilots for service agents in 2024—to structurally reduce cost-to-serve.

Quick Commerce Inflection

Quick commerce unit economics are inflecting. Dmarts generated positive adjusted EBITDA in December 2024. The gross profit margin for integrated verticals nearly doubled to 4% during 2024, driven by higher daily orders per store, larger basket sizes, improved supplier conditions, and growing advertising contribution. The company pruned underperforming Dmart locations while growing the segment's revenue, an unusual and encouraging combination. Multi-vertical customers spend on average 5x more than food-only customers, creating a powerful cross-selling dynamic.

Material Below-EBITDA Headwinds

However, material below-EBITDA items constrain the path to net profitability. Interest expense ran approximately €255 million in FY2024, up from €238 million in FY2023, reflecting the weighted average convertible bond coupon of 1.8% on €3.8 billion outstanding plus the €1.8 billion term loan. Post the Q1 2025 bond buybacks of approximately €896 million, interest expense should decline meaningfully.

Share-based compensation is estimated at roughly €360 million (approximately 0.8% of GMV), excluded from adjusted EBITDA but a real economic cost. Depreciation and amortization runs approximately €236 million, elevated by Glovo acquisition-related intangibles. The tax provision jumped to €339 million in FY2024 from €142 million—notably high for a net-loss-making entity—reflecting profitability in high-tax jurisdictions (MENA, Korea) while losses elsewhere generate no tax benefit. The net loss narrowed to €882 million from €2.4 billion, but DHER remains firmly loss-making on an IFRS basis with FY2025 net losses estimated around negative €560 million.

Regulatory & Currency Headwinds

Spain's employment-based rider model imposes an estimated €100 million annual recurring cost that is baked into FY2025 guidance. Italy's Glovo rider reclassification provision of €253 million is the most significant one-off, and the June 2025 EU antitrust fine of €329 million (for cartel behavior predating the Glovo acquisition) adds to the legal overhang. Aspex Management, the activist shareholder, estimates total potential fines and penalties at €1.4 billion—far exceeding peers.

Currency exposure is a persistent headwind. The company operates in over 70 countries, with significant exposure to the Korean won, UAE dirham, Turkish lira, and Argentine peso. Hyperinflationary accounting applies to Turkey and Argentina. Management provides no specific hedging commentary, suggesting limited formal programs. FX headwinds ultimately forced a mid-year revision of FY2025 EBITDA guidance from €975–1,025 million down to €900–940 million in August 2025.

Balance Sheet Transformation: The Talabat IPO

IPO Execution & Proceeds

The Talabat listing on the Dubai Financial Market on December 10, 2024 was the largest global tech IPO of 2024. Originally planned as a 15% stake sale, overwhelming demand—books were covered "within minutes"—led Delivery Hero to upsize to 20%, raising gross proceeds of approximately AED 7.5 billion (~€1.8 billion) at AED 1.60 per share. The IPO valued Talabat at roughly $10.1 billion, remarkably close to Delivery Hero's entire market capitalization at the time.

Debt Reduction Strategy

The proceeds transformed DHER's balance sheet. Cash surged to €3.8 billion at year-end 2024 from €1.7 billion. Net debt fell 55% to €1.9 billion, and leverage dropped to approximately 2.5x net debt/adjusted EBITDA from an estimated 4.2x pre-IPO. Management immediately deployed proceeds into convertible bond buybacks: a February 2025 tender offer targeted all remaining 2025 bonds (€48 million at 99.375% of par), all 2026 bonds (€594 million at 97.5%), and up to €350 million of 2027 bonds. By end of Q1 2025, approximately €896 million in bonds had been retired, reducing cash to €2.9 billion.

Capital Structure & Allocation

The remaining debt structure includes the €1.8 billion term loan due 2029, approximately €540 million in 2027 convertible bonds, and the €1 billion 3.25% 2030 convertible bond. The weighted average convertible coupon of 1.8% remains low, though the 2030 bond at 3.25% reflects the higher-rate environment. Capital expenditure ran approximately €305 million in FY2024, trending upward from €280 million, primarily reflecting Dmart buildout and technology investment. No dividend policy exists—nor has Delivery Hero ever paid a dividend—and no share buyback program has been announced. Capital allocation priorities remain debt reduction and organic investment. CEO Östberg has stated explicitly: "We just want to keep that momentum and not be distracted by M&A."

FCF Conversion Targets

The mid-term target for adjusted EBITDA to free cash flow conversion is >60%, with a long-term target of approximately 70%. The FCF definition was updated in February 2025 to align with IFRS standards (cash flow from operating activities minus net capex minus lease payments), which better reflects industry norms.

Asia: The Portfolio's Unresolved Question

The Foodpanda Saga

The Foodpanda saga is the single most value-destructive narrative in Delivery Hero's recent history. In September 2023, the company explored selling Foodpanda operations across seven Southeast Asian markets. Negotiations—widely believed to involve Grab—were terminated in February 2024 when CEO Östberg declared "the strength of our APAC business has become evident." Then in May 2024, Uber agreed to acquire Foodpanda Taiwan for $950 million plus a $300 million equity investment in Delivery Hero. Taiwan's Fair Trade Commission blocked the deal in December 2024 on competition grounds (combined 90% market share). Uber formally terminated in March 2025, paying a ~$250 million termination fee.

Southeast Asia Competitiveness

Foodpanda is competitively disadvantaged across Southeast Asia. Grab holds 55% regional market share (up from 54% in 2024), while ShopeeFood has overtaken Foodpanda as the number-two player with 14% share. Foodpanda exited Thailand in May 2025 after 13 years and cumulative losses estimated at $399 million. The one bright spot was the March 2025 acquisition of Deliveroo's Hong Kong assets, consolidating Foodpanda as the sole major competitor to Meituan's KeeTa in that market.

South Korea Crown Jewel

South Korea (Baemin) is the crown jewel within Asia, estimated by Barclays to generate roughly €700 million in annual EBITDA. However, competitive pressure from Coupang Eats is intensifying, and Asia segment GMV declined 8% in constant currency in FY2024. Management characterized 2024 as a "transition year" due to the free-delivery phase-out for non-subscribers, and subscriber order frequency continued growing at double-digit rates, but the Korea situation demands close monitoring.

Competitive Dynamics & Industry Structure

Market Size & Growth

The global food delivery market is valued at approximately $290–380 billion in 2024 and projected to grow at a 9–10% CAGR through 2030. Quick commerce adds another dimension, valued at $75–185 billion and growing at 12–34% depending on definitions. The industry has decisively shifted from growth-at-all-costs to sustainable unit economics, with advertising emerging as the critical profit lever.

Peer Benchmarking

Among peers, DoorDash is the clearest outperformer: FY2025 revenue of $13.7 billion (+28%), GAAP net income of $935 million, and aggressive international expansion through Wolt and the Deliveroo acquisition. DoorDash trades at 25–30x forward EV/EBITDA. Uber Eats generated $13.7 billion in FY2024 delivery revenue (+13%) with annualized ad revenue exceeding $2 billion and a delivery EBITDA margin of 4–5% of gross bookings, trading at 15–18x. Grab is accelerating: delivery GMV grew 16–26% through 2025 with EBITDA margins expanding to 2.1% of GMV.

Valuation Arbitrage

Delivery Hero's EV/adjusted EBITDA of approximately 7.7x represents a stark discount to DoorDash and Uber. On EV/revenue of just 0.52x, DHER is arguably the cheapest major food delivery platform globally. The discount reflects geographic complexity (70+ countries), governance risk (activist pressure), regulatory overhang, and persistent IFRS losses. The Prosus acquisition of Just Eat Takeaway for €4.1 billion—a 49% premium to the 3-month VWAP—adds a new competitive dimension, as Prosus also controls iFood in Brazil and is DHER's largest shareholder.

Emerging Competitive Threats

A critical emerging risk is Meituan's entry into Saudi Arabia via dark stores in September 2024, directly challenging Delivery Hero's most profitable geography. While early-stage, Meituan's operational capabilities in rapid commerce are formidable.

Analyst Views & Market Narrative

Sell-Side Consensus

The analyst community remains overwhelmingly constructive: 11 Buy ratings, 6 Hold, 1 Sell across 18–19 covering analysts, with an average 12-month price target of €27–28 (63–80% upside from ~€17). JPMorgan (Overweight, €28), Barclays (Overweight, €35), Morgan Stanley (Overweight, €34), Jefferies (Buy), UBS (Buy), and Bernstein (Outperform) anchor the bull case. Deutsche Bank is the notable bearish voice with a Neutral rating.

Management Credibility Gap

The gap between analyst targets and the share price is itself a signal. Management's guidance track record is mixed. FY2022 saw a painful revenue miss that triggered a 30% stock plunge and a public CEO apology. FY2024 guidance was technically met, but the downward audit revision from €750 million to €693 million eroded trust. FY2025 guidance was revised mid-year on FX headwinds from €975–1,025 million to €900–940 million, with the actual result of €903 million landing at the low end. Analysts note a recurring pattern: ambitious initial targets followed by mid-year cuts, creating negative revision cycles. FCF delivery, however, has consistently surprised positively.

The Activist Situation

The activist situation is now the dominant market narrative. Aspex Management (~9.2% stake) has publicly accused CEO Östberg of "value destruction" and "flawed strategy," escalating in March 2026 to demand the removal of the CEO and entire management team. Sachem Head Capital secured a supervisory board seat in 2024. Delivery Hero announced a strategic review in December 2025, appointing JPMorgan as financial adviser, and the Foodpanda Taiwan sale to Grab for $600 million in March 2026—$350 million below the failed Uber deal—was described by management as "a key first step" while Aspex criticized the value erosion. The outcome of the strategic review is arguably the single most important catalyst for the stock.

Share Dilution & Market Risk

Shares outstanding increased 8.14% year-over-year due to convertible bond dilution, a headwind for per-share metrics. The stock's beta of 1.57 means it amplifies broader market moves, adding volatility for institutional holders.

Conclusion: A Sum-of-Parts Story Awaiting Its Catalyst

Operational Track Record

Delivery Hero's operational trajectory is genuinely impressive—tripling EBITDA in two years, achieving first-ever positive free cash flow, expanding take rates, and approaching quick commerce breakeven. The FY2025 guidance framework of roughly €1 billion in adjusted EBITDA and >€200 million in FCF, if delivered, would represent a mature, profitable food delivery enterprise.

Three Unresolved Questions

The investment case hinges on three unresolved questions.

First, what is the portfolio worth in parts? The Talabat IPO valued MENA alone at ~$10 billion; Baemin in Korea is estimated at €700 million+ in annual EBITDA; the market assigns approximately €5 billion to the entire group. The sum-of-parts arithmetic is compelling but requires a catalyst to unlock.

Second, can management retain credibility? The pattern of ambitious guidance followed by mid-year revision, combined with the Italy provision surprise and the Taiwan deal value erosion, creates a persistent trust deficit. The activist campaign from Aspex and Sachem Head may force either a governance change or an accelerated breakup.

Third, how does the regulatory and competitive environment evolve? The EU Platform Work Directive (transposition deadline December 2026), potential fines totaling €1.4 billion, Meituan's MENA entry, and Coupang's Korea pressure each represent material risks.

Valuation & Catalyst Case

At 7.7x adjusted EBITDA and 0.52x revenue, the valuation embeds extreme pessimism. If the strategic review produces meaningful asset sales or if Foodpanda's remaining portfolio finds buyers at reasonable prices, the re-rating potential is substantial. But Delivery Hero has been a "cheap for a reason" stock for two years, and conviction requires a view that the catalyst is imminent rather than perpetually around the corner.
