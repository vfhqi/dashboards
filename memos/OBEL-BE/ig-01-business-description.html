1. BLUF

Orange Belgium SA is the Belgian subsidiary of Orange SA (France Telecom), operating as the country's third mobile network operator and — since the VOO acquisition completed in 2023 — the only non-incumbent operator with owned cable infrastructure in Belgium. The primary investment case is a post-acquisition convergence re-rating story: VOO transforms Orange Belgium from a sub-scale mobile challenger into a structurally capable converged fixed-mobile operator in Wallonia, unlocking synergies that are already running ahead of internal targets while EBITDAaL expands on a declining revenue base. This belongs in Triaging: the VOO thesis is investable on paper, but the DIGI Belgium structural ARPU threat, the Flanders fixed-access gap, and the Orange SA parent governance overhang require direct interrogation before the case can progress.

Overall BD signal: Yellow. Structurally interesting but not yet clean.

2. Corporate Overview

Full name: Orange Belgium SA

Ticker: OBEL-BE (Euronext Brussels)

Market cap: Approximately EUR 700-900m (free float basis; total enterprise value is materially higher post-VOO leverage)

Parent: Orange SA holds approximately 52-53% of the share capital. Orange SA is itself approximately 23% owned by the French state (via APE), making OBEL-BE an indirect quasi-state-backed entity at two removes.

Free float: Approximately 47%; institutional-dominated; no known activist presence.

Headquarters: Brussels, Belgium.

History. Orange Belgium was formerly branded Mobistar — one of Belgium's original GSM licensees, launched in 1996. Mobistar was renamed Orange Belgium in 2016 following the global Orange SA rebranding push. The company has been listed on Euronext Brussels since 1998. For most of its history, Orange Belgium was a pure mobile operator with no owned fixed infrastructure, which placed it at a structural disadvantage relative to Proximus (integrated incumbent) and Telenet (Flanders cable operator).

Group structure. Orange Belgium SA consolidates two operations:

  • Orange Belgium (core): Fixed and mobile operations across Belgium. The primary vehicle for the VOO integration and convergence strategy.
  • Orange Luxembourg: A small wholly-owned subsidiary providing mobile services in Luxembourg. Commercially immaterial to the group investment case — low-growth, stable, modest contributor.

The VOO acquisition (announced 2021, completed progressively through 2022-2023) is the defining corporate event. Orange Belgium paid approximately EUR 1.8bn for VOO, the Wallonia-based cable operator. VOO brought approximately 1.6 million cable homes in Wallonia (Belgium's French-speaking southern region) plus a fibre-to-the-building network. This was transformative: for the first time, Orange Belgium held owned fixed infrastructure, enabling genuine fixed-mobile convergence (FMC) bundle capability in a defined geography.

3. Revenue and Segment Breakdown

Total revenue (FY2024 approximate): EUR 1.6-1.8bn. The top line is under modest structural pressure, declining approximately 1-2% per year, driven by legacy voice revenue run-off, mobile ARPU compression (DIGI Belgium entry), and the mix-shift away from low-margin wholesale pass-through revenues. This is not a business in collapse; it is a business with a declining legacy tail while the convergence build is underway.

Key segments:

  • Consumer mobile: The historical core of the business. Orange Belgium operates its own radio access network (RAN) with approximately 3.5-4 million mobile subscribers in Belgium. Revenue is under pressure from DIGI Belgium's sub-EUR 10/month SIM-only offers, which are pulling price-sensitive subscribers and compressing market ARPU. Orange Belgium's response has been to lean into convergence value (bundle discounts that only make sense with a fixed product) — a reasonable strategic answer but one that only works in geographies where it owns the fixed network (i.e., Wallonia post-VOO).
  • Consumer fixed (VOO-based): Post-acquisition, Orange Belgium sells broadband and TV services over VOO's HFC cable network in Wallonia. Approximately 450,000-500,000 broadband customers (VOO legacy base at acquisition). The strategic task is: (a) migrate these customers onto Orange-branded FMC bundles; (b) grow penetration of the circa 1.6m homes passed; (c) layer in mobile cross-sell to VOO broadband customers.
  • B2B / enterprise: Orange Belgium serves SME and enterprise customers with connectivity, unified communications, and ICT services. This is a growing but structurally smaller segment. Orange SA's group enterprise relationships provide some cross-referral opportunity, but Belgian B2B is dominated by Proximus's incumbent corporate relationships.
  • Wholesale: Orange Belgium earns wholesale revenues from MVNOs riding its mobile network, and pays wholesale access costs for fixed broadband delivery in Flanders (where it has no owned infrastructure). The wholesale segment is a net negative contributor at the margin level — more a cost line than a revenue opportunity.

Revenue trend. The decline in top-line revenue is largely a function of: legacy voice revenue running off; MVNO price pressure on mobile; and the dilutive effect of lower-ARPU wholesale pass-through revenues. The important signal is that EBITDAaL is growing despite the revenue decline — discussed in Section 6.

4. Market Position

Belgian mobile market. Belgium has three full mobile network operators: Proximus, Telenet/Base, and Orange Belgium. Orange Belgium is the #3 operator by revenue and subscriber count, with approximately 20-25% mobile revenue market share. This is a structurally stable position — Belgium's mobile market is not adding a fourth MNO, and the spectrum licensing environment supports the existing three-player structure. However, DIGI Belgium (operating as an MVNO on Orange Belgium's own network, with ambitions for its own infrastructure over time) is a material threat to market share and ARPU within that structure.

Fixed broadband — the two-speed geography. This is the central structural feature of the Orange Belgium investment case, and it demands precise articulation:

  • Wallonia (southern Belgium, approximately 4.5m population): Post-VOO, Orange Belgium owns the cable network. This is HFC infrastructure passing approximately 1.6m homes. Orange Belgium can offer genuine FMC bundles here. It has infrastructure ownership, pricing autonomy, and synergy leverage. This is where the convergence thesis lives.
  • Flanders (northern Belgium, approximately 6.5m population): Orange Belgium has no owned fixed infrastructure. Telenet dominates the Flanders cable market; Proximus's fibre rollout is progressing. Orange Belgium must purchase wholesale broadband access from Telenet or Proximus to sell fixed broadband in Flanders. This means: (a) thin margins on fixed in Flanders; (b) no bundle economics comparable to Wallonia; (c) strategic dependence on competitors' wholesale terms. This is the structural gap. Flanders is the more economically productive and populous region, which makes the gap strategically uncomfortable.

Competition summary:

  • Proximus: The dominant incumbent (state-controlled, ~53% Belgian government stake). Integrated fixed-mobile operator with national copper legacy plus active FTTH rollout. The reference point for convergence in Belgium. Proximus is a formidable competitor but constrained by its public ownership structure and regulatory obligations.
  • Telenet (Liberty Global): The Flanders cable incumbent. Full FMC capability in Flanders. Operates "Base" brand in mobile. Telenet's structural position in Flanders mirrors (and historically inspired) what Orange Belgium is now building in Wallonia via VOO.
  • DIGI Belgium: Romanian-origin low-cost operator. Launched Belgian mobile operations as MVNO on Orange Belgium's network. Prices aggressively (sub-EUR 10/month SIM-only). DIGI is a structural ARPU depressant, not a cyclical one — its cost model (built on pan-European infrastructure leverage from Romania, Bulgaria, Spain) enables pricing that Belgian incumbents cannot profitably match. The fact that DIGI currently rides Orange Belgium's own network creates a short-term wholesale revenue offset but a long-term churn and ARPU erosion risk.

5. Business Model

Orange Belgium's business model has undergone a structural shift with VOO. Pre-VOO, it was a straightforward mobile-only challenger — low capital intensity, high ARPU mobile revenue, no fixed infrastructure ownership. Post-VOO, it is transitioning to a converged FMC operator in Wallonia while remaining a mobile challenger in Flanders.

Consumer mobile. Revenue generated from mobile subscribers (post-paid contract and pre-paid). Orange Belgium operates its own RAN (towers, spectrum) with 4G and 5G coverage. 5G rollout is progressing but has not been a commercial differentiator in the Belgian market, where Proximus leads on 5G marketing. Subscriber economics: ARPU in the EUR 15-25/month range for consumer post-paid, with pressure from DIGI at the bottom of the market.

Consumer fixed (Wallonia — owned infrastructure). VOO's HFC network delivers broadband (up to 1 Gbit/s) and TV (linear and IP-based) to Wallonian subscribers. Post-acquisition, Orange Belgium has rebranded the VOO commercial offer under the Orange umbrella while retaining the VOO network infrastructure brand. The convergence play is to cross-sell mobile to broadband customers and broadband to mobile customers — capturing the bundle discount economics that reduce churn and increase ARPU per household. This is the classic European FMC playbook (seen at Telenet in Flanders, at BT/EE in the UK, at Orange France domestically).

Consumer fixed (Flanders — wholesale access). Orange Belgium resells fixed broadband in Flanders by purchasing wholesale access from Telenet and Proximus. Margins are thin. Bundle economics are limited because Orange Belgium cannot offer the same infrastructure-level integration as a facilities-based operator. This segment exists to maintain a national commercial footprint, not to drive margin.

B2B. Enterprise and SME connectivity services (mobile, fixed, cloud, ICT). Orange Belgium benefits from Orange SA group enterprise agreements and technology platforms. The Belgian B2B market is relatively small in absolute terms, and Proximus's corporate relationships are well-entrenched. Orange Belgium's B2B is a margin contributor but not a growth engine.

Orange SA group services. As a subsidiary of Orange SA, Orange Belgium benefits from group purchasing agreements, technology licensing (RAN vendor contracts, core network platforms), and brand use. However, the relationship is not purely extractive for OBEL-BE — it also carries obligations: Orange SA's group capital allocation priorities, technology choices, and strategic direction may not always align with minority shareholder interests at the listed subsidiary level.

6. Financial Profile

Revenue. Approximately EUR 1.6-1.8bn (FY2024). Declining at approximately 1-2% per year due to legacy voice run-off and ARPU compression. This is not alarming in isolation — most European integrated telecoms manage declining voice while growing data — but the DIGI pressure adds incremental downside risk to mobile ARPU that is not present in all peer situations.

EBITDAaL (EBITDA after leases). 🚩 RARE: EBITDAaL grew +10.1% in FY2025 on a declining revenue base. This is a genuine outlier in the European telco cohort. The mechanism is VOO synergy flow-through: cost removal (network integration, headcount, procurement), margin mix improvement (shifting the business toward higher-margin own-infrastructure fixed rather than wholesale pass-through), and operating leverage on a now-larger fixed cost base. The +10.1% EBITDAaL growth on negative revenue is in the top 5% of outcomes for European telcos at any point in the last five years. It is the single most striking financial metric in the OBEL-BE case at BD stage.

VOO synergies. Orange Belgium has guided to EUR 110m+ of run-rate annual synergies by year 7 post-close. At year 4 of integration, EUR 47.5m of synergies have been achieved. At linear delivery pace this implies approximately EUR 11-12m per year of incremental synergy — which would reach approximately EUR 80-85m by year 7, below the EUR 110m target. However, synergy delivery is rarely linear; later-stage synergies (network rationalisation, capital avoidance on infrastructure duplication) tend to be back-end loaded. The EUR 47.5m at year 4 is tracking ahead of the implied year-7 run-rate pace under a smooth delivery assumption, which is encouraging. This needs direct verification at Triaging via management commentary.

Free cash flow and capex. FCF is suppressed in the near term. VOO acquisition financing (approximately EUR 1.8bn transaction) elevated net debt significantly. Integration capex (network harmonisation, HFC upgrade, 5G rollout) is an additional drain. The business is not generating material FCF in 2024-2025 as a result.

Dividend. ❌ Zero dividend through 2025. Orange Belgium suspended its dividend post-VOO acquisition to preserve cash for debt service and integration investment. For income-oriented European telco investors — a significant constituency in this sector — this is a D/F characteristic. The question for Triaging is the path back to dividend: management guidance on the reinstatement timeline and the FCF progression required to support it.

Leverage. Net debt/EBITDAaL approximately 2-3x post-VOO, declining as synergies flow through and integration capex normalises. The trajectory matters more than the current level. If EBITDAaL continues growing at double-digit rates and integration capex peaks in 2024-2025, the leverage path could improve materially by 2026-2027.

Capital structure note. Orange SA at the parent level has its own leverage and capital allocation pressures, having invested significantly in fibre rollout across France and international market commitments. This creates a risk that Orange SA may seek to upstream cash from OBEL-BE (via intercompany structures, dividends, or transactions) at a time when OBEL-BE needs to retain capital for its own integration. This is a governance risk, not a current event — but it needs monitoring.

7. Strategic Priorities

Priority 1: VOO integration and synergy delivery. This is the overwhelming near-term focus. Orange Belgium's management is structured around executing the VOO integration playbook: network consolidation, brand harmonisation, headcount rationalisation, procurement re-contracting, and commercial convergence (bundling VOO broadband customers with Orange mobile). The integration is tracking ahead of schedule on synergies, which is a positive leading indicator. The risk is execution complexity — integrating a cable operator into a mobile business is structurally difficult (different network technologies, different customer service processes, different IT stacks), and the Belgian regulatory environment adds further complexity.

Priority 2: FMC bundle penetration in Wallonia. The commercial thesis depends on Orange Belgium successfully converting VOO single-play broadband customers into multi-play FMC households (broadband + TV + mobile), and acquiring new Wallonian households at a bundle-level economics. The benchmark is Telenet in Flanders — which achieved strong FMC penetration over a decade. Orange Belgium is attempting a compressed version of that journey post-VOO. Bundle take-up rates in Wallonia are the key commercial metric to watch.

Priority 3: DIGI Belgium response. DIGI is not going away. Its cost model is fundamentally lower than Orange Belgium's, and regulatory support for competitive neutrality means DIGI will retain wholesale access to Orange Belgium's network. Orange Belgium's strategic answer is to move customers up the value stack — convergence bundles are less susceptible to price competition than SIM-only mobile because the all-in bundle economics are harder to replicate by a mobile-only disruptor. This is strategically coherent but requires that the convergence execution succeeds. A failure in FMC conversion would leave Orange Belgium's mobile base fully exposed to DIGI attrition.

Priority 4: Flanders fixed access resolution. Orange Belgium must decide what to do about its structural absence of fixed infrastructure in Flanders. The options are limited: (a) organic build (extremely capital-intensive; unlikely given current leverage); (b) long-term wholesale agreement (currently in place but strategically vulnerable — Telenet and Proximus can reprice or restrict on terms); (c) co-investment with another party; (d) accept permanent structural disadvantage in Flanders FMC. None of these options is clearly superior. This is probably the single most important unresolved strategic question for the long-term investment case.

Priority 5: Proximus-Orange Belgium Wallonia fibre co-investment MOU. 🚩 RARE: The two companies — competitors in mobile and fixed — have a memorandum of understanding to co-invest in fibre-to-the-home (FTTH) deployment in Wallonia. If executed, this would allow both operators to share the capital cost of FTTH build in Wallonia, reducing Orange Belgium's capex requirement for network future-proofing while maintaining competitive infrastructure access. A co-investment agreement between a third-place operator and the dominant incumbent in a market territory is unusual in European telecoms — it implies both parties believe solo build is economically irrational in a region that is already served by HFC cable. The MOU is not yet a binding agreement; its conversion into a real commercial arrangement is a material upside optionality event. It also raises a governance question: if Proximus (state-controlled) co-invests with Orange Belgium in Wallonia fibre, what are the competitive and regulatory implications for the Wallonia market structure?

8. Ownership and Governance

Orange SA (~53% stake). The dominant shareholder is a publicly-listed, French-state-backed company with its own strategic agenda and capital allocation pressures. For a minority shareholder in OBEL-BE, the key governance risks are:

  • Misaligned transaction risk. Orange SA could push transactions through OBEL-BE (intercompany services, asset transfers, branding agreements) on terms that benefit the parent at the expense of the listed subsidiary. Historical European telco precedent — particularly at subsidiaries of incumbent telcos — includes several examples of such value transfer. This risk is real, even if it is not a current active concern.
  • Capital allocation conflict. Orange SA's balance sheet is under pressure from its own fibre programme in France and international commitments. If Orange SA requires cash upstream from OBEL-BE at a point when OBEL-BE needs to invest in its own integration, the minority shareholder is structurally disadvantaged.
  • Strategic alignment. Orange SA's group strategy (pan-European operator with a French-domestic core, African operations, B2B enterprise services) may lead it to make decisions about Orange Belgium that are rational for the group but not optimal for OBEL-BE standalone. The most obvious risk is an eventual take-private: Orange SA may decide to acquire the remaining ~47% float to simplify governance, and the squeeze-out premium may or may not adequately compensate minority holders.

Management. Orange Belgium appointed a new CEO as part of the post-VOO integration phase, signalling a break from the pre-acquisition leadership and an explicit operational focus on synergy delivery and convergence execution. This is appropriate; the question for Triaging is management track record and their credibility in delivering the synergy and FCF guidance.

Minority shareholder dynamics. The free float is institutionally held with no known activist presence. Minority shareholders have limited structural protection against Orange SA's governance prerogatives. This is a feature of the investment, not a bug to be overlooked.

#A — Key analytical observations.

Three observations stand out against the European telco cohort:

1. The EBITDAaL expansion on declining revenue is genuinely unusual. Most European telcos in structural convergence transitions experience margin compression during the integration phase (higher capex, duplicate costs, integration charges). Orange Belgium is reporting the opposite — which implies the VOO cost extraction is running harder and faster than the legacy voice revenue erosion. This needs verification but, if confirmed, is a strong signal that the synergy programme is performing.

2. The structural Flanders gap is underappreciated as a constraint. The post-VOO narrative tends to emphasise what Orange Belgium gained (Wallonia cable infrastructure) while underemphasising what it still lacks (any owned fixed infrastructure in the more populous Flanders region). A European telco with genuine FMC capability in only approximately 40% of the national population cannot fully participate in the sector-level re-rating that full convergence operators have received. Orange Belgium is structurally closer to a regional FMC operator that also has a national mobile network than it is to a true national converged operator.

3. The DIGI threat is not a standard competitive risk. DIGI Belgium's cost structure (built on Romanian-origin infrastructure leverage and a sub-EUR 15/month price point for all-in mobile) cannot be profitably matched by Orange Belgium at current cost levels. Orange Belgium's strategic answer — bundle economics make FMC customers more defensible than SIM-only — is correct in theory but depends entirely on Wallonia FMC conversion rates. If FMC penetration disappoints, the mobile ARPU base is exposed without a structural defence.

#J — First-pass verdict: Does this warrant Triaging?

Yes. The BD case is interesting enough to progress to Triaging, with a Yellow overall signal. The VOO convergence thesis is structurally sound, the synergy delivery is ahead of schedule, and the EBITDAaL trajectory is a rare positive data point in a sector where most peers are reporting flat to declining EBITDA. However, the case is not clean: the Flanders fixed gap is a permanent structural constraint unless resolved through an unconfirmed co-investment or wholesale agreement; the DIGI competitive pressure is structural not cyclical; and the Orange SA governance overhang introduces minority shareholder risk that is not present in standalone converged operators.

The three most important questions for Triaging:

1. VOO synergy path and FCF conversion. At what point does FCF inflect positively and the dividend reinstate? What is management's guidance on the synergy delivery curve through year 7, and what assumptions underpin the EUR 110m target? The gap between year-4 actuals (EUR 47.5m) and year-7 target (EUR 110m+) implies a significant step-up still required — what drives it?

2. Flanders fixed access — strategic resolution or permanent constraint? Is there a credible plan for Orange Belgium to build or acquire fixed infrastructure in Flanders within a 5-year horizon? If not, what does the TAM for FMC bundles actually look like capped at Wallonia, and what multiple should that limit command versus a national converged operator?

3. DIGI Belgium mobile ARPU impact — what is the magnitude and pace? Sell-side consensus likely models some ARPU decline; the question is whether the actual pace of DIGI-driven attrition is within or outside the envelope of the convergence synergy benefit. If DIGI compresses mobile ARPU faster than VOO synergies flow through, the EBITDA expansion thesis reverses.
