AIXA — Change Forces

AS IG Q02 | 06-May-26

Key Findings

  • Bridge period — SiC to AI/Opto pivot: [AS] The next 12 to 24 months represent a pivotal "bridge" period where near-term weakness in Silicon Carbide (SiC) is expected to be offset by a structural surge in Indium Phosphide (InP) demand for AI data centers; Aixtron is the dominant supplier of InP MOCVD tools required in the manufacturing of lasers for optical/fibre-based AI datacenter communications.
  • Leadership stability — dual contract extensions: [AS] CEO Felix Grawert's contract extended through August 2030; CFO Christian Danninger's contract extended through 2029; both extensions were secured proactively before the downturn deepened, providing a stable two-person Executive Board; departure of long-standing Supervisory Board Chair Schindelhauer is the primary change.
  • Strategy — G10 ramp-up phase, 300mm pivot, AI positioning: [AS] Strategic priority has shifted from the "high investment phase" (2023/24) to full commercial ramp of the G10 tool family; the company is positioning itself as "tool of record" for InP laser manufacturers serving the AI transceiver market; long-term pivot toward 300mm GaN is underway.
  • Financial profile — dramatic FCF recovery: [AS] Most dramatic change: the transition from cash-burning (2024) to highly cash-generative (2025); FCF surged to €182M from -€72M; cash reached €225M; equity ratio rose to 88%; zero bank debt; company has rebuilt its "firepower" to weather the soft 2026.
  • Demand — opto surge vs. SiC digestion: [AS] Demand for optical interconnects (Indium Phosphide) is surging, driven by 800G and 1.6T transceiver upgrades in AI data centers; simultaneously, SiC customers are in a "digestion phase" with high idle capacity following aggressive building in 2022/23; the demand profile is shifting from broad-based SiC/EV rally to a highly concentrated AI/Opto push.
  • Competition — MOCVD dominance holds, SiC threatened: [AS] Aixtron holds 71% MOCVD market share and ~90% GaN market share; Chinese competitors (AMEC, Naura, TOPEC) are offering tools at prices up to 75% lower than Western counterparts; Veeco is the primary GaN/Opto rival; SiC faces intense competition from ASM International, Tokyo Electron, and Applied Materials.
  • Disruption — 300mm transition and 800V AI power shift: [AS] The combination of the 300mm wafer transition and the 800V AI power shift represent powerful disruptive forces hitting simultaneously; while Aixtron is well-positioned, the technical risk of these transitions is significant; InP is rapidly taking share from copper in data centers.
  • Political — regulatory environment worsening: [AS] The regulatory environment has worsened significantly: US tariff threats on German exports, tightening export controls on China, and German export licensing delays are now core drivers of current strategic decision-making rather than tail risks; Malaysia plant and UK export shift are direct responses.
  • Revenue/demand cycle — constructive interference thesis: [AS] Morgan Stanley's thesis: different end-market cycles (EV, AI, Datacom) will align for a massive surge in 2027; the company has successfully managed its most recent peak-to-trough without permanent balance sheet damage; the next up-cycle is already being priced in for 2027.
  • Historical track record — revenue delivery strong, TSR poor: [AS] Aixtron has a strong record of hitting revenue and operational targets; the G10 family was launched across SiC, GaN, and AsP within 24 months; communication, execution, and visibility have been more volatile than front-end peers; LTI TSR tranche underperformed despite sustainability wins.
  • Overall company rating — Yellow: [AS] Aixtron is in a period of high-intensity internal and external change; leadership and balance sheet are solid (Green/Yellow), but shifting revenue mix toward AI and regulatory/political headwinds (Red/Orange) create a complex near-term setup; rating would move to Green once Malaysia plant is operational and 2027 recovery visibility improves.

Brief Company Description

Aixtron SE is the global technology leader in MOCVD equipment, targeting Power Electronics (GaN and SiC), Optoelectronics, and Specialty LEDs; revenue from equipment sales and after-sales services (20% of 2025 revenue); asset-light model with strong balance sheet; pivoting from EV-led growth to AI-led growth while foundational leadership and technology remain consistent.

  • Core business: [AS] Aixtron SE is the global technology leader in Metal-Organic Chemical Vapor Deposition (MOCVD) equipment, which is used to deposit thin layers of compound semiconductor materials onto wafers.
  • Target segments: [AS] The company operates a focused business model targeting high-growth segments including Power Electronics (GaN and SiC), Optoelectronics (Lasers, Micro LEDs), and Specialty LEDs.
  • Revenue streams: [AS] Revenue is primarily derived from the sale of deposition tools (equipment revenue) and a growing after-sales service business, which accounted for 20% of total revenue in 2025.
  • Global presence: [AS] Aixtron operates globally with a presence in approximately 10 countries, including key manufacturing and R&D hubs in Germany, the UK, and Italy, and extensive sales/service networks across Asia and the USA.
  • Historical market evolution: [AS] Historically, the company was heavily reliant on the LED market, but over the last three years, it has pivotally expanded into Wide-Bandgap (WBG) power electronics to serve the electric vehicle (EV) and renewable energy sectors.
  • Technology impact: [AS] The company's technology enables the production of energy-efficient devices that reduce power conversion losses by up to 40% compared to traditional silicon.
  • Core competitive advantage: [AS] Aixtron's core competitive advantage lies in its Planetary Reactor technology, which offers superior uniformity and high-throughput for volume manufacturing.
  • Current product flagship: [AS] The current product flagship is the G10 system family, introduced between 2022 and 2023, which includes dedicated tools for SiC, GaN, and AsP (Arsenide Phosphide) applications.
  • Geographic revenue concentration: [AS] Geographically, revenue is highly concentrated in Asia, which represented 66% of FY24 sales, followed by Europe (24%) and the Americas (10%).
  • AI infrastructure positioning: [AS] In the next three years, Aixtron is positioned to benefit from the AI infrastructure build-out, specifically through the demand for optical interconnects and GaN-based power supply units in data centers.
  • 300mm transition: [AS] The company is also transitioning toward 300mm wafer technology, having recently joined a major 300mm GaN Power Electronic Program with its Hyperion tool.
  • Asset-light model: [AS] Aixtron's business model is characterised as "asset-light" with a strong balance sheet and no external bank debt.
  • Leadership: [AS] Strategic leadership is provided by CEO Dr. Felix Grawert and CFO Dr. Christian Danninger, both of whom have had their contracts extended through 2029/2030.
  • Constructive interference thesis: [AS] Future revenue growth is expected to be driven by "constructive interference" where previously uncorrelated end-market cycles (EVs, AI, Datacom) align in 2027.
  • GaN market share: [AS] The company maintains a dominant market share (approx. 90%) in the GaN MOCVD tool market.
  • Malaysia expansion: [AS] Operational expansion is underway with a new €40 million manufacturing facility in Malaysia planned to start operations in 2027.
  • Upstream AI leverage: [AS] Aixtron serves as an "upstream leverage point" in the AI optical value chain, supplying the tools needed by laser manufacturers like Coherent and Lumentum.
  • Degree of change — Yellow: [AS] While Aixtron is undergoing a significant strategic pivot toward AI-driven optoelectronics, its core business model as an MOCVD equipment supplier remains stable; the rating reflects the transition from EV-led growth to AI-led growth, but with foundational leadership and technology remaining consistent.

Change in Leadership

The CEO and CFO both had their contracts extended proactively (through 2030 and 2029 respectively), providing strong senior-level continuity; the long-standing Supervisory Board Chair stepped down after 30 years; the Executive Board was reduced to two members (CEO + CFO) following COO departure, compensated by a new six-person Executive Committee; headcount reduction implemented in early 2025.

  • CEO contract extension: [AS] The Supervisory Board extended CEO Felix Grawert's contract early in September 2024, securing his leadership through August 2030.
  • CFO contract extension: [AS] CFO Christian Danninger's contract was extended for five years starting May 2024, with a 20% increase in fixed compensation to €360,000 p.a.
  • Executive Board size: [AS] Since October 2023, the Executive Board has consisted of only two members (CEO and CFO) following the departure of COO Jochen Linck.
  • Supervisory Board succession: [AS] Kim Schindelhauer transitioned off the Supervisory Board in May 2025, marking the end of a three-decade era of influence; Ingo Bank was elected to the Supervisory Board at the May 2025 AGM, bringing fresh external oversight experience.
  • Executive Committee created: [AS] To support the two-person Executive Board, an Executive Committee (EC) was established, comprising six experienced executives responsible for product portfolio and technology development.
  • Headcount reduction: [AS] Aixtron implemented a mid-single-digit million Euro expense for voluntary headcount reduction in the operations area in early 2025.
  • Compensation alignment: [AS] Variable compensation targets for the Executive Board are now directly linked to the company's strategic performance and sustainability (ESG) goals; R&D successes and product development milestones are key KPIs for management bonuses.
  • Sustainability LTI achievement: [AS] Sustainability goals achieved a 143% target realization in the 2023-2025 LTI tranche, highlighting a top-down focus on ESG.
  • Investor relations continuity: [AS] Christian Ludwig remains the VP of Investor Relations, maintaining consistent communication through the market downturn.
  • Investor engagement: [AS] The company conducted over 500 investor meetings in 2025, up from 450 in 2024, to maintain transparency during the cycle shift.
  • Recruitment and culture pivot: [AS] Aixtron centralised its internal recruitment process in 2025, focusing on social media presence to attract top talent; the leadership is pushing for a "modern corporate culture" with a sophisticated leadership style and strong, autonomous teams.
  • Leadership degree of change — Yellow: [AS] The long-term contract extensions for the CEO and CFO provide a stable core; the major change is the departure of the long-standing Supervisory Board Chair and the reduction in the size of the formal Executive Board, which has been compensated for by the creation of the broader Executive Committee.

Change in Strategy and Structure

Core "diversified equipment" strategy remains, but structural shift toward Asian manufacturing (Malaysia) and consolidation of the product portfolio around the G10 family represent significant tactical evolutions; AI optical positioning as "tool of record" for InP laser manufacturers is the most consequential new strategic move; APEVA OLED entity removed from consolidation; Italian site divestiture confirms footprint rationalisation.

  • Segment diversification strategy: [AS] Aixtron's strategy relies on serving uncorrelated end markets (Consumer, IT, AI, EV) to cushion fluctuations in any single market.
  • Core portfolio focus: [AS] The company focuses on just three main product lines: GaN Power, SiC Power, and Optoelectronics (G10-AsP).
  • Asset-light model: [AS] Aixtron maintains a business model with low capital intensity, prioritising high R&D spend over massive internal manufacturing assets.
  • Malaysia manufacturing expansion: [AS] A strategic shift toward localised manufacturing in Asia is underway with the Kulim, Malaysia plant, aiming for better regional customer support and cost optimisation.
  • Italian site divestiture: [AS] The Italian production site was removed from the scope of consolidation in 2025 as part of an operational streamlining effort.
  • G10 ramp-up phase: [AS] Strategic priority has shifted from the "high investment phase" (2023/24) to the full commercial ramp of the G10 tool family.
  • 300mm pivot: [AS] A major long-term strategic goal is the transition from 200mm to 300mm wafer equipment, particularly for GaN.
  • AI optical tool of record positioning: [AS] The company is positioning itself as a "tool of record" for the Indium Phosphide (InP) manufacturers serving the AI transceiver market.
  • M&A strategy: [AS] While no major deals closed recently, Aixtron's strategy includes "targeted acquisitions" to fill technology gaps, though organic growth remains the primary focus.
  • Partnership ecosystem: [AS] The company emphasises an "innovation eco-system," collaborating with sub-suppliers and universities like RWTH Aachen.
  • APEVA OLED removal: [AS] APEVA SE, the company's OLED focused entity, was removed from consolidation in mid-2025.
  • Working capital optimisation: [AS] A current structural priority is reducing the inventory-to-sales ratio, which swelled during the supply chain crisis of 2022/23; R&D costs were reduced in 2025 by 11% by bringing more external contract work in-house.
  • US tariff mitigation — UK export shift: [AS] Management is monitoring US tariff developments closely and may shift export flows through its UK subsidiary to avoid German-specific tariffs.
  • Customer concentration reduction: [AS] Strategy involves diversifying the customer base to reduce reliance on single large players, though laser customers (Coherent/Lumentum) remain dominant in the opto segment.
  • Capacity for ramp: [AS] Current capacity supports up to 60 tools per quarter, which is being expanded to meet projected 2027 demand; management claims the ability to repurpose idle SiC capacity for Opto tools if demand spikes unexpectedly.
  • Nvidia Lumentum/Coherent investment validation: [AS] Nvidia recently announced investments of approximately USD $2bn each in Lumentum and Coherent, totaling around USD $4bn, aimed at expanding advanced photonics technology and manufacturing capacity for next-generation AI data centers.
  • Strategy degree of change — Yellow: [AS] The core "diversified equipment" strategy remains, but the structural shift toward Asian manufacturing (Malaysia) and the consolidation of the product portfolio around the G10 family represent significant tactical evolutions.

Change in Priorities and Ambition

The ambition to be the #1 player in compound semiconductors is unchanged; the priority shift is tactical: from aggressive CapEx (Innovation Center) to operational efficiency and cash preservation while waiting for the next market upturn; CEO is excited about the AI datacenter opportunity as a "second growth driver"; cautious and "sober" on SiC in 2026; targeting a "sovereign European supply chain" while expanding in Asia.

  • Cash position rebuilding — top priority: [AS] A top qualitative priority for the CEO is "rebuilding a strong cash position" after the heavy investment cycle of 2023/24.
  • Technology leadership focus: [AS] CEO Felix Grawert emphasises being the "recognised technology leader" rather than following industry standards; this was recently recognised by the German Innovation Award.
  • Market share aggression in SiC: [AS] The company is not content with its current position in SiC (approx. 37% share) and is aggressively targeting share gains vs. incumbents like ASM.
  • AI pivot intensity: [AS] Management commentary reflects high excitement about the AI datacenter opportunity, describing it as a "second growth driver" that de-risks the story.
  • Operating discipline in soft market: [AS] There is a renewed focus on "operating results" in a "soft market environment," with a goal of maintaining margins despite lower top-line growth.
  • Dividend logic: [AS] The decision to maintain a stable dividend (€0.15) despite lower net profit reflects an ambition to demonstrate cash flow resilience to shareholders.
  • GaN growth narrative: [AS] Leadership expects the GaN tool market to triple by the end of the decade, driven by power efficiency and AI adoption.
  • Wait-and-see on SiC: [AS] Management has adopted a "cautious" and "sober" outlook for SiC in 2026, refusing to chase low-margin business during the downturn.
  • Global footprint ambition: [AS] The goal is to establish a "sovereign European supply chain" while simultaneously expanding in Asia to capture local market dynamics.
  • G10 optimisation phase: [AS] The focus has shifted from "developing" the G10 to "optimising" its productivity and total cost of ownership (TCO) for customers.
  • Visibility realism: [AS] CEO Grawert admitted that "visibility for the fiscal year '26 remains low", reflecting a pragmatic approach to guidance.
  • ESG leadership target: [AS] The goal is to publish SBTi-aligned climate targets by end-2025 and achieve "Target Approved" status by end-2026.
  • Market cycle preparation: [AS] Leadership is leveraging the current "market consolidation" phase to prepare for the next growth wave expected in 2027.
  • Priorities degree of change — Yellow: [AS] The ambition to be the #1 player in compound semiconductors is unchanged; the priority shift is tactical: from aggressive CapEx (Innovation Center) to operational efficiency and cash preservation while waiting for the next market upturn.

Financial Profile

The most dramatic change is the transition from cash-burning (2024) to highly cash-generative (2025); FCF surged to €182M from -€72M; cash reached €225M; equity ratio 88%; zero bank debt; R&D and CapEx normalising post-G10 launch; 2026 is a guided bridge year at €520M revenue with 16-19% EBIT margin; EPS projected to recover from €0.60 (2026E) to €1.30 by 2028E.

  • Revenue trajectory: [AS] Revenue reached €557 million in 2025, down 12% YoY, but representing a 13% CAGR since 2020; 2026 revenue is guided at €520 million (+/- €30 million).
  • Gross margin compression: [AS] Gross margins peaked at 44% in 2023, declined to 41% in 2024, and dropped to 40% in 2025; 2026 guided at 41-42%.
  • EBIT margin: [AS] 2025 EBIT margin was 18%, down from 25% in 2023; 2026 guided at 16% to 19%, reflecting a transition year with mid-single-digit million restructuring expenses.
  • Net profit trajectory: [AS] Net profit: 2023 €145m → 2024 €106m → 2025 €85m (declining with revenue).
  • FCF recovery — most dramatic change: [AS] Massive surge in 2025 to €182 million from negative €72 million in 2024, driven by working capital reduction and lower CapEx.
  • Cash and liquidity: [AS] Cash and cash equivalents reached €225 million at end-2025, compared to €65 million in 2024; net cash of approx. €222 million; zero bank debt; equity ratio increased to 88% by end-2025.
  • Inventory reduction: [AS] Inventories decreased in 2025, with a further 20% reduction target for 2026 to free up additional cash.
  • R&D normalisation: [AS] R&D spend was at "peak level" in 2024 (€88 million) to finalise the G10 family, before moderating in 2025; Aixtron reduced R&D costs in 2025 by 11% by bringing more external contract work in-house.
  • CapEx cycle: [AS] CapEx peaked in 2024 at €99 million (Innovation Center) and is expected to moderate to €30-40 million range through 2028.
  • Order backlog: [AS] Stood at €308 million as of March 2025, providing moderate visibility but down from the 2023 peak.
  • Profitability vs. peers: [AS] Aixtron's EBIT margins (18%) trail the semi-cap peer median (25%), but analysts see scope for catch-up during the 2027 recovery.
  • EPS trajectory: [AS] EPS projections show rising from €0.60 in 2026E to €1.30 by 2028E as growth resumes.
  • Currency risk: [AS] Guidance for 2026 is based on a budget rate of $1.20/€, up from $1.10 in 2025, presenting a slight headwind.
  • AI revenue share estimate: [AS] Management estimates that approximately 50% of 2027 revenues will be tied to AI data center infrastructure.
  • Financial profile degree of change — Green: [AS] The most dramatic change is the transition from a heavy investment, cash-burning phase (2024) to a highly cash-generative profile (2025); the company has successfully "rebuilt its firepower" to weather the soft 2026.

Value Chain — Demand

Demand profile is shifting from broad-based SiC/EV rally to a highly concentrated AI/Opto push; InP laser demand is surging for AI datacenter optical interconnects; SiC is in a "digestion phase"; GaN growing moderately; LED/MicroLED soft; after-sales demand (spares/services) growing steadily; the SiC digestion is deeper and longer than initially expected, creating a temporary revenue vacuum.

  • Opto surge — InP for AI: [AS] Demand for optical interconnects and lasers (Indium Phosphide) is surging, driven by 800G and 1.6T transceiver upgrades in AI data centers.
  • SiC digestion phase: [AS] Customers in the SiC space are currently in a "digestion phase," with high levels of idle capacity following aggressive building in 2022/23; SiC players are currently delaying capacity expansion until fab utilisation rates recover to above 70%.
  • GaN momentum: [AS] Demand for GaN power tools is growing moderately, bolstered by AI server power supply units (PSUs) which reduce energy losses by 50% compared to silicon.
  • LED and MicroLED soft: [AS] The LED and Micro LED markets are described as "soft" or "largely unchanged," with significant project postponements seen in 2025.
  • EV deceleration: [AS] Slowing EV momentum in the West has tempered SiC demand, though long-term adoption remains a structural tailwind.
  • China investment paradox: [AS] Despite overcapacity, Chinese customers continue to invest in SiC tools to build domestic infrastructure and establish "epi foundries"; order intake in early 2025 was primarily driven by Asian customers, compensating for "weaker demand from western players."
  • Wafer size transition demand: [AS] Demand is increasingly focused on tools that support the transition from 6-inch to 8-inch wafers for better economics.
  • Strategic ordering catalyst: [AS] Customers may return to "aggressive strategic ordering" if lead times begin to extend again in 2027.
  • After-sales demand growth: [AS] Revenue from spares and services is growing (€112 million in 2025) as the installed base of G10 systems expands.
  • Hyperscaler capex dependency: [AS] Demand for Aixtron's opto tools is indirectly but heavily tied to the CapEx trends of major cloud hyperscalers.
  • Coherent and Lumentum CapEx signal: [AS] Aixtron's largest opto customers, Coherent and Lumentum, recently reported CapEx 35% above expectations, signalling strong near-term demand.
  • 800V GaN catalyst: [AS] The upcoming shift to 800V direct current distribution in AI data centers (e.g., NVIDIA Kyber) is expected to trigger a new wave of GaN tool orders.
  • 3D sensing growth: [AS] Growing demand for lasers in LiDAR and advanced driver-assistance systems (ADAS) provides a non-AI growth pillar for optoelectronics.
  • Qualification cycle lead time: [AS] Long sales and qualification cycles for new GaN tools mean today's development activities won't translate to revenue until 2026/27.
  • Demand degree of change — Orange: [AS] The demand profile is shifting from a broad-based SiC/EV rally to a highly concentrated AI/Opto push; the "digestion" in SiC is deeper and longer than initially expected, creating a temporary revenue vacuum.

Value Chain — Competition

Aixtron holds 71% MOCVD market share and ~90% GaN market share; Chinese competitors offer tools at prices up to 75% lower than Western counterparts but lag on performance and IP; Veeco is the primary GaN/Opto rival; SiC competition is intense from ASM International, Tokyo Electron, and Applied Materials; "tool of record" status in datacom lasers is the most defensible current moat; competitive intensity is rising but Aixtron's installed base provides a 24-month buffer.

  • MOCVD market dominance: [AS] Aixtron holds a 71% market share in the MOCVD segment (GaN, LED, Opto), making it the undisputed incumbent.
  • GaN near-monopoly: [AS] The company maintains a near-monopoly 90% market share in GaN-on-Silicon deposition tools.
  • SiC competition intensity: [AS] In the SiC market, Aixtron faces intense competition from ASM International (LPE), Tokyo Electron, and Applied Materials.
  • Veeco threat: [AS] Veeco Instruments is Aixtron's primary rival in GaN and optoelectronics, with some experts predicting Aixtron could lose share in GaN over the next decade as Veeco catches up in R&D.
  • Chinese entrants — pricing pressure: [AS] Local Chinese competitors like AMEC, Naura, and TOPEC are aggressively targeting the MOCVD market; Chinese competitors are reportedly offering tools at prices up to 75% lower than Western counterparts, supported by state subsidies.
  • IP and R&D barriers: [AS] Aixtron uses its robust IP portfolio to prevent new entrants from infringing on its deposition technologies, forcing them to create their own from scratch; high R&D intensity and complex gas flow management create a high barrier for new entrants.
  • SiC market share gain: [AS] Aixtron achieved a 37% market share in SiC CVD in 2023, up significantly from prior years.
  • Differentiation strategy: [AS] Aixtron focuses on "total cost of ownership" (TCO) and process stability to compete against lower-priced rivals.
  • Tool of record — G10-AsP: [AS] The G10-AsP is currently the industry's "tool of record" for datacom lasers, providing a significant competitive advantage.
  • 300mm race: [AS] The race is on to be the first to commercialise a reliable 300mm GaN platform; rumours suggest Asian competitors are also active in this space.
  • European geopolitical advantage: [AS] Experts view Europe as the "safest region" for technology development amid US-China trade tensions, potentially favouring Aixtron over US-based rivals.
  • Vertical integration risk: [AS] Some large customers are attempting to develop their own deposition tools in-house, though this remains technically challenging.
  • Service network advantage: [AS] Aixtron's established global sales and service organisation (13 subsidiaries) provides a barrier for new Chinese players trying to expand globally.
  • Commoditisation threat: [AS] Substrates and early-stage epitaxy are becoming "commoditised," but high-end multi-wafer tools like Aixtron's maintain more defensible niches.
  • Competition degree of change — Yellow: [AS] While competitive intensity is rising — particularly from China and ASM in SiC — Aixtron's massive installed base and "tool of record" status in GaN and Opto provide a strong defensive moat for the next 24 months.

Value Chain — Disruption

The combination of the 300mm transition and the 800V AI power shift represent powerful disruptive forces hitting simultaneously; InP is rapidly taking copper share in data centers; Micro LED commercial delays continue; the 8-inch SiC wafer transition provides a 2.5x capacity gain that may temporarily reduce new tool needs; China's push for a fully independent semiconductor supply chain is disrupting global trade flows; humanoid robotics identified as a new long-term demand driver.

  • 300mm transition — most significant disruptive force: [AS] The shift from 200mm to 300mm wafers is the most significant disruptive force, potentially re-shuffling market share in GaN.
  • 800V architecture disruption: [AS] NVIDIA's new 800V direct current distribution for AI servers is disrupting the traditional power supply value chain, favouring GaN over silicon.
  • InP taking copper share: [AS] Indium Phosphide (InP) is rapidly taking share from copper in data centers, creating a massive new opportunity for Aixtron's AsP tools.
  • Silicon-to-GaN replacement: [AS] The increasing adoption of GaN in white goods (washing machines) and fast chargers is disrupting traditional silicon chip sales.
  • Micro LED delays: [AS] Technical challenges in scaling Micro LED for mass consumer displays have disrupted growth expectations for this segment.
  • AI-onboard power disruption: [AS] Replacing traditional silicon around GPUs with GaN could reduce power losses by 50%, a disruptive shift in chip-level architecture.
  • Co-packaged optics: [AS] The migration toward Co-packaged Optics (CPO) is increasing device complexity and wafer intensity, benefiting Aixtron's high-uniformity tools.
  • Domestic localisation disruption: [AS] China's push for a fully independent semiconductor supply chain is disrupting global trade flows and creating new domestic rivals.
  • 8-inch SiC wafer transition: [AS] The disruptive transition to 8-inch SiC wafers provides a 2.5x capacity gain, which may temporarily reduce the number of new tools needed.
  • Export restrictions: [AS] German and EU dual-use regulations are a disruptive factor, occasionally blocking sales to major customers in China.
  • AI efficiency-driven demand acceleration: [AS] AI power intensity is forcing data centers to move to wide-bandgap materials much faster than traditional industrial sectors.
  • Humanoid robotics — new long-term driver: [AS] Emerging AI applications like humanoid robots are being identified by management as new potential long-term demand drivers.
  • 800V EV platform shift: [AS] The shift from 400V to 800V in EVs is a key structural disruption favouring SiC, though the rollout has slowed.
  • Inventory scrapping — deep cyclical disruption signal: [AS] The practice of "scrapping" old inventory to clean up balance sheets is a sign of deep cyclical disruption in the equipment sector.
  • "China +1" strategy impact: [AS] Manufacturers are building new plants in Southeast Asia (Malaysia, Vietnam) to disrupt their own historical reliance on China-based production.
  • Subsidised competition disruption: [AS] State-backed financing in Asia is disrupting the "unlevel playing field" for European and US equipment makers.
  • Disruption degree of change — Orange: [AS] The combination of the 300mm transition and the 800V AI power shift represent powerful disruptive forces hitting simultaneously; while Aixtron is well-positioned, the technical risk of these transitions is significant.

Value Chain — Political and Regulatory

The regulatory environment has worsened significantly; US tariff threats (25% on German products), tightening export controls on China, and German export licensing delays are now core drivers of strategic decision-making; Malaysia plant and UK export shift are direct responses; EU Taxonomy compliance is a growing driver; CHIPS Acts provide an indirect benefit; overall degree of change is Red.

  • US tariff risk: [AS] US President Donald Trump's threat of 25% tariffs on semiconductors and German products is a major concern for 2026/27; Aixtron is exploring shifting production to its UK facility to mitigate potential US tariffs on German exports.
  • German export licensing: [AS] German government export licenses are required for all Aixtron tools; historical delays have significantly impacted revenue timing; largely addressed but remains a persistent monitorable risk.
  • EU Dual-Use Regulation: [AS] Aixtron falls under EU dual-use regulations, limiting its ability to ship certain high-end tools to non-NATO countries.
  • China export controls: [AS] Increasing US and EU restrictions on "leading-edge" technology to China are a persistent headwind, particularly for 300mm systems.
  • EU Taxonomy compliance: [AS] The EU Taxonomy Regulation is a key regulatory framework, with Aixtron focusing on "environmentally sustainable" sales to maintain investor support; taxonomy-aligned sales reached a record high achievement of 147% of target in 2025.
  • Malaysian incentives: [AS] The decision to build in Malaysia was likely influenced by local government growth initiatives and a favourable trade environment.
  • CHIPS Act indirect benefit: [AS] While Aixtron is German, it benefits from the broader CapEx wave triggered by the US and European CHIPS Acts.
  • Sovereign supply chain push: [AS] There is a growing political push in Europe to build "sovereign" semiconductor supply chains to reduce reliance on Asia.
  • Public funding dependency: [AS] Aixtron's R&D activities often utilise public funding from the German government and EU, which is subject to political shifts.
  • Forced divestiture/postponement risk: [AS] Political pressure can lead to "postponements" or cancellations of projects, as seen in the Chinese market during the 2024 restrictions.
  • Bifurcated supply chain: [AS] Trade barriers have effectively created a "bifurcated" supply chain where Western and Chinese components are no longer interchangeable.
  • Political/regulatory degree of change — Red: [AS] The regulatory environment has worsened significantly due to the looming threat of US tariffs and tightening export controls on China; these political factors are no longer "tail risks" but are core drivers of current strategic decision-making (e.g., Malaysia plant, UK export shift).

Value Chain — Supply Side

The supply chain is moving from "scarcity and crisis" (2022) to "oversupply and liquidation" (2025); focus has shifted from "getting parts at any cost" to "inventory reduction and cost optimisation"; vulnerabilities remain in critical components like pumps and delivery modules; energy costs in Europe are a disadvantage versus Chinese competitors; lead times currently under control but could stretch rapidly if demand returns.

  • Supplier partnership model: [AS] Aixtron involves key suppliers early in projects to ensure foresight in the medium-to-long term supply chain.
  • Supply chain vulnerabilities: [AS] The company still faces vulnerabilities in the supply chain for critical components like pumps and delivery modules.
  • Logistics efficiency focus: [AS] Management is currently working on "operational efficiency" in its logistics and storage topics during the 2026 market pause.
