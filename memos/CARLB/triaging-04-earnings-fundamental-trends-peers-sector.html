KEY FINDINGS

  • Carlsberg is executing solidly and tracking ahead of the peer group on margin expansion momentum. FY2025 organic EBIT grew 5% with operating margin expansion of 50bp to 15.7%, while Q1 2026 organic revenue beat analyst forecasts (3.6% vs 2.8% expected). The company is not standing still; it is accelerating.
  • Britvic is a genuine margin and synergy story, not just an optical revenue booster. DKK 2.2bn EBIT contribution from Britvic in its first full partial year, with ~30% of GBP 110m cost synergies delivered and the programme running ahead of plan. The 2026 guidance explicitly targets 30-40% synergy realisation, adding a reliable EPS tailwind that peers do not have.
  • Q1 2026 is the strongest recent data point: organic volume +2.8%, organic revenue +3.6%, all three regions growing. Asia returned to growth (+3.4% volume, +1% rev/hl) after a weak period — a critical confirmation for bulls given Chongqing Brewery's direct relevance to the Asia segment.
  • AB InBev is the operationally tightest peer. 101bp EBITDA margin expansion in FY2025, underlying EPS +6%, net debt/EBITDA at 2.87x — structurally more profitable (35.8% EBITDA margin) and deploying capital better. It is the benchmark Carlsberg needs to chase, not merely match.
  • Heineken is the clearest underperformer. Organic revenue +1.6%, EPS growth only +3.6%, total volumes declined 2.1% — the worst volume print in the peer group. This is not a crisis but it signals a company sacrificing volume for price without the operational leverage to turn it into superior EPS momentum.
  • Asahi is the biggest surprise risk in the peer group. Cyberattack on September 2025 has delayed FY2025 full results into May 2026+ and caused a 4.6% core operating profit decline in the nine-month period. This is idiosyncratic, not sector-driven, but it removes a credible European premium beer competitor from the competitive conversation for the near term.
  • Chongqing Brewery 2025: revenue +0.53%, net profit +10.43%. Volume flat (+0.68%) in a market where total Chinese beer output fell 1.1% YoY — Chongqing slightly outgrew the sector. The profit uplift (10x revenue growth rate) signals meaningful operating leverage/mix improvement within Carlsberg's China platform. Direct read-through: Asia margin improvement at group level has credibility.
  • Sector-wide: premiumisation and pricing are carrying the day; volume is universally weak. Every peer reported flat to negative total volume. Revenue per hectolitre growth (+3-4% across peers) is the universal driver. This is a benign backdrop for Carlsberg, which has premium and alcohol-free as declared growth vectors — but it means the thesis is riding a sector tailwind, not pure operational alpha.
  • The single largest unresolved risk is demand-side deterioration in Western Europe. CEO Aarup-Andersen was explicit in Q1 commentary that "global consumer does not appear to be moving toward a significantly more positive stance." All peers with Western European exposure face this. For Carlsberg, with ~40% of revenue in Western Europe + Britvic UK, this is material.
  • Guidance is credible but not heroic. 2-6% organic EBIT growth for FY2026 is the same range issued by both Heineken and AB InBev — this is the sector consensus band. Carlsberg's Q1 beat adds confidence in delivery of the upper half of the range, but there is no company-specific reason to expect Carlsberg to break away from the peer band unless Britvic synergies or Asia momentum accelerates materially.

Heineken is underperforming on volume and EPS. Asahi is structurally impaired by the cyberattack fallout and has not yet reported full-year numbers.
  • Carlsberg's position: Upper-middle of the peer group. Not best-in-class operationally (AB InBev retains that title), but the Britvic integration provides a company-specific earnings catalyst that none of the other pure-beer peers have in 2026.
  • Three most significant trend reversals or accelerations Jan–Apr 2026:
    • (1) Carlsberg Asia recovery: After weakness in 2024 and H1 2025, Q1 2026 delivered +3.4% volume growth and Chongqing Brewery's 2025 profit grew +10.4% — Asia narrative is rehabilitating.
    • (2) Asahi structural dislocation: A cyberattack in Sept 2025 has created an ongoing disruption to reporting, operating performance in Japan/East Asia, and investor confidence. This is unusual for a large-cap peer and creates a competitive gap Carlsberg can exploit in Asia.
    • (3) AB InBev sequential improvement: Q4 2025 showed volume stabilisation vs three prior quarters of decline, and the 101bp EBITDA margin expansion was the strongest in the peer group — signalling disciplined execution under pressure.

Pricing per hectolitre of +3-4% is offsetting volume softness universally. The sector is in a period of "quality over quantity" growth — value-additive for established premium portfolios.
  • Sector-wide headwinds Jan–Apr 2026: Western European consumer confidence remains fragile; Middle East conflict (geopolitical risk) was explicitly mentioned by Carlsberg management in Q1 commentary; FX headwinds (particularly JPY/EUR cross, USD/local currency impacts for AB InBev) are creating noise in reported numbers for several peers.
  • Relative data availability note: Asahi has not published FY2025 final results as of 30 Apr 2026 (delayed due to cyberattack); this limits the precision of any Asahi comparison and increases its effective analytical risk weight.

Rating: YELLOW-GREEN

  • Summary judgement: Carlsberg's organic revenue growth is in the middle of the peer pack on an FY2025 basis but tracking ahead in Q1 2026 — the most recent data point. The Britvic-driven reported revenue explosion (+18.8%) is M&A-inflated, but organic dynamics are sound and accelerating.

    • Heineken: organic net revenue +1.6% (BEIA basis), with net rev/hl +3.8% offsetting volume decline of 2.1%
    • AB InBev: revenue +2.0% reported; rev/hl +4.4%; total volume -2.3% — pure pricing/mix story
    • Kirin Holdings: revenue ¥2,433bn, +4.1% YoY; operating profit +19.3% — strongest top-to-bottom conversion in the peer group
    • Asahi (9-month basis): revenue +0.6% YoY — weakest organic growth in the group, compounded by cyberattack
    • Chongqing Brewery: revenue +0.53%; volumes +0.68% — slightly outperforming Chinese market (sector -1.1%)
  • Q1 2026 Carlsberg — the most recent data: Organic volume +2.8%, organic revenue +3.6%, reported revenue +3.0% (DKK 20.7bn). All three regions in growth. This beats analyst estimates of 1.4% volume and 2.8% revenue — a meaningful positive surprise.
  • Regional breakdown (Carlsberg Q1 2026): Western Europe organic volume +1.2%; Asia +3.4%; CEEI (Central & Eastern Europe & India) +4.6%. Organic rev/hl +1% across all regions. CEEI is the standout performer by volume; Asia is the recovery story.
  • Pricing power signals: Carlsberg organic rev/hl grew +1.4% in FY2025 and +1% in Q1 2026. The trade-off profile differs from peers: AB InBev and Heineken sacrificed more volume for more pricing; Carlsberg managed to grow both volume (+2.8%) and rev/hl (+1%) simultaneously in Q1 2026. This is the most constructive combination in the peer set.
  • Volume and market share signals: Carlsberg's +2.8% Q1 2026 organic volume is the only confirmed positive volume print in the peer group for recent periods (Heineken -2.1% full year; AB InBev -2.3% full year; Asahi impaired). This is a differentiating signal, though Q1 seasonality should temper confidence.
  • Strategic category performance (Q1 2026): Premium beer +3%; soft drinks +10%; alcohol-free brews +7%; Beyond Beer -2%. International brands: Carlsberg brand +10%, Tuborg +4%, 1664 Blanc +2%. The +10% Carlsberg brand volume in Q1 2026 is exceptional — brand momentum is real.
  • Chongqing Brewery read-through: Revenue grew +0.53% in a -1.1% Chinese market — modest outperformance. Net profit +10.43% implies significant operating leverage on flat volumes, consistent with price mix improvement and cost efficiency. For Carlsberg's Asia segment, this is a credible positive signal on China business quality even if headline growth is subdued.
  • Britvic as a revenue complication: The DKK 15.6bn Britvic revenue in FY2025 (from 16 Jan 2025) inflates reported revenue by ~21 percentage points. Organic revenue growth excluding Britvic is in the 4-5% range. The risk: analysts over-anchor to reported 18.8% and over-extrapolate; the clean organic number is the right baseline and it is now fully in the base.
  • Sceptical / bearish interpretation: Carlsberg's organic growth rate of 4-5% in FY2025 was supported by price/mix on largely flat volumes. Q1 2026 volume growth (+2.8%) is encouraging but is one quarter's data; Western European consumer confidence remains subdued and CEO commentary explicitly flagged uncertainty. If volume growth stalls in Q2-Q3 2026 and pricing room diminishes as input cost pressures abate, the organic growth rate could fade back toward 2-3%.
  • Peer forward guidance convergence: All three major peers (Carlsberg, Heineken, AB InBev) guided to 2-6% organic operating profit growth for 2026. Carlsberg's Q1 beat provides the earliest empirical read that it can reach the mid-to-upper end of that range — but it is one data point.

Among the comparable European brewers, Carlsberg is now ahead of Heineken on the margin level and closing the gap on expansion trajectory.

  • Carlsberg's 50bp margin expansion is best-in-class among European beer peers, surpassing Heineken's 41bp expansion and achieved on top of meaningful M&A integration complexity. It represents roughly DKK 445m of incremental operating profit on the base, primarily from synergies, operational leverage, and mix improvement.
  • Britvic synergy decomposition: ~30% of GBP 110m in synergies (~GBP 33m / ~DKK 300m) was delivered in FY2025, with significant acceleration in H2. For 2026, management guided to 30-40% incremental synergy realisation — meaning cumulative 60-70% through 2026. The synergy programme is a multi-year margin expansion engine that competitors cannot replicate.
  • Organic Carlsberg margin (ex-Britvic): Approximately DKK 11.8bn operating profit on ~DKK 73.5bn revenue = ~16.1% ex-Britvic. The legacy beer margin is healthy but Britvic is a dilutive mix element currently. As synergies flow through, Britvic will become more accretive. The margin trajectory is structurally positive but synergy-dependent.
  • Operating leverage analysis: Carlsberg's organic EBIT grew 5% while organic revenues grew ~4-5% — broadly breakeven operating leverage on the pure beer side. The big leverage is coming through from Britvic, not legacy beer. The margin expansion story is synergy-dependent, not structurally driven by high fixed-cost leverage in the base business. This matters for sustainability assessment.
  • Heineken margin context: +41bp to 15.2% on 1.6% organic revenue growth is a solid result but was achieved partly through gross savings of >EUR 500m. Gross savings programmes have diminishing returns and Heineken does not have a Britvic-equivalent structural catalyst for margin expansion beyond its existing cost programme.
  • AB InBev margin context: 101bp EBITDA margin expansion to 35.8% on -2.3% volume is exceptional. However, this is a structural architecture difference — AB InBev's EBITDA margin sits at a level that limits incremental expansion. Leverage (2.87x net debt/EBITDA) remains above target (2.0x) and constrains capital deployment.
  • Asahi margin deterioration: 9-month core operating profit -4.6% against flat revenue growth is a meaningful margin compression signal. The cyberattack was the primary cause in Japan, but European underperformance was also cited. This is multi-factor headwind, not a one-off.
  • Kirin margin surprise: Normalised operating profit +19.3% on revenue growth of 4.1% implies dramatic operating leverage — roughly 4-5x revenue growth rate translating to EBIT. Driven by Japan beer growth and cost discipline. However, FY2026 guidance calls for -6.7% normalised OP — this was a catch-up year.
  • Sceptical / bearish interpretation: The underlying organic margin expansion (ex-Britvic M&A effect) may be more modest. If Britvic synergies underwhelm or the beverage business faces volume pressure in the UK consumer environment, the margin story could disappoint. Persistent wage inflation in the UK is a structural headwind that synergy savings may not fully offset. The 50bp is real but it needs Britvic to work.

SECTION 7 — EPS Quality & Accounting

Rating: YELLOW

  • Summary judgement: Carlsberg's earnings quality is adequate but not exceptional. The Britvic acquisition introduces complexity — stepped-up assets, integration charges, and non-recurring items that can obscure the underlying cash conversion trend. Management's use of the MPM (Management Performance Measure) adjusted earnings metric is necessary but requires scrutiny, as it excludes a range of real economic costs.
  • Cash conversion and operating cash flow: Free cash flow data was not separately disclosed in Q1 2026 or the preliminary FY2025 results release. The FY2025 DKK 2.2bn Britvic EBIT contribution is broadly credible (Britvic was a ~GBP 350m EBITDA business pre-acquisition), implying cash conversion should be strong. However, integration capex and working capital absorption from Britvic could suppress reported FCF in 2025-2026 vs a steady-state basis.
  • One-off items and adjustments (FY2025): Carlsberg's MPM framework excludes: acquisition-related amortisation of intangibles, integration costs, restructuring charges, and hyperinflationary adjustments. The gap between reported and adjusted EBIT is material, likely DKK 1-2bn+. The 22.7% reported operating profit growth includes Britvic acquisition accounting step-ups.
  • Working capital trends: No detailed NWC breakdown available in Q1 2026. The Britvic acquisition brings a UK-focused FMCG business with typically tight NWC (soft drinks have rapid cash collection cycles) — a modest structural positive for group cash conversion.
  • Inventory and receivables: No unusual aging signals were disclosed by Carlsberg management in FY2025 or Q1 2026 commentary. Beer has shelf-life constraints that prevent inventory build, which is a natural quality check.
  • Comparison to peers on EPS quality: AB InBev's FCF of USD 11.3bn in FY2025 is exceptional cash conversion at ~53% of EBITDA. Heineken's gross savings programme flowing through to profit needs to be assessed against whether these are structural or timing-driven. Kirin's +¥197bn FCF swing was partly working capital improvement — a one-time benefit. For Carlsberg, the quality of earnings is adequate but the key DD question is: how much of the EPS uplift is real synergy vs accounting treatment?
  • Hyperinflationary markets: Post-Ukraine disposal (late 2022), this exposure has reduced significantly. India and some Central Asian markets carry residual currency risk. Modest quality discount relative to AB InBev's Americas-heavy base.
  • Sceptical / bearish interpretation: The MPM adjusted EPS of DKK 61 (+11.1%) excludes real costs — integration costs for Britvic, amortisation of acquired brands, and restructuring charges are real economic costs to shareholders. On a fully-loaded IFRS basis, EPS growth is likely significantly lower. Any thesis built on the +11.1% adj EPS figure should stress-test what the reported (non-adjusted) EPS trajectory looks like.

SECTION 8 — Sceptical Summary

Overall confidence in positive thesis: YELLOW-GREEN

  • Reverse-engineer the bear case: The bullish Carlsberg thesis rests on three pillars: (1) Britvic synergies delivering multi-year margin expansion, (2) Asia (particularly China) recovering and growing, (3) core beer business sustaining organic revenue growth of 4-5%. If any two of these pillars weaken simultaneously, the EPS trajectory — currently supporting 8-12% growth through 2026-2027 — could fade to 3-5%. At 20-25x P/E, the difference between 10% and 4% EPS growth is highly material to valuation.
  • The largest single risk is UK consumer demand deterioration. Britvic is a UK-centric soft drinks and beer business. The UK consumer environment in 2026 is under pressure (wage inflation lagging, cost-of-living residual effects). If Britvic's UK revenue misses expectations, the synergy narrative is intact but the growth narrative breaks.
  • Geopolitical risk was explicitly flagged by management. CEO commentary at Q1 2026 noted the Middle East conflict as a watchpoint — Carlsberg has no direct exposure but secondary demand effects (consumer confidence, energy prices) in European markets are real. Exposure to Central & Eastern Europe adds additional sensitivity.
  • Asahi's cyberattack — a competitive opportunity. Asahi is distracted and disrupted through at least Q2 2026. In European premium beer (where Peroni and Asahi Super Dry compete with 1664 Blanc and Carlsberg premium), this creates a shelf-space and attention opportunity. Asahi will recover — this is a temporary window, not a permanent advantage.
  • Volume versus pricing sustainability. Carlsberg's Q1 2026 +2.8% organic volume is a positive surprise, but the FY2025 base showed barely any volume growth. If pricing per hectolitre decelerates as commodity input cost pressures ease and supermarket negotiations shift back to retailers, volume needs to carry more of the revenue growth burden. Carlsberg has not yet demonstrated it can deliver both sustainably across multiple quarters.
  • Early warning signals absent but watchpoints live: No working capital deterioration visible; no inventory aging concerns; no guidance downgrade; no division underperformance announced. Key watchpoints for Q2 2026: (1) Western European organic volume (can Q1's +1.2% be sustained?), (2) Britvic synergy realisation update at H1 2026, (3) Asia volume confirmation — can +3.4% Q1 be maintained?
  • Relative peer risk: Heineken's weakness creates a trap. Heineken underperforming creates relative attractiveness for Carlsberg in a peer comparison. But both companies have the same guidance range (2-6% organic OP growth) and face similar macro environments. The risk is that consensus over-rates Carlsberg's differentiation relative to Heineken and under-rates the structural difficulty of consistent outperformance.
  • China concentration risk at Chongqing. Chongqing Brewery's revenue grew only +0.5% in 2025 in a market where total beer output fell 1.1%. The resilience is notable but the growth ceiling is real — China's beer market is mature, volumes declining, and premium pricing is absorbing a diminishing cohort of willing premium consumers. Slow-burn drag, not a near-term crisis.
  • Currency and commodity headwinds are latent. The DKK/GBP relationship is relevant given Britvic's UK cost base; the DKK/Eastern European currency basket matters for CEEI. Input cost pressures (aluminium, barley, glass) are currently benign but cyclically variable. No specific 2026 commodity hedging detail was provided in Q1 trading statement.
  • Overall confidence calibration: The positive thesis is plausible, grounded in real data (Q1 2026 beat, Britvic synergy ahead of plan, Asia recovery), and supported by a peer group where Carlsberg is tracking second-best on most metrics. The principal risk is execution — Britvic integration complexity, UK demand, and the width of the guidance range all counsel against high conviction at this stage of the analytical process. This is appropriate for a triaging note: the stock passes the initial screen and warrants deeper DD investigation.
