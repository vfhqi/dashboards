KEY FINDINGS

1. Reported growth masks genuine recovery. FY2025 revenue -4.5% reported but +7.3% constant-currency. CHF appreciation is the dominant distortion, not demand destruction. The PCT segment recovered from CHF 193m trough toward CHF 247m, a real cyclical upturn that reported numbers obscure.

2. AEIS is the clearest evidence of COMT's underperformance being partly structural, not purely cyclical. Same WFE end-market, same RF power exposure, yet AEIS delivered +21% revenue growth FY2025 with expanding EBITDA margins. The divergence is too large to attribute to FX alone. Market share and product competitiveness are live questions.

3. The H2 2025 collapse is the most concerning data point. H1 2025 was +20% YoY — tracking toward guidance. H2 fell sharply enough to miss the full-year guidance floor. This pattern is consistent with demand pull-forward by customers in H1, followed by inventory digestion in H2. It introduces serious doubt about the quality of the apparent recovery.

4. IXS is a value destroyer at current scale. EBITDA margin of -6.8% in FY2025 means the X-ray/industrial segment is consuming capital and management bandwidth. It is not a rounding error: it is dragging consolidated EBITDA margin below 11%. Until IXS returns to profitability, COMT's blended margin structure is impaired.

5. The Penang capex creates a near-term FCF and margin illusion. CHF 42.5m spent in FY2025 on Penang compressed FCF to CHF 8.5m (from CHF 41.4m). The investment is strategically rational as a USD-cost hedge, but it is front-loaded pain. FCF normalisation depends on Penang reaching productive utilisation — timeline unclear.

6. Management credibility is the key constraint on valuation re-rating. CMD Nov 2025 cut peak revenue target by ~CHF 130m at the midpoint (CHF 670-770m vs CHF 800-900m). FY2025 guidance missed its own floor. 2026 guidance is qualitative only. AEIS provides quantitative guidance and delivers against it. This credibility gap is a P/E multiple depressor.

7. Q1 2026 orders +22.3%, book-to-bill 1.4 — this is a genuine leading indicator. Order intake leads revenue by one to two quarters in this industry. If sustained, H1 2026 revenue should recover materially. The risk is that Q1 orders are front-loaded ahead of Section 232 tariff certainty and do not reflect underlying demand normalisation.

8. WFE sector tailwind is real but unevenly distributed. TSMC's $52-56bn capex plan and SEMI's +9% WFE forecast for 2026 are structurally positive. But COMT's PCT content per wafer start is smaller than large WFE peers, and AEIS appears better positioned to capture incremental RF power content at leading-edge nodes.

9. The risk budget for COMT at Triaging is not yet justified for full DD. The setup has identifiable bull and bear cases, a recognisable cyclical recovery pattern, and a depressed-but-improving order book. However, the AEIS divergence, the IXS drag, the credibility deficit, and the CHF structural headwind together constitute four unresolved questions that require SS-level work before committing research capital to DD.

2. REVENUE GROWTH & ORGANIC DYNAMICS

COMT FY2025 revenue: CHF 456.6m. Reported -4.5% YoY. Constant-currency +7.3%. The CHF appreciated approximately 12-14% against USD in calendar year 2025, which accounts for essentially all of the reported decline and then some.

The constant-currency figure is analytically important. It tells us that underlying volumes and prices in local currency grew. But this does not make the CHF headwind less real. COMT earns approximately 60% of revenue in USD/EUR but incurs the majority of its cost base in CHF. This structural mismatch compounds over time if CHF appreciation persists.

AEIS reported +21% FY2025 revenue growth in USD terms. AEIS has a similar RF power WFE end-market exposure. The divergence versus COMT reported growth (-4.5%) is approximately 25 percentage points. Even adjusting COMT to constant-currency (+7.3%), the divergence is still 14 percentage points. This requires explanation. Three hypotheses: (1) AEIS has stronger market share in leading-edge WFE RF power applications; (2) AEIS has newer product cycles with higher content per tool; (3) COMT's H2 collapse was company-specific demand timing, not a secular share loss. The data does not yet definitively favour any single hypothesis.

The PCT revenue history is instructive. Peak: CHF 381m. Trough: CHF 193m. FY2025: CHF 247m. This is a partial recovery of approximately 28% from trough, reaching roughly 65% of the prior peak. If the WFE cycle completes its recovery, PCT at CHF 300m+ is plausible on a 12-18 month horizon. But the CMD Nov 2025 cut of peak group revenue target to CHF 670-770m (from CHF 800-900m) implies that management no longer believes PCT alone can drive the original ambitious target.

The H1/H2 2025 split requires direct attention. H1 revenue was +20% YoY — strong, ahead of group guidance at that point. H2 collapsed. The most benign explanation is customer order timing: WFE customers in Asia-Pacific periodically pull forward orders to secure supply, then destabilise subsequent period revenue. A less benign explanation is that the H1 recovery was partially driven by temporary demand (one-time tool refreshes, restocking) that did not compound. The truth matters for forecasting 2026 revenue cadence.

Q1 2026: Revenue -4.5% YoY reported. But the order book tells a different story. Orders +22.3% YoY. Book-to-bill 1.4. In this sector, a book-to-bill above 1.2 is a leading indicator of revenue inflection within 1-2 quarters. The -4.5% Q1 revenue decline is a lagging measure of H2 2025 weakness flowing through backlog. The order picture is forward-looking and materially more positive.

Section 232 tariffs (effective January 2026) affect US-imported components. COMT manufactures in Switzerland and Penang. The company has not quantified the revenue headwind definitively. The Penang routing as mitigation depends on Malaysian origin classification.

Pricing power: there is limited direct evidence that COMT has been able to pass through CHF appreciation systematically. If RF power customers (semiconductor OEMs like AMAT, Lam Research, TEL) face COMT price increases, they have an alternative in AEIS. This competitive dynamic constrains COMT's pricing power on its RF power products more than on its PCT proprietary platforms where switching costs are higher.

The 2026 guidance is qualitative: "significantly exceed 2025." Translating this to numbers: CHF 457m x 1.15-1.25 would imply CHF 525-570m. That is a plausible range consistent with the order book. But the absence of quantitative guidance — particularly after the FY2025 guidance miss — means sell-side must model without anchoring from the company. AEIS provides numeric guidance and has delivered against it. This asymmetry affects investor trust.

WFE sector context supports the recovery thesis. TSMC has guided $52-56bn capex for 2026, up materially. SEMI forecasts WFE +9% for 2026. These numbers create genuine demand pull for RF power systems, plasma power management, and advanced process control. COMT is not being left out of this cycle — the Q1 order intake confirms participation. The question is participation rate relative to peers.

Revenue section rating: Orange. Real recovery is underway in constant-currency and order terms. But reported growth is negative, the H2 collapse is unexplained, and AEIS divergence is too large to dismiss.

3. MARGIN PROFILE & EVOLUTION

COMT FY2025 EBITDA margin: 10.1%. This is a significant deterioration from what should be a mid-cycle level for an industrial technology company. For context, COMT's own CMD has implied peak margins above 20% at CHF 670-770m revenue. At CHF 457m, 10.1% reflects both volume underutilisation and the IXS division drag.

IXS EBITDA margin of -6.8% is the most acute problem. A negative EBITDA margin means the division is cash-negative at the operating level before capex. The X-ray inspection systems business was intended to be a higher-margin, more stable industrial segment diversifying COMT away from semiconductor cyclicality. Instead, it is amplifying downside in a cyclical trough. The core question: does IXS have a credible path to profitability, or is it a strategic mistake?

The gross margin trajectory relative to peers is a critical unknown requiring SS data. COMT's cost base is largely fixed (Swiss manufacturing labour, R&D in CHF). Below a revenue threshold — likely somewhere around CHF 380-400m for the PCT division — fixed cost absorption deteriorates rapidly and EBITDA margin collapses. The FY2025 experience (CHF 457m, 10.1% EBITDA margin) suggests that threshold is higher than management previously modelled.

AEIS margin is expanding. The specific numbers require confirmation, but AEIS has consistently demonstrated that RF power for WFE has 20%+ EBITDA margins at scale, and that those margins expand as revenue grows due to operating leverage. COMT at 10.1% versus AEIS at an estimated 20%+ is not a cyclical gap alone. It is a structural gap driven by: (a) higher CHF cost base per unit revenue, (b) IXS losses, (c) Penang ramp costs, and (d) potentially higher R&D spend as a percentage of sales.

The Penang facility is a CHF 42.5m capex commitment in FY2025. This investment is strategically sound: Malaysian manufacturing shifts a portion of COMT's cost base into USD/MYR, providing a natural hedge against CHF appreciation on the cost side. But the facility was still ramping in 2025, meaning fixed costs are being incurred without full revenue conversion. Once Penang reaches designed utilisation — likely 2026-2027 — the margin impact should be meaningfully positive.

FCF margin in FY2025: CHF 8.5m on CHF 457m revenue = 1.9%. This compares to CHF 41.4m FCF in FY2024. The collapse is entirely explained by Penang capex (CHF 42.5m). Normalised maintenance capex FCF margin would be closer to 10-11%.

CMD Nov 2025 margin targets: the company has implied EBITDA margins of 20-22% at peak revenue of CHF 670-770m. At FY2025 CHF 457m revenue and CHF 46.3m EBITDA, the incremental margin required is approximately 50-60 cents of EBITDA per additional CHF of revenue. That is a plausible operating leverage ratio for this type of business if the fixed cost base is genuinely fixed and Penang runs efficiently.

Section 232 tariffs affect margin through two channels: (1) increased cost of US-sourced components, if any; (2) price pressure from US-based competitors (like AEIS) who have no tariff exposure. If AEIS can offer comparable RF power products at a lower all-in cost to US OEMs because it has no import tariff exposure, it has a structural margin advantage in that customer segment. This is a genuine competitive risk.

Margin section rating: Red/Orange. IXS losses, CHF cost base, and Penang ramp costs create a margin structure that is materially below peers and below COMT's own history. The recovery path is identifiable but dependent on multiple simultaneous improvements.

4. EPS TRAJECTORY & EARNINGS DELIVERY

FY2025 net income: CHF 12.2m. FY2024 net income: approximately CHF 35m (implied by -65% YoY decline). This is a severe earnings decline on an already-depressed base. The PCT segment, which drives the majority of COMT's profitability, was recovering in H1 and then fell back in H2. IXS losses compounded the damage.

The dividend cut from CHF 1.50 to CHF 0.50 (-67%) is a clear signal. Swiss industrial companies rarely cut dividends unless the board believes the earnings shortfall is not transient. At CHF 0.50, the payout ratio on CHF 12.2m net income is approximately 16%, which is reasonable but implies either the board is being conservative about future earnings or that the board looked at FCF of CHF 8.5m and concluded it could not sustain CHF 1.50 at current FCF conversion.

COTN-CH versus AEIS on EPS: the divergence mirrors the revenue and margin divergence but is amplified. AEIS with +21% revenue growth and expanding margins is likely delivering 30-40%+ EPS growth in FY2025. COMT delivered -65% EPS growth. The gap is extraordinary for two companies in nominally the same WFE RF power market.

Recovery setup and operating leverage: the PCT revenue peak was CHF 381m. EPS section rating: Orange. The earnings collapse is real and severe. The recovery path exists and is mathematically coherent. The key uncertainties are IXS path to profitability, Penang ramp timing, and whether AEIS is taking share from COMT in the RF power market permanently or cyclically.

Comparison to AEIS: AEIS provides specific quarterly guidance and annual frameworks. It has delivered against these targets in the current WFE recovery. The guidance culture at AEIS is a meaningful investor confidence input. At COMT, the contrast is stark.

Guidance/credibility section rating: Red. Two consecutive major guidance events (CMD downgrade + FY2025 miss) combined with qualitative-only 2026 guidance create a credibility deficit that will persist until COMT demonstrates delivery against a target.

7. WFE SECTOR DYNAMICS & COMT POSITIONING

The WFE cycle is in a recovery phase. TSMC's $52-56bn capex guidance for 2026 is the clearest signal. This is not incremental capex from a single node transition — it reflects AI accelerator demand, advanced packaging, and continued N3/N2 ramp. For RF power suppliers, TSMC tool orders translate into RF power demand within 6-12 months as tools are installed and qualified.

SEMI's WFE forecast of +9% for 2026 is a sector aggregate. It includes memory recovery (which is RF-intensive at EUV and dry etch steps), logic/foundry (TSMC-driven), and specialty nodes. The broad-based nature of the recovery is a positive for COMT — it is not a single-customer story.

COMT's position in WFE enabling: the PCT segment generates RF/HF power for plasma etching and deposition systems, primarily as a sub-supplier to Tier 1 WFE OEMs (AMAT, Lam Research, TEL, KLA). The PCT business is therefore a second-order play on WFE capex: the OEM must win the tool order from a fab, then sub-order the RF power components from COMT or AEIS.

The Synertia RF platform is COMT's most current-generation PCT product. It targets higher-power, higher-frequency applications required for advanced etch and deposition at sub-5nm nodes. If Synertia is gaining design wins at OEMs, this is a medium-term revenue driver with attractive margin characteristics.

Is this company-specific or sector-wide underperformance? The evidence points to a mixture. Sector-wide: the WFE cycle trough affected all sub-suppliers in 2023-2024. Company-specific: the H2 2025 collapse and the 14-point constant-currency gap versus AEIS in FY2025 is not fully explained by sector dynamics. COMT appears to have specific product cycle issues (IXS losses, possibly slower Synertia adoption) and structural cost issues (CHF base) that are independent of the sector.

China WFE dynamics: US export controls restrict advanced semiconductor equipment shipments to China. COMT's Penang manufacturing location is relevant: Malaysia-manufactured products face different origin classification than Swiss-manufactured products, which may affect their eligibility for Chinese customer supply.

AI semiconductor tailwind: how much reaches COMT? The answer is through TSMC's leading-edge foundry ramps (N2/N3 for Apple, NVIDIA, AMD chips). Both drive etch and deposition tool orders, which drive RF power demand. COMT participates. The question is whether COMT's market share in the AI-driven capex wave is growing, stable, or declining relative to AEIS.

Sector tailwind rating: Yellow-Green. The WFE tailwind is real and visible. COMT is positioned to benefit. But the benefit is partially offset by export controls, FX, and potential market share dynamics versus AEIS.

8. CURRENCY & STRUCTURAL HEADWINDS

CHF appreciation is the single most persistent structural headwind facing COMT. Approximately 60% of COMT's revenue is denominated in USD and EUR. Costs are predominantly in CHF (Swiss R&D, Swiss headquarters, Swiss manufacturing workforce). When the CHF strengthens, reported revenue in CHF terms falls, but the cost base does not adjust proportionally.

In FY2025, the CHF/USD rate movement explains the entirety of the difference between -4.5% reported revenue growth and +7.3% constant-currency growth. Numerically: CHF 457m reported on a +7.3% constant-currency basis implies constant-currency revenue of approximately CHF 510-520m. The FX drag was approximately CHF 53-63m, or around 11-12% of revenue translated into a margin headwind. This is a substantial annual effect that recurs as long as CHF stays elevated.

AEIS comparison: AEIS operates predominantly in USD. Its cost base and revenue base are both USD-denominated. It has no CHF structural risk. When WFE revenue grows in USD terms, AEIS captures it fully. When COMT's WFE revenue grows in USD/EUR terms, a significant portion is translated away into CHF strength. This systematic disadvantage means COMT must grow faster in volume/local currency terms than AEIS just to report the same CHF growth rate.

Penang manufacturing as FX hedge: the logic is sound. If COMT manufactures in Penang (Malaysian Ringgit and USD cost environment), those production costs are USD-adjacent rather than CHF. As Penang scales to full utilisation, a growing share of COMT's variable cost base will be in currencies that align with its revenue. But the transition is not instantaneous.

Section 232 tariffs (US imports, effective January 2026): COMT ships products from Switzerland and Penang to US-based WFE OEMs. Swiss-origin goods imported into the US may face tariff exposure. The impact is threefold: (1) direct tariff cost increase on Swiss-origin shipments to the US; (2) competitive disadvantage versus AEIS (US-domestic, no tariff exposure); (3) potential for OEM customers to qualify AEIS as an alternative supplier to avoid tariff complications.

The combined FX + tariff headwind creates a structural margin gap versus US-domiciled peers that is not easily closed by operational improvement alone.

Currency/structural headwinds rating: Red. CHF appreciation is persistent, structural, and large (11-12% revenue impact in FY2025). Section 232 adds a new layer of uncertainty. Penang is the right strategic response but is a 2-3 year execution project, not a 2026 fix.

Is COMT gaining or losing versus peers?

On reported metrics, COMT is losing. Revenue growth, margin trajectory, EPS direction, and guidance credibility all rank last in the peer group for FY2025. On a constant-currency basis and on forward order indicators (book-to-bill 1.4, orders +22.3%), the gap narrows. But a company that requires constant-currency adjustment and forward-looking order data to look competitive is, by definition, not currently outperforming.

Company-specific underperformance vs sector-wide headwind?

Both are present. The WFE cycle trough was sector-wide and explains a portion of COMT's FY2025 weakness. But the magnitude of COMT's underperformance versus AEIS (14pp gap even on constant-currency basis) and the specific IXS losses are company-specific.
