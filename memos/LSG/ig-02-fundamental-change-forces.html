Lerøy Seafood Group: Change Forces Analysis Brief company description

Lerøy Seafood Group ASA is a global seafood corporation headquartered in Bergen, Norway, functioning as a fully vertically integrated supplier of both red fish and whitefish.

The company processes between 350,000 and 400,000 tonnes of seafood annually, which translates to approximately 5 million meals served every day across the globe.

Lerøy's operational structure is divided into three primary business segments: Farming, Wild Catch, and Value-Added Processing, Sales and Distribution (VAPS&D).

The Farming segment produces finfish, primarily Atlantic salmon and trout, across three distinct regions in Norway: Lerøy Aurora in the North, Lerøy Midt in Central Norway, and Lerøy Sjøtroll in the West.

In addition to its domestic Norwegian farming operations, Lerøy holds a 50% ownership stake in Scottish Sea Farms Ltd., which provides exposure to salmon farming in Scotland.

The Wild Catch segment operates through Lerøy Havfisk and Lerøy Norway Seafoods.

Lerøy Havfisk owns and operates 10 trawlers that hold licenses to fish approximately 8% to 9% of the total Norwegian cod quotas north of 62 degrees latitude.

These wild catch licenses include an operational obligation tied to Lerøy Norway Seafoods, which operates 10 processing plants and purchasing stations in Norway to process the wild-caught whitefish.

The VAPS&D segment represents the downstream engine of Lerøy's integrated value chain, managing the processing, sales, and distribution of the company's own products alongside significant third-party seafood.

This downstream division employs approximately 2,600 to 3,000 people and maintains processing and sales operations across 18 countries globally.

Geographically, Lerøy's revenue is heavily concentrated in Europe, which accounted for approximately 75% of sales in 2025, followed by the Asia/Pacific region at 18%, and the Americas at 6%.

The company has actively expanded its presence in emerging markets over the last three years, opening new branch offices in South Korea, Vietnam, and Thailand to drive category innovation and build local demand for Norwegian trout and salmon.

Financially, Lerøy achieved record revenue of NOK 34.4 billion in 2025, up from NOK 31.1 billion in 2024 and NOK 30.9 billion in 2023.

The business model is highly integrated to capture margin across the value chain, with the VAPS&D segment successfully surpassing its 2022 strategic target by generating an operational EBIT of NOK 1,290 million in 2025.

Looking toward the next three years (N3Y), Lerøy's corporate strategy is explicitly geared toward achieving NOK 50 billion in total revenue by 2030.

To support this future growth, the company is targeting 220,000 gutted weight tonnes (GWT) of farming harvest in Norway by 2030, utilizing only its existing license capacity.

The company is also targeting NOK 2 billion in EBIT from the VAPS&D segment by 2030, driven by a projected 25% increase in sold volumes and deeper market penetration.

Norne Securities highlights that Lerøy's recent measures in genetics, smolt production, and sea-based technology are driving significant biological improvements, creating a strong foundation for earnings growth in 2025 and beyond.

Industry experts emphasize that Lerøy deliberately differentiates its business model by focusing on value-added products and strategic partnerships, rather than simply pushing whole fish into the global spot market.

Value chain — Demand

Global demand for Atlantic salmon has demonstrated remarkable resilience, expanding at an estimated 9.5% compound annual growth rate (CAGR) between 2012 and 2024.

The consumer landscape is undergoing a structural shift toward health and sustainability, with buyers actively seeking low-carbon protein alternatives.

DNB Carnegie analysts observe that the global rise in chronic health issues is acting as a secular tailwind for salmon, given its high Omega-3 fatty acid content.

Lerøy's customer purchasing data indicates a pronounced pivot away from traditional whole fresh fish toward highly processed, convenience-oriented value-added products.

To capitalize on this sentiment shift, Lerøy is heavily expanding its downstream assortment to include ready-to-eat meals, poke bowls, sushi, and modified atmosphere packaging (MAP) portions.

The 2024 and 2025 fiscal years were characterized by severe salmon spot price deflation, driven by an unexpectedly massive surge in Norwegian biological harvest volumes.

During the third quarter of 2025 alone, global consumption growth for salmon spiked by approximately 11% year-over-year as retail channels utilized the lower prices to run aggressive promotional campaigns.

DNB Carnegie highlights that this prolonged period of excess supply and low spot prices has effectively created new, "sticky" long-term demand by increasing the frequency of household salmon consumption.

Moving into 2026, the supply-demand balance is projected to aggressively tighten, with global supply growth stalling to an estimated 1.1% and Norwegian output actually contracting by 1.9%.

DNB Carnegie projects that this sudden supply U-turn in 2026 will collide with the newly established retail demand base, potentially driving a 30% year-over-year demand shock that will push spot prices significantly higher.

Contract pricing dynamics act as a critical shock absorber for Lerøy during these volatile spot cycles.

Throughout 2025, the company maintained a contract share hovering between 24% and 30%, which provided vital margin protection when spot prices temporarily fell below the cost of production.

Conversely, in the Wild Catch segment, severe quota reductions have handed Lerøy massive upstream pricing power.

During the fourth quarter of 2025, the extreme scarcity of whitefish drove realized prices for cod, haddock, and saithe up by 30%, 47%, and 22% respectively compared to the prior year.

Lerøy's routes to market are evolving, with a deliberate corporate strategy to disintermediate traditional spot-market wholesalers in favor of direct, long-term B2B partnerships.

The company has set a hard target to increase the share of revenue derived directly from strategic retail and foodservice clients from approximately 60% in 2025 to 70% by 2030.

Geographically, Europe remains the dominant demand engine, absorbing 75% of Lerøy's total sales in 2025 and compounding at an 11% annual growth rate since 2020.

However, the Asia/Pacific region is exhibiting the most aggressive category expansion, driven by the rising popularity of Japanese-style cuisine across the continent.

To capture this Asian demand, Lerøy has abandoned passive export models and opened direct physical branch offices in South Korea, Vietnam, and Thailand to actively build local market share.

DNB Carnegie points out that China has now surpassed the US as a structural growth market for Norwegian salmon, with Chinese import volumes surging 38% year-over-year in late 2025.

Taking a probing view on hidden demand slowdowns, there are clear cracks appearing in the US consumer market.

DNB Carnegie reports that Norwegian Atlantic salmon export values to the US plummeted by 37% and 32% year-over-year in recent months, severely impacted by a weaker USD and intense competition from South American producers.

Furthermore, DNB Carnegie warns that the looming threat of US import tariffs, which could theoretically reach 40% for Norwegian salmon, represents a massive tail-risk that could aggressively destroy end-user demand and force a radical rerouting of global trade flows.

Internally, Lerøy's management has quietly conceded that persistent general economic inflation and high absolute price points negatively affected demand in certain European consumer segments throughout 2023 and 2024.

Value chain — Competition

The global Atlantic salmon farming sector is a highly consolidated oligopoly, heavily dominated by a few massive operators like Mowi, SalMar, Cermaq, and Lerøy Seafood Group.

DNB Carnegie analysts note that the industry is characterised by limited supply growth from traditional farming regions, which structurally supports high baseline prices and intense margin competition among incumbents.

Traditional sea-based aquaculture is protected by virtually impenetrable barriers to entry, primarily driven by the extreme scarcity and exorbitant cost of government-issued farming licenses.

In Norway, production capacity is rigidly governed by the Traffic Light System, which restricts or expands a farmer's Maximum Allowed Biomass (MAB) across 13 production areas based on environmental indicators like sea lice impact.

The Norwegian government's recent "Havbruksmeldingen" white paper proposes a radical regulatory disruption that would replace the MAB framework with a strict quota system based entirely on sea lice emissions.

DNB Carnegie analysts argue that this proposed regulatory shift will heavily favour Lerøy, as the company's pre-emptive investments in submersible and closed-containment technologies structurally reduce its sea lice emissions compared to traditional peers.

SalMar is traditionally viewed as the highest-quality operator and the sector's cost-leader due to its prime geographical locations in Central Norway.

However, DNB Carnegie observes that SalMar recently lost some of its cost advantage and suffered earnings hits in 2024 and 2025 due to severe biological challenges, including string jellyfish and elevated sea lice pressure.

Mowi leverages its position as the world's largest salmon producer to maintain a fiercely competitive, fully integrated value chain that spans from internal genetics to proprietary feed manufacturing and massive downstream processing.

Bakkafrost defends a unique competitive moat in the Faroe Islands by producing exceptionally large, superior-quality salmon that commands premium market pricing.

To further insulate its margins, Bakkafrost operates the most vertically integrated supply chain in the industry, producing its own fishmeal and fish oil to buffer against volatile global commodity feed prices.

Grieg Seafood operates as a mid-sized competitor that is heavily reliant on massive investments in post-smolt facilities in Rogaland to shorten the time its fish spend in the ocean and improve biological survival rates.

As traditional oceanic supply growth stalls, land-based farming utilizing Recirculating Aquaculture Systems (RAS) and flow-through technologies has emerged as the most credible disruptive threat to incumbent market share.

Norne Securities points out that land-based pioneers like Salmon Evolution are proving the commercial viability of these systems, operating fully compliant facilities that bypass the severe regulatory constraints choking sea-based farmers.

Gigante Salmon is another disruptive new entrant utilizing land-based flow-through technology to completely eliminate sea lice and escape risks while targeting industry-low capital expenditure metrics.

These land-based competitors are actively disintermediating the traditional geographic supply chain by building massive farming facilities directly adjacent to major global end-markets.

DNB Carnegie highlights Nordic Aqua Partners as a prime example, as it farms salmon locally in China to bypass the massive air freight and logistics costs that hamstring traditional Norwegian exporters.

Offshore aquaculture represents another potential frontier for disruptive capacity expansion, but the competitive landscape has frozen due to the unpredictable nature of the Norwegian regulatory and tax regime.

SalMar successfully harvested its first offshore production cycles but has completely halted further deep-water investments until the Norwegian government provides stable, long-term framework conditions.

In terms of product substitutes, farmed salmon competes favorably against terrestrial proteins like beef and pork due to its vastly superior feed conversion ratio and significantly lower greenhouse gas footprint.

Alternative plant-based proteins and mycoprotein substitutes (such as Quorn) are aggressively expanding their product mix to capture health-conscious consumers.

However, these alternative protein competitors are currently struggling with intense consumer price sensitivity driven by prolonged food inflation in key markets like the UK.

To protect against disintermediation, traditional salmon farmers are increasingly bypassing spot-market wholesalers to build direct, highly integrated B2B partnerships with massive global retail chains.

Morgan Stanley analysts observe that the broader global fisheries industry is experiencing a surge in M&A activity as companies scramble to secure finite aquaculture resources and consolidate downstream sales channels.

Lerøy's VAPS&D segment acts as a massive competitive shield, allowing the company to absorb upstream spot-price volatility while simultaneously capturing margin from the rising consumer demand for highly processed convenience meals.

Value chain — Disruption

The global salmon farming industry is undergoing a structural technological transition away from traditional nearshore open net pens toward land-based recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS), offshore platforms, and semi-closed containment.

This systemic pivot is heavily driven by intensifying biological pressures, restrictive environmental regulations, and the finite availability of ideal nearshore farming sites.

In Norway, the looming "Havbruksmeldingen" (Aquaculture White Paper) proposes a radical disruption to the traditional maximum allowed biomass (MAB) licensing regime by introducing a strict quota system based directly on sea lice emissions.

DNB Carnegie analysts argue that this specific regulatory disruption will structurally favour Lerøy Seafood Group, as the company has pre-emptively invested massive capital into new shielding technologies across its Sjøtroll and Midt regions.

To aggressively defend its competitive moat, Lerøy has deployed NOK 1.2 billion into advanced shielding technologies since 2023.

The company is actively scaling four distinct defensive technologies: submerged farming, semi-contained farming, laser delousing systems, and fully closed containment systems.

Early biological data from these disruptive systems is highly compelling, with Lerøy's submerged farming sites recording approximately 60% fewer sea lice treatments in 2025 compared to adjacent conventional sites.

Furthermore, Lerøy has committed to building three fully closed containment units scheduled for operation in the first quarter of 2027 to capitalize on the Norwegian government's proposed environmental flexibility scheme.

Industry experts view land-based RAS as the ultimate disruptor because it completely removes fish from severe ocean climates, eliminating biological threats and allowing for high-density production with a smaller geographical footprint.

Land-based pioneers are aggressively disintermediating traditional trade routes by constructing commercial-scale facilities directly adjacent to major end-markets in Asia and North America.

Norne Securities highlights Nordic Aqua Partners as a prime example of this disruption, noting that the company achieved the best harvest weights in the land-based sector during the first quarter of 2026 at its facility in China.

This localized production trend is accelerating globally, evidenced by Pure Salmon Japan securing a USD 180 million preferred note investment from Fortress Investment Group to construct a 10,000-tonne RAS facility.

In a similar vein, Proximar Seafood has established operations at Mt. Fuji in Japan, utilizing AI-enabled camera monitoring to track biomass development and optimize feeding inside its grow-out facility.

HC Wainwright analysts note that the disruptive threat of RAS is heavily counterbalanced by extreme capital intensity and inflation risks.

The broker points out that AquaBounty Technologies was forced to pause construction on its 10,000-tonne Ohio facility after estimated CapEx skyrocketed past USD 450 million, ultimately leading to the fire sale of its operational assets.

Seeking to mitigate both the biological risks of the ocean and the extreme capital costs of pure RAS, a new cohort of hybrid flow-through competitors has emerged in Norway.

Norne Securities observes that Salmon Evolution, utilizing a hybrid flow-through model, is already fully compliant with newly proposed land-based regulations and operates as an industry pioneer without major biological hiccups.

The same broker notes that competitors like Gigante Salmon and Andfjord Salmon are also advancing flow-through concepts that pump water from depths below the sea-lice belt, targeting ultra-low capital expenditures ranging from NOK 70 to NOK 115 per kilogram.

Beneath the heavy infrastructure changes, Silicon Valley technology firms are introducing cross-sectorial AI and machine learning to fundamentally disrupt how salmon are monitored and fed.

Companies like Aquabyte and Stingray are deploying advanced machine-vision algorithms inside open net pens to automate sea lice counting and biomass estimation.

As an intermediate defensive strategy against disruption, traditional incumbents like Lerøy and Grieg Seafood are aggressively expanding their land-based post-smolt capacities.

Rearing smolt to a much larger size in controlled freshwater facilities drastically shortens the required time fish must spend in the hazardous marine environment.

Disruption is also sweeping through the upstream feed value chain as companies seek sustainable alternatives to marine ingredients and deforestation-linked soy.

Morgan Stanley highlights the use of algal oil innovations, such as Veramaris, which provides the Omega-3 equivalent of 66 tonnes of wild forage fish, disrupting traditional reliance on finite pelagic fisheries.

Value chain — Political and regulatory

The Norwegian aquaculture industry is currently navigating an unprecedented level of political and regulatory disruption, fundamentally altering the operating conditions for major incumbents like Lerøy Seafood Group.

The most severe regulatory shock over the last three years was the implementation of the Norwegian resource rent tax on aquaculture, which took effect retroactively from January 1, 2023.

This tax imposes an additional 25% levy on income derived from the sea-phase production of salmon and trout.

Combined with the standard Norwegian corporate tax rate of 22%, Lerøy's eligible aquaculture operations are now subject to a massive total nominal tax rate of 47%.

Lerøy's management views this tax as highly damaging, publicly stating that it strips vital capital from the industry and impairs the sector's ability to drive global competitiveness and rural employment.

The complexity of the resource rent tax has created significant accounting volatility, particularly regarding the implementation effect on standing biomass.

In a bold legal maneuver, Lerøy actively changed its 2022 tax declarations for two of its subsidiaries to claim deductions for costs incurred to raise fish prior to the tax's implementation.

Supported by third-party legal assessments, the company believes there is a preponderance of probability that it will win a potential court case against the government on this issue.

Consequently, Lerøy reversed NOK 1.0 billion of the original NOK 1.7 billion deferred tax implementation effect in its Q4 2024 accounts, signaling its willingness to aggressively litigate against perceived unfair taxation.

A new regulatory paradigm was proposed on April 10, 2025, when the Norwegian government introduced the "Havbruksmeldingen" (Aquaculture White Paper).

This white paper suggests a radical departure from the traditional Maximum Allowed Biomass (MAB) restrictions, proposing instead a quota system based strictly on sea lice emissions.

Analysts at DNB Carnegie believe that if this sea-lice quota system is fully implemented, Lerøy will emerge as a net outperformer relative to peers.

DNB Carnegie notes this advantage stems from Lerøy's preemptive, heavy capital investments in submersible pens and closed-containment technologies across its Lerøy Sjøtroll and Lerøy Midt regions.

However, the political process remains highly volatile, and on June 12, 2025, a cross-party majority in Parliament decided to temporarily retain the existing traffic light system while further assessing the white paper's alternatives.

Under the current "traffic light system," coastal production areas are categorized by environmental impact, directly dictating whether farmers can expand or must contract their biomass.

Lerøy Sjøtroll operates in production areas 3 and 4, which were both designated as "red" zones in 2024 due to high sea lice pressure on wild salmon.

This red designation forced a mandatory 6% reduction in commercial license capacity in these regions, stripping Lerøy of approximately 2,500 tonnes of Maximum Allowed Biomass (MAB) from autumn 2024.

To counterbalance these punitive reductions, the Norwegian Government introduced an "Environmental flexibility scheme" designed to reward sustainable innovation.

Under this scheme, previously withdrawn license capacity can be reinstated if the farmer utilizes fully closed containment systems.

Lerøy is actively exploiting this regulatory loophole, having committed to building three fully closed containment units with the first fish deployments planned for Q1 2027.

Beyond farming, Lerøy faces severe regulatory headwinds in its upstream Wild Catch division, which is heavily dependent on government-issued fishing quotas.

Analysts at Norne Securities highlight that cod quotas have been drastically reduced over recent years, leading to a significantly weakened operating basis for Lerøy's whitefish activities.

For 2025, Lerøy Havfisk suffered a massive 32% reduction in its cod quota and a 2% cut to its haddock quota compared to the prior year.

The regulatory outlook for wild catch remains bleak in the near term, with 2026 indications pointing to further cuts of 16% for cod, 19% for North saithe, and 27% for South saithe.

These quota reductions are structurally tied to strict operational activity obligations, meaning Lerøy Havfisk must deliver its catches to specific land-based processing plants owned by Lerøy Norway Seafoods to maintain its licenses.

Internationally, Lerøy's value chain is threatened by shifting macroeconomic policies and the potential weaponization of trade tariffs.

There is a recognized political risk that the United States could impose heavy tariffs on Norwegian salmon, which would severely disrupt established global trade routes and depress European spot prices.

Additionally, the company is managing significant legal and compliance risks stemming from a European Commission investigation into alleged price-fixing in the spot market between 2011 and 2019.

DNB Carnegie analysts currently model a 50% probability that Lerøy will face a "worst-case" fine related to this EU probe, negatively impacting their valuation by NOK 2.6 per share.

Finally, the industry is subject to an escalating production fee, which for 2025 was set at NOK 0.965 per kilogram of salmon and trout harvested in Norway.

Revenue/demand cycle

Analysts at DNB Carnegie explicitly categorize Lerøy Seafood Group and its primary peers as operating in a "Not cyclical" industry, anchoring this view on the steady, secular growth of global protein demand.

Taking a probing view, this conventional sell-side narrative fundamentally misrepresents the extreme short-term revenue volatility inherent in the global salmon market.

Lerøy's executive management directly contradicts the non-cyclical broker narrative, explicitly warning investors in their recent annual report that the company operates in an industry characterized by "substantial historical cyclicality."

The salmon revenue cycle is generally not driven by traditional macroeconomic demand recessions, but rather by sudden biological and regulatory supply shocks that dictate violent shifts in spot pricing.

Analysts at Morgan Stanley observe that global fish prices experience sharp cyclical swings, often accelerating rapidly due to supply-side constraints before falling abruptly when demand cools or volumes unexpectedly surge.

Morgan Stanley notes that the precipitous rise in global fish prices triggered by the post-pandemic recovery peaked in 2022 and 2023, with the cycle softening materially throughout the subsequent two years.

The 2025 fiscal year represented a severe trough in the salmon spot price cycle, driven by an unexpectedly massive 10% to 11% surge in Norwegian harvest volumes.

This sudden supply glut temporarily crushed spot prices, with Lerøy reporting that benchmark prices in mid-2025 fell by approximately NOK 30.00 per kilogram compared to the same period in the prior year.

However, Lerøy's management views these cyclical price troughs as a necessary mechanism for long-term category expansion, arguing that periods of cheaper salmon successfully establish new, elevated baselines for global consumer demand.

Moving into 2026, the supply-demand cycle is projected to violently invert, with global supply growth plummeting to an estimated 1.10%.

Within Norway specifically, this supply cycle is turning negative, with total harvest volumes expected to actually contract by 1.90% in 2026.

Consequently, analysts at DNB Carnegie project a sharp cyclical rebound in spot prices, forecasting an average of EUR 7.30 (NOK 82.00) per kilogram in 2026.

DNB Carnegie expects this pricing cycle to peak in 2027 at EUR 7.90 (NOK 90.00) per kilogram, before experiencing a slight normalization to EUR 7.70 (NOK 88.00) per kilogram in 2028.

To combat this aggressive upstream spot price cyclicality, Lerøy utilizes a highly integrated business model designed specifically to reduce total corporate revenue volatility.

The downstream Value-Added Processing, Sales and Distribution (VAPS&D) segment acts as a highly effective counter-cyclical hedge to the upstream Farming division.

When the salmon spot price cycle crashes, as it did in 2025, the VAPS&D segment experiences massive margin expansion due to drastically cheaper raw material inputs and higher capacity utilization.

Furthermore, Lerøy actively smooths its revenue predictability by consistently locking approximately 25% to 30% of its total harvest volumes into fixed-price contracts.

Morgan Stanley points out that alternative seafood segments follow entirely different cyclical patterns; for instance, whitefish and surimi prices are currently rising due to compounding supply issues among US processing companies.

Historical track record of delivery

At its 2022 Capital Markets Day, Lerøy Seafood Group established highly ambitious, multi-year operational and financial targets aimed for completion by the end of 2025.

The most prominent of these strategic goals was the explicit mandate for the Value-Added Processing, Sales and Distribution (VAPS&D) segment to reach an operational EBIT of NOK 1.25 billion.

Lerøy successfully delivered on this ambitious downstream target, generating a record operational EBIT of NOK 1,290 million for the full 2025 financial year.

Management explicitly attributes this structural outperformance to internal operational improvements, expanded downstream capacity utilization, and successful category penetration across global end-markets.

Conversely, the company slightly missed its corresponding 2022 strategic goal to harvest 200,000 gutted weight tonnes (GWT) from its Norwegian farming operations by 2025.

Total Norwegian harvest volume ultimately landed at 195,555 GWT for the year.

Despite missing the long-term 200,000 GWT aspiration, it is notable that Lerøy executed perfectly against its formally revised short-term guidance, hitting the exact 195,000 GWT forecast communicated to the market in late 2024.

In terms of overall capital efficiency, the company's historical delivery has significantly underwhelmed its previous strategic ambitions.

Over the past three years, Lerøy's average Return on Capital Employed (ROCE) languished at approximately 11%.

This systemic underperformance against the prior 18% benchmark forced executive leadership to formally reduce the company's long-term ROCE target down to a more realistic 15% at the 2026 Capital Markets Day.

A primary external drag on this historical capital return profile has been the severe, politically mandated reduction in Barents Sea whitefish quotas.

Demonstrating the severity of this external shock, Lerøy Havfisk was forced to absorb a massive 32% year-over-year reduction in its cod quota in 2025.

This externally imposed volume collapse severely compressed margins within the land-based processing infrastructure, dragging the Wild Catch segment to a negative operational EBIT of NOK 29 million in the fourth quarter of 2025.

To proactively combat these external margin pressures, the organization has demonstrated a formidable track record of delivering internal productivity gains through the "Lerøy Way" business system.

The company aggressively scaled these internal continuous improvement initiatives, successfully documenting accumulated financial gains of NOK 400 million between 2021 and 2025.

On the biological front, Lerøy is successfully transitioning away from years of volatile performance by decisively executing its protective technology rollout.

Sites equipped with the company's newly deployed submerged farming technology recorded an impressive 60% reduction in necessary sea-lice treatments compared to conventional open-net pens in 2025.

DNB Carnegie analysts highlight that this successful technological deployment drove a massive earnings surprise, with Lerøy's fourth quarter 2025 operational EBIT beating consensus estimates by an impressive 15% to 20%.

DNB Carnegie observes that this operational momentum has clearly persisted, as an early trading update for the first quarter of 2026 revealed harvest volumes of 39.9 thousand tonnes, beating Bloomberg consensus expectations by 3%.

Despite the severe earnings volatility caused by low spot prices and unpredictable tax regimes, Lerøy has maintained a highly reliable track record of shareholder distributions.

DNB Carnegie notes that the Board's proposed dividend of NOK 2.50 per share for the 2025 financial year landed exactly in line with their estimates while significantly surpassing broader market consensus by 19%.

A notable blemish on the historical track record is the joint venture Scottish Sea Farms, which has consistently struggled to deliver predictable volume output.

The Scottish operations suffered severe, incident-based mortality related to Amoebic Gill Disease (AGD) in recent years, forcing management to heavily restructure site operations and accept subdued harvest volumes of just 16.4 thousand tonnes (Lerøy's 50% share) in 2025.
