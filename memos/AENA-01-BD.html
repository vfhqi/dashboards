Company Overview and Business Model

Aena is a four-segment global airport leader with a highly diversified revenue model balancing regulated aeronautical fees with higher-margin commercial, real estate, and international concession revenues, generating record €6.38bn consolidated revenue in 2025.

  • Revenue model: Four primary segments; Airports, Real Estate Services, AIRM concession, International shareholdings #I
    • Airports segment covers both aeronautical and commercial activities in Spain
    • By balancing heavily regulated aeronautical fees with higher-margin commercial, real estate, and international concession revenues, Aena has cultivated a highly diversified and resilient revenue model
  • Network scale: Oversees vast infrastructure assets spanning Europe and the Americas as the world's leading airport management company by passenger volume #I
  • Revenue record: €6.38bn total consolidated revenue in 2025, representing a 9.5% year-on-year increase #I

Aeronautical Activities and Passenger Traffic Trends

Post-pandemic traffic recovery has been extraordinarily robust, with an all-time record 384.8m passengers in 2025, driven by a 5.9% surge in international traffic that benefits Aena's revenue mix.

  • Domestic network: 46 airports and two heliports across Spain, plus five military air bases open to civil aviation #I
    • Includes major global hubs: Adolfo Suarez Madrid-Barajas and Josep Tarradellas Barcelona-El Prat
    • Heavily trafficked tourist gateways in the Balearic and Canary Islands
  • Revenue base: Aeronautical revenues amounted to €3.35bn in 2025, derived from regulated airport charges for airfield, terminal, and security infrastructure #I
  • Traffic record: 384.8m passengers globally in 2025, a 4.2% increase from previous year; all-time high #I
    • Spanish network accounted for 321.6m passengers
    • International traffic surged +5.9%, offsetting a slight -0.3% dip in domestic travel
  • Revenue quality: Point-to-point international traffic generally commands higher airport charges than domestic or connecting flights; mix shift is structurally positive #A
  • Forward guidance: Management anticipates period of stabilisation following aggressive post-COVID rebound; forecasts +1.3% pax growth for Spanish network in 2026 #I

Commercial Operations

The commercial segment functions as a critical growth engine, consistently outpacing passenger traffic growth through aggressive retail optimisation and premium service expansion, with VIP services demonstrating exceptional demand elasticity.

  • Growth dynamics: Commercial segment consistently outpaces passenger traffic growth through aggressive retail space optimisation and premium service expansion #J
  • 2025 performance: Commercial revenues reached €1.98bn, up 11.0% YoY, pushing commercial revenue per passenger up 8.2% to €6.19 #I

  • Legacy dispute resolution: Finalised agreements with commercial operators to resolve pandemic-era MAG discounts, securing +€28.4m positive impact on Q1 2026 income statement #I

Real Estate and Airport Cities

The Real Estate segment capitalises on vast airport landbanks through long-term leasing, with the "Airport Cities" initiative creating integrated logistics, hotel, and commercial hubs around Madrid and Barcelona.

  • Revenue performance: Real Estate Services grew 10.5% in 2025 to €131.9m, fueled by cargo logistics (+9.0%) and general real estate (+35.5%) #I
  • Business model: Long-term monetisation strategy by leasing land and surface rights for hangars, cargo warehouses, and commercial developments #I
  • Growth initiative: "Airport Cities" involves significant urban planning and technical developments to create integrated logistics, hotel, and commercial hubs around Madrid and Barcelona airports #I

Regulatory Framework and Strategic Investments (DORA)

The DORA framework governs Aena's Spanish network in five-year cycles; DORA III (2027-2031) proposes €12.88bn total investment with contentious tariff increases, triggering severe resistance from airlines demanding 4.9% cuts and a PP Senate amendment to freeze tariffs.

Overview of the Regulatory Environment

  • Framework structure: The Airport Regulation Document (DORA) is the primary regulatory instrument, established under Act 18/2014 #I
    • Ensures the network is managed as a service of general economic interest
    • Sets parameters for capacity, service quality, investments, and tariff paths over five-year periods
    • DORA II covers 2022-2026; DORA III proposal covers 2027-2031

DORA II (2022-2026) Directives

  • Tariff path: Approved September 2021; initially mandated a freeze on airport charges through 2025, setting IMAP at €9.89/pax #I
  • Investment baseline: Formally recognised €2.25bn in regulated investments, averaging €450m annually across the network #I
    • July 2025: Council of Ministers approved additional €351m in regulated investment for urgent infrastructure adaptations, capacity needs, and cybersecurity regulations
    • This pushed forward actions originally intended for DORA III
  • Quality monitoring: 17 service quality indicators spanning passenger satisfaction, waiting times, and facility availability; directly tying operational compliance to tariff bonuses or penalties #I
  • 2026 tariff increase: Freeze ended when CNMC authorised 6.31% rate increase for 2026, bringing adjusted IMAAJ to €11.02 inclusive of pandemic and border-control cost recoveries #I

DORA III (2027-2031) Proposal

  • Trigger: Board approved DORA III proposal in February 2026, in response to infrastructure operating near technical limits following robust post-pandemic rebound #I
  • Traffic assumptions: Models estimated 1.69bn passengers over the five-year stretch, peaking at 347m in 2031 #I
  • Investment scale: Most aggressive capex cycle in two decades; €12.88bn total network investments, €9.99bn classified as regulated #I
    • Designed to ensure sufficient capacity without disrupting ongoing operations
  • Cost structure: Financial structure reflects structural shifts in cost base #A
    • Regulated OPEX projected to scale from €1.98bn in 2027 to €2.31bn by 2031
    • Predominantly driven by stricter maintenance and security requirements; 36% of total regulated costs by end of cycle
  • Tariff financing: Proposed average annual tariff increase of €0.43 per passenger to satisfy a targeted 9.00% WACC #I
  • Quality expansion: Service quality mandate broadened from 17 to 26 compliance indicators to guarantee passenger satisfaction during extensive terminal renovations #I

Stakeholder Pushback and Government Relations

  • Severity: Aena's aggressive investment and tariff strategy has triggered severe resistance from both the airline industry and political entities #J
  • Airline demands: ALA and major carriers demand 4.9% annual tariff reduction, arguing Aena's 9.00% WACC is excessive compared to their proposed 6.35% #I
  • Ryanair retaliation: In direct response to 2026 tariff adjustments, Ryanair announced withdrawal of 1.2m seats across regional Spanish airports #I
  • Political headwind: Popular Party (PP) passed a Senate amendment to freeze tariffs through 2031 via the Sustainable Mobility Bill #I
    • Management immediately warned that imposing a statutory price ceiling would disfigure the economic model
    • Would force the Board, bound by fiduciary duties, to drastically curtail or suspend the €9.99bn regulated investment programme
  • Ownership defence: Vigorously defends hybrid 51% state-owned / 49% private structure #A
    • Spanish Constitution grants central state exclusive regulatory competence over general interest airports
    • This effectively shields management model from regional co-management demands
