[C] IG Query #2 — Change Forces Analysis

HTO-GR | Hellenic Telecommunications Organisation (OTE)

European Telco Cohort — Sub-cohort A | May 2026

1. KEY FINDINGS

*Written last. Sharpest conclusions from the full analysis.*

  • The primary change catalyst is real and quantifiable. The convergence of fibre monetisation (FTTH utilisation rate rising from 22% to 33%+ between March 2024 and September 2025), ICT/enterprise revenue acceleration (+33% in 2024, +57% in Q4 2025), and the Romania exit freeing capital are together sufficient — in combination — to drive 10-15% EPS CAGR over 3 years. This is not a single-variable story, which adds resilience. Rating: B.
  • OTE is executing a textbook flywheel, not a treadmill, but the compounding cycle is still early. Network investment → fibre take-up rising → ARPU uplift → wholesale revenue from rivals (Vodafone, Nova) paying OTE access fees → incremental cash flow reinvested. The inflection visible in Q4 2025 (fixed segment return to retail revenue growth for the first time in four years) is the earliest credible signal that the flywheel is turning. Most European peers have not crossed this threshold. Rating: B+ on the flywheel test.
  • ICT/enterprise is the underappreciated growth driver. System Solutions revenue up 57.8% in Q4 2025 is a striking number; driven by EU Recovery and Resilience Fund-linked public sector contracts. This is a durable multi-year tailwind given Greece's RRP deployment timeline through 2026-2027. No European telco peer in the cohort has this scale of near-term government digitisation spend as a growth overlay.
  • Capital returns story is aggressive by European telco standards. €532m total shareholder payout for 2026 against underlying FCF of ~€570-580m implies effectively 100% FCF payout — unusual at this stage of a fibre cycle, when most operators are rationing returns to fund capex. Net debt/EBITDA of 0.4x gives OTE balance sheet headroom rare in the sector. 🚩 RARE outlier on leverage: OTE is the least levered integrated European telco by a significant margin.
  • The Romania exit was strategically correct and shareholder-friendly. Romania was a structurally challenged, sub-scale mobile operation sold for €70m (mobile) plus previously €295m (fixed) — total exit ~€365m. Capital repatriated to Greece, tax benefits crystallising in 2026. The group is now a pure Greek domestic story, which simplifies the investment case.
  • Competitive intensity risk is manageable but not negligible. Three-player market (OTE/Cosmote Telekom ~7.4m subs, Vodafone Greece ~4.0m, Nova/United ~3.2m) is structurally rational. Nova is funded by United Group (€750m refinancing Oct 2024) and Vodafone is committing €1bn to network upgrades through 2029. OTE's wholesale deals with both rivals create partial alignment of financial interest — rivals pay OTE access fees on OTE's fibre, partially offsetting competitive pressure on retail. Elegant structure.
  • New CEO Costas Nebis is an operator upgrade over Tsamaz. Tsamaz (2010-2024) was primarily a restructuring/survival CEO who stabilised the business through the Greek crisis. Nebis (from July 2024) is a growth and digital transformation CEO, with a 2030 vision built around three pillars: development, transformation, diversification. The strategic cadence shift is genuine. Rating: B on management.
  • Predictability is MEDIUM-HIGH by telco standards. OTE's revenue mix (recurring mobile, broadband, pay-TV, ICT) is predominantly contracted, and fibre rollout trajectory is clearly measurable. The ICT/enterprise segment introduces some lumpiness (large project wins). The macro overlay (Greece GDP +2.1-2.4% forecast) is benign. Consensus estimates for 2026 look beatable.
  • ❌ EBITDA margin is not expanding at the pace one would expect. FY2025 adjusted EBITDA (AL) of €1,373m is only +2% on 2024's €1,347m, against revenue growth of 3.9%. The margin dilution from low-margin ICT/wholesale revenues is real and structural — ICT scale-up brings revenue without proportional margin. The flywheel on the cost side (truck roll reduction, field engineer leverage post-fibre) has not yet translated to visible margin expansion. This is the primary bear case and the key monitoring variable.
  • The Goldilocks test is passed, but narrowly. OTE is not pricing in 20% EPS growth. It is, however, a stock where credible 12-15% EPS CAGR over 3 years is achievable via: (a) fibre monetisation ramp, (b) ICT/enterprise growth, (c) mobile ARPU recovery, (d) Romania tax benefits in 2026, (e) buyback-driven EPS accretion. The question is whether consensus has already priced this in. At ~18 EUR/share and ~4.7% dividend yield (May 2026), the market is not paying for the growth story — it is paying for the yield. That asymmetry is the opportunity.

3. CHANGE IN LEADERSHIP

Rating: Green

  • Costas Nebis assumed the role of Chairman and CEO of OTE Group on 1 July 2024, succeeding Michalis Tsamaz who had led the group for 13 consecutive years (2010-2024). Tsamaz was OTE's longest-serving CEO in history, appointed during the depths of the Greek sovereign crisis. (Athens Times, 2024)
  • Tsamaz's mandate was fundamentally defensive: stabilise OTE through the Greek crisis, reduce headcount, sell non-core assets, and restart the dividend. He delivered on all three. By 2023, OTE was a cash-generative, low-leverage, dividend-paying operator with a cleaned-up portfolio. But the strategy had run its course — there was no credible growth narrative under Tsamaz.
  • Nebis brings a different profile. His background is in operational management and digital transformation within the Deutsche Telekom ecosystem. He is a growth-and-execution CEO, not a restructuring CEO. The strategy he has articulated — development, transformation, diversification — is explicitly forward-looking and positions OTE against a 2030 horizon, not a crisis-management playbook.
  • The three pillars of Nebis's strategy as presented in late 2024/early 2025: (1) Development — accelerate fibre and 5G rollout, monetise network leadership; (2) Transformation — digitise OTE's own operations, adopt AI and cloud infrastructure, migrate to common DT platforms; (3) Diversification — grow ICT/enterprise services, expand B2B wallet share, leverage DT's international procurement and platform capabilities. (Athens Times, 2025)
  • The strategic aspiration: transform OTE from "leading Greek telco" into a "top digital telecom provider in Europe by 2030." This is a significant step-up in ambition. In prior years, OTE's investor messaging was almost entirely focused on yield and FCF sustainability. The shift to a growth narrative is meaningful for the investment case.
  • Nebis's first operational year (H2 2024 — H1 2025) coincided with accelerating revenue growth. Whether the acceleration is attributable to his decisions or to lagged payoff from prior network investment is not yet determinable — but the correlation is positive.
  • The Cosmote Telekom rebrand (April 2025) — combining the local Cosmote brand with Deutsche Telekom's T — is a tangible signal of DT integration deepening. This brings access to DT's procurement scale, shared technology platforms, B2B product stack (MagentaONE, Magenta AI), and international brand recognition. The rebrand was Nebis's first major visible initiative.
  • DT's stake is now 55.6% (increased from ~50% via progressive buybacks). DT has been a consistent incremental buyer of OTE stock over a decade. The directional signal is clear: DT has no intention of diluting OTE or spinning it out. OTE is a strategic asset within DT's European portfolio. This is relevant for governance risk — minority shareholders are protected by DT's own shareholder interest in maintaining OTE's financial health.
  • Compared to peers: KPN has had CEO continuity (Joost Farwerij since 2022, operational background); Swisscom has a CEO from within telco; Elisa's management is long-tenured. In this cohort, OTE's CEO succession is the most recent and therefore the most uncertain — but also the most clearly differentiated toward a growth mandate. Rating: B (new, promising, but insufficient track record under Nebis specifically).
  • One risk: Nebis is a DT-ecosystem executive. OTE's independence as a listed entity depends on DT not increasing its stake beyond the level that would trigger a mandatory offer or squeeze-out. As DT's stake creeps toward 56-60%, the governance optionality for minority shareholders narrows.
  • The management team around Nebis includes CFO and operational leads drawn from within OTE's long-tenured team, providing continuity. The combination of a new strategic CEO + experienced operational CFO/COO is generally a constructive profile for execution reliability.
  • Operational metrics under Nebis's early tenure are positive: Q4 2025 marks the strongest quarterly EBITDA growth rate of the year (+2.3%); fixed retail returned to growth for the first time in 4 years; wholesale net additions onto OTE's fibre doubled year-on-year (60k in 2024 → 135k in 2025). These are early positive signals.

4. CHANGE IN STRATEGY / STRUCTURE

Rating: Green

  • The most structurally significant change is the Romania exit. OTE disposed of Telekom Romania Mobile (sold to Vodafone/Digi, completed October 2025) and had previously sold Telekom Romania Communications to Orange Romania for €295m. Total proceeds from Romanian disposals: ~€365m. OTE is now a pure Greek domestic operator. This is a strategic simplification that reduces complexity and concentrates management attention on a market where OTE is structurally dominant. (Deutsche Telekom press releases, 2024-2025)
  • The TERNA FIBER acquisition (November 2025, €380m) runs in the opposite direction — but the logic is consistent. Rather than building from scratch in semi-urban/rural areas, OTE acquired a 26-year concession asset covering 480,000 households that was already partially built under an EU-funded framework. This is a capital-efficient way to extend the network beyond the natural reach of purely commercial FTTH deployment. The concession structure provides revenue certainty (wholesale access fees) for a 26-year horizon. (TipRanks, Globe and Mail, November 2025)
  • The incumbent-dilemma lens is instructive here. OTE's response to the dilemma (build fibre vs. watch OTT/cable erode ARPU) has been decisiveness, not delay. The €3bn investment programme announced in late 2021 is being executed: 2.1m homes passed by end 2025, targeting 3m+ by 2027 (with TERNA FIBER extending potential reach to 3.5m). This is a faster and broader commitment than many European peers.
  • The wholesale strategy is a structural change that transforms competitive dynamics. Rather than treating fibre as a vertical integration weapon (our network, our retail only), OTE has concluded wholesale access deals with Vodafone and Nova, turning rivals into paying customers. In 2025, OTE effectively doubled wholesale net additions (135k vs 60k in 2024). 44% of Vodafone/Nova FTTH subscribers are now on OTE's network. This monetises the fibre investment at a faster pace and creates a partial revenue hedge against retail market share loss. 🚩 RARE: Few European telcos with this level of wholesale openness on a newly built fibre network — Openreach (BT) is the structural analogy; OTE is doing this voluntarily to accelerate monetisation.
  • The 5G strategy is operationally ahead of most European peers. OTE achieved 99% population coverage with 5G, and 70%+ population coverage with 5G SA (Standalone) by end 2025. 5G SA matters because it enables network slicing — which is the technical foundation for B2B/IoT premium services and for OTE's new FWA offering (Cosmote 5G WiFi). No European telco peer in this cohort has publicly reported 70%+ 5G SA coverage at this stage.
  • FWA as a rural retention strategy is new and tactically rational. OTE identified rural churn to Starlink as a specific problem in 2024 (fixed broadband subscribers fell 0.1% in FY2024 — a small but notable signal). The Cosmote 5G WiFi launch (January 2025) using 5G SA network slicing is a direct response. This is a better solution than accepting churn: it uses existing 5G infrastructure at low incremental capex to provide home broadband in areas where FTTH is uneconomic. (TelcoTitans, 2025)
  • ICT strategy: OTE positions itself as Greece's leading system integrator for enterprise and public sector. This is a strategic overlay on top of the connectivity business — it elevates OTE from a pipe provider to a digital transformation partner. The EU Recovery and Resilience Fund (Greece's RRP "Greece 2.0") is a massive tailwind: €32bn total plan, of which 21.4% is committed to digital transformation. OTE is a primary beneficiary as the dominant provider of enterprise IT, cloud, and cybersecurity services to Greek public sector entities. The RRP deployment timeline runs through 2026-2027, providing multi-year revenue visibility for the ICT division.
  • DT platform alignment (shared platforms, common IT stack) is not yet fully realised but the direction is set. As OTE migrates onto DT's common platforms, operating cost efficiency should improve — procurement scale, shared technology investment, reduced duplication. This is a medium-term (3-5 year) driver, not yet in the numbers.
  • The brand integration (Cosmote Telekom, April 2025) is a structural signal of deeper DT alignment. The MagentaONE product launch (converged fixed+mobile bundle) mirrors DT's convergence strategy in Germany and other markets. Converged bundles reduce churn significantly — typical European telco evidence suggests FMC (Fixed-Mobile Convergence) customers have 30-40% lower churn than single-product customers.

5. CHANGE IN PRIORITIES / AMBITION

Rating: Green

  • The clearest shift in ambition is the 2030 European-level vision — "top digital telecom provider in Europe." This is a step-change from the post-crisis framing of OTE as a Greek yield stock with stable dividends and declining debt. The ambition upgrade is credible in the context of DT's backing, the ICT opportunity, and the network leadership OTE now has in Greece.
  • Capital allocation priorities have visibly shifted. Under Tsamaz, the primary priority was debt reduction (from a peak leverage of ~3x EBITDA post-crisis). Net debt is now 0.4x EBITDA — effectively at zero leverage in real terms. The new priority is: (a) continued fibre/5G investment, (b) ICT/enterprise growth, (c) aggressive shareholder returns. The €532m 2026 payout is explicitly 100% of FCF — a signal that management believes the business generates excess capital beyond what the investment programme requires. This is a materially different capital allocation posture from most European telcos, which are still running leverage at 2-3x while funding fibre rollouts.
  • The 2026 guidance represents a credible acceleration: EBITDA growth guided at ~3% (vs ~2% delivered in 2025); organic FCF guided at €570-580m; capex maintained at ~€600m. The guidance implies that revenue growth (fibre monetisation + ICT) is starting to feed through to bottom line, even if margin expansion is not dramatic.
  • Targets are specific and measurable, which is analytically useful. Homes passed: 2.1m by end 2025 (on track), 3m+ by 2027 (now including TERNA FIBER extension to 3.5m). 5G SA coverage: 70%+ already achieved. FTTH utilisation: now 33% — target implicitly 40%+ as the wholesale agreement ramps. ICT revenue: no specific target given, but the cadence (+33% in 2024, +58% in Q4 2025) suggests management is happy with the trajectory.
  • Compared to peers: KPN has a mature fibre story with explicit margin expansion targets (EBITDA margin now 44.6%); Swisscom is in a late-stage fibre completion with dividend stability as primary messaging; Elisa (Finland) is the most advanced on operational leverage (margins above 40% and declining capex intensity). OTE is earlier in the cycle than all three — which creates more upside on margin if execution continues, but also more uncertainty.
  • The ICT/enterprise ambition is the highest-risk/highest-reward component. System Solutions revenue at +57.8% in a quarter is extraordinary — but it is also lumpy (large project wins), and growth rates at this level are not sustainable beyond the RRP cycle. The key question is whether OTE can convert RRP-funded public sector relationships into durable commercial ICT contracts at normal market pricing once EU funding fades. No guidance given on this.
  • Predictability rank vs European telco cohort: OTE ranks HIGH on mobile and fixed connectivity (recurring, contracted); MEDIUM on ICT (project-dependent); MEDIUM on wholesale fibre adoption rate (depends on competitor behaviour); LOW on the pace of margin expansion. Overall predictability: MEDIUM-HIGH. Credible 18M-3Y EPS forecast is achievable with ±15% error bands.

6. FINANCIAL PROFILE

Rating: Yellow (positive trajectory; margin stasis is the drag)

  • FY2025 headline numbers: revenues €3,464m (+3.9% YoY); adjusted EBITDA (AL) €1,373m (+2.0% YoY); FCF €543m; net debt €553m; net debt/EBITDA 0.4x. Q4 2025 was the standout quarter: revenue +8.7% YoY (of which ICT a significant driver), EBITDA (AL) +2.3% YoY, FCF Q4 €168m (+15.9% YoY). (Investegate, Investing.com, February 2026)
  • The revenue acceleration in H2 2025 is encouraging and not simply statistical — it reflects (a) FTTH monetisation beginning to show in ARPU trends, (b) ICT/enterprise project revenue recognition, (c) mobile ARPU recovery through prepaid-to-postpaid migration. Q4's 8.7% revenue growth is the strongest quarterly reading in several years.
  • EBITDA margin dynamics require careful interpretation. Group-level EBITDA margin is approximately 38-40%. The dilutive effect of ICT revenue (system integration contracts have lower margins than pure connectivity) is real and intentional — management is explicitly growing a lower-margin segment to build digital services capabilities. The risk is that ICT scales but margin never recovers. The bull case is that as ICT matures and OTE moves up the stack (cloud services, managed services, cybersecurity), margins improve. This is a 3-5 year story, not 2026.
  • Capex intensity: ~€600m guided for 2026 on ~€3.5bn revenue base = capex intensity of ~17%. This is elevated vs a steady-state operator (typically 12-15%) but expected for an operator in active fibre rollout. Capex as % of revenue is expected to decline as FTTH build peaks (likely 2027-2028 at 3m+ homes passed). Post-build, operating leverage should emerge: fewer field engineer deployments, lower maintenance cost per connection, faster fault resolution via remote diagnostics.
  • FCF quality is high. OTE's reported FCF of €543m in 2025 converts at approximately 39% of revenue — very high by European telco standards. The 2026 organic FCF guidance of €570-580m (ex Romania tax benefits) implies modest organic growth. With the €750m 2026 gross FCF guidance including the Romanian tax wind-down benefit (~€170-180m one-off), investors need to normalise for the underlying level.
  • Balance sheet is exceptional. Net debt of 0.4x EBITDA puts OTE in a different category from European telco peers: KPN guided ~2.4x; Swisscom ~1.5x; Telekom Austria ~2.0x. OTE's financial flexibility is such that even with the TERNA FIBER acquisition (€380m), leverage remains well below 1x. This is structurally important: OTE can fund the UFBB project, pay 100% of FCF back to shareholders, and still have balance sheet firepower for bolt-on ICT acquisitions if required.
  • EPS trajectory: In Q3 2025, EPS rose 39.5% YoY. This is partly amplified by Romania disposal accounting and tax credits, but the underlying trajectory is positive. Right-to-left thinking: if OTE can sustain revenue growth of 3-5% and maintain EBITDA margins at 38-40% while holding capex flat or declining, FCF should grow 8-12% annually. A buyback programme at current share price levels (€18) is EPS-accretive. Combined with organic EBITDA growth of 3%, a credible 10-15% EPS CAGR for 2026-2028 is constructible without heroic assumptions.
  • Red Queen detection: Multi-year revenue flat + elevated capex + no ARPU improvement = Red Queen. OTE does not exhibit this pattern as of end-2025. Revenue is growing at 3-4% organically; ARPU trends are positive in both mobile and broadband; fibre take-up is rising (utilisation 22% → 33% in 12 months). The Red Queen risk is most acute if: (a) ICT growth collapses post-RRP, (b) retail mobile ARPU stagnates, (c) capex remains elevated beyond 2027. These are monitorable variables.
  • Compared to European telco peers on financial profile: OTE's FCF yield (~7% at €18/share), leverage (0.4x), and payout ratio (100% FCF) together make it a standout in the European telco universe. The comparable that comes closest is Elisa (Finland), which also has low leverage and high payout ratios — but Elisa is priced at a significant premium (EV/EBITDA ~13-14x vs OTE's implied ~10-11x). The valuation discount for OTE reflects Greece country risk premium — which may be mispriced given the improvement in Greek sovereign credit and macroeconomic trajectory.

7. VALUE CHAIN — DEMAND

Rating: Green

  • Demand drivers are structurally sound. Greece's residential broadband market is underpenetrated on fibre vs. Western European averages — copper DSL still represents the majority of connections. The upgrade path to FTTH is not a matter of "if" but "when," and OTE is positioned as the primary provider of that upgrade. FTTH penetration within passed homes reached ~40% by September 2025 — ahead of schedule and above management's initial targets.
  • Mobile demand remains healthy. COSMOTE Telekom holds ~7.4m subscribers vs 4.0m for Vodafone and 3.2m for Nova — dominant market share in a three-player market. The prepaid-to-postpaid migration trend (higher ARPU) is ongoing. Healthy ARPU growth in Q3 2025 was explicitly referenced in management commentary. Data consumption growth (driven by video, streaming, 5G SA capabilities) is secular and supportive.
  • Pay-TV demand: OTE/Cosmote added 15k TV subscribers in 2024, benefiting from a content-sharing agreement with Nova for sports rights. The ability to offer live sports content alongside broadband is a retention driver in the Greek market, where football is significant. The agreement also reduces content cost duplication (OTE and Nova share rights costs rather than competing in expensive auctions). This is an intelligent demand-side management decision.
  • ICT/enterprise demand is the highest-growth demand vector and the most complex to forecast. Greece's EU RRP ("Greece 2.0") commits €32bn of which ~21% to digitalisation — approximately €6.7bn over 2021-2026. OTE is the dominant private sector beneficiary of this spending, providing cloud, IT infrastructure, cybersecurity, and managed services. System Solutions revenues grew 33.5% in 2024 and 57.8% in Q4 2025 — these reflect project wins in government and enterprise IT. The pipeline is large, the timeline is defined (RRP runs to 2026-2027), and OTE's position as market leader in enterprise IT gives it structural advantage in contract renewal and extension.
  • Tourism as a demand vector is indirect but meaningful. Greece received record-level tourism in 2025 — travel receipts up 11% H1 2025 vs H1 2024. Roaming revenues benefit; data demand in tourist hotspots (Athens, the islands) supports network utilisation metrics. Tourism also sustains consumer confidence and wage growth, supporting mobile ARPU resilience.
  • Demand risk: Fixed voice (legacy telephony) continues to decline — this is a structural headwind present across all European telcos. OTE's legacy fixed voice revenues erode at approximately 5-8% annually, partially offset by broadband ARPU uplift from fibre. This is a manageable offset arithmetic.
  • The competitive interaction with Vodafone and Nova on the demand side is nuanced. Both are growing their own FTTH subscriber bases — but a growing proportion of those are on OTE's wholesale network. This means demand growth by rivals actually creates incremental OTE revenue (wholesale access fees), creating a partial alignment of interest. The demand competition is primarily in the retail acquisition zone, not the network monetisation zone.
  • Demand predictability is HIGH for mobile, MEDIUM-HIGH for broadband (dependent on FTTH ramp pace), and MEDIUM for ICT (lumpy project timings). Overall demand quality: B+ by European telco standards.

8. VALUE CHAIN — COMPETITION

Rating: Yellow (manageable intensity; Nova funding a wildcard)

  • The Greek market has a stable three-player structure: OTE/Cosmote Telekom (market leader across fixed and mobile), Vodafone Greece (mobile and fixed challenger), Nova (United Group-owned, fixed + pay-TV + mobile post-Wind acquisition). The market has not seen disruptive new entry and MVNO penetration is minimal — near-zero by European standards (OnOff.gr analysis). This is structurally supportive for incumbents.
  • Vodafone Greece: Committed €1bn to network upgrades through 2029. This is a serious investment by a credible operator. However, Vodafone's Greek unit operates at smaller scale (~4m subs) and does not own a fibre network — it accesses OTE's fibre wholesale (44% of its FTTH subs are on OTE's network). Vodafone's investment is primarily 5G and its own fibre nodes, but it depends structurally on OTE's backbone. This limits the competitive threat from a network quality perspective.
  • Nova (United Group): Emerged from the merger of Wind Hellas (mobile) and Forthnet/Nova (cable + pay-TV). United Group refinanced €600m → €750m in October 2024, providing investment capacity. Nova's converged bundle (mobile + cable broadband + pay-TV) is a genuine competitive product in urban markets where its cable network has strong coverage (primarily Athens). The sports content agreement between OTE/Cosmote and Nova (content sharing) reduces the arms race dynamics in sports rights — a constructive development for both sides.
  • The flywheel vs treadmill test at the competitor level: Vodafone and Nova are running hard to compete (the treadmill) while OTE benefits from their efforts (wholesale fees + scale leadership). This asymmetry is structurally favourable for OTE. The creative destruction risk from either competitor building a rival fibre network at scale is low — the economics of overbuild in a market where OTE has first-mover advantage and wholesale agreements are already in place make this unattractive.
  • Convergence competition: FMC (Fixed-Mobile Convergence) bundles are the frontier competition. OTE's MagentaONE launch (April 2025) is its converged bundle product. Vodafone Greece has its own convergence play. This is a repeat of the convergence battles seen in the Netherlands (KPN vs VodafoneZiggo), Belgium (Proximus vs Telenet), and Sweden (Telia vs Telenor). In each case, incumbents with network scale have maintained competitive position against cable-led challengers. OTE's position is stronger than most: it controls both the fixed fibre network and the dominant mobile network. Customers who converge on OTE are structurally sticky.
  • ❌ Pricing pressure: The search results indicate that competitive pricing remains a challenge, particularly in mobile (prepaid segment) and in broadband (price as a retention tool). OTE has not demonstrated the ability to take above-inflation price increases in mobile in the way KPN did (October 2024 price increase noted in earnings commentary). This is a partial failure on the pricing-power test. However, the fibre ARPU uplift over copper (customers migrating to FTTH accepting higher monthly charges for speed upgrade) is a structural pricing mechanism that offsets this.
  • EETT regulatory posture: OTE is designated as SMP (Significant Market Power) operator in fixed markets, obligating it to provide wholesale access at regulated prices. The EC has opened an in-depth investigation into fixed wholesale high-quality access in Greece — this could impose tighter price controls on leased lines / business connectivity. However, the wholesale access framework also serves OTE by forcing Vodafone and Nova to use OTE's infrastructure rather than build their own — a structural advantage.
  • Starlink risk in rural markets: OTE explicitly identified rural churn to Starlink as a problem in 2024. The FWA launch (Cosmote 5G WiFi, January 2025) is the tactical response. This is a new competitive dynamic not present 2-3 years ago. Satellite broadband (Starlink, eventually Amazon Kuiper) offers a genuinely disruptive alternative in areas where OTE's FTTH economics are marginal. The TERNA FIBER acquisition (extending FTTH to rural/semi-urban via EU subsidy) is the structural response. Whether FWA + subsidised FTTH is sufficient to neutralise Starlink over a 5-year horizon is uncertain.

9. VALUE CHAIN — DISRUPTION

Rating: Yellow (manageable near-term; Starlink + AI are the longer-horizon risks)

  • Technology disruption risk in telecom takes several forms: (a) access technology disruption (cable, FWA, satellite vs fibre); (b) application-layer disruption (OTT services destroying voice/SMS revenue); (c) AI-enabled competitor or low-cost entrant; (d) platform bypass (messaging apps replacing SMS; Google/Microsoft hosting direct enterprise connectivity).
  • Fixed voice erosion is well-advanced and already in OTE's financials — legacy voice revenues declining at 5-8%/year. This is not a new disruption; it is a known attrition rate that management factors into guidance. The residual risk is whether the decline accelerates (higher substitution) or stabilises (elderly/rural customer segments remaining on fixed voice longer than expected). OTE's Greek market has structural reasons to believe stabilisation is nearer than in markets with younger demographics.
  • Cable overbuild: Nova's cable network (the former Forthnet infrastructure) is concentrated in urban Athens and Thessaloniki. It does not represent a national overbuild threat. The risk of a second national fibre network being built by Nova or Vodafone is low — OTE's first-mover advantage, wholesale access pricing, and TERNA FIBER concession together make competitive overbuild uneconomic. The incumbent-dilemma is therefore resolved in OTE's favour more cleanly than in markets like the UK (Openreach vs Virgin Media) or Sweden.
  • Satellite (Starlink) disruption is the most genuine near-term disruptive risk in OTE's specific geography. Greece has a large number of islands and rural areas — exactly the population distribution that is hardest to serve with fibre and most vulnerable to satellite. OTE's FWA response and TERNA FIBER subsidised extension are tactically sound but satellite's economics will improve over time (as SpaceX launches more satellites, latency and price improve). OTE's rural vulnerability is greater than Netherlands/Finland/Switzerland peers. Rating vs cohort: D on rural disruption exposure — this is a structural geographic disadvantage.
  • AI disruption to OTE's ICT business is a double-edged risk. On the downside, AI tools may reduce the complexity (and therefore cost) of IT implementations, compressing OTE's enterprise IT margins over time. On the upside, AI creates new service categories (AI infrastructure, enterprise AI deployment, cybersecurity enhanced by AI) that OTE can sell. OTE/Cosmote has launched Magenta AI as a product — early days, but aligned with the DT ecosystem which has more AI development resources than OTE could generate independently.
  • OTT messaging disruption (WhatsApp etc.) has already reduced SMS/voice revenues. This is fully embedded in forecasts. The remaining risk is marginal.
  • 5G SA as an enabler of new B2B services (industrial IoT, network slicing for enterprise) is a positive disruption for OTE — it creates new revenue categories. OTE's 70%+ 5G SA population coverage is a genuine competitive advantage vs European telcos that have not yet deployed SA cores. This positions OTE to offer latency-sensitive enterprise services (logistics, manufacturing, port management) in a country where shipping and logistics are economically significant. 🚩 RARE in European telco cohort on 5G SA coverage at this stage.
  • Creative destruction analysis: the most likely disruptive scenario is gradual — Starlink takes 5-10% of rural households over 5 years, satellite economics improve, and OTE's rural ARPU slowly erodes despite FWA deployment. This is a manageable, not existential, disruption. The urban/suburban FTTH business (80%+ of revenue) faces no credible near-term technological disruption.

10. VALUE CHAIN — POLITICAL AND REGULATORY

Rating: Yellow (country-specific risk manageable; EC investigation a monitoring item)

  • Greece regulatory environment has improved structurally since the sovereign crisis. The primary surplus is maintained (2.3-2.9% of GDP projected 2025-2027); the credit rating has been upgraded progressively; fiscal compliance has improved. This is relevant for OTE because sovereign risk premium directly affects OTE's cost of debt and the equity risk premium applied to Greek-listed stocks.
  • EETT (Hellenic Telecommunications & Post Commission) is OTE's primary regulator. OTE is designated SMP in fixed markets with standard EU-framework obligations: access to local loop, transparency, non-discrimination, price control, cost accounting, accounting separation. These are known obligations, not new risks. EETT operates within the European Electronic Communications Code framework, limiting idiosyncratic regulatory risk.
  • The EC investigation into fixed wholesale high-quality access in Greece (opened during the review period) is the most relevant active regulatory risk. If the EC finds EETT's market analysis inadequate and requires tighter price regulation on leased lines, OTE's enterprise/B2B connectivity margins could face compression. The severity depends on the specific market definition and pricing analysis — outcome uncertain, timeline likely 12-18 months from opening.
  • Rural broadband concession framework: Greece's UFBB project (the vehicle through which OTE acquired TERNA FIBER) is a public-private partnership with a 26-year concession. The regulatory framework governing concession returns and wholesale pricing is set at contract inception and provides long-term revenue visibility. The risk is that the Greek state modifies concession terms — unlikely given the EU co-funding structure, which locks in terms to satisfy EC state aid conditions.
  • DT governance overlay provides an additional layer of regulatory protection. OTE is a strategic European telco asset owned by one of Europe's largest operators (Deutsche Telekom, the dominant German incumbent). DT's political relationships in Brussels and its experience navigating European regulatory processes provide OTE with access to regulatory intelligence and advocacy capabilities that a standalone operator of OTE's size would not have.
  • Windfall tax/sector levy risk: Several European governments have discussed or imposed windfall taxes on telcos in the context of fiscal consolidation. Greece's fiscal position (primary surplus, RRP-funded investment) makes this risk lower than in more fiscally stressed markets. No current indication of telco-specific levies in Greece.
  • Privacy/data regulation risk: OTE handles significant personal data (mobile, broadband, enterprise). GDPR compliance is standard. No specific enforcement action or material risk identified.
  • Greek political risk: Greece is politically stable by Mediterranean standards under the current centre-right New Democracy government (Kyriakos Mitsotakis). Investment climate reforms and RRP execution are flagship government priorities. Political risk of a sudden shift in economic policy that would negatively affect OTE is low in the near term. The 1.2% Greek state stake in OTE is residual and carries no strategic influence.
  • Compared to peers on regulatory risk: KPN, Swisscom, and Elisa operate in markets with very stable regulatory environments and no open EC investigations. OTE's regulatory risk is marginally higher but not materially different from the European incumbent baseline. The Greek country risk premium (visible in the yield spread vs German Bunds) already prices in some regulatory uncertainty.

11. VALUE CHAIN — SUPPLY SIDE

Rating: Green

  • OTE's supply side has two primary components: network infrastructure suppliers (Nokia, Ericsson, Huawei for mobile; Corning/Prysmian for fibre) and human capital (technical workforce for network rollout and maintenance, IT/ICT professionals for the enterprise division).
  • Equipment supply chain: OTE's 5G rollout uses Nokia and Ericsson (standard European NatCo profile aligned with DT's vendor strategy). Fibre infrastructure supply (cables, termination equipment) is subject to global commodity pricing. Fibre glass and cable prices were elevated in 2022-2023 due to supply chain pressures but have moderated. OTE's scale (as a DT subsidiary) provides procurement advantages — access to DT's framework contracts with Nokia, Ericsson, and Corning gives OTE better pricing than standalone operators of its size.
  • Human capital is a monitoring risk. Greece's economy has seen emigration of skilled technical workers during the crisis decade (2010-2020). The recovery has partially reversed this, and OTE's technology employer brand (backed by DT) helps attract talent. The ICT division's growth requires not just telco engineers but IT consultants, cloud architects, and cybersecurity professionals — a skills category in short supply across Europe. OTE's ability to retain and recruit this talent at competitive Greek salaries (lower than Western Europe) is an advantage vs competitors offering equivalent roles in London or Amsterdam.
  • TERNA FIBER integration: the acquired entity (formerly BROADBAND INFRASTRUCTURE DEVELOPMENT PROJECTS/TERNA FIBER) brings with it a partially built network and the operational capability to continue construction. Integration of this entity into OTE's operational structure is a supply-side execution risk over 2026-2027. At €380m acquisition cost for a 26-year concession covering 480k premises, the cost per premises passed is manageable (~€792/premise), and the EU co-funding reduces OTE's effective cost of deployment.
  • Energy supply: Telco networks are significant energy consumers (data centres, cell sites, exchanges). Greece's energy transition is ongoing — OTE has committed to sustainability targets aligned with DT's group-level net zero trajectory. Energy cost management is relevant: Greek electricity prices are influenced by the energy mix and wholesale power market volatility. No acute energy supply risk identified.
  • OTE's shared infrastructure agreements (mast sharing, duct sharing) with Vodafone and Nova reduce network rollout costs for all parties. These are standard European NRA-mandated sharing frameworks, supplemented by commercial agreements. The sharing framework is a stable supply-side efficiency, not a risk.

12. VALUE CHAIN — MACROECONOMIC

Rating: Green

  • Greece's macroeconomic environment is a genuine tailwind for OTE — arguably more supportive than any other European telco's home market in this cohort. Greece GDP growth is forecast at +2.1-2.4% for 2025-2026 (OECD, European Commission), significantly above the Eurozone average of ~1.0-1.2%. This is a structural outperformance driven by post-crisis recovery, investment, and tourism.
  • Tourism is a direct tailwind. Greece recorded record inbound travel receipts in 2025 (H1 up 11% YoY), and further resilient growth is forecast for 2026 (INSETE). Tourism creates roaming revenue for OTE; supports consumer confidence and wage growth; boosts data traffic at network hotspots. This is a structural advantage vs KPN (Netherlands: low tourism intensity) or Elisa (Finland: cold, low tourism). Tourism-sector demand for connectivity services (hotels, hospitality, event infrastructure) is also a growing B2B segment for OTE.
  • EU Recovery and Resilience Fund: Greece's €32bn RRP is the largest per-capita EU recovery fund allocation in Europe relative to GDP. Digital transformation accounts for 21.4% (~€6.7bn) with deployment running through 2026-2027. OTE's ICT division is a primary private-sector beneficiary. This is a material, time-limited, and quantifiable macro tailwind. Comparable in some respects to the infrastructure spending tailwind in defence-sector stocks — government commitment is locked in; execution risk lies with OTE's project delivery.
  • Greek sovereign credit improvement: Greece has been progressively upgraded by credit rating agencies since 2018. The improvement in sovereign credit quality narrows the Greek risk premium embedded in OTE's equity cost of capital. As Greece approaches investment-grade normalcy, institutional investors who were previously unable or unwilling to hold Greek equities become natural buyers. This creates a structural rerating tailwind for OTE's equity multiple over a 3-5 year horizon.
  • Inflation and consumer spending: Greek inflation is moderating in line with Eurozone trends. Real wage growth (supported by minimum wage increases and employment recovery) supports consumer ability to pay for higher-tier broadband and mobile services — the ARPU uplift story requires household income growth. Current macro trajectory is supportive.
  • Geopolitical risk: Iran conflict (the current macro backdrop for this research exercise) has a limited direct impact on OTE/Greece specifically. Greece is a NATO member, not directly in the Iran conflict zone. Energy price spikes (a second-order Iran conflict effect) would increase OTE's energy costs, but OTE is a relatively energy-efficient operator vs industrial-sector peers. Tourism to Greece is not materially affected by Iran-region conflicts based on historical precedent. Macro risk rating: LOW-MEDIUM relative to European peers.
  • Compared to peers: The Greek macro backdrop (GDP growth +2.1-2.4%, RRP tailwind, tourism boom) is superior to Netherlands (GDP ~1.0%), Switzerland (stable but static), Sweden (recession in 2023-2024 now recovering), and Finland (GDP negative 2023, recovering 2024-2025). OTE's macro environment is a genuine differentiator in this cohort. Rating: A- on macro backdrop.

13. REVENUE / DEMAND CYCLE

Rating: Green

  • OTE's revenue cycle is at an inflection point. Fixed retail revenue returned to growth in Q4 2025 — the first positive quarter in four years. This is the critical data point for the demand cycle thesis: the erosion from legacy fixed voice has been sufficiently offset by broadband and TV ARPU uplift that the fixed segment is now growing in aggregate. If this holds for 4-6 consecutive quarters, it reprices the narrative from "managing decline" to "growing business." (Q4 2025 earnings call, Investing.com)
  • Mobile revenue cycle is healthy. COSMOTE Telekom commands ~53% of Greek mobile subscriber market and ~60%+ of mobile revenue market (higher ARPU postpaid mix). Mobile grew in every quarter of 2025 per CEO Nebis's Q4 commentary. The prepaid-to-postpaid migration is a multi-year ARPU improvement dynamic. 5G SA rollout (70%+ population) enables speed-tier upselling. Mobile is OTE's most stable, most predictable revenue line.
  • ICT revenue cycle is at peak acceleration (RRP-driven) and will likely normalise post-2027. The current growth rate (+33% in 2024, +58% in Q4 2025) is not a steady-state run rate. The cycle question: can OTE sustain 10-15% ICT growth after the RRP tailwind fades (2027+)? The answer depends on: (a) converting RRP-funded relationships into commercial contracts, (b) competitive dynamics in enterprise IT (major consulting firms, smaller local IT services providers, AWS/Microsoft direct enterprise deals). This is the highest uncertainty revenue line.
  • Wholesale revenue (from Vodafone/Nova using OTE's fibre) is a new and growing revenue stream with a multi-year ramp profile. 135k net wholesale additions in 2025 (vs 60k in 2024) — doubling. If rivals' FTTH subscriber bases continue growing and the share using OTE's wholesale network holds at 44%, wholesale revenue could become a meaningful contribution (~€150-200m annually at maturity) to OTE's fixed segment. This is a new revenue stream not yet fully reflected in consensus estimates.
  • The revenue cycle comparison vs European telco peers: KPN is at late-stage FTTH (broadly complete, now in monetisation/margin phase); Swisscom is building fibre at scale but slower; Elisa is the most advanced on margin per customer. OTE is at mid-stage FTTH — early enough in monetisation that the growth is real, late enough in build that the capex intensity should begin declining within 2-3 years. This is a favourable position on the telco capex/return cycle.
  • Revenue predictability: total revenues in OTE's four main segments (mobile retail, fixed retail, wholesale, ICT) are predominantly recurring or multi-year contracted. Mobile ARPU is sticky (SIM contracts typically 12-24 months). Fixed broadband/TV is contract-based. ICT is project-based (lumpy). Wholesale is frame agreement-based (Vodafone, Nova). Overall revenue predictability is MEDIUM-HIGH.

14. HISTORICAL TRACK RECORD OF DELIVERY

Rating: Yellow (improving under Nebis; Tsamaz era was stabilisation not growth)

  • OTE's track record under Tsamaz (2010-2024) must be assessed on its own terms. He delivered: (a) debt reduction from ~3x EBITDA to 0.5x by 2023; (b) headcount reduction from ~16,000 to ~11,000 through voluntary redundancy schemes; (c) Romania network exit (fixed: sold to Orange; mobile: sold to Digi/Vodafone); (d) dividend restart and progressive growth; (e) fibre investment programme launch. By restructuring standards, a strong execution record.
  • The weakness in Tsamaz-era delivery: (a) fixed retail revenue never returned to growth — it declined consistently from 2012-2024; (b) EBITDA margins did not expand despite cost reduction (mix shift to ICT offset cost savings); (c) mobile market share held but OTE was never the aggressor in pricing or innovation during this period; (d) ICT growth was solid but not transformative.
  • Under Nebis (July 2024 — May 2026, ~22 months): the evidence base is limited but positive. Q4 2025 marks first fixed retail revenue growth in 4 years. ICT growth has accelerated significantly. FTTH utilisation has risen rapidly (22% → 33% in 12 months). Wholesale net additions doubled. The Cosmote Telekom rebrand and MagentaONE launch are tangible execution events. TERNA FIBER acquisition completed in 4 months from announcement — efficient execution of a complex asset transaction.
  • Guidance delivery: OTE has historically guided conservatively and delivered at or above guidance. The 2025 guidance (given February 2025) was for ~2% EBITDA growth; delivered approximately 2%. For 2026, guidance is ~3% EBITDA growth — management's history suggests this is achievable if current Q4 momentum is sustained. The 2026 FCF guidance (€570-580m organic) is similarly conservative-seeming given Q4 2025 FCF run-rate.
  • Compared to peers on delivery track record: KPN has a consistent 5-year track record of meeting EBITDA and FCF targets, which is partly why it trades at a premium. Elisa has the most consistent margin expansion track record in European telco. Swisscom is consistent but flat. OTE's track record is shorter on growth delivery (primarily a restructuring track record) — this is the key discount reason vs KPN/Elisa, and also the source of upside if Nebis delivers 2-3 years of growth.
  • FTTH rollout track record: OTE delayed its original fibre completion target (2025) to 2027 and expanded the footprint goal (from 3m to 3.5m via TERNA FIBER). TelcoTitans noted this pushout in 2024 as a negative signal. In context, the revised schedule reflects a scope increase (more homes, EU rural extension), not project failure. The utilisation rate (40%+ within passed homes) exceeds the industry benchmark for a network at this stage of build. Execution quality on the build itself is sound.
  • Systems Solutions (ICT) track record is excellent: +33% in 2024, building on prior years of double-digit growth. OTE has demonstrated the ability to win and execute large-scale public sector IT projects — a differentiating capability that requires regulatory relationships, project management scale, and domain expertise. This is not easily replicated by competitors or new entrants.

15. COHORT CQ ANSWERS — Sub-cohort A

Rating: Green (all 5 CQs have substantive answers; OTE passes 4 of 5 clearly)

CQ1: What is the primary change catalyst — and is it large enough to move 3-year EPS materially (>15% vs consensus)?

The primary change catalyst is a convergence of three simultaneous inflections: (1) FTTH monetisation reaching an inflection point — utilisation rising from 22% to 33% in 12 months, wholesale net additions doubling — generating ARPU uplift and new revenue streams; (2) ICT/enterprise acceleration driven by EU RRP spending, with System Solutions revenue up 33-58% in recent quarters; (3) Romania exit releasing capital (€365m total proceeds + tax benefits in 2026), simplifying the group and enabling 100% FCF payout. Individually, each is a meaningful positive; together, they create a EPS trajectory that is credibly 15%+ above consensus over 3 years. The right-to-left arithmetic: OTE at ~€18 trades at ~10x forward EBITDA; if EBITDA grows 3%/year and capex intensity declines as fibre build peaks, FCF grows 8-12%/year; combined with a buyback programme, EPS grows 12-15%/year. Consensus likely does not fully price the ICT trajectory or the wholesale ramp. Verdict: YES, the catalyst is large enough if executed. It is not a single binary event (merger, disposal, restructuring) but a multi-variable operational inflection — lower probability of a sharp near-term re-rating, higher probability of a sustained 3-year compounding story.

CQ2: Is fibre rollout complete or in progress, and what is the evidence on monetisation yield (ARPU uplift per fibre subscriber vs copper)?

Rollout is in mid-progress. As of end-2025: 2.1m homes passed (target); 567k FTTH subscribers; utilisation rate 33% (up from 22% 12 months prior); ~40% take-up rate within passed homes (above industry average for this stage). Target: 3m+ homes by 2027 (including TERNA FIBER extension to 3.5m). Monetisation evidence is positive: FTTH customers generate a structural ARPU premium over copper DSL (speed-tier pricing, bundle lock-in). The exact ARPU differential is not publicly disclosed by OTE, but the fixed retail return to revenue growth in Q4 2025 (first time in 4 years) is the aggregate evidence that FTTH ARPU uplift is now exceeding legacy fixed voice decline — the crossover point most investors were waiting for. The wholesale dimension amplifies monetisation: 135k wholesale net additions in 2025 generate access fee revenue from rivals using OTE's network. The network penetration rate exceeding 40% within passed homes is 🚩 RARE by European standards at this stage of build — it indicates OTE's commercial execution on FTTH is best-in-class, not average. The ARPU uplift story is real, the evidence is present, and the trajectory is accelerating.

CQ3: What is the competitive intensity in the domestic market — structural consolidation trend or persistent price competition?

The market has structurally consolidated. Three players (OTE, Vodafone, Nova) control ~95% of mobile and fixed broadband revenue. MVNO penetration is near-zero. Nova's emergence from the Wind/Forthnet convergence is complete. No new entrant threat is credible in fixed or mobile. Structural consolidation is the right characterisation. However, persistent price competition remains a feature in mobile (prepaid segment) and in broadband (acquisition offers). The key nuance: OTE's wholesale deals with both Vodafone and Nova create a partial financial interdependency that reduces the incentive for destructive price competition — if Nova prices aggressively and steals OTE retail subscribers, but those subscribers remain on OTE's wholesale fibre, OTE still earns revenue. This is an alignment-of-interest structure that moderates competitive intensity in a way that is not visible from the subscriber loss data alone. The content-sharing agreement on sports rights (OTE/Cosmote and Nova) similarly moderates the arms race in content spend. On balance: competitive intensity is MODERATE (not low, not high) — better than UK, Belgium, or France; comparable to Netherlands and Finland. Rating: B on competitive structure.

CQ4: What is the capital returns story (dividend sustainability, buyback capacity) and does it constrain or enable the change thesis?

Capital returns ENABLE the change thesis rather than constraining it. The €532m 2026 total shareholder return (dividend of €0.8777/share + €177m buyback) is 100% of underlying FCF — meaning management believes the business generates more cash than the investment programme requires. Net debt of 0.4x EBITDA provides extreme balance sheet flexibility — OTE could simultaneously fund the TERNA FIBER integration, the ongoing FTTH build, the 5G SA rollout, and pay 100% of FCF to shareholders without leverage exceeding 1.5x. 🚩 RARE: this is the most aggressive capital return programme in the European telco cohort, achieved at the lowest leverage. The dividend yield of ~4.7% at current prices is fully covered (payout ratio 62% of earnings) and growing (22% DPS increase proposed for 2026). The buyback programme is EPS-accretive at current prices. Capital returns do not constrain the change thesis — they are evidence of confidence in the investment programme's ability to generate excess cash, which is itself a signal about management's assessment of the inflection point.

CQ5: Management quality — operator CEO or utility manager?

Costas Nebis (appointed July 2024) is an operator CEO, not a utility manager. The distinction: a utility manager optimises for stability, dividend security, and regulatory compliance. An operator CEO drives growth through operational execution, competitive positioning, and portfolio optimisation. Nebis's first 22 months include: the Cosmote Telekom rebrand (brand execution), MagentaONE launch (product execution), TERNA FIBER acquisition (M&A execution), doubling of wholesale net additions (commercial execution), and the 2030 vision announcement (strategic execution). These are the actions of an operator, not a utility manager. The three-pillar strategy (development, transformation, diversification) is growth-oriented, not defensive. One caveat: Nebis comes from the DT ecosystem and OTE's 2030 vision includes "transition to common platforms with Telekom Group" — some element of his mandate is integrating OTE more deeply into DT's operational model, which serves DT's shareholder interests. Whether this perfectly aligns with OTE minority shareholder interests is a governance question worth monitoring. On balance: Nebis rates as B+ — an operator CEO in his early tenure, with credible growth orientation, not yet proven through a full strategic cycle.
