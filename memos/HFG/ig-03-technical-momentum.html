1. Price Action Summary (L12M)

Hilton Food Group's share price has experienced a dramatic decline over the last twelve months, falling from its 52-week high area near 914p in mid-2025 to approximately 525p in mid-April 2026. The decline has not been gradual. It has occurred in two sharp, catalyst-driven legs down, interspersed with periods of low-conviction drift.

Phase 1: Stability and Modest Strength (Apr-Sep 2025)

Through the first half of 2025, HFG traded in a relatively stable range between approximately 800p and 910p. The stock reached its 52-week high of 914p during this period, supported by reasonable FY2024 results and a broadly constructive outlook for the core meat processing business. The FTSE 250 itself was grinding higher, and HFG participated in that move. Volume was unremarkable during this phase -- consistent with a mid-cap stock in a holding pattern rather than one attracting fresh capital.

The interim results published on 3 September 2025 showed revenue growth of 10.3% to GBP4,214.6m from continuing operations, which was taken positively by the market. However, there were early signs of strain in the seafood and vegetarian/vegan divisions that would later prove consequential.

Phase 2: First Profit Warning and Collapse (Nov 2025)

The first major break came in November 2025. The company issued a trading update warning that ongoing operational issues and subdued demand would make profit growth in 2026 difficult. Specifically, the UK seafood division was being hammered by softer white-fish demand, driven by high raw material costs and household budget pressure. More critically, operational disruptions at Foppen -- the company's Dutch-headquartered seafood subsidiary -- were escalating. Greek facility approvals had been delayed by the US government shutdown, preventing production resumption.

The market's reaction was savage. HFG shares fell 22% in a single session. This was a genuine capitulation event: volume surged, holders who had been sitting on losses or expecting a recovery were flushed out, and the stock broke decisively below its trading range. The share price fell from the ~750p area to below 600p.

FY2025 adjusted pre-tax profit guidance was narrowed to GBP72-75m, below the prior year's GBP76.1m. This was not a catastrophic miss in absolute terms, but the signal mattered more than the magnitude. The market interpreted it as a business losing control of a key division.

Phase 3: Second Leg Down -- Guidance Disappointment (Jan 2026)

The second capitulation came on 30 January 2026. Hilton Food published its FY2025 preliminary results and 2026 outlook. Revenue came in at GBP4,214.6m with adjusted PBT of GBP73.2m (including discontinued operations) and GBP69.0m from continuing operations. Adjusted EPS was 56p, down 7.4% year-on-year.

The real damage came from the 2026 guidance. Management guided adjusted PBT of GBP60-65m for FY2026, well below the analyst consensus of GBP68.6m. The company cited continued challenges in seafood, vegetarian, and vegan businesses, plus caution around red meat inflation. US stock write-offs at Foppen were expected to be "significantly higher than previously indicated." Restrictions on smoked salmon exports from the Greek facility to the US market were expected to persist through at least H1 2026.

Shares dropped 10.8% on the day. Volume was extraordinary: 26.3 million shares traded against an average session volume of approximately 1.7 million -- a 1,460% spike. This was the largest volume day in HFG's recent history. Berenberg and Deutsche Bank both lowered their price targets the same day.

The stock hit its 52-week low of approximately 448p in the weeks following this announcement, representing a peak-to-trough decline of over 50% from mid-2025 levels.

Phase 4: Tentative Recovery Attempt (Feb-Apr 2026)

Since the February 2026 lows, HFG has attempted a modest recovery. The share price has crawled from the ~450p area back to approximately 525p by mid-April 2026. This represents a ~17% bounce from the trough, but it remains a fraction of the overall decline.

The recovery has been characterised by low volume and narrow daily ranges -- not the hallmarks of aggressive accumulation. The stock has been trading in a roughly 480-540p range for several weeks. There is no clear breakout signal yet.

On the positive side, insider buying has provided a floor of sorts. Mark Allen's GBP199k purchase at 468p on 30 January 2026 -- the very day of the results selloff -- was a meaningful signal. More recently, Bindi Foyle bought 3,761 shares at 529p on 15 April 2026. These are not transformative in volume terms, but they demonstrate that people with inside knowledge of the business consider the current price attractive.
