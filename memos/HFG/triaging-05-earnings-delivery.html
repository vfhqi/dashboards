Brief company description(s)

Hilton Food Group operates as a leading international multi-protein food business, supplying major retail partners with high-quality meat, seafood, and vegetarian products across 19 global markets.

The company's business model is built on highly integrated, long-term supply agreements with global retailers like Tesco, Ahold, and Woolworths, utilizing volume-based packing rates that ensure predictable cash flow.

Over the last three years, Hilton demonstrated a robust historical financial trajectory, with adjusted profit before tax climbing 15.3% to £76.1 million in 2024, underpinned by a 4.4% volume increase.

During 2025, performance was mixed; while the core red meat and fresh prepared food operations remained highly resilient with stable volumes, total adjusted profit before tax declined 3.8% to £73.2 million.

The 2025 profit decline was heavily driven by sustained input cost inflation and weak consumer demand in the UK white-fish market, alongside significant operational and regulatory disruption at the Foppen smoked salmon business.

Extreme Protein Price Dynamics: Consumer demand was heavily influenced by extreme protein price dynamics throughout the year. During the first half, the company experienced staggering raw material inflation of approximately 30% in beef and up to 60% in core whitefish species like cod and haddock.

Cost-of-Living & Consumer Caution: Faced with these unprecedented price increases, consumer sentiment has weakened, leading shoppers to become highly cautious with their weekly grocery spending. Customers are actively seeking greater value by trading down to products with lower overall weights or cheaper price points, such as higher-fat content minced beef.

Reformulation to Mixed-Proteins: To protect sales volumes and offer affordable options, Hilton accelerated the rollout of "mixed meat" protein ranges. By incorporating lower-cost proteins like pork and chicken into traditionally beef-based mince, burgers, and meatballs, the company successfully adapted to cost-conscious purchasing behaviors across Europe and APAC.

Seafood Substitution Strategy: Similar reformulation strategies were aggressively deployed in the seafood category to combat collapsing demand. The company reformulated its popular fishcake ranges with alternatively sourced, cheaper whitefish and launched hake products in tactical brands to provide a lower-cost alternative to expensive cod and haddock.

Expert View on Seafood Pricing Destruction: An industry expert previously serving as Head of Procurement at Hilton Seafood noted that retail prices for a standard 250-gram pack of cod have surged from £3 five years ago to between £7.50 and £10. The expert warned that fully passing this extreme level of inflation onto consumers naturally results in a severe destruction of end-market volume demand.

The "Eat-In" Premiumization Trend: Conversely, there remains a strong polarized demand for premium products as consumers substitute expensive restaurant visits with high-quality at-home dining experiences. Hilton successfully expanded its premium ranges with Wagyu steaks, grass-fed beef, and elevated Christmas food-to-order products, capturing this persistent "eat-in" behavioral shift.

Expert View on Steak Volumes: However, a Commercial Director at Kepak Group observed that the massive inflation passed onto premium cuts has still caused an overall decrease in total retail steak sales volumes. Because Hilton is primarily a retail packer without alternative foodservice outlets to seamlessly redirect unsold premium cuts, this drop in high-value throughput negatively impacts their unit packing economics.

Structural Decline in Meat Alternatives: Consumer purchasing behavior has decisively shifted away from highly processed meat alternatives over the past year. The Dalco vegan and vegetarian business faced severe structural headwinds and changing consumer demand patterns, resulting in a £9.8 million non-cash impairment charge on its acquired goodwill.

Expert View on Plant-Based Sentiment: An industry expert and former Head of Insights at Tesco Ireland highlighted that the plant-based category has taken a "hammering" over the last 18 months. Consumers are increasingly rejecting these products due to poor taste credentials and highly complex, ultra-processed ingredient lists.

Broker View on Whole Proteins: Shore Capital analysts corroborated this behavioral shift, noting that mature consumer food markets are demonstrating a clear structural preference for whole, natural proteins. The broker highlighted that this return to whole foods comes at the direct expense of consumer desire for heavily processed, carbohydrate-rich artificial foodstuffs.

Broker View on Promotional Cycles: Shore Capital also warned that the unprecedented retail beef price increases—rising over 50% year-on-year in Europe and over 30% in the UK and Australasia—are fundamentally disrupting retailer behavior. The broker observed that these extreme prices are forcing retailers to adjust their normal promotional cycles and substitute lower-value items, which creates a near-term detrimental effect on Hilton's overall profitability.

Pricing Power and Pass-Through Dynamics: From a pricing power perspective, Hilton operates a highly derisked, volume-based packing model that contractually allows it to pass raw material inflation directly through to its retail partners. While this protects Hilton's absolute gross margins, it artificially inflates group revenue while simultaneously depressing the end-consumer's appetite for volume.

Broker View on Core Resilience: Despite these severe inflationary pressures severely testing consumer demand, Shore Capital viewed Hilton's ability to maintain flattish core volumes of 0.2% as a major positive. The broker argued that this volume stability in the face of elevated beef prices proves the deep integration and enduring strength of Hilton's global customer relationships.

Growth in Convenience Categories: Demand for convenience and fresh prepared foods remains a robust, reliable growth engine for the company. Fueled by rising consumer preference for ready-to-eat and easier meal solutions, this category saw double-digit volume growth in Central Europe, underpinning the strategic rationale for the newly announced £30 million capacity expansion in Poland.
