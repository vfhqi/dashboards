KEY FINDINGS

  • Offshore losses are the single biggest near-term risk. The Q4 2025 NOK ~100m provision from AF Offshore Decom on a fixed-price campaign is not automatically a one-off — the "Cockroaches Never Alone" heuristic applies directly. Management stated "all planned campaigns are complete," but the language is precise: it does not preclude cost tail risk on campaigns already completed, or margin erosion on new work being tendered. The Claxton acquisition, announced weeks after the loss disclosure, dramatically expands AF's exposure to exactly the contract structures that caused the Q4 2025 provision. This is the central risk flag at Triaging stage.
  • Building margin recovery of ~6% in Q4 2025 is real but fragile. Q4 2025 Building EBT came in 48% above consensus — a genuine beat driven by project completions and mix improvement into renovation/public. However, Q4 2025 order intake was -37% YoY, the forward pipeline is thinner, and the residential market, while recovering, has not returned to 2021-22 starts volumes. Sustaining 5-6% into 2026 requires the public/commercial mix to hold and the residential pipeline to continue its recovery trajectory.
  • Claxton is a strategically coherent acquisition, but the timing is uncomfortable. GBP 63.5m / ~4.6x revenue for a 14% EBIT-margin technology-led services business with proprietary tools (SWAT, SABRE, MWR) and 30+ years in North Sea decommissioning is defensible on paper. The concern is that AF is doubling down on offshore decom risk immediately after disclosing a loss — before the root cause of that loss has been publicly explained. Integration bandwidth, combined with simultaneous Offshore and Building execution demands, is a legitimate question.
  • Civil Engineering at 9% Q4 2025 is a high-watermark, not a run-rate. Full-year 2025 Civil EBT margin was 6.5-7.4% depending on metric — respectable but below the Q4 anomaly. The forward risk is margin compression as projects won in the current competitive tender environment begin to execute. Norwegian public construction demand via NTP 2025-2036 (NOK 95bn+ budget) is a structural tailwind, but the bidding pool has deepened.
  • Order intake deceleration (-23% group, -37% Building, -35% Civil in Q4 2025) is not yet structurally alarming, but the buffer is narrowing. Backlog of NOK 44.7bn covers ~16-17 months of LTM revenue. The question is not solvency but margin quality of backlog additions — particularly if new Civil work is being won at thinner spreads than the legacy book.
  • Management has three simultaneous execution demands in 2026: Offshore resolution, Claxton integration, Building sustain. Amund Tøftum has been CEO since 2020, guided the company through a meaningful margin recovery (EBT 2.5% in 2023 → 5.2% in 2025), and has operating background in the Offshore division. That track record is encouraging. The concern is bandwidth and the coincidence of risks — Offshore, Sweden contract termination (E4 Förbifart Stockholm, ~NOK 100m in shutdown costs), and a bolt-on acquisition all landing in the same earnings period.
  • The stock is not an obvious buy or sell at Triaging. The FCF profile is genuinely strong (NOK 3.0bn OCF in 2025, ~184% EBT conversion), the dividend is growing (NOK 6.50 proposed, +30%), and the Civil backlog is high-quality NTP-linked work. But the risk-reward for a concentrated position is complicated by three unresolved debates simultaneously: Offshore tail, Claxton integration, and Building intake sustainability.
  • Preliminary Triaging verdict: PROGRESS to ESA — with Offshore and Claxton as red-flag watch items. The business quality is sufficient to warrant deeper work. The margin recovery is real. But this name requires ESA-level diligence on the Offshore loss mechanics and Claxton deal structure before any investment decision.

2. Debate #1: Offshore Losses — Isolated or Pattern?

Section Rating: ORANGE-RED

  • AF Offshore Decom was established as a joint venture between AF Gruppen (formerly 100% owned since inception, with a 2021 partnership with Aker Solutions announced to create a "leading global decommissioning company") and has over 45 completed offshore installation decommissioning projects and more than 5 million project man-hours of experience. It has safely removed and recycled more than 400,000 tonnes of North Sea oil and gas infrastructure over two decades, giving it one of the longest operational track records of any decom contractor globally.
  • The Q4 2025 NOK ~96m loss at the Offshore segment level — with the "approximately NOK 100 million project loss" explicitly identified as the cause — is notable for its size relative to the quarterly revenue of NOK 577m. A ~17% revenue-to-loss ratio on a single project is a material cost overrun by any measure. This is not a minor line-item provision; it is a loss that moved the needle on a record-revenue quarter.
  • Management's statement that "all planned offshore campaigns have now been completed" is important but requires careful interpretation. It asserts that the physical work programmes are done, which eliminates forward execution risk on those specific campaigns. It does not rule out: (i) residual cost tail from demobilisation, waste disposal, and regulatory closeout on completed campaigns; (ii) variation claims and counter-claims from subcontractors or clients that can extend cost uncertainty for 12-24 months post-completion; or (iii) warranty or latent defect costs.
  • The "Cockroaches Never Alone" model applies with full force here. Fixed-price offshore decom contracts are inherently difficult to cost precisely — the sub-surface and subsea environments contain unknowns that rarely favour the contractor. The history of offshore EPC/decom contracting is replete with examples where a first disclosed provision was followed by a second, larger provision as surveys and invoices from subcontractors were settled. The Worser, Odder, Longer, Further heuristic reinforces this: when the first provision appears, it typically underestimates the final quantum.
  • AF Gruppen did not specify in the Q4 2025 report which specific campaign generated the NOK 100m loss. This lack of granularity is itself a red flag at Triaging stage. Investors cannot assess whether the overrun was idiosyncratic (e.g., a unique subsea topology, unexpected material encounter) or systemic (e.g., cost-estimating methodology failure, subcontractor management deficiency) without knowing the project. The absence of a named campaign or specific causal explanation is characteristic of the early stage of a cost overrun disclosure.
  • The Aker Solutions partnership announced in 2021 was designed to create a combined decommissioning and recycling platform. However, the Q4 2025 results commentary refers only to "AF Offshore Decom" as the loss-making entity — there is no specific reference to the Aker JV structure in relation to the loss. This ambiguity about ownership and cost-sharing of the problematic campaign is another data point requiring clarification at ESA.
  • AF Offshore Decom's contract pipeline remains active: a January 2026 contract with Ithaca Energy covers the engineering, receipt, dismantling, and recycling of the FPF-1 floating production platform from the UK sector (valued NOK 225-275m excluding VAT), and a consortium with Heerema Marine Contractors is active on a separate North Sea multi-year removal campaign. New work continues to flow — the market is not drying up.
  • The critical question for risk assessment is the contractual structure of the new Ithaca/FPF-1 contract and the Heerema consortium work. If these are also fixed-price or lump-sum contracts, the same cost-overrun exposure exists on those campaigns. If they are reimbursable or unit-rate-plus structures, the risk profile is materially lower. This structural question about the forward book is what Cockroaches Never Alone demands be answered before progressing.
  • The Claxton acquisition, announced in April 2026, expands AF's offshore decom platform through a technology-led services model. Claxton primarily sells services and equipment (well abandonment, wellhead removal, conductor severance) rather than taking full campaign EPC risk. This is a different risk profile from AF Offshore Decom's whole-platform removal/recycling model — Claxton's EBIT margin of ~14% on ~GBP 82m revenue suggests the market compensates proprietary technology services better than execution contracting. However, integrating Claxton while simultaneously managing Offshore Decom cost tails creates dual demands on the same management team.
  • Historically, AF Offshore Decom's operational record before Q4 2025 appears largely clean from publicly reported major provisions — there is no evidence from available data of prior years' large offshore project writedowns. This is relevant: if the Q4 2025 loss is truly isolated, it breaks a long track record rather than continuing a pattern. However, the absence of prior large writedowns partly reflects the lumpy, campaign-based nature of the business — AF does relatively few large projects per year, so a single bad campaign can erase significant margin.
  • The Norwegian Continental Shelf decommissioning pipeline is large and growing: over 600 fixed platforms and 10,000+ offshore wells are scheduled for decommissioning across the North Sea over the next three decades. The North Sea Offshore Decommissioning Market is projected to grow at 9%+ CAGR to 2031. The structural tailwind is real and persistent — the question is whether AF can execute these campaigns profitably under fixed-price structures, not whether the market exists.
  • The competitive landscape for offshore decom has intensified. Heerema Marine Contractors (a consortium partner on some AF jobs, a competitor on others) has global scale. Allseas, Saipem, and Boskalis all have removal vessel capacity. The decom market is growing but the contractor pool is not thinning — bid competition for large campaigns is active, which creates pricing pressure that in turn raises the probability of margin shortfalls on aggressively priced bids.
  • At the ESA stage, the single most important question to resolve is: what was the specific project, what caused the overrun, and what is the contractual status of the completed campaign? If AF's management can provide a specific, non-generic explanation of the cost driver (e.g., "unforeseen well integrity issue requiring additional plug and abandonment procedures on Platform X"), that increases confidence that the loss is contained. If the explanation remains vague, the Cockroaches model demands a larger probability weighting on further provisions.
  • The section rating is ORANGE-RED because: the provision is material, the contractual risk model in offshore decom is inherently back-ended, the Claxton acquisition increases rather than reduces offshore risk exposure, and the absence of a specific causal explanation in the Q4 2025 report maintains elevated uncertainty about whether the full cost has been recognised.
  • One partially mitigating data point: the Q4 2025 Offshore segment revenue of NOK 577m implies that campaign revenues were booked but costs exceeded them by ~NOK 100m. If the campaign is now complete and invoiced, there is no future revenue recognition to defer additional costs against — the loss is in the accounts. This is better than an ongoing campaign where provisions can continue to be needed in future periods.

3. Debate #2: Building Margin Recovery — Structural or Seasonal?

Section Rating: YELLOW

  • AF Gruppen's Building (Bygg/Construction) segment delivered Q4 2025 EBT ~6% (implied from "very good profitability" and the Investing.com/Kepler data point of 48% above consensus). For the full year 2025, the group EBT margin was 5.2% versus 3.5% in 2024. Building is the largest revenue segment by most periods, and its margin trajectory is the primary driver of the group's re-rating from ~2.5% to 5%+ margins over 2023-2025.
  • The bull case on Building is that the recovery is structural, driven by: (i) a deliberate pivot away from residential fixed-price contracts (which were the source of losses in 2022-23 as materials inflation hit during execution) toward public and commercial construction; (ii) improved risk selection and bid discipline across subsidiaries; (iii) the recovering Norwegian housing market (house prices +5-6% in 2025, building permits picking up), which is beginning to generate positive option value on residential work; and (iv) the renovation and refurbishment segment (AF Byggfornyelse, ÅBF, FAS) which is structurally less volatile than new-build.
  • The bear case is simpler: Q4 2025's strong Building result reflects project completions from a backlog that was won in a less competitive market (2022-23), and the pipeline being filled now — Q4 2025 order intake of NOK 2,579m vs NOK 4,070m a year earlier, -37% — is thinner and at different risk/margin characteristics. The backlog at NOK 10,460m is ~2.5 quarters of revenue, which is reasonable but not exceptional. If 2026 order intake remains subdued, revenue pressure will arrive in H2 2026.
  • The Q4 2025 report identifies very good results at AF Byggfornyelse, AF Bygg Oslo, AF Bygg Østfold, Haga & Berg, Strøm Gundersen, Strøm Gundersen Vestfold, ÅBF, and FAS — eight of the ten construction subsidiaries named. Only AF Håndverk (weak) and LAB Entreprenød/HTB (good, not very good) underperformed. This breadth of delivery is a positive data point: it suggests the recovery is not dependent on one large project completion in one subsidiary.
  • Norway's residential construction market is in recovery but not yet at volume. The Nordea data confirms housing construction "picking up" and building permits rising, with new home sales +20% YoY in the past year. However, housing starts lag permits by 6-18 months, and construction contractors only see revenue when physical work begins. The residential pipeline being generated now will translate into Building segment work in late 2026 and 2027 — not in H1 2026.
  • The pivot to public construction demand is visible in the backlog composition. The Building segment has increasingly captured healthcare, education, and public office projects under competitive tender, partially substituting for the decline in private residential developer commissions. This is strategically correct for margin stability — public clients are less likely to cancel and the contracts tend to be unit-rate rather than fully fixed-price — but public sector project pipelines are also subject to political and budgetary risk.
  • The -37% YoY Q4 2025 order intake in Building is significant in the context of the segment's scale. NOK 4,070m won in Q4 2024 was a high comparator (possibly including some large contract wins), so a statistical base effect is plausible. However, the full-year 2025 Building order intake trend should also be inspected: if Q4 2025 was the weakest quarter in a deteriorating sequence, that is a different signal from a single-quarter timing issue.
  • The Swedish construction operations — folded under Sweden segment, not Building — add a complicating data point. Sweden had "very strong profitability" in Q4 2025 on stable activity, but the E4 Förbifart Stockholm contract termination (Swedish Transport Administration cancelled the contract, resulting in ~NOK 100m shutdown and termination costs) is an example of how execution risk in Nordic construction can materialise suddenly. AF has disputed the termination, which means legal costs and management distraction are ongoing.
  • Renovation and refurbishment work (AF Byggfornyelse, ÅBF) represents a structurally more defensive sub-segment within Building. Renovation demand is less correlated with the new-build cycle — it is driven by maintenance obligations, energy efficiency regulations, and tenant/owner preferences. The growing share of renovation in the Building backlog is a positive structural feature for margin consistency.
  • The risk of margin compression as new Building work is won in a more competitive environment is real. If residential developers — who were largely absent from the market in 2022-24 — return with large new-build projects, the competition for those contracts will be intense. Margins on competitively tendered new-build residential are structurally lower than for renovation or public construction. AF's discipline in bid selection — a key behavioural change since the 2022-23 margin difficulties — must be maintained even as order volumes come under pressure.
  • The Kepler Cheuvreux Buy upgrade in February 2026 at NOK 180 explicitly cited the strong Q4 Building result and the margin recovery arc. The November 2025 Hold (with raised target to NOK 196) reflected the same analyst's view that the valuation had run ahead of near-term earnings visibility — suggesting even the bull-case broker acknowledges the sustainability of Building margins is not a certainty.
  • The 5.2% group EBT margin for full-year 2025 compares to a peer like Veidekke at ~2.5-4.7% (a wide range reflecting Veidekke's own segment mix). AF is clearly outperforming the peer group at the current moment, which raises the question of mean reversion. Exceptional margin periods in Norwegian construction have historically normalised as competitors price more aggressively into the same workload.
  • Monitoring trigger for ESA: H1 2026 order intake in Building. If Q1 and Q2 2026 order intake stabilises at NOK 3,000m+ per quarter, the -37% Q4 2025 reading was likely timing/mix noise. If H1 2026 Building order intake is consistently below NOK 2,500m/quarter, the thesis that the pipeline is refilling at a sufficient rate to sustain 5%+ margins will be under genuine pressure by H2 2026.
  • The section rating is YELLOW — the recovery is real and multi-subsidiary breadth confirms genuine operational improvement, but the combination of -37% order intake decline and a thin residential new-build pipeline creates material uncertainty about 2026 profitability. This is a debate that time (specifically Q1 2026 results in May 2026) will begin to resolve.

4. Debate #3: Claxton Acquisition — Value Creator or Value Destroyer?

Section Rating: YELLOW

  • Claxton Engineering Services was acquired from Acteon Group for GBP 63.5m enterprise value (approximately NOK 800m) in April 2026, pending Norwegian Competition Authority approval and expected to complete in Q2 2026. The acquisition corresponds to approximately 0.77x revenue (GBP 82m average 2023-25) and approximately 5.5x EBIT at the 14% EBIT margin, implying a multiple of roughly 4.6-5.5x depending on normalisation. For a technology-led services business with proprietary tools and 30+ years of demonstrated market position, this is not an expensive entry point — comfortably below typical industrial technology acquisition multiples of 8-12x EBIT.
  • Claxton's core value proposition is not execution contracting — it is tool-enabled services for well abandonment and wellhead removal. The SWAT (Suspended Well Abandonment Tool) has been deployed on over 100 well abandonment projects worldwide. The SABRE abrasive cutting system and Multi-Wellhead Retrieval (MWR) tool are proprietary equipment that Claxton deploys, maintains, and continuously develops. This gives Claxton a level of IP protection and pricing power that AF Offshore Decom's whole-platform removal business does not have — a structurally important distinction.
  • The strategic rationale is coherent: AF brings large-scale project execution and heavy lifting capability (AF Environmental Base Vats, AF's heavy marine equipment access); Claxton brings precision tools and well services technology. The combination addresses the full decommissioning value chain — from well abandonment (Claxton) to platform removal and recycling (AF Offshore Decom). This vertical integration within decommissioning is the right strategic direction for a company wanting to differentiate from execution-only decom contractors.
  • The GBP 82m average revenue over 2023-25 and 14% EBIT margin implies EBIT of roughly GBP 11.5m (~NOK 145-150m). At GBP 63.5m consideration, the transaction is funded from AF Gruppen's liquidity reserves — the company had net interest-bearing receivables of NOK 1,274m as at 31 December 2025. This means Claxton is funded without recourse to external debt or equity issuance, which is credit-positive and demonstrates a balance sheet that can absorb the acquisition without stress.
  • The risk of timing is significant. AF is acquiring Claxton at a moment when: (i) its own offshore decom division has just disclosed a NOK 100m loss; (ii) North Sea decom activity is structurally growing but project award timing is lumpy; and (iii) management has three simultaneous demands (see Debate #6). Acquiring a GBP 63.5m business — even a well-run one — during a period of operational stress in the parent is a governance question. The board's decision to proceed suggests confidence that the Q4 2025 Offshore loss is ring-fenced; the market's reaction will be the test of whether that confidence is warranted.
  • Key-person risk at Claxton is a genuine concern. Technology-led services businesses of Claxton's scale and heritage depend significantly on senior engineers who carry institutional knowledge of the proprietary tools and long-standing client relationships in the North Sea. Aberdeen-based engineering talent is competitive — Acteon group's other businesses, Saipem, SLB, and Halliburton all compete for the same experienced well decom engineers. Integration incentives (retention packages, equity participation) will be a critical execution item.
  • Claxton's geographic footprint is a positive: headquartered in Aberdeen, with facilities in Great Yarmouth, and strategic hubs in Stavanger, Houma (Louisiana, USA), Singapore, and Abu Dhabi. This is not a UK-only business — it has a meaningful international pipeline, particularly relevant in the context of the Gulf Coast and Southeast Asian decommissioning markets that are early-stage but growing. This international optionality is an undisclosed upside in the NOK 800m purchase price.
  • The 14% EBIT margin Claxton achieved over 2023-25 is the key financial claim to interrogate at ESA level. The North Sea decom market is cyclically sensitive to oil price (operators accelerate decom in lower oil environments and sometimes defer in higher ones) and to regulatory timelines set by UK and Norwegian authorities. A sustained commodity price downturn or regulatory relaxation could soften Claxton's revenue base — though the 30-year structural decom pipeline makes acute revenue loss unlikely.
  • AF's stated intention for Claxton to "operate as an independent business unit within the Offshore business area" is the right model for preserving operational continuity — it avoids the forced integration that kills acquired engineering service businesses. This management approach is consistent with AF's decentralised operating model (Building has 10+ semi-autonomous subsidiaries) and is a positive governance signal.
  • The section rating is YELLOW — strategically the right acquisition at a defensible price, but the timing coincidence with the Offshore loss and the integration bandwidth question lower the conviction versus a cleaner entry point.

5. Debate #4: Civil Engineering Margins — Sustainable at 7-9%?

Section Rating: GREEN-YELLOW

  • AF Gruppen's Civil Engineering segment delivered Q4 2025 EBT margin of 9.0% (EBT NOK 313m on revenue NOK 3,116m) — the strongest quarterly performance in the segment's recent history. For the full year 2025, Civil reported EBT margin of 7.4% and operating margin of 6.5% (different conventions). This compares to full-year 2024 margins of 7.5%/6.8%, implying broadly stable margins despite a 19% revenue growth. The ability to grow revenue 19% YoY while holding margins is a genuine operational achievement.
  • The NTP 2025-2036 is the dominant structural driver. The Norwegian government's NOK 95.2bn transport investment plan over 12 years (presented March 2024, submitted to the Storting) creates a multi-year visible demand pipeline for civil contractors. Rail investment alone includes $40bn+ over the period. AF Gruppen, as Norway's largest domestic civil contractor by most measures, is structurally positioned to capture a disproportionate share of NTP-driven tender flow. This is not speculative — NTP demand is already in the Civil backlog (NOK 17,966m at end-2025).
  • The NTP demand mix is shifting from large capital projects to maintenance and operations ("prioritisation of resources on preserving existing transport infrastructure"), with fewer flagship mega-projects. This shift is potentially margin-supportive: large, complex, first-of-kind infrastructure projects carry the highest risk of cost overruns; maintenance and upgrade work is more predictable. However, the shift also means smaller average contract values and more competitive bid pools.
  • Alliance contracting structures — where the client (typically Norwegian Public Roads Administration, Statsbygg, or Bane NOR) and contractor collaborate on cost management and share risk/reward — have been a meaningful driver of Civil's margin improvement. Alliance contracts reduce the adversarial fixed-price dynamic and create incentives for cost efficiency and innovation. AF has been active in alliance structures on Norwegian rail and road projects. If the NTP contract mix moves toward these structures, Civil margins have support.
  • The mean reversion argument rests on one empirical observation and one structural one. The empirical: Civil's Q4 2025 result (9.0%) is well above any reasonable normalised expectation — it reflects project completions, possibly final account settlements, and potentially some seasonal effects (Norwegian winter contracts often see catch-up billings in Q4). The full-year 7.4% is a more representative number. The structural: as Civil grows revenue (up 19% in 2025) by winning new work, the margin profile of that new work will dilute the legacy book's margins as high-margin legacy projects complete and new, competitively tendered work begins.
  • Veidekke is the most relevant peer. Veidekke's Q2 2025 overall profit margin was 4.7%, against AF's group 5.2% for the full year. AF is clearly outperforming on a margin basis. However, Veidekke has a different segment mix — it has a larger residential building exposure, which was under pressure in 2023-24. The relevant comparison is Veidekke's civil engineering margins specifically, which Veidekke does not separately disclose in the same granularity. Available data suggests Norwegian civil contractors operate in the 4-8% EBT margin range depending on project mix, with the upper end achievable only with a favourable mix of NTP alliance work.
  • The Civil backlog of NOK 17,966m at end-2025 represents approximately 6 quarters of forward Civil revenue at the 2025 run-rate of ~NOK 11.2bn annually. This is healthy backlog cover — but the key question is whether the newest additions to the backlog were won at similar margins to the existing book, or at thinner spreads due to competitive pressure.
  • Competition in Norwegian civil engineering has historically been moderate at the large-project tier — the pool of contractors capable of executing large tunnels, rail, and highway projects is limited (AF, Veidekke, Skanska Norway, NCC, and occasional international entrants). This oligopolistic market structure has historically supported above-average margins for qualified participants. The NTP's emphasis on maintenance over mega-projects may lower the barrier to entry for smaller contractors on individual work packages.
  • Hydroelectric power construction is a significant sub-segment within Civil — AF has been active in hydropower rehabilitation and expansion, driven by Norway's grid electrification ambitions. Hydropower projects tend to be technically complex, which advantages established contractors and supports margin premiums.
  • The section rating is GREEN-YELLOW. The 7-9% EBIT margin range is above normalised peer expectations but is backed by genuine structural demand (NTP), a differentiated contract mix (alliances), and a deep operational track record in Norwegian civil construction. The mean reversion risk is real but not imminent — the primary risk factor is how competitively the new additions to the backlog were priced, which is an ESA-level diligence item.

6. Debate #5: Order Intake Deceleration — Timing or Trend?

Section Rating: YELLOW

  • Q4 2025 group order intake of NOK 9.59bn was down -23% YoY from NOK 12.51bn. The decline was broad-based: Building -37% (NOK 2,579m vs NOK 4,070m), Civil -35% (NOK 3,150m vs NOK 4,843m), and Sweden -22%. The only segments not declining were Offshore (where campaign activity is irregular) and potentially Energy/Environment. A -23% group decline in a single quarter is material and cannot be dismissed as minor noise.
  • The 16-17 month backlog buffer (NOK 44.7bn against LTM revenue of ~NOK 32bn) provides significant protection against near-term revenue risk. AF Gruppen is not a company in imminent revenue stress — it has contracted work through approximately mid-2027 at current revenue run-rates. However, the backlog buffer is not infinite, and if order intake does not recover in H1 2026, revenue visibility for H2 2027 and beyond begins to thin.
  • The Q4 2024 comparator was strong — NOK 12.51bn in a single quarter is above the normalised quarterly run-rate (annual order intake of ~NOK 40-45bn implies ~NOK 10-11bn/quarter on average). The Q4 2025 decline partly reflects a tough base. However, the magnitude of the decline (-23%) suggests a meaningful underlying slowdown, not purely a statistical artefact.
  • Building order intake at NOK 2,579m in Q4 2025 is concerning not just in isolation but in the context of the Building backlog at NOK 10,460m at end-2025. If Q4's intake run-rate of ~NOK 2.5-3bn/quarter persists, the Building backlog will begin to decline in 2026.   • The section rating is YELLOW — Tøftum's track record is the primary mitigant, and the decentralised operating model distributes risk. But the 2026 agenda is genuinely demanding, and management bandwidth risk is not trivial when Offshore, Claxton, and Building are all simultaneously in focus.
