COMPANY DESCRIPTION

AF Gruppen ASA (Oslo: AFG.OL) is a Norwegian contracting and industrial group founded in 1985 and headquartered in Oslo. With FY2025 revenue of approximately NOK 31.99 billion (~EUR 2.9bn), it is the second-largest construction contractor in Norway behind Veidekke and competes directly with Skanska Norge, NCC, and the Swedish peers in its home markets.

Business model: AF Gruppen operates as a multi-segment construction and civil engineering contractor with six reporting divisions plus a small property development arm.

Revenue Mix by Segment (approximate, FY2024/2025):

  • Civil Engineering (Anlegg): Norway's roads, railways, tunnels, bridges, ports, energy plants. Largest and highest-margin segment (~NOK 9-10bn revenue, 9-10% EBIT margin). The flagship business. ~30% of group revenue.
  • Construction (Bygg): Residential, commercial, public building — new-build and rehabilitation. Norway-focused. ~NOK 8-9bn revenue, margins recovering toward 5-6%.
  • Betonmast: Acquired 2019 (66%), fully integrated by 2022-23. Building contractor with specialist concrete/masonry capability. NOK ~4-5bn revenue. Historically a drag; improving toward 5% margin by 2025.
  • Sweden: Building and civil operations in Sweden. Structurally challenged market. NOK ~3-4bn revenue. Near-zero to negative margin in 2022-2024, improving in 2025.
  • Energy and Environment: Demolition, remediation, energy efficiency. NOK ~2-3bn revenue, stable mid-single-digit margins.
  • Offshore (AF Offshore Decom): Removal, recycling, and decommissioning of offshore oil and gas platforms. NOK ~1.5-2bn revenue. High-volatility segment — project-by-project, loss-prone on fixed-price.
  • Property: Residential development (largely through Betonmast Boligbygg). Small, loss-making, winding down. Homes sold contracted sharply from 216 in 2022 to 53 in 2023.

Geography: ~80%+ Norway, ~15% Sweden, growing UK/International exposure via Offshore and now Claxton (Aberdeen).

Business model characteristics: Fixed-price contracting dominates across segments; revenue visibility via order backlog (NOK 44.7bn at end-2025 = ~1.4x annual revenue); labour-intensive with significant subcontractor pass-through; negative working capital structure (advance payments from clients).

SECTION 1: CHANGE IN LEADERSHIP

L12M (May 2025 – May 2026):

  • CEO Amund Toftum (in role since August 2020) remains in post. Background is operational — former EVP Offshore, EVP Construction, project director within AF. No change signalled. Animal CEO assessment: Toftum is an insider with deep project knowledge, appropriate for a contractor navigating operational underperformance. His public language on 2024 was candidly self-critical ("gap in performance has been too wide") — a positive signal vs the obfuscation typical in construction boardrooms.
  • CFO Anny Oen confirmed since 2022, having joined AF in 2009 as Group Accounting Manager. Her tenure covers the full 2022-2025 underperformance period and recovery — she has full context. Some older sources reference Sverre Haerem as CFO but this appears outdated. Oen's continuity of institutional memory is a positive.
  • Divisional leadership: Post-2024 strategy review, there is evidence of second-level management action, particularly in Sweden. AF Bygg Vast, AF Bygg Ost and AF Anlaggning Vast were specifically cited as loss-making units requiring management intervention. No publicly named management changes but restructuring at unit MD level is inferred from the 2024 strategy review language.
  • Board composition: Employee representation enhanced via agreement forgoing corporate assembly — employees have increased board representation. PWC continues as external auditor. No major board changes flagged in 2025.
  • Claxton integration leadership: Claxton will operate as an independent business unit within the Offshore division, with its own senior executives joining AF Gruppen. This adds an Aberdeen-based management layer (~400 employees). Cultural integration risk (UK vs Norwegian contractor cultures) is non-trivial given AF's prior Swedish integration challenges.
  • Construction segment leadership: Following the 2024 strategy refresh, project management and productivity have been elevated as leadership mandates. This implies accountability changes for project managers and divisional heads, even if not publicly announced. The pattern matches the Betonmast integration — operational personnel changes driven by results rather than announced proactively.

N36M outlook:

  • Toftum's tenure will be judged on 2028 target delivery. If Sweden and offshore drags are not resolved by 2026-27, board patience will be tested — his credibility is now on a four-year runway.
  • The 2024 strategy refresh is essentially a public self-critique acknowledging that Betonmast integration absorbed management bandwidth and degraded project controls. Management is aware of the problem; the question is execution capacity.
  • No succession signals visible. Key-person risk exists — Toftum's operational background is directly relevant to the recovery task; his departure would be destabilising.

Degree of change: YELLOW — CEO stable, CFO has continuity, divisional management restructuring ongoing, and Claxton adds new international management complexity. The leadership team is known but has underdelivered significantly against its own targets over three years. The key question is whether operational improvement reflects genuine management capability improvement or favourable conditions (NTP cycle, input cost deflation).

*What would change it to Orange/Red:* A CEO departure or a second major project failure triggering board-level intervention.

SECTION 2: CHANGE IN STRATEGY/STRUCTURE

L12M:

  • New 2028 strategy launched (developed throughout 2024, presented at CMD/annual results Feb 2025): three pillars — project management excellence, productivity improvement, and organisational adaptability. A meaningful departure from the 2021-24 strategy, which was growth-led (NOK 40bn / 7% margin by 2024). The new strategy is operationally focused — a tacit acknowledgement that scale was pursued faster than management capacity allowed.
  • 2028 targets restated: 7% EBIT margin (unchanged) and 7% annual revenue growth, plus halving climate/environmental footprint. The margin target reprieve is notable — 7% was also the 2024 target. The runway has been extended by four years without revising the margin ambition downward.
  • Betonmast integration declared largely complete: The full merger of AF Gruppen Sverige AB and Betonmast Sverige AB was planned for 2023. By 2025 Betonmast Norway is reporting improved profitability (5.3% margin in Q1 2025). The multi-year drag is converting to improvement, though Sweden remains challenged.
  • Offshore: strategic pivot to growth via Claxton. The acquisition of Claxton Engineering Services (April 2026, GBP 63.5m for GBP 82m average revenue / ~14% operating margin business) is the most significant strategic move in the period. AF is doubling down on offshore decommissioning and adding offshore wind installation equipment. This is not defensive — it is growth-oriented M&A into a structurally expanding market.
  • Claxton is headquartered in Aberdeen, operates from Great Yarmouth, has ~400 employees, and owns a proprietary fleet of surface and subsea risers plus offshore wind installation equipment.
  • Transaction financed from AF's liquidity reserves (net cash position supports this). Norwegian Competition Authority approval pending as of May 2026.
  • Property development: de-emphasis. Homes sold collapsed from 216 (2022) to 53 (2023). No public exit announcement but the segment is visibly being wound down. This removes a loss-making drag and capital commitment from the balance sheet.
  • Sweden: restructuring, not exit. Sweden remains in the portfolio despite multi-year losses. The decision appears to be operational fix vs disposal. The best-performing Swedish units (Kanonaden, AF Prefab, AF Bygg Syd, AF Harnosand) demonstrate a quality portfolio within Sweden — the problem is concentrated in specific geographic units, suggesting the fix is personnel and project selection rather than market exit.

N36M:

  • Claxton closes Q2 2026 and immediately becomes the key execution watch-point. At GBP 63.5m EV for GBP 82m revenue and 14% operating margin, the acquisition looks financially attractive (EV ~0.8x revenue). Risk: fixed-price offshore work can deteriorate rapidly as the Dutch shelf episode demonstrated.
  • If 7% group EBIT margin is to be achieved by 2028, Sweden must exit loss-making territory, offshore must sustain profitability post-Dutch shelf wound, Claxton must not generate a provision, and Civil Engineering must hold 9-10% margins through the NTP cycle.
  • The strategy narrative ("project management, productivity, adaptability") is appropriate but generic. The specific mechanisms — better contract selectivity, stronger cost control systems, improved project manager accountability — are not publicly detailed. This makes independent verification difficult.

Degree of change: ORANGE — The 2028 strategy is a material reset from the failed growth playbook. Claxton is a significant structural addition to the portfolio. The direction is clearer, but credibility of the 7% target depends on three simultaneous recoveries (Sweden + Offshore + sustained margin improvement in core Norway).

*What would change it to Red:* Another large acquisition before Claxton is integrated, or a further offshore provision within 12 months of Claxton closing.

SECTION 3: CHANGE IN PRIORITIES/AMBITION

L12M:

  • CEO tone shifted measurably. Toftum's 2024 characterisation ("gap in performance has been too wide") is more self-critical than prior year language, which tended toward blaming market conditions (Swedish housing, cost inflation). This honesty is a positive signal in a sector prone to excusing underperformance.
  • Productivity and project management elevated to top priority. This replaces the prior emphasis on volume growth and market share. In construction, genuine productivity improvement requires better project selection, tighter contract structuring, and stronger site management accountability. The new strategy implies AF recognises it bid too aggressively or managed too loosely on contracts that required provisions.
  • ESG and sustainability ambition raised explicitly: New goal to halve climate/environmental footprint by 2028. Consistent with Norway's regulatory trajectory and enhances positioning on public sector tenders where ESG criteria increasingly influence award decisions. Zero serious personal injuries stated as a prerequisite for profitable growth — not a platitude in a sector where safety culture directly affects labour costs (insurance, retention, productivity).
  • Offshore ambition accelerated despite losses. The Claxton acquisition demonstrates that management has not lost conviction in the offshore growth story despite three years of provisions. This is either confidence based on pipeline visibility or under-appreciation of fixed-price execution risk. The 14% operating margin Claxton delivers (vs near-zero from Dutch shelf contracts) suggests AF is explicitly targeting the technology-differentiated, equipment-led part of offshore decom rather than the commodity installation/decom execution that caused losses.
  • Dividend raised 30%: NOK 6.50 for FY2025 (vs NOK 5.00 for FY2024). At ~NOK 31-35/share, this represents approximately 2% yield. The board is signalling financial recovery conviction with capital returns. This is a credibility signal — boards typically do not raise dividends 30% if they expect near-term earnings deterioration.
  • Capital discipline improving: Return on capital employed of 25.6% in Q1 2025 is above the 20% ROCE strategic target. This is a step-change from the ROIC of 6.22% cited in older data, suggesting FY2025 improvements are real and not just margin arithmetic.

N36M:

  • The pivot from growth to profitability in ambition language is positive but must be validated through project margins over the next 2-3 years. False Friend risk: improving order backlog (record NOK 44.7bn) does not guarantee improved margins if new contracts are mispriced.
  • The simultaneous 7% growth + 7% margin target creates inherent tension — growing into a soft Swedish market and expanding offshore (inherently high-variance) while trying to rebuild margin is a complex execution task.

Degree of change: ORANGE — Genuine and measurable shift in management rhetoric toward quality-over-quantity, supported by the Claxton acquisition rationale and 30% dividend increase. The question is whether behavioural change in project selection and site management matches the language.

*What would change it:* Delivery of 6%+ EBIT margin in FY2026 would confirm the pivot is real. A further provision in the Offshore segment would confirm it is not.

Key observations:

  • ❌ 2021-2024 financial targets definitively missed. Target: NOK 40bn revenue + 7% EBIT margin by 2024. Actual: NOK 30.6bn revenue, 3.5-4.5% margin across the period. Revenue shortfall ~NOK 9-10bn (25%); margin shortfall 250-350bps. A substantial miss that places AF Gruppen in the "aspirational targets not analytically grounded" category.
  • Recovery underway but incomplete. FY2025 margin improved to 5.2% (EBT basis), the best in L4Y, and Q4 2025 standalone margin reached 7.1% — the first quarter to hit the stated target. This is genuine improvement. But the full-year target of 7% has not been achieved, and offshore continues to drag.
  • Negative working capital is a structural positive. Working capital of NOK -3.4bn (FY2024) and NOK -3.9bn (FY2023) reflects advance payments from clients — typical for a Norwegian contractor with public sector dominance. FCF conversion is exceptionally strong: FY2025 operating cash flow NOK 3.04bn vs NOK 1.65bn EBT = ~185% conversion.
  • Balance sheet is clean. Net interest-bearing receivables of NOK 1.27bn at end-2025 (net cash). Credit facilities: NOK 2bn rolling with DNB + NOK 1.5bn sustainability-linked facility with Handelsbanken (2023). The Claxton acquisition (~NOK 800m) will consume liquidity reserves but will not stretch the balance sheet materially — net debt post-Claxton still below 0.5x EBT.
  • Sweden drag quantified. At NOK 3-4bn revenue and -2% to -3% margin vs Norway-equivalent ~5-6%, the Sweden drag equated to NOK 60-120m annual EBIT headwind for 3 years — NOK 180-360m cumulative opportunity cost. Improving in 2025 (Kepler upgrade driven partly by Sweden recovery).
  • 🚩 Offshore provisions: cockroach pattern. Three separate offshore project loss events: H2 2023 (Dutch continental shelf, first adjustment), August 2024 (Dutch continental shelf, second ~NOK 240m adjustment), Q4 2025 (AF Offshore Decom, ~NOK 100m loss). The aggregate write-down across these three events likely exceeds NOK 500m. This is not random project-by-project bad luck — it is a structural problem in the fixed-price offshore business model.
  • Peer comparison — EBIT margins (FY2024/2025 approximate):
  • Strabag: EBIT ~4.5% (EBITDA 9.4%, raising 2025 target to ≥4.5% EBIT)
  • Veidekke: ~4-5% EBIT (Norway-focused, comparable structure)
  • Skanska Group: ~4-5% EBIT (diversified geography, lower Norway concentration)
  • AF Gruppen FY2025: 5.2% EBT — at the upper end of Scandinavian peers, pulled by Civil Engineering's 9-10% margin
  • ROCE recovery. Q1 2025 ROCE of 25.6% signals the operational improvements are feeding through to capital returns. The strategic 20% ROCE target (previously unmet) appears achievable on a sustained basis if 2025 margins hold.

N3Y (2026-2028) outlook:

  • Consensus trajectory: NOK 33-36bn revenue by 2028 at 6-7% margin if Sweden and offshore resolve.
  • The 7% group EBIT margin by 2028 requires: (a) Civil Engineering holds ~9-10% margin through NTP cycle, (b) Construction/Betonmast/Norway reaches 5-6%, (c) Sweden reaches breakeven to +2%, (d) Offshore/Claxton generates 10%+ combined margin. All achievable individually — simultaneously more demanding.
  • Claxton's 14% operating margin, if sustained, is materially accretive to group offshore margins. The segment-level maths work if Claxton doesn't generate a provision.

Degree of change: ORANGE — Clear financial recovery in FY2025 after three years of underdelivery. FCF conversion and balance sheet are structurally sound. But 7% full-year group margin not yet achieved, and the three offshore provisions remain an unresolved structural concern. Backlog record is supportive but insufficient alone.

SECTION 5: VALUE CHAIN — DEMAND

Customer behaviour and volume trends:

  • Norway public sector: primary demand driver, structurally supportive. The National Transport Plan (NTP) 2025-2036, approved by Storting in spring 2024, allocates BNOK 95.2 over 12 years. Rail alone receives NKr 435bn (~US$40bn). Roads, bridges, tunnels, ports, and harbour infrastructure command substantial allocations. This is the backbone of AF Gruppen's civil engineering order pipeline. The E6 Roterud-Storhove contract alone (AF, Nye Veier client) is NOK 6.35bn, fixed-price, execution 2025-2030.
  • Order backlog at record NOK 44.72bn (end-2025) is ~1.4x annual revenue. Civil Engineering backlog specifically: NOK 17,966m at end-2025 (up from NOK 16,433m at end-2024). This suggests demand is running ahead of supply capacity in Norwegian civil — a supplier's market.
  • CQ-C1 implication — order book quality: Civil Engineering backlog is the highest-quality component: public sector client (Nye Veier, Statens vegvesen, Bane NOR), long duration (E6 runs to 2030), contract terms typically include index-linked price adjustment mechanisms protecting against input cost inflation. Construction backlog quality is more mixed — residential and commercial clients with greater cyclicality.
  • CQ-C5 implication — recurring vs lump-sum: Almost entirely project-based (fixed-price or remeasured lump-sum). No recurring revenue streams — no O&M, no FM, no concession income. This creates inherent earnings volatility and is the structural driver of the offshore provision pattern.
  • Norway housing: trough-to-recovery. Dwelling permits -16.8% in 2023 and -5.9% H1 2024. Rising interest rates and high construction costs drove the slowdown. But new home sales rose 20%+ in the year to early 2025, and Norway construction output is projected to grow at 3.4% AAGR 2025-2028. AF's Construction and Betonmast residential exposure should benefit from this cyclical tailwind.
  • Sweden: still weak but improving. Swedish construction remains subdued. AF's Swedish units reported near-zero margins in 2022-2024, with revenue declines of -29% in Q1 2024. Recovery is likely 1-2 years behind Norway. Kepler Cheuvreux's upgrade to Buy (mid-2025) was partly driven by evidence of Sweden turning the corner.
  • Offshore decom demand: structural growth. North Sea Offshore Decommissioning Market growing at >5% CAGR. BP Andrew field contract (awarded, runs to 2031-32), multiple Ithaca Energy contracts (2026 deliveries), and the Claxton acquisition together position AF as a top-tier decom player. Contract awards include facilities from the UK sector of the North Sea — NOK 225-275m per platform disassembly contract.
  • Offshore wind installation: emerging option. Claxton's equipment portfolio (marine risers, subsea installation tools) is applicable to offshore wind monopile installation and servicing. North Sea offshore wind build-out accelerating significantly through 2030. Very early stage for AF but a genuine growth option unlocked by the Claxton transaction.
  • Demand Pulse Reversion check: The record backlog warrants scepticism. Is this structural (NTP-driven multi-year) or a temporary front-loading? On balance, the NTP pipeline is multi-year, legally committed, and relatively insensitive to short-term fiscal pressures — unlike residential or commercial construction. However, order intake in Q4 2025 (NOK 9.59bn) was below Q4 2024 (NOK 12.51bn) — an early indicator of potential deceleration worth watching.
  • Norwegian defence: emerging demand. NATO 2% target pushes Norwegian defence infrastructure investment. Military bases, runways, fortifications, and logistics infrastructure are growing construction sub-segments where AF has relevant civil capabilities and Norwegian security clearances.

Degree of change: GREEN — Demand is genuinely strong and structurally supported. NTP is the most durable demand driver in the Nordic construction sector. The shift from housing-led to infrastructure-led demand is positive for AF's margin mix. The only negative demand signal is Q4 2025 order intake deceleration.

SECTION 6: VALUE CHAIN — COMPETITION

Industry structure:

  • Norwegian construction: concentrated oligopoly at the top. The market for contracts above NOK 500m is effectively restricted to 5-6 companies with balance sheet depth, bonding capacity, and track record: Veidekke (largest, ~NOK 37bn revenue), AF Gruppen (~NOK 32bn), Skanska Norge, NCC Norge, Eiffage Norway (international entrant), and Strabag for selective projects. This structural concentration creates rational pricing dynamics in good times and can produce dangerous underbidding in competitive downturns.
  • Veidekke outscaling AF. Veidekke's ~NOK 37bn revenue vs AF's ~NOK 32bn means Veidekke is pulling ahead. Historically Veidekke has also maintained slightly better margins. For AF to achieve 7% margins while growing at 7%, it must outperform Veidekke on profitability — not impossible but historically the harder comparative.
  • Sweden: intensely competitive with structural disadvantage. AF Gruppen entered Sweden at scale via Betonmast but into a market where Skanska, NCC, Peab, and Besqab have entrenched positions and lower-cost structures from decades of local optimisation. The Swedish unit losses reflect structural competitive disadvantage compounded by bad contracts — not solely market conditions.
  • Offshore decom: specialised niche, high barriers. AF Offshore Decom competes against Aker Solutions, Wood Group, and international players (Heerema, Allseas). The firm uses JV structures where capability gaps exist (the BP Andrew contract is a JV with THREE60 Energy). Claxton adds proprietary technology (risers, subsea equipment) that creates genuine differentiation — the 14% Claxton operating margin vs near-zero from commodity offshore contracts makes this case.
  • False Friends check — backlog growth does not equal margin growth. A record backlog is only valuable if it contains well-priced contracts. The Dutch shelf losses and Q4 2025 offshore provision occurred on contracts that were in the backlog when originally booked. The quality of each new contract win matters as much as its headline value.
  • New entrants and international competition: Eiffage Norway won the E18 Rugtvedt-Langangen road contract — a signal that French/European contractors are willing to compete on major Norwegian projects. However, sustained local presence requires Norwegian safety culture embed, local subcontractor relationships, and bonding capacity — meaningful barriers that limit international penetration to selective large contracts.
  • Pricing power assessment. In Norwegian civil, tight market conditions combined with a small bidder pool give contractors some pricing power — consistent with Civil Engineering maintaining 9-10% margins. In Swedish building, pricing power is low — excess capacity and weak demand. In offshore decom, fixed-price contracts remain standard, which structurally limits upside and concentrates execution risk on the contractor.

Degree of change: YELLOW — Competitive landscape stable but AF's relative position in Sweden is structurally challenged and requires resolution. Offshore differentiation increasing materially via Claxton. Civil Engineering is the crown jewel and remains competitively well-positioned in a supply-constrained market.

SECTION 7: VALUE CHAIN — DISRUPTION

Technology and new-model disruption:

  • BIM/digital project management: Norwegian public clients (Nye Veier, Bane NOR) now mandate BIM on major infrastructure projects. AF Gruppen is digitally competent but not a technology leader. The risk is that more productive foreign contractors deploying AI-assisted project management and cost estimation erode the local advantage over a 5-10 year horizon. Not an imminent threat.
  • Modular and prefabricated construction: AF Gruppen owns AF Prefab i Mälardalen (Sweden), cited as a strong-performing unit within the troubled Sweden segment. This demonstrates both capability and appetite for prefab. However, Norway's heavy use of cast-in-place concrete (seismic zone, high wind loads, complex ground conditions) limits the pace of modular adoption relative to UK, Germany, or Scandinavia generally.
  • AI in project estimation and risk management: Fixed-price contractors deploying AI-assisted cost estimation, ground condition modelling, and schedule optimisation could materially reduce provision frequency. The Dutch shelf losses and Swedish project write-downs suggest AF's current project controls are insufficient for complex offshore and challenging market conditions. AI tooling applied to contract risk assessment could directly address this structural weakness.
  • Offshore wind installation technology: Claxton's equipment portfolio (surface and subsea risers, installation tools) is applicable to offshore wind foundation installation and servicing. As North Sea offshore wind build-out accelerates (Netherlands, UK, Norway all expanding capacity significantly through 2030), demand for specialised installation equipment is growing rapidly. This is a genuine technology-adjacent growth option unlocked by the Claxton acquisition.
  • Carbon and green construction: Norway's construction sector faces regulatory and client pressure to reduce embodied carbon. Low-carbon concrete (geopolymer, SCM-based) is commercially available but adds cost. AF's 2028 goal to halve its climate footprint requires material investment in green construction methods — creating a cost headwind unless passed through to clients. Simultaneously, this is a tendering advantage on ESG-scored public contracts.
  • Labour market and automation: Norwegian construction wages are among Europe's highest. Labour productivity in construction has been effectively flat for 20 years sector-wide. Robotic formwork, autonomous plant machinery, and 3D concrete printing remain pre-commercial in civil engineering at scale. Limited near-term disruption but a medium-term productivity opportunity.

Degree of change: YELLOW — Disruption risk is real but medium-term. The most actionable signal is the Claxton acquisition opening an offshore wind equipment angle. AI-driven project risk management is the most relevant internal disruption watch-point given the provision history.

SECTION 8: VALUE CHAIN — POLITICAL AND REGULATORY

Norwegian political environment:

  • NTP 2025-2036 is the single most important political fact for AF Gruppen. BNOK 95.2 committed to transport infrastructure over 12 years, with the largest allocations in road and rail (NKr 435bn rail alone). Norwegian infrastructure investment has been bipartisan for decades — both Labour/Centre and Conservative/Progress governments have supported NTP spending. Political reversal risk is low.
  • Nye Veier model: beneficial for contractors. Nye Veier (state-owned road developer established 2015) operates on design-and-build contracts with clearer risk allocation and performance incentives than traditional Statens vegvesen road administration contracts. AF Gruppen's direct engagement with Nye Veier (E6 Roterud-Storhove) represents the best risk-adjusted contract structure available in Norwegian civil.
  • Norway: EU EEA member, not EU member. This matters for offshore regulatory purposes. Norwegian offshore decom operates under PSA (Petroleum Safety Authority Norway) rules — stringent but well-established. UK North Sea operations (growing via Claxton) operate under NSTA (North Sea Transition Authority) — different regulatory framework requiring navigation but well-understood in the sector.
  • UK Offshore: North Sea Transition Deal + operator windfall taxes. The UK political environment for North Sea operators (BP, Shell, Ithaca, Harbour Energy) has been turbulent — windfall taxes since 2022 have reduced operator cashflows and potentially compressed decom timing decisions. However, the NSTA actively enforces decom obligations. Net effect on AF/Claxton: uncertainty on individual project timing, but decommissioning cannot be permanently avoided.
  • Sweden construction: regulatory complexity. Swedish housing market is hampered by Plan and Building Act complexity and slow planning processes — a political problem that successive Swedish governments have acknowledged but not resolved. AF's Swedish exposure is partly held hostage to Swedish regulatory reform.
  • ESG regulation: CSRD and EU Green Taxonomy. AF Gruppen falls under CSRD as a large European-listed company. The climate halving goal (2028) is aligned with CSRD requirements. Compliance work is management bandwidth-consuming but not a direct business risk.
  • Norwegian labour law: Strong union tradition, IA agreements (inclusive workplace), and apprenticeship requirements. AF's safety culture and labour relations appear well-managed. The ZERO serious injuries prerequisite for profitable growth is stated publicly — consistent with a company that understands the link between safety culture, labour retention, and long-term cost structure.
  • 🚩 RARE: Norwegian Competition Authority review of Claxton. The acquisition is subject to NCA review. While AF is acquiring a UK entity, the NCA reviews acquisitions by Norwegian companies above certain thresholds. Approval is expected but creates a 1-2 quarter closing uncertainty.

Degree of change: GREEN — Political backdrop is firmly supportive. NTP is multi-year, bipartisan, and legally committed. UK decom regulatory obligations are structural demand generators. Main political risk is indirect: UK windfall taxation on operators (reducing cashflow available for decom spending) and Swedish housing regulatory inertia.

SECTION 9: VALUE CHAIN — SUPPLY SIDE

Supplier economics and input costs:

  • Labour: dominant input cost, tight in Norway. Norwegian construction wages are among Europe's highest. Labour constitutes ~35-40% of AF Gruppen's direct cost base. The construction sector labour shortage eased somewhat from the 2022 peak but skilled civil engineers, tunnel workers, and offshore decom technicians remain scarce. AF's apprenticeship programmes, safety culture, and established employer brand are competitive advantages in labour attraction — particularly in Norwegian civil engineering.
  • Materials: input cost inflation materially eased. Construction cost index rose 3.3% YoY in Q1 2024 (materials +3.9%, labour +3.1%) — well below the 2022 peak of ~8-10%. Fixed-price contractors who locked in 2021-2022 contracts before the inflation spike suffered; new contracts signed in 2024-2025 at post-spike input prices have significantly better cost recovery. This is a structural tailwind for 2025-2027 margins.
  • Subcontractors: AF Gruppen uses subcontractors extensively — approximately 40-50% of project costs on large civil contracts are subcontracted. In a tight Norwegian market, subcontractor availability and pricing directly affects margin. AF's scale gives preferential access to quality subcontractors and stronger negotiating position.
  • Equipment — Claxton adds capital intensity. Pre-Claxton, AF Gruppen's capex was modest (~2-3% of revenue). Claxton adds a meaningful proprietary equipment fleet (marine risers, subsea equipment, offshore wind installation tools). This creates an equipment-leasing revenue stream within offshore and adds some capital intensity — but the 14% operating margin on GBP 82m revenue suggests this model is highly cash-generative.
  • Energy costs: Norwegian structural advantage. Norwegian construction benefits from the country's abundant, cheap hydroelectric power. AF's Norwegian operations are sheltered from European energy cost shocks that affect UK (Claxton), Swedish, and Continental European peers. This is a structural input cost advantage vs Strabag, Sacyr, or Ferrovial.
  • Swedish input cost dynamics: Sweden faced sharper input cost inflation in 2022-23 (SEK weakness vs EUR, higher European energy exposure). AF's Swedish unit write-downs are partly attributable to contracts priced in 2021-22 at SEK-denominated inputs that then inflated faster than NOK equivalents. This dynamic is reversing as input costs normalise and the SEK stabilises.
  • Supply concentration: offshore decom recycling. For specialised offshore decom, the supply chain (heavy lift vessels, waste processing capacity) is highly concentrated globally. AF's ownership and operation of AF Environmental Base Vats (a 700,000m2 recycling and processing facility in Norway) provides vertical integration in platform disposal — a meaningful competitive moat in the Norwegian and North Sea decom segment that peers cannot easily replicate.

Degree of change: YELLOW — Input cost inflation has substantially eased from 2022 peak. The key supply-side risk is labour scarcity in Norwegian civil (tight market, structural wage inflation). Claxton adds equipment capital intensity to the supply chain profile — manageable but changes the asset base character of the offshore division.

SECTION 10: REVENUE/DEMAND CYCLE

Cyclicality, predictability, and cycle stage:

  • Norwegian civil engineering: mid-cycle with structural NTP floor. Norway's NTP-driven infrastructure cycle is long (12-year plans) and relatively insensitive to short-term economic fluctuations (government spending, not private capex). The NTP 2025-2036 is in its early execution years — peak construction activity typically lags plan launch by 3-5 years as projects progress through planning and procurement. This suggests peak civil activity for AF Gruppen is 2027-2030.
  • Norwegian building/construction: early-to-mid recovery. The 2022-2024 period was the cyclical trough driven by rising interest rates and high construction costs. Dwelling permits -16.8% in 2023 and -5.9% H1 2024. New home sales rose 20%+ in the year to early 2025, and Norway construction output is projected to grow 3.4% AAGR 2025-2028. AF's Construction and Betonmast residential exposure is in the early recovery phase — upside from here is meaningful.
  • Sweden: still at or near trough. Swedish construction more severely affected than Norway. AF's Swedish units reported near-zero to negative margins 2022-2024. Recovery likely 1-2 years behind Norway, timing dependent on Swedish rate normalisation and housing market recovery.
  • Offshore decom: secular growth, not cyclical. Decom is driven by platform age and regulatory obligation, not oil price directly (though low oil prices delay timing decisions). The structural aging of North Sea fields (UK, Norway, Netherlands) means decom volumes are accelerating regardless of the oil price cycle. The BP Andrew field contract runs to 2031-32 — a multi-year revenue commitment.
  • Order backlog as forward indicator. Record NOK 44.72bn at end-2025 (1.4x revenue) suggests demand is running ahead of capacity. Historically in Norwegian construction, this is associated with improved contract pricing — consistent with Q4 2025 Civil Engineering margin of 9.5%. However, Q4 2025 order intake (NOK 9.59bn) was below Q4 2024 (NOK 12.51bn) — worth monitoring for a potential deceleration.
  • Demand Pulse Reversion check. The record backlog warrants scepticism — is this structural or front-loaded? The E6 Roterud-Storhove alone (NOK 6.35bn) is transformative for one year's intake. The risk is that 2024-2025 intake represents a cluster of major NTP awards that does not repeat at the same rate in 2026-2027. This is a material risk to the FY2026-27 revenue growth story.
  • FCF cycle dynamics. AF's negative working capital means it generates cash ahead of revenue recognition. In an expanding cycle, working capital becomes a cash source; in a declining cycle, it reverses sharply. FY2025 FCF conversion of ~185% (operating cash flow / EBT) likely reflects a working capital inflow cycle. Sustainable FCF conversion is more likely ~150-165%. Still excellent by construction sector standards.

Degree of change: GREEN — The demand cycle is in a favourable phase: Norwegian civil at or near peak activity phase, Norwegian building recovering, offshore decom structurally growing. The only negative cycle signals are Sweden (still trough), Q4 2025 order intake deceleration, and the risk of NTP contract lumping creating a 2026-27 intake air pocket.

SECTION 11: VALUE CHAIN — MACROECONOMIC

L18M (Nov 2024 – May 2026):

  • Norges Bank rate cycle: Norges Bank held rates at 4.5% through late 2024 before beginning a cautious easing cycle. By Q1-Q2 2026, the policy rate is approximately 3.5-4.0%. This easing has begun to unlock the Norwegian residential market (new home sales +20% YoY in early 2025). Direct benefit to AF's Construction and Betonmast residential pipelines, with a 6-12 month lag from rate cuts to groundbreaking.
  • Norwegian economy: resilient and fiscally insulated. Norway's petroleum fund (Government Pension Fund Global) provides fiscal insulation unavailable to EU peers. GDP growth ~1.5-2% in 2025. Construction sector as % of GDP at 5.2% — above EU average. Employment tight. The government's ability to sustain NTP spending is not meaningfully constrained by economic conditions.
  • Input cost inflation: materially moderating. Construction cost index growth slowed to ~3% in 2024 from 8-10% in 2022. Energy price normalisation, supply chain recovery, and moderating wage growth all support margin recovery. This is one of the key structural enablers of the 2025 profitability improvement — new contracts are priced with accurate cost assumptions.
  • NOK currency: The Norwegian krone has been relatively weak vs EUR and GBP throughout 2023-2025. This is minor for AF's domestic Norwegian operations, marginally negative for Sweden (SEK-denominated revenues) but not a primary driver.
  • European macro: soft but limited direct impact. Germany in technical recession, France under fiscal pressure, Sweden/Finland contracting. AF's direct European exposure is limited (Norway-dominant, Sweden only). However, weak European demand reduces Norwegian export revenues over time, creating second-order fiscal pressure. Not an immediate risk given Norway's sovereign wealth fund.

N3Y (2026-2028):

  • NTP execution cycle: The 2025-2036 NTP represents the most visible structural demand driver. E6, E18, railway renewal, port and harbour programmes will sustain contract awards through 2029-2030. AF is positioned to capture ~20-25% of major Norwegian civil volume. This is the most durable multi-year macro tailwind available in Nordic construction.
  • European defence spending uplift. NATO 2% GDP target is driving Norwegian and broader Nordic defence infrastructure investment. Military bases, runways, fortifications, and logistics infrastructure represent a growing construction sub-segment. AF's civil engineering capability and Norwegian security clearances position it well for this emerging demand category.
  • North Sea energy transition. The EU REPowerEU programme and UK Energy Security Strategy both drive offshore wind capacity expansion. North Sea operators are simultaneously accelerating decommissioning (aging fields) and offshore wind installation (new fields). AF/Claxton is positioned for both vectors. This is potentially the most materially undervalued demand driver in AF's medium-term portfolio.
  • Norwegian carbon pricing. Norway's carbon tax trajectory (NOK 2,000+/tonne CO2e by 2030) creates dual effects: cost pressure on embodied carbon in construction, and demand opportunity from energy efficiency retrofits, renewable energy infrastructure, and green building requirements. AF's ESG commitments and Energy and Environment segment position it to capture this demand.
  • Swedish macro recovery. Sweden likely turns positive for construction in 2026-2027 as rate normalisation unlocks housing demand. AF's Sweden segment should benefit — but competitive position remains structurally challenging and recovery may be more muted than macro improvement suggests.

Degree of change: GREEN — The macro backdrop for 2026-2028 is supportive. Norway's fiscal insulation, NTP execution, and offshore energy transition all drive demand. Input cost moderation supports margin expansion. Sweden recovery is a medium-term tailwind. The main macro risk is a sharper-than-expected European recession reducing Norwegian export revenues and NTP fiscal capacity — currently a low-probability scenario.

SECTION 12: HISTORICAL TRACK RECORD OF DELIVERY

L3Y delivery vs guidance and expectations:

FY2022:

  • Revenue: NOK 31.2bn — well below the NOK 40bn 2024 target trajectory (would have required 28% growth over 2 years)
  • EBIT margin: 4.5% — well below the 7% 2024 target
  • CEO acknowledged Construction and Sweden profitability as "not satisfactory" — public candour but too late
  • Betonmast integration ongoing; Swedish units immediately loss-making post-acquisition
  • ❌ Material miss vs medium-term targets set in 2020-21; management awareness of problem but no rapid remedy

FY2023:

  • Revenue approximately NOK 30.6bn — flat to declining despite a growth-led strategy
  • EBIT margin approximately 3.5% — deterioration from 2022
  • Major write-downs across three segments simultaneously:
  • Betonmast Boligbygg (property development) — significant project portfolio write-downs
  • Goodwill write-down NOK 20m (Q4 2023)
  • Swedish unit goodwill impairments
  • Dutch continental shelf offshore provision: First large offshore project adjustment in H2 2023 — precise amount not confirmed separately from 2024 adjustment
  • Property development effectively collapsed: homes sold 53 vs 216 in 2022
  • Sweden: negative result, revenue trajectory declining sharply
  • ❌❌ Worst year of the period. Multiple simultaneous negative surprises across property, Sweden, and offshore — a cockroach cluster confirming systemic rather than isolated execution problems.

FY2024:

  • Revenue NOK 30.64bn — still below FY2022 level, confirming the NOK 40bn target was completely unachievable
  • EBT margin 3.6% — marginal improvement on FY2023 but below FY2022 delivery
  • Second Dutch continental shelf provision: ~NOK 240m adjustment August 2024
  • Q4 2024: offshore segment again loss-making (~NOK 100m loss), driving Q4 consensus miss despite record revenue
  • Building segment: 48% above consensus (a structural positive — core Norway contracting is improving)
  • Civil Engineering: 9.5% margin (the clear crown jewel)
  • Dividend: NOK 5.00/share maintained — board chose not to cut despite three years of underperformance
  • Kepler Cheuvreux upgrade to Buy driven by Q2 2025 results — implying analyst consensus was already de-risked by then

  • Revenue NOK 31.99bn — recovering but NOK 8bn below the 2024 target set in 2021
  • EBT margin 5.2% — best in L4Y but still 180bps below 7% stated target
  • Q4 2025 standalone EBT margin 7.1% — first quarter to hit the target
  • Offshore: STILL loss-generating in Q4 2025 (~NOK 100m write-down) — third consecutive year of offshore provisions
  • Order backlog: record NOK 44.72bn
  • Operating cash flow: NOK 3.04bn (strong FCF conversion)
  • Dividend: NOK 6.50 (+30%) — management signalling conviction in recovery

Track record verdict:

  • Delivery vs own guidance: Consistently and materially poor across FY2021-2024 on every stated target (revenue and margin). Recovery in FY2025 is genuine and measurable but the targets were not met.
  • Delivery vs quarterly consensus: Mixed picture. Building segment repeatedly surprises positively. Offshore segment repeatedly surprises negatively. Civil Engineering is generally in-line to slightly positive. Sweden has been a consistent negative surprise. The pattern is consistent with a management team that understands its core Norway civil and building operations but has struggled with complexity additions (Betonmast integration, offshore fixed-price, Sweden market).
  • 🚩 Cockroaches Never Alone — CONFIRMED. Three offshore provisions in three consecutive years (FY2023, FY2024, FY2025). This is not random project-level bad luck — it is a structural execution problem in the offshore fixed-price contracting model. The Claxton acquisition adds another offshore fixed-price entity to the portfolio. Significant attention required.
  • Turnarounds Take Longer Than You Think — LIVE ILLUSTRATION. AF Gruppen's operational turnaround has now consumed 3-4 years of management attention and investor patience without delivering the 7% full-year target. The new 2028 strategy extends the runway by another three years. This is a classic pattern in construction sector turnarounds where margin improvement is sequentially impeded by new problem areas.
  • Transformation Play / Q-Turn status: Partially in progress. Civil is stable and excellent (9-10% margin). Construction is recovering. Betonmast is improving (5.3% Q1 2025). Sweden: nascent recovery. Offshore: genuinely unclear — three provisions suggest a structural problem in the business model, not project-specific bad luck. The Q-Turn is real in some segments and not yet real in others.

Degree of change: RED — The delivery track record across L3Y is materially below targets on both revenue and margin dimensions. Multiple concurrent negative surprises over a 36-month window (property, offshore, Sweden) suggest systemic execution challenges, not isolated events. Management has been increasingly candid but delivery has consistently lagged rhetoric. The 2025 recovery is genuine but partial, and the offshore provision in Q4 2025 means the worst problem area has not been resolved.

*What would change it to Orange:* Two consecutive years (FY2026-2027) of 6%+ full-year EBIT margin with zero offshore provisions. That would establish a new delivery credibility baseline.
