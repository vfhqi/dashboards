Overall Summary

Instalco AB's fundamental investment case over the next 24 months hinges on the company's ability to execute a comprehensive operational turnaround—dubbed "Instalco 2.0"—following founder Per Sjöstrand's return as CEO in mid-2025, with the primary strategic objective being the restoration of EBITA margins to the historical target of at least 8.0% from the current 6.4% achieved in full-year 2025. The equity narrative is characterized by heightened execution risk stemming from elevated financial leverage, a deliberate pause in M&A activity, and persistent macroeconomic headwinds affecting the Nordic construction sector. The following synthesis presents the stack-ranked key drivers that will determine share price performance through 2027, integrating conservative financial analyses, bearish risk scenarios, and critical assessments of management's self-help initiatives.

The margin recovery trajectory represents the single most critical near-term catalyst, with Instalco reporting a 7.2% EBITA margin in Q4 2025—up from 5.4% in the prior-year period and marking the first year-over-year EBITA growth in seven quarters—though the full-year 2025 adjusted margin of 6.4% remains materially below the 8% target. Sell-side expectations remain cautiously optimistic, with DNB Carnegie forecasting a 7.0% adjusted EBITA margin for 2026 and 7.6% for 2027, translating to 2026 net sales of SEK 14,264 million, EBITDA of SEK 1,453 million, EBIT of SEK 944 million, and adjusted EPS of SEK 2.57, while noting approximately 15% upside potential to earnings if the full 8% target is achieved ahead of schedule. However, the bearish counterargument highlights structural risks including aggressive tender competition driven by the Swedish construction downturn, persistent pricing pressure that limits profitable add-on contracts, and the potential for severe summer under-absorption of the workforce similar to the utilization collapse experienced in Q3 2025.

Balance sheet management constitutes the second critical driver, with net debt to EBITDA improving sequentially from a 3.3x peak at the end of Q3 2025 to 2.8x by year-end, supported by robust operating cash flows that delivered a rolling twelve-month cash conversion rate of 108%, comfortably exceeding management's 100% target. DNB Carnegie projects rapid deleveraging with bank-defined leverage estimated at approximately 2.8x by year-end 2025, falling to 2.0x by 2026, 1.4x by 2027, and 1.0x by 2028, with the recent Board proposal to reduce the dividend to SEK 0.50 per share—down from consensus expectations of SEK 0.68—expected to further accelerate debt reduction in the first half of 2026. The bearish perspective posits severe downside risks, as DNB Carnegie explicitly warns that given the high financial risk embedded in the balance sheet, any major execution missteps could trigger the necessity of a capital raise, and if bank-defined leverage breaches the 3.0x threshold during structurally weak mid-year quarters, Instalco might violate debt covenants, forcing a highly dilutive equity issuance to satisfy lenders and stabilize the decentralized group.

The deliberate pause in M&A activity—historically contributing 20% to 26% of annual revenue growth between 2019 and 2022 with average annual capital deployment of approximately SEK 800 million—has fundamentally altered the equity story, as elevated leverage peaking at 3.3x net debt to EBITDA in Q3 2025 forced management to strictly limit new acquisitions to prioritize debt reduction, cash conversion, and operational efficiency within the existing portfolio. The strategic entry into Germany via the multi-step acquisition of Fabri AG—where Instalco acquired an initial 24% minority stake for approximately SEK 173 million in late 2024 and currently anticipates exercising its option to acquire a majority stake during H2 2026—represents a critical future growth catalyst, providing access to a market approximately five times larger than the combined Nordics. DNB Carnegie estimates that triggering the stage-two call option in 2026 would require a cash outlay of approximately SEK 350 million and could increase the group's pro forma EBITDA by roughly SEK 140 million, or approximately 10%. The bearish interpretation emphasizes that intensifying competition from deep-pocketed peers and aggressive private equity firms threatens to drive up acquisition multiples, post-acquisition synergies are rarely immediate with cultural friction often meaning "one plus one isn't two," and Instalco's decentralized model relies heavily on retaining original founders through structural earn-outs and three-year lock-in agreements, after which many entrepreneurial managers bristle at corporate oversight and opt to leave the conglomerate, triggering severe talent drain and loss of critical local client relationships.

Organic growth dynamics reached a critical inflection point as Q4 2025 reported positive organic growth of 4.9%—the first meaningful organic expansion in several quarters—particularly driven by the Swedish segment and strong industrial project wins in Finland, though full-year 2025 organic growth remained slightly negative at minus 0.3%. The order backlog reached SEK 9,510 million at the end of 2025, representing an organic increase of 7.5% for comparable units, with management deliberately prioritizing profitability over volume through strict discipline in project selection to avoid locking the company into low-margin contracts during the market trough. The structural shift toward recurring service and maintenance contracts—which reached 38% of total group sales in Q4 2025, up dramatically from only 11% in 2018—provides a highly stable, high-margin revenue stream that insulates the business from the cyclical new construction market. Skeptical analysts argue that the Nordic construction downturn created massive overcapacity leading to cutthroat tender competition and structural pricing pressure, and if a significant portion of Instalco's SEK 9.5 billion backlog was secured during this period of depressed pricing, the execution of these fixed-price contracts could lead to severe margin erosion over the next 12 to 18 months.

Macroeconomic headwinds remain a persistent overhang, as the Nordic installation market remains heavily exposed to the sharp downturn in Swedish and Finnish residential construction triggered by rising interest rates and elevated material prices from geopolitical conflicts, though recent Riksbank policy rate cuts to 1.75% have initiated a cautious recovery in consumer sentiment while the overall construction environment remains fragile. The bearish "lower for longer" thesis posits that the anticipated recovery in Swedish and Finnish housing markets is a temporary illusion driven by minor interest rate adjustments rather than structural demand recovery, with high construction material costs and chronic skilled labor shortages remaining structural barriers preventing a return to pre-2022 volume levels, potentially forcing Instalco and peers into intensified price wars for a shrinking pool of public contracts that would structurally erode installation margins and render current sell-side forecasts highly optimistic.

The Instalco 2.0 internal efficiency program introduces a minimum operational baseline for project management, risk assessment, and profitability follow-up across all subsidiaries, enforced through a new country-based organizational structure featuring dedicated Chief Operating Officers and an internal mentorship program where leaders from highly profitable branches actively coach executives of underperforming units. Furthermore, aggressive FTE reductions intended to protect near-term profitability could severely hollow out local production capacity, with the bearish thesis positing that this capacity destruction would leave the company structurally unable to capitalize on new project volumes when the Nordic construction market eventually recovers, permanently impairing long-term organic growth.

Industry-wide cash flow pressures compound the operational challenges, as recent survey data indicates that 69% of finance leaders observed an increase in late customer payments over the past twelve months, forcing companies to tie up more capital in accounts receivable and adding structural friction to the cash conversion cycle. Looking forward, the investment thesis is contingent upon management successfully threading a narrow operational needle: delivering margin expansion through centralized efficiency measures without alienating the entrepreneurial culture that historically drove acquisitive growth, reducing leverage sufficiently to resume M&A activity by late 2026 while maintaining covenant headroom through seasonal cash flow troughs, and executing profitably on a backlog that may have been secured under depressed pricing conditions during the construction downturn. The analysis reveals significant asymmetric risk, as any significant operational misstep, prolonged margin compression, or failure to enforce the new Instalco 2.0 strictures without causing internal friction and talent attrition could severely strain covenants given the high net debt to EBITDA ratio of 2.8x at the end of 2025, potentially forcing a dilutive capital raise. Conversely, successful execution of the turnaround roadmap, coupled with a resumption of M&A activity and gradual Nordic construction market recovery, could unlock the material upside embedded in current sell-side estimates that deliberately maintain conservatism regarding the pace and sustainability of margin normalization back to the 8% target.

Key Driver 1: Margin Recovery and the 8% EBITA Target The 'Instalco 2.0' Roadmap and the Founder's Return

Instalco's primary financial and strategic priority is restoring its EBITA margin to its historical target of at least 8.0%. Following a period of margin compression and elevated leverage, founder and main shareholder Per Sjöstrand returned as CEO in mid-2025 to lead a turnaround. The recovery roadmap, dubbed "Instalco 2.0," shifts the corporate focus from pure acquisition-driven volume growth to rigorous operational excellence and margin protection. This framework introduces a new country-based organizational structure with dedicated Chief Operating Officers designed to enforce continuous improvement and stricter risk management. Management has also implemented a mentorship program where leaders from high-performing subsidiaries coach the CEOs of underperforming units.

To bridge the gap back to the 8% target, the company has identified critical operational levers, including increasing revenue per full-time equivalent (FTE), normalizing annual bad debt provisions, and consolidating back-office functions. Progress is actively monitored through new operational KPIs that prioritize profitability and disciplined project selection over top-line expansion.

Recent Performance and Plausibility of the 8% Target

Recent quarterly results suggest that the margin stabilization efforts are beginning to yield tangible benefits. In the fourth quarter of 2025, Instalco reported an EBITA margin of 7.2%, up from 5.4% in the prior-year period. This marked the first time in seven quarters that the company achieved year-over-year EBITA growth, breaking a prolonged pattern of margin decline. For the full year 2025, the adjusted EBITA margin landed at 6.4%, which remains below the 8% target but demonstrates resilience in a highly challenged construction market.

The plausibility of reaching the 8% threshold hinges on the continued optimization of the Swedish segment and a sustained shift toward higher-margin service and maintenance contracts. During Q4 2025, service revenues remained strong, accounting for 38% of total net sales. Furthermore, the company's order backlog of installation projects exceeded last-twelve-months (LTM) installation sales, providing a solid foundation for improved capacity utilization heading into 2026.

Sell-Side Expectations and Forecast Shifts

Analysts have closely tracked Instalco's margin trajectory, gradually adjusting expectations as the turnaround takes shape.

DNB Carnegie anticipates an adjusted EBITA margin of 7.0% in 2026 and 7.6% in 2027, taking a cautious stance that leaves approximately 15% upside potential to earnings if the full 8% target is achieved ahead of schedule. For 2026, the broker forecasts net sales of SEK 14,264 million, EBITDA of SEK 1,453 million, EBIT of SEK 944 million, and adjusted EPS of SEK 2.57. Following the stronger-than-expected Q4 2025 results, DNB Carnegie raised its 2026 and 2027 adjusted EPS estimates by 3%, driven by improved revenue outlooks in Sweden and a 30 basis point margin hike for the Rest of Nordics segment.

Key Driver 2: Leverage, Cash Conversion, and Balance Sheet Risk Current Balance Sheet Health and Cash Conversion Trajectory

Instalco's leverage and cash conversion metrics have become central focal points for investors evaluating the company's financial resilience. At the end of the fourth quarter of 2025, the company's net debt to EBITDA ratio stood at 2.8x, representing a significant sequential improvement from the 3.3x peak recorded at the end of the third quarter. This deleveraging was driven by robust operating cash flows, resulting in a rolling twelve-month cash conversion rate of 108%, comfortably exceeding management's 100% target. This strong cash generation was heavily supported by net working capital (NWC) releases, as the company intensified its focus on invoicing efficiency and the collection of accounts receivable. Management has emphasized that improvements in the percentage of completion (POC) ratio and disciplined cash flow forecasting at the subsidiary level have been critical to reinforcing the balance sheet.

Seasonality and Capital Allocation Pressures

The company's working capital profile is highly seasonal, characterized by a build-up in unbilled revenues and contract assets during the summer months, followed by a strong flush of invoicing in the fourth quarter. Consequently, leverage tends to peak intra-year during the second and third quarters. This mid-year peak is further exacerbated by the scheduled payment of shareholder dividends, minority interest buyouts, and performance-based earn-outs tied to historical acquisitions. To secure long-term financial flexibility, Instalco extended its SEK 3.4 billion credit facility in 2025, ensuring adequate liquidity margins to cover these seasonal outflows. Furthermore, the credit facility includes mechanisms that link interest rates to the achievement of specific sustainability KPIs, offering marginal interest cost relief if environmental targets are met.

Sell-Side Expectations and Covenant Headroom

DNB Carnegie analysts view Instalco's financial health as materially better than the headline metrics might suggest. The broker highlights that while reported leverage hit 3.3x in late 2025, the calculation used for bank covenants relies on an adjusted EBITDA figure. This definition explicitly excludes extraordinary items such as the SEK 64 million receivables write-down related to Northvolt and SEK 65 million in restructuring costs. Adjusting for these factors, bank-defined leverage was estimated to be just below 3.0x during the seasonal peak and declined to approximately 2.8x by year-end. Looking ahead, the broker projects a rapid deleveraging profile, forecasting net debt to adjusted EBITDA to fall to 2.0x by 2026, 1.4x by 2027, and 1.0x by 2028. The recent Board proposal to reduce the dividend to SEK 0.50 per share—down from consensus expectations of SEK 0.68—is expected to further accelerate debt reduction in the first half of 2026.

Peer Comparisons and Macroeconomic Pressures

Macroeconomic conditions continue to strain cash flows across the broader corporate landscape, directly impacting the installation sector. Recent survey data indicates that 69% of finance leaders have observed an increase in late customer payments over the past twelve months. This industry-wide deterioration in payment reliability forces companies to tie up more capital in accounts receivable, adding structural friction to the cash conversion cycle. Peer companies in the Nordic installation market have grappled with identical cash flow dynamics. Bravida Holding experienced a sharp decline in cash conversion to 63% during its third quarter, driven by the build-up of contractual assets on large, long-term projects where advanced payments were amortized. However, Bravida managed to recover to a 79% cash conversion rate by the end of the year and boasts a much stronger balance sheet overall, with a net debt to EBITDA ratio of just 1.1x. Similarly, Assemblin Caverion Group maintains strict financial discipline, generating a rolling twelve-month cash conversion rate of 107.5% at the end of its fiscal year and targeting a net debt to adjusted EBITDA ratio of less than 2.5x.

The Bearish View: Covenant Breach and Capital Raise Risks

Despite the recent working capital improvements, a bearish interpretation poses severe risks to Instalco's equity narrative. DNB Carnegie explicitly warns that given the high financial risk currently embedded in the balance sheet, any major missteps in execution could trigger the necessity of a capital raise. A short seller might argue that the Q4 2025 cash flow beat was merely a temporary year-end flush of receivables that will immediately reverse in early 2026, trapping capital right as seasonal demands peak. If the Nordic construction recovery stalls, the company could face prolonged under-absorption and shrinking organic EBITDA, rapidly eroding its covenant headroom. In such a scenario, planned capital allocations, such as the multi-phase acquisition options for the German platform Fabri AG, could become significant cash drains that the balance sheet cannot support without fresh equity. Should bank-defined leverage breach the 3.0x threshold during the structurally weak mid-year quarters, Instalco might violate its debt covenants, forcing a highly dilutive equity issuance to satisfy lenders and stabilize the decentralized group.

Key Driver 3: Resumption of M&A-Driven Growth Historical Reliance on M&A and the Current Acquisition Pause

Instalco's historical growth strategy has relied heavily on the continuous acquisition of profitable, high-quality installation companies with strong local market ties. Between 2019 and 2022, this aggressive M&A agenda served as the company's primary engine for expansion, contributing between 20% and 26% to its annual revenue growth. Over the past five years, the company's average annual capital deployment for acquisitions amounted to approximately SEK 800 million. However, this rapid consolidation phase has been deliberately paused by management to prioritize debt reduction, cash conversion, and operational efficiency within the existing portfolio. Elevated leverage, which peaked at 3.3x net debt to EBITDA in the third quarter of 2025 before settling near 2.8x at year-end, forced the company to strictly limit new M&A activity to safeguard its long-term financial flexibility and covenant headroom.

Sell-Side Forecasts and Shifting Expectations

Analysts highlight that M&A has historically been the critical earnings growth driver for Instalco, and the current absence of this engine has fundamentally altered the equity story. DNB Carnegie notes that without active M&A supplementing the top line, organic earnings faced severe downward pressure throughout 2024 and 2025, prompting consistent downward revisions to sell-side EPS and EBITA estimates over the last 12 months. The broker argues that the ongoing pause in acquisitions heavily restricts the stock's near-term valuation upside, as the market is no longer pricing in the premium typically awarded to aggressive serial acquirers. However, if the balance sheet rapidly deleverages toward the 2.5x target by mid-2026, a resumption of M&A activity could serve as a major catalyst for multiple expansion, potentially driving the valuation back toward its historical average of 14.0x to 14.5x forward EBITA.

The Fabri AG Acquisition and German Expansion

A central pillar of the future M&A narrative is the multi-step acquisition of the German installation group Fabri AG, which represents a strategic entry into a market approximately five times larger than the combined Nordics. In late 2024, Instalco acquired an initial 24% minority stake in Fabri for approximately SEK 173 million, paid largely through newly issued shares. Fabri shares Instalco's highly decentralized, acquisition-driven DNA and has already rapidly expanded its own platform to encompass 22 local operating companies. Instalco's management team currently anticipates exercising its option to acquire a majority stake in Fabri during the second half of 2026, assuming the German platform meets its pre-negotiated profitability thresholds.

Key Driver 4: Organic Growth, Order Backlog, and Pricing Dynamics Organic Revenue Momentum and Recent Performance

After a prolonged period of declining volumes, Instalco has reached a critical inflection point in its organic revenue trajectory. In the fourth quarter of 2025, the company reported that organic growth turned positive, reaching 4.9%. This marked a significant operational milestone, as it was the first time in several quarters that organic expansion meaningfully supported the top line in a highly challenging market. This recovery was particularly driven by the Swedish segment and strong performance in securing industrial projects in Finland. Despite this strong finish to the year, the full-year 2025 organic growth remained slightly negative at minus 0.3%, reflecting the severe market headwinds experienced in the first half of the year.

Order Backlog Quality and the Shift to Service

The plausibility of sustaining positive organic growth is heavily supported by the recent expansion and quality of Instalco's order backlog. At the end of 2025, the total order backlog reached SEK 9,510 million, representing an organic increase of 7.5% for comparable units. Management has deliberately prioritized profitability over sheer volume, maintaining strict discipline in project selection to avoid locking the company into low-margin contracts during the market trough.

To further insulate the business from the highly cyclical new construction market, Instalco has structurally shifted its revenue mix toward recurring service and maintenance contracts. Service revenues are not included in the traditional order backlog but provide a highly stable, high-margin revenue stream. By the fourth quarter of 2025, service and maintenance work accounted for 38% of total group sales. This represents a dramatic strategic evolution from 2018, when service work constituted only 11% of total sales.

Peer Dynamics and Macro Context

The broader Nordic construction and installation market continues to operate well below its historical peaks. Total market volumes in the Swedish installation sector remain approximately 15% below the peak levels seen in 2021, largely due to the collapse of new residential construction and elevated material costs triggered by geopolitical conflicts. Peer commentary confirms these mixed market conditions. Caverion has similarly noted that while service demand remains robust, the projects market continues to be impacted by delays in decision-making and severe price pressure in new construction.

Bravida has echoed this sentiment, reporting that while order intake has shown pockets of strong growth, aggressive price pressure remains highly concentrated in southern Sweden and Finland.

The Bearish View: Pricing Pressure and Margin Erosion

Despite the optical improvements in organic growth and backlog size, a skeptical short-seller could argue that the quality of this backlog remains highly suspect. The Nordic construction downturn has created massive overcapacity in the sector, leading to cutthroat tender competition and structural pricing pressure as desperate firms bid aggressively just to keep their workforces employed. A bearish thesis would posit that as Instalco attempts to enforce its new "Instalco 2.0" efficiency mandates, local managers may struggle to deliver these low-priced projects profitably, leading to project write-downs and a failure to meet consensus earnings expectations.

Key Driver 5: Macroeconomic Headwinds and the Construction Cycle The Nordic Construction Downturn and End-Market Demand

The Nordic installation market remains heavily exposed to broader macroeconomic cycles, particularly the sharp downturn in Swedish and Finnish residential construction. Rising interest rates and elevated material prices triggered by geopolitical conflicts previously suppressed new housing starts to historically low levels. While recent policy rate cuts by the Riksbank to 1.75% have initiated a cautious recovery in consumer sentiment, the overall construction environment remains fragile. Public sector spending has acted as a critical counterweight to this private market weakness. Government investments in defense, healthcare, correctional facilities, and water infrastructure continue to drive baseline demand across Sweden and Norway. In Finland, the macroeconomic environment remains particularly challenging, with residential construction virtually stalled and growth relying almost entirely on the energy transition and industrial projects.

Peer Commentary and Industry Dynamics

Commentary from across the Nordic building technology and construction sector highlights a unified strategy of prioritizing margins over volume in this suppressed environment.

Assemblin Caverion Group has noted that despite challenging market conditions, underlying order intake has remained resilient in specific public niches, even as private building development lags.

Construction peer Peab confirms that while the housing market stabilized at a low level in late 2025, broader industry growth is heavily dependent on public investments in civil engineering and defense infrastructure.

Jefferies observes that contractors across Europe have actively shifted away from high-risk fixed-price contracts to protect profitability amid supply chain disruptions and persistent input cost inflation.

Sell-Side EPS and Revenue Revisions

The persistent macroeconomic headwinds have triggered multiple downward revisions to sell-side forecasts for Instalco over the past 12 months.

DNB Carnegie significantly cut its 2025 to 2027 adjusted EBITA estimates by 4% to 7% following a disappointing third quarter in 2025. The broker also reduced its 2025 adjusted EPS estimates due to higher anticipated net financial costs and severe margin contraction in the Swedish business caused by summer under-absorption. Further estimate trims occurred later in the year, with DNB Carnegie cutting 2025 adjusted EPS by another 1% to reflect lower organic revenue assumptions and the near-term costs of internal efficiency measures.

The Bearish Perspective: A 'Lower for Longer' Scenario

A skeptical short-seller could easily construct a 'lower for longer' thesis regarding the Nordic construction cycle. Under this bearish scenario, the anticipated recovery in the Swedish and Finnish housing markets is merely a temporary illusion driven by minor interest rate adjustments, rather than a structural return of demand. High construction material costs and a chronic shortage of skilled labor remain structural barriers that will prevent the industry from returning to pre-2022 volume levels.

If private end-market demand fails to accelerate meaningfully in 2026, Instalco and its peers will face intensified price wars for a shrinking pool of available public contracts. This cutthroat competition would structurally erode installation margins, delay the absorption of overhead costs, and render current sell-side EPS and revenue forecasts highly optimistic.

Key Driver 6: Internal Efficiency and 'Instalco 2.0' Execution The 'Instalco 2.0' Restructuring and Cost-Saving Initiatives

Instalco's transition from an acquisition-heavy growth model to an internally focused efficiency strategy is encapsulated in the 'Instalco 2.0' initiative. This comprehensive operational framework introduces a minimum standard, or "baseline," for project management, risk assessment, and profitability follow-up across all subsidiaries. To enforce these operational standards, management implemented a new country-based organizational structure featuring dedicated Chief Operating Officers tasked with monitoring local execution. The company also launched an internal mentorship program where leaders from highly profitable branches actively coach the executives of underperforming units.

Tangible cost-saving measures began aggressively in late 2024, including the termination of approximately 100 subcontractor agreements and the reduction of 110 full-time employees (FTEs). Management simultaneously executed the closure or merger of eight underperforming subsidiaries that had collectively generated negative EBITA. These structural adjustments resulted in one-off restructuring costs of approximately SEK 65 million and a non-cash asset write-down of SEK 25 million. Beyond structural bloat, Instalco has been actively addressing acute operational inefficiencies, such as the severe resource under-absorption that severely compressed margins in the Swedish segment during the summer of 2025. The company has also focused on normalizing its bad debt provisions, which were unusually elevated following customer bankruptcies, highlighted by a SEK 64 million write-down related to Northvolt.

The Inmatiq mation Start-Up

A critical component of Instalco's organic growth and future margin profile is the recent launch of Inmatiq, a new business area dedicated to property and industrial automation. Built entirely on the successful start-up blueprint used for the group's technical consulting arm, Intec, Inmatiq focuses on energy efficiency and digital system integration. The automation subsidiary rapidly scaled to 75 full-time employees by the first quarter of 2025. However, this rapid expansion resulted in initial under-utilization of staff, creating a temporary drag on consolidated group margins during its start-up phase.

Sell-Side Expectations and Financial Impact

DNB Carnegie notes that the closure of the eight underperforming subsidiaries alone is expected to systematically lift the group's adjusted EBITA margin by 35 basis points. The broker calculates that relying entirely on workforce reductions to restore the targeted 8% EBITA margin would require cutting roughly 250 employees, which is viewed as an unrealistically high figure. Consequently, DNB Carnegie expects the margin recovery to be driven by a balanced combination of targeted FTE reductions, increased employee utilization, and normalized bad debt provisions. While the broker previously cut near-term adjusted EPS estimates due to conservative assumptions regarding Inmatiq's utilization rates, expectations have stabilized as the automation business begins to identify broader commercial opportunities. DNB Carnegie estimates that if the Instalco 2.0 efficiency plan is fully successfully executed, there is a tangible 5% to 15% upside potential to their 2026 and 2027 EBITA forecasts.

Plausibility and the Bearish Perspective

Despite management's optimism regarding operational improvements, a skeptical short-seller could argue that the core tenets of Instalco 2.0 directly conflict with the company's historical DNA. For over a decade, Instalco's acquisition strategy relied entirely on promising local founders a high degree of entrepreneurial freedom and decentralized control.

DNB Carnegie explicitly warns that imposing strict, centralized operational mandates and rigorous Group-level follow-ups carries immense execution risk. If local managing directors feel micromanaged by the new country-level COOs or bristled by centralized KPIs, they may rapidly lose their entrepreneurial drive or choose to leave the conglomerate entirely.

Furthermore, aggressive FTE reductions intended to protect near-term profitability could severely hollow out local production capacity. A bearish thesis posits that this capacity destruction would leave the company structurally unable to capitalize on new project volumes when the Nordic construction market eventually recovers, permanently impairing long-term organic growth.
