Getlink SE Forward Guidance Assessment: €1bn EBITDA Target Credible But Dividend Policy Strains Cash Generation Key Takeaways

The €1 billion EBITDA target by 2030 appears credible but slightly aggressive, sitting ~2% above sell-side consensus of €983 million and requiring a ~3.2% CAGR from 2025's €859 million base.

Management's progressive dividend policy (€0.80 in 2026 rising to €1.00 by 2030) represents a 23-30% beat versus consensus but raises sustainability concerns given elevated capex requirements.

Capital expenditure remains structurally elevated at €170-220 million annually through 2032, materially above the ~€55 million historical average, compressing Free Cash Flow to an estimated €300-440 million per year.

Share buybacks are functionally blocked as Eiffage's 29.4% stake sits just below the 30% mandatory takeover threshold, limiting management's capital return flexibility to exceptional dividends only.

The €516 million ElecLink profit-sharing liability combined with the new dividend policy will consume an estimated 110% of projected Free Cash Flow over 2026-2030, forcing reliance on existing cash reserves.

A proposed ~200% increase in UK business rates represents a significant margin headwind, with management actively contesting the Valuation Office Agency's assessment ahead of a late March 2026 final decision.

ElecLink 2 regulatory rejection by both UK and French authorities creates execution uncertainty, while the potential €826 million investment requirement sits entirely outside current capex guidance, posing hidden risk to outer-year cash generation.

Revenue, Growth, Demand, and End Markets Rating: Yellow

HSR Targets: Management expects high-speed rail passenger volumes to grow by 2.3 million by 2030 compared to 2025. Beyond 2030, Getlink is preparing for a structural acceleration, targeting an additional 10 million passengers over the next decade to reach approximately 22 million by 2035.

HSR Conservatism: Sell-side analysts view management's 2030 high-speed rail target as conservative. Jefferies notes that the guidance for an additional 2.3 million passengers by 2030 actually sits roughly 5% below the prevailing consensus expectation of a 3.0 million passenger increase.

HSR Capacity: Eurostar recorded an all-time high of 11.8 million passengers in 2025, driven by the resumption of direct Amsterdam-London services. To serve long-term demand, Eurostar signed a fleet order for 30 firm and 20 optional Alstom Avelia Horizon double-decker trains. These units, which offer approximately 20% more capacity per train, are expected to enter service between 2031 and 2033.

Passenger Shuttles: LeShuttle achieved a market-leading 56.1% car market share in 2025 despite fierce ferry competition. The division successfully optimized yields, reporting a 3% increase in average Shuttle yield year-over-year. However, early 2026 volumes have been soft, with February passenger vehicle traffic down 6% year-over-year due to the calendar effect of an earlier Easter.

Freight Volumes: LeShuttle Freight faces ongoing headwinds, with 2025 truck traffic down 3% to 1.16 million vehicles. This decline was driven by a subdued UK economic environment, weakness in the automotive segment, and aggressive ferry pricing. Weakness has persisted into 2026, with January and February truck volumes declining 3% cumulatively.

Freight Outlook: Management expects underlying freight demand to recover alongside UK manufacturing and retail activity. The company anticipates that recent EU-UK sanitary and phytosanitary agreements, alongside a recovery in auto production, will act as core catalysts for general cargo and fresh food transport.

Ferry Competition: Analysts expect ferry operators to face mounting cost pressures that will force more rational pricing. Jefferies highlights that new regulatory requirements, such as the UK ETS scheme and rising port and wage costs, should push competitor prices higher, ultimately benefiting LeShuttle volumes.

Macro Tailwinds: Geopolitical instability, particularly the Iran conflict, may indirectly support Getlink's competitive positioning. Morgan Stanley points out that higher oil and bunker fuel prices would substantially hamper the cost base of ferry competitors, while the fully electrified Tunnel operator remains relatively insulated.

Border Friction: The European Union's Entry/Exit System (EES) will be gradually implemented across Eurotunnel sites between April and September 2026. Getlink has invested in digital controls, facial recognition, and smart border technology to mitigate delays and turn the mandate into a competitive advantage over slower ferry ports.

ElecLink Volatility: The electricity interconnector reported 2025 revenues of €225 million, a 20% decline year-over-year. This drop reflected both the continued normalization of the Franco-British energy market and significant technical suspensions of the cable between September 2024 and February 2025, as well as a brief outage in May 2025.

ElecLink Visibility: Despite recent technical disruptions, forward revenue visibility for the interconnector remains exceptionally strong. As of mid-February 2026, ElecLink had already pre-sold 81% of its capacity for the current year, securing approximately €242 million in revenue subject to delivery.

ElecLink Upside: Analysts view the interconnector's underlying market dynamics as highly supportive. Jefferies notes that current electricity price spreads remain materially above 2021 levels and well ahead of the asset's original business plan.

Europorte Stability: The rail freight division posted 2025 revenues of €172 million, representing 2% growth year-over-year. This expansion was driven by sustained traction activities across France and the targeted acquisition of Electrofer, which broadens Europorte's infrastructure management capabilities.

Guidance Credibility: Getlink is targeting €1 billion in consolidated EBITDA by 2030, representing a new phase of accelerated growth. Sell-side analysts generally view this top-line and margin trajectory as credible but slightly ahead of current market models. Jefferies highlights that the €1 billion target sits approximately 2% above their tracked consensus of €983 million.

INVESTOR DAY 2026

Breakdown of Eurotunnel's predominantly fixed-cost operating model

Historical Efficiency: The company has demonstrated a consistent track record of stringent cost control over recent years. The pandemic-era "Shield" program successfully delivered €40 million in structural savings between 2020 and 2022. This was subsequently followed by the Eurotunnel Performance Plan (EPP), which generated an additional €25 million in savings from 2023 to 2025 through lean management and procurement discipline.

Recent Opex: For the full year 2025, Eurotunnel's operating expenses rose by a strictly controlled 2% to reach €531 million. This moderate increase reflected strengthened operational resources for maintenance, expanded customs operations following the acquisitions of ASA and BIMS, and higher domestic taxation. These upward cost pressures were effectively mitigated by lower underlying energy costs and ongoing productivity enhancements.

Energy Normalization: Normalizing European electricity markets provided a tangible tailwind to the group's cost base in 2025. The decrease in wholesale electricity costs allowed Eurotunnel to lower its Electricity Value Adjustment (EVA) energy surcharge passed on to freight customers from €22 to €19. Overall, electricity purchases represent approximately 8% of the group's total fixed cost intensity.

AI Integration: To further drive operational efficiency, Getlink is actively deploying artificial intelligence across its maintenance and asset management protocols. mated health checks of infrastructure assets are being conducted in real-time to ensure 99.7% tunnel availability. These proprietary data-assisted decision-making tools are helping to continually optimize both capital investments and ongoing maintenance costs.

Asset Maintenance: Maintenance of the aging 30-year-old tunnel infrastructure and rolling stock remains a core operational expense. The company employs a centralized purchasing function and strict competitive bidding to manage sub-contracting costs efficiently. Furthermore, Eurotunnel has focused heavily on optimizing train maintenance by redesigning workflows and concentrating short-term programmed activities to limit downtime.

Supply Chain Risks: The wider macroeconomic environment continues to present supply chain vulnerabilities for heavy infrastructure operators. Getlink spends over €400 million annually with several hundred suppliers, creating indirect exposure to broader European inflation and potential material shortages. To structurally mitigate these risks, the group relies on a heavily localized supply network, with 69% of top-tier suppliers located in France and 23% based in the UK.

Business Rates: A significant upcoming headwind to operating margins is a proposed sharp increase in UK business rates. The Valuation Office Agency (VOA) has indicated a potential rateable value increase of approximately 200% for the Eurotunnel asset. Management officially considers this planned increase to be unjustified and is continuing negotiations with supervisory authorities ahead of a final valuation decision expected by late March 2026.

EES Implementation: The gradual implementation of the EU's Entry/Exit System (EES) between April and September 2026 introduces additional operational complexity and cost. Getlink has proactively invested heavily in digital border infrastructure to transform this regulatory burden into a competitive advantage over slower rival ferry operators. Despite these preparations, the full financial impact of EES staffing and processing on operating expenditure run-rates remains a focal point for late 2026.

ElecLink Profit-Sharing: The ElecLink interconnector carries a unique cost dynamic driven entirely by a regulatory profit-sharing mechanism. In 2025, Getlink recorded an €80 million operating expense provision for this expected profit sharing, representing an increase of 5% year-over-year despite lower division revenues. The total cumulative liability sitting on the balance sheet for this unresolved obligation reached €516 million at the end of 2025.

Profitability Expansion: The core Eurotunnel segment maintained an exceptionally high profitability profile throughout 2025. The flagship division reported a robust EBITDA margin of 55.7%, marking a 0.8 percentage point improvement over the prior year. The Europorte rail freight subsidiary also effectively expanded its margins, generating an EBITDA of €34 million on €172 million of revenue.

EBITDA 2030: Getlink formally unveiled a long-term strategic ambition to achieve €1 billion in consolidated EBITDA by 2030. This target represents an aggressive acceleration phase from the €859 million delivered in 2025, requiring an estimated compound annual growth rate of roughly 3.1% (calculated). Management insists that this earnings growth will naturally be delivered alongside a measurable improvement in the conversion rate of EBITDA directly into Free Cash Flow.

2026 Guidance: For the immediate term, management set a 2026 EBITDA target range of €820 million to €860 million. This baseline assumes an exchange rate of £1=€1.165 and strictly factors in recent prevailing electricity market prices for the ElecLink division. The midpoint of this guidance represents a slight decline from the 2025 actuals, largely reflecting the absence of a €55 million one-off insurance compensation that was received for ElecLink outrages in 2025.

Jefferies View: Jefferies highlights that the 2025 EBITDA beat was heavily supported by the ElecLink insurance payout, noting that core traffic finished the year at a somewhat slow pace. The analyst desk points out that the €820 million to €860 million guidance for 2026 implies a 1% cut to street consensus at the midpoint. However, Jefferies emphasizes that the €1 billion EBITDA target for 2030 actually sits roughly 2% above their tracked consensus expectation of €983 million.

Morgan Stanley View: Morgan Stanley views the 2025 EBITDA outperformance favorably, noting it was driven almost entirely by robust operational expenditure delivery at the Fixed Link. The broker observed that after a front-loaded first half, management aggressively tightened controls on wages, maintenance, and taxes in the second half of the year. Looking ahead, Morgan Stanley is seeking clarity on exactly how Getlink intends to offset the impending 2026 UK business rates hike and the expiration of ARENH regulated electricity tariffs in France.

Interest, Tax, and Other Below-EBIT Items Rating: Green

Leverage: Net debt ended 2025 at €3,392 million, representing a healthy structural reduction of €184 million year-over-year. The group's net debt to current EBITDA ratio successfully dropped to 3.9x, down from 4.3x at the end of 2024.

Liquidity: The company maintains an exceptionally robust cash position of €1,498 million, even after absorbing €314 million in dividend payments and executing significant early debt repayments during the year.

Debt Cost: Getlink's current average cost of debt stands at an attractive 4.75%, with the actual cash cost tracking even lower at 3.97%.

Maturity Profile: The debt structure is deliberately long-term, providing immense visibility with approximately 50% of the group's total debt maturing post-2040.

Refinancing: Near-term refinancing requirements are highly limited over the next five years. Management successfully issued €600 million in new Green Bonds maturing in April 2030, carrying an attractive 4.125% annual coupon.

Bond Repayment: Proceeds from the new 2030 Green Bonds, alongside existing balance sheet cash, were intelligently deployed to early-retire €850 million of outstanding Green Bonds that were previously scheduled to mature in October 2025.

Restructuring: Management explicitly noted at the 2026 Investor Day that broader, holistic debt restructuring is not yet economically palatable under current market rate conditions.

Credit Ratings: The group's financial solidity was validated by recent upgrades, bringing Getlink SE's rating to management's target level of BB+ at both S&P and Fitch. The Eurotunnel sub-group debt securitization vehicle (CLEF) was similarly upgraded to BBB+ by S&P Global Ratings.

Rate Exposure: The group is highly insulated against interest rate volatility, with over 75% of its total debt held at fixed rates. The risk of unfavorable rate movements on the core Term Loan is mitigated by fixed-rate B tranches, inflation-indexed A tranches, and fixed-rate C tranches.

Derivative Hedges: For specific tranches that will eventually revert to variable rates (C1a, C2b, C2e), Getlink employs comprehensive fixed/variable rate hedging contracts to lock in visibility. At the end of 2025, the fair value liability of these interest rate derivatives decreased by €150 million to €192 million, largely driven by the mathematical impact of higher long-term market rates.

Currency Mix: The company maintains a carefully balanced currency exposure to naturally hedge its cross-Channel operations, with precisely 42% of its debt denominated in British Pounds (GBP).

Tax Regime: Getlink's theoretical baseline tax charge is calculated based on the prevailing French corporate tax rate of 25.83%.

Tax Income: Despite strong operating profitability, the group actually recorded a net tax income of €4 million through the P&L for the 2025 financial year, following a €13 million net tax income in 2024.

Loss Carryforwards: The primary driver of this highly favorable cash tax position is a massive shield of historical carried-forward losses. At the end of 2025, the current consolidated tax group held €1.02 billion in cumulative tax losses that can be carried forward indefinitely.

Legacy Deficits: Furthermore, an additional €1.81 billion in legacy deficits from the old TNU SA consolidation group remains available to offset future taxable profits for specific subsidiaries, notably FM and Europorte.

Pillar 2 Impact: Exposure to additional minimum taxation under the OECD's GloBE Rules (Pillar 2) is currently estimated to be immaterial, as the group meets the core tests for temporary protection schemes across all operational jurisdictions.

Post-2052 Risk: Morgan Stanley analysts highlight a significant structural tax shift looming in the distant future, noting that the Eurotunnel concession effectively acts as two separate tax regimes.

Tax Step-Up: According to Morgan Stanley, after 2052, Getlink's effective tax rate will automatically jump to 59% as predetermined compensation for the initial 10-year concession extension agreed upon in 1994.

Jefferies View: Jefferies views the balance sheet structure highly favorably, noting that the robust cash position and manageable debt profile directly support the company's ability to step up dividend commitments without straining structural liquidity.

Cash Returns, Dividends, and Leverage Rating: Orange

Dividend Reset: Management announced a significant upward reset to the shareholder return policy, proposing a dividend of €0.80 per share for the 2025 financial year, payable in 2026.

2030 Target: The new dividend framework includes a formal commitment to a progressive annual increase of €0.05 per share over the next five years, targeting exactly €1.00 per share by 2030.

Consensus Beat: Jefferies highlights that this new dividend trajectory is a material upgrade, sitting approximately 23% to 30% ahead of the prevailing consensus estimates for the 2025-2030 period.

Cash Pile: The company maintains an exceptionally high gross cash position of nearly €1.5 billion, which historically operated closer to the €300 million to €600 million range pre-pandemic.

Market Disappointment: Morgan Stanley notes that despite the attractive 4.5% forward dividend yield, the market reaction to the 2026 Capital Markets Day was somewhat underwhelming due to the distinct lack of a share buyback or exceptional dividend announcement.

Eiffage Ceiling: A major structural constraint to any future share buyback program is Eiffage SA, which recently acquired additional shares in March 2026 to bring its ownership to 29.4% of capital and 29.5% of voting rights.

Takeover Threshold: Morgan Stanley explains that under French market regulations, a 30% holding triggers a mandatory takeover offer, meaning that any Getlink share cancellations from a buyback would artificially push Eiffage over this critical threshold.

Buyback Improbability: Consequently, analysts view substantial share buybacks from 2027 onwards as highly unlikely unless Eiffage specifically seeks a temporary regulatory exemption or purposefully dilutes its stake.

FCF Coverage: Jefferies calculates that the consensus dividend trajectory fully absorbs all projected Free Cash Flow generation over the forecast years, particularly as capital expenditure steps up for mid-life rolling stock refurbishments.

Net Leverage: The group's net debt to current EBITDA ratio successfully dropped to 3.9x at the end of 2025, representing a marked improvement from the 4.3x recorded at the close of 2024.

Capital Expenditure, Working Capital, and M&A Rating: Orange

Elevated Capex: Management formally guides to a sustained, elevated capital expenditure cycle of €170 million to €220 million per year between 2025 and 2032.

Jefferies View: Jefferies warns that this capex cycle represents a substantial increase from the ~€55 million historical average seen prior to the original ElecLink build.

FCF Squeeze: The broker estimates that elevated investments will compress underlying Free Cash Flow to a range of €300 million to €440 million annually through 2031.

Tunnel Modernisation: The bulk of current expenditure is directed toward Eurotunnel infrastructure, including the Passenger Shuttle Mid-Life Programme and ERTMS signalling upgrades.

Capacity Uplift: Management expects the ERTMS implementation alone to eventually unlock up to 20% additional Tunnel capacity following regulatory approval.

Opex Synergies: This heavy investment cycle is explicitly designed to drive operational efficiencies, targeting a 15% to 20% structural reduction in future rolling stock maintenance costs.

Execution Risk: The shuttle refurbishment programme has already faced tangible disruption, forcing Eurotunnel to reorganize the project following the termination of a core supplier's contract in 2025.

ElecLink 2: Getlink is actively studying a second 1GW electricity interconnector (ElecLink 2) to be installed in the South Tunnel in partnership with RTE.

Regulatory Pushback: Morgan Stanley highlights a major near-term hurdle, noting that regulators on both sides of the Channel have currently rejected the need for new interconnection capacity in the short term.

Funding Overhang: Jefferies points out that an ElecLink 2 project would likely require an investment similar to the original €826 million cable.

Capex Exclusion: Importantly, Jefferies notes that this potential mega-project currently sits entirely outside of management's €170 million to €220 million annual capex guidance, representing a massive hidden risk to outer-year cash generation.

Customs Expansion: Getlink Customs Services (GCS) is rapidly expanding its footprint through targeted bolt-on M&A to capture market share in post-Brexit administrative complexity.

Recent Deals: In 2025, GCS acquired French customs brokers ASA and BIMS for an acquisition price of €12 million, subsequently recording €11 million in provisional goodwill.

Geographic Reach: The division also acquired Customs 4 All (C4A) at the Swiss border in late 2025 and continues to screen for opportunistic purchases across fragmented European logistics corridors.

Europorte Consolidation: The rail freight division is similarly executing bolt-on deals, having acquired a majority equity interest in Electrofer during 2025 to strengthen its infrastructure management capabilities.

Profit-Sharing Liability: A massive working capital headwind looms in the form of the ElecLink regulatory profit-sharing mechanism, which accumulated a €516 million balance sheet provision by the end of 2025.

Cash Outflows: Morgan Stanley calculates that the combination of this looming ElecLink profit-sharing payout and the new dividend policy will consume 110% of projected Free Cash Flow over the 2026-2030 period.

Asset Disposals: As part of its broader renovation and concession management programmes, Getlink occasionally rationalizes its fixed asset base, recording a €21 million net loss on the disposal or write-off of assets in 2025.
