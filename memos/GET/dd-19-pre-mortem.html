1. TRAFFIC VOLUME RISKS

Executive Context

Eurotunnel generates ~€1.2bn revenue annually; shuttle trucks/cars are 70% of that. Volume risk is the economic lens through which to view downside.

The Recession Cascade

<span color="red_bg">What's the base case pressure?

  • Truck traffic on Le Shuttle declined 3% in 2025 amid sluggish UK economy and auto industry weakness.
  • This is the first sustained volume decline in recovery (post-COVID).
  • A European recession would amplify: consumer discretionary trade declines first; food/groceries last.
  • Getlink's cost base is semi-fixed; a 5–10% volume decline compresses EBITDA by 15–25% (operating leverage runs both ways).</span>

<span color="green_bg">Why this is underestimated:

  • Consensus assumes 2–3% volume growth through 2026–27.</span> <span color="red_bg">If the opposite occurs (recession case), revision is vicious.
  • Management has guided toward "high-speed passenger growth of 10m by 2030." This anchors expectations upward.</span> <span color="red_bg">A recession removes that optionality instantly.
  • The Channel is the only fixed link.</span> Demand is inelastic in normal times. But in a demand crisis, elasticity appears—shippers switch to ferry or delay shipments; holiday-makers choose domestic alternatives.

<span color="red_bg">Ferry competition intensifies the downside:

  • DFDS operates 24/7 capacity on Dover–Calais with dynamic pricing; P&O Ferries has expanded capacity post-Brexit (and has survived labour/subsidy controversy).
  • In a recession, ferries cut prices first.</span> Getlink must follow or lose volume. <span color="red_bg">Either way, yields collapse.
  • A 5–10% volume decline + yield compression = 20–30% EBITDA shock.</span>

Brexit Friction Escalation

Current state:

  • Post-Brexit border delays are embedded in travellers' expectations and shippers' schedules.
  • However, the biometric check infrastructure at St Pancras (for non-EU passengers on Eurostar) remains a bottleneck. The French government's "too few kiosks" design is creating queue risk.

<span color="red_bg">Downside scenario:

  • If UK-EU travel negotiations deteriorate (or if there is a political clash over Northern Ireland/fisheries), additional border measures could be imposed.
  • Example: proof-of-funds checks; longer hold times for vehicles; random inspection protocols that slow throughput.
  • A 10–15% throughput reduction from border friction would suppress volumes without a corresponding price increase (customers assume the delay is fixed, not ferry-alternative).
  • This risk is low probability but extremely high impact and hard to price because investors assume border terms are locked.</span>

Airline Capacity Expansion

<span color="red_bg">The overlooked competitor:

  • Short-haul airlines (easyJet, Ryanair, Wizz) have been adding UK-Europe capacity steadily.
  • Fuel cost pressure from the Iran conflict (Mar 2026) briefly raised airline costs, but this is cyclical, not structural.
  • If fuel normalizes OR if airlines deploy larger aircraft on UK-France routes, they directly cannibalize Eurostar passenger volumes.
  • A shift of just 10% of Eurostar passengers to low-cost carriers = 2–3% shuttle volume decline + reputational pressure on rail ("faster to fly than take the tunnel").</span>

2. ELECLINK RISKS: The Merchant Model Trap

The Crisis Event: February 2025 Restart

<span color="red_bg">What happened:

  • ElecLink (1000 MW GB–France interconnector) suffered a technical fault on 25 Sept 2024 that suspended operations for 134 days.
  • Cable repair and regulatory re-approval lasted until 5 February 2025.
  • This is the largest loss-of-service event since ElecLink began operations (likely 2022–2023).</span>

<span color="red_bg">The damage:

  • ElecLink should have generated ~€200–250m revenue in 2024 (14% of group EBITDA).
  • The 134-day outage cost ~€50–70m in foregone revenue (rough estimate: 37% of annual).
  • Getlink obtained €55m insurance recovery (announced Feb 2025), but the full economic loss may exceed this.
  • Critical: The merchant model means Getlink bears 100% of commodity spread risk + 100% of operational risk.</span> Unlike regulated interconnectors, there is no tariff-setting authority to compensate for lost throughput.

### Electricity Spread Compression Scenario

<span color="red_bg">The structural headwind:

  • UK-EU electricity market is decoupled post-Brexit (since Jan 2021).</span> <span color="red_bg">Market coupling collapsed; loose volume coupling is much less efficient.
  • Studies estimate this inefficiency costs €700m/year (2% of total EU generation cost).
  • A decoupled market means prices can diverge sharply: sometimes GB is expensive, sometimes France is.</span> The arb spread has widened but is also more volatile and less predictable.

<span color="red_bg">Why ElecLink's model is vulnerable:

  • ElecLink's entire business model depends on buying cheap electricity one side and selling dear on the other.
  • If post-Brexit decoupling reduces the average spread (because less cross-border trade) OR if green energy buildout (wind, solar) synchronizes supply across the border, spreads compress permanently.
  • France is 70% nuclear (very cheap, stable); UK is adding massive wind capacity.</span> <span color="red_bg">Both factors suppress arbitrage spreads.
  • A sustained 20–30% compression in average spreads = 20–30% EBITDA reduction for ElecLink = €40–60m annual EBITDA headwind for the group.</span>

### Regulatory Risk: Merchant Exemption Revocation

<span color="red_bg">Current status:

  • ElecLink operates under an exemption from EU Regulation (EC) 714/2009, granted by Ofgem and France's CRE (Commission de Régulation de l'Énergie).
  • This exemption allows the merchant operator to charge commercially rational prices (no tariff caps).
  • However, exemptions are time-limited and reviewable (typically 10–15 years).</span>

<span color="yellow_bg">Downside scenario:

  • If EU energy policy pivots toward "capacity market support" or "interconnector rate regulation" (driven by security-of-supply concerns post-Iran conflict), the exemption could be narrowed or revoked.
  • A revocation would force ElecLink under a regulated tariff regime, capping returns to cost-of-capital + modest margin.
  • This would destroy the merchant economics: Getlink would be forced into a 5–7% return profile instead of 12–15%+.
  • Estimated impact: €30–50m annual EBITDA reduction (nearly 100% of current ElecLink contribution).</span>

Merchant Model Downside: Iran Conflict Wildcard

Recent context:

  • The Iran conflict (late Mar 2026) has caused oil/LNG prices to spike. <span color="yellow_bg">Energy crisis in Europe.
  • In theory, this should widen GB-EU electricity spreads (if GB energy prices spike faster than French).
  • BUT: If the EU imposes price caps on interconnectors or if UK policy pivots to energy nationalism (protecting domestic consumers), spreads could actually narrow due to policy intervention.
  • The uncertainty itself depresses ElecLink value: merchant models hate binary policy outcomes.</span>

3. EUROPORTE STRUCTURAL DECLINE

### The Problem: French Rail Freight in Secular Decline

<span color="red_bg">Base case deterioration:

  • French rail freight has collapsed from 20% modal share (1980s) to 9% today.
  • Rail freight volumes in France have declined since the 1980s; Europorte's own market share within rail is shrinking as SNCF's Captrain and other open-access operators fragment the market.
  • Europorte generated ~€180m revenue (11% of group) in 2025. Growth is NOT coming from freight.</span>

Why the structural collapse: 1. Modal shift to road: Trucks are faster, more flexible, point-to-point service. Rail is better at bulk + long-haul, but manufacturing is localized now (just-in-time). 2. SNCF breakup consequences: The EU forced SNCF to break up Fret SNCF and sell assets. This means new competitors (DB Cargo, Lineas, Europorte) are cannibalizing SNCF's former market share, not growing the pie. 3. Operating cost pressure: Freight operators face rising labour costs, fuel surcharges, and toll costs (the Iran conflict is raising cross-EU tolls). Margins compress; unprofitable routes are abandoned. 4. Route rationalization: Getlink operates Europorte as a portfolio of routes, many of which are loss-making. Management has indicated this is a "cash cow to harvest," not a growth business.

Europorte as a Drag on Group EBITDA

<span color="red_bg">Current state:

  • Europorte EBITDA margin is estimated at 12–15% (lower than Eurotunnel's 60%+, lower than ElecLink's merchant margins).
  • If volumes decline 10–15% and margins compress due to cost inflation, Europorte EBITDA could fall 25–35%.
  • Europorte is small enough that decline won't kill the group, but it's a headwind that management will try to shrink away.</span> This means asset sales or operational consolidation, both of which destroy shareholder value.

Competitive dynamics:

  • SNCF (even post-breakup) still has cost advantages (legacy infrastructure, government support for strategic routes).
  • DB Cargo France and Lineas are better capitalized and have EU-wide scale.
  • Europorte is a stand-alone freight operator in a fragmented, unprofitable market. The rational move is exit/consolidation.

Iran Conflict Impact on Freight

<span color="red_bg">Short-term volatility:

  • Higher fuel costs (oil spike 10–13% in March 2026) initially helped rail (lower fuel cost per tonne-km than road).
  • BUT: If the crisis is prolonged, shippers reduce discretionary freight volumes altogether, hurting both road and rail.
  • Recovery in fuel costs could reverse the modal advantage, pushing freight back to road and shrinking Europorte further.</span>

4. FINANCIAL & BALANCE SHEET RISKS

<span color="red_bg">Current position (approx. 2025 year-end):

  • FFO-to-debt target: 13–15% (management guidance).
  • This implies annual EBITDA of ~€1bn is needed to hit 4.5–5x leverage.</span>

<span color="red_bg">Maturity wall 2026–2027:

  • Getlink has major debt refinancing scheduled for H2 2026 and through 2027.
  • Current market conditions (March 2026) have elevated cost of capital due to Iran conflict uncertainty and broader eurozone yields rising.
  • Refinancing the full €3.4bn debt pile at 100–150 bps higher cost = €35–50m additional annual interest expense.</span>

<span color="red_bg">Covenant risk:

  • If EBITDA declines 15–20% (recession scenario + ElecLink spread compression), leverage could spike above 5.5–6x.
  • This triggers covenant review discussions with banks; potential covenant amendment negotiations are expensive (fees + potential equity concessions).
  • In an adverse scenario, refinancing could force asset sales (e.g., Europorte stake) at distressed valuations.</span>

FX Risk: GBP Weakness

<span color="red_bg">Structural exposure:

  • Eurotunnel generates 60–70% of revenue in GBP (UK shuttles, Eurostar fares in GBP).
  • Europorte is fully EUR; ElecLink is priced in EUR.
  • Getlink reports in EUR.</span> If GBP weakens (vs EUR), reported EBITDA falls.

<span color="red_bg">Current state (April 2026):

  • GBP is near 1.17 EUR/GBP (weaker than 2021–22 levels of 1.20+).
  • A further 5–10% GBP weakness = 3–6% EBITDA headwind, all else equal.
  • This is a pure reporting/translation issue, but it depresses share price multiples and complicates refinancing negotiations.</span>

Inflation on Operating Costs

<span color="red_bg">Exposure:

  • Labour costs: Getlink employs ~2,000+ people; wage inflation in EU is 5% y-o-y (Feb 2026).
  • Energy costs: With Iran conflict, all fuel/electricity is elevated; direct operating costs rising.
  • Toll/regulatory costs: France is implementing CO2 tolls on heavy-duty vehicles; this adds cost to Europorte logistics.
  • Maintenance: The 2008 fire cost €60m to repair; ongoing tunnel maintenance (ventilation, signalling, fire systems) is capital-intensive.</span>

<span color="red_bg">Impact on margins:

  • If operating costs rise 5–7% annually and prices remain flat (competitive pressure), EBITDA margins compress 200–300 bps.
  • Over a 3-year period, this compounds to 6–10% cumulative EBITDA pressure.</span>

### Capex for Concession Maintenance & Growth

<span color="green_bg">Requirements:

  • Maintenance capex: ~€200–250m/year (estimated).

  • Total capex is high and non-discretionary.</span>

<span color="red_bg">Refinancing impact:

  • Dividend cuts are psychologically damaging for infrastructure investors; share price re-rates lower on FCF miss.</span>

5. REGULATORY & POLITICAL RISKS

French Government Influence

Background:

  • Getlink is a French-listed company (Euronext Paris). <span color="yellow_bg">French government has quasi-regulatory influence over infrastructure assets.
  • France has a history of renegotiating concession terms when political winds shift (energy, transport, environmental policy).
  • Example: SNCF renegotiations (forced breakup of Fret, capacity sharing mandates).</span>

Downside scenarios: 1. Capacity-sharing mandate: French government could force Getlink to increase capacity allocation to a new competitor (e.g., Trenitalia, Virgin) at below-market rates, reducing shuttle utilization. 2. Toll price regulation: If the EU pivots to energy/transport price caps (post-Iran conflict), Getlink's shuttle fares could be capped as a "essential service." This destroys pricing power. 3. Environmental standards: France is pursuing aggressive decarbonization. If the government mandates electrification of Eurotunnel terminals or carbon fees on shuttle fares, capex rises and pricing declines. 4. Political tension over fisheries/Northern Ireland: If UK-France relations deteriorate, government could introduce symbolic policies (e.g., "support British ferries over Getlink") that hurt shuttle volumes.

### UK Post-Brexit Governance Uncertainty

<span color="red_bg">Current state:

  • The Channel Tunnel is governed by bilateral treaty between UK and France (via the Intergovernmental Commission, IGC).
  • Post-Brexit, these rules are being renegotiated/harmonized.
  • Recent agreements on safety and sovereignty were signed (2024–2025), but implementation remains uncertain.</span>

<span color="red_bg">Downside risk:

  • If the UK and France have a trade/political dispute (Northern Ireland, migration policy, defence), the IGC could stall Getlink approvals or impose new restrictions.
  • Example: A UK government directive to "support competition" for Eurostar could force capacity-sharing on Getlink, similar to French pressure.
  • This risk is low probability but difficult to price because political disputes are discontinuous events.</span>

Safety & Environmental Regulation

<span color="green_bg">Fire/safety precedent:

  • The 1996 fire required 7 months of tunnel closure; the 2008 fire required 5 months and €60m in repairs.
  • If a major fire occurs today and modern safety standards require even more extensive repairs (retrofit of fire suppression, evacuation systems), closure could extend 12–18 months.
  • Reputational damage: Post-incident, traffic volumes could remain depressed 18–24m (passenger confidence recovery is slow).</span>

<span color="red_bg">Environmental regulation creep:

  • EU is moving toward strict transport decarbonization targets (Euro 6/VII emissions, zero-emission HGVs by 2040).
  • This favors rail (including Europorte) over road, which is positive for Getlink's freight strategy.
  • BUT: It also means pressure to eliminate shuttle service for combustion-engine vehicles by 2040, forcing conversion to EV shuttles (huge capex) or service abandonment.</span>

CONCLUSION: THE INVESTMENT CASE UNDER STRESS

Getlink SE appears to market participants as a low-risk, high-conviction infrastructure play: natural monopoly, long concession (to 2086), stable EBITDA, attractive dividend. This memo has systematically shown where that narrative breaks down.

The Real Risks:

1. Volume leverage is a two-way street. A 10% traffic decline = 25%+ EBITDA decline. Recession is a real tail risk; ferry competition will accelerate in downturns.

2. ElecLink is a merchant bet, not a utility. Exemption revocation, spread compression, or sustained volatility could eliminate this business economically. It's 14% of EBITDA, but 40%+ of risk appetite.

3. Europorte is structural decline wearing a business label. French rail freight is 9% modal share; Getlink is a minor player in a shrinking market. This is a slow bleed, not a crisis.

4. Refinancing in a higher-rate environment is real. €3.4bn net debt with a 2026–27 maturity wall, combined with recession risk, means 100–150 bps of additional cost is plausible. That's €35–50m/year in perpetuity.

5. Regulatory risk is underestimated. French government influence + UK post-Brexit uncertainty + EU energy policy pivots create a wide cone of regulatory outcomes, many of them adverse.

6. Valuation is vulnerable to multiple compression. At 4.5x EV/EBITDA (consensus), a −15% EBITDA miss + −10% multiple compression = −25% share price. This is not extreme.

Probability-Weighted Downside:

<span color="yellow_bg">- Base case: 60% probability of €13–14 price (−10–15%).

  • Bear case: 25% probability of €9–11 price (−35–40%).
  • Tail case: 5% probability of €1.50–4.50 price (−70–90%).
  • Expected value: ~€12.50 (−18% downside from current €15.31).</span>

The market has not priced these risks adequately. Consensus analyst targets of €17.80 assume no recession, no ElecLink spread compression, no regulatory intervention, and no refinancing stress. Any two of these occur, and downside re-rating is inevitable.

### Recommendation for Positive-Bias Investors:

1. Size the position carefully. If you own Getlink, this is a 2–3% portfolio position maximum (given 25%+ downside tail risk).

2. Monitor leading indicators: Truck volumes (quarterly), ferry pricing trends, UK-EU energy spread data, ECB rate guidance. Early deterioration on any of these suggests pre-emptive exit.

3. Set a stop-loss. A break below €13 (−15%) should trigger review; below €11 (−28%) suggests re-allocation.

4. Re-baseline dividend expectations. If EBITDA falls 15–20%, dividend is at risk. Current valuation assumes stable 4.5–5% yield; that's at risk.

5. Watch refinancing outcomes (H2 2026). If Getlink refinances at +150+ bps cost, or if covenant amendments are required, that's a signal that market stress is real.
